This reissue was one of THE marquee releases that I was looking forward to in 2009, and I'm not disappointed in the least. ONE FOOT IN THE GRAVE has always been my favorite Beck album, striking the perfect balance between his goofball Dada humor, experimentalism, folk-country traditionalism, and surprisingly affecting songwriting. It's sad, it's funny, it's dark, it's goofy, it's charming, it's weird - it's just a whole shaggy mess of great music. "He's A Mighty Good Leader", "Painted Eyelids", "Hollow Log", "I've Seen The Land Beyond", and lots of others from this record all hold high rankings on my list of favorite Beck tunes. It's been close to my heart ever since I first heard it almost 15 years ago (jesus fuck), and I will rep for it hard and without mercy.

So, a reissue with a whopping 16 bonus tracks is just about the best thing I could ask for. The bonus tracks are basically an entire extra album, and are every bit as good as anything on the original LP. If anything, they might even be a MORE consistent listen than the original LP. Every one stands up strong against its officially-released coutnerparts, and there are many, many lost gems here. The original single version of "It's All In Your Mind" absolutely slays the slick version that showed up on SEA CHANGE - the weird, droning open tuning that he uses on this version is just ridiculously heartbreaking. Calvin Johnson: 1, Nigel Godrich: 0. "Feather In Your Cap" is another song that Beck returned to later in his career with diminishing returns - the unadorned version here is as good as it gets. "Close To God" is a dark dose of heavy blues that uses Calvin Johnson's baritone to maximum psychedelic effect, and "Woe On Me" is a dustbowl-folk pastiche that would have been right at home among the country tracks on the official ONE FOOT album.

It's the best sort of reissue: one that makes a classic album somehow even MORE classic. And especially when compared to the disappointing, halfhearted reissue of ODELAY from '08, it's as compelling a reason as ever for Beck to finally start cleaning out his archives and getting his vast catalog of unheard music out into the world.

 hprill Feb 24 2008
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Released around the same time as Mellow Gold, One Foot in the Grave is something of an antithesis to that other album. Devoid of electronics, samples, or indeed anything other than vocals and acoustic guitars, this is about as minimalist as Beck would ever become. The songs sound pretty rootsy, and they manage to convincingly combine Beck's deadpan vocals and postmodern stylings with some very traditional folk and blues. "He's a Mighty Good Leader" is typically Beck, and yet it could just as well be some religious gospel blues tune from the 1930s. "I Get Lonesome" shows how intense Beck can become if stripped down to the bare essentials, just as "Asshole" shows that his sardonic humour works much better this way too.

Despite the sparse arrangements, all the songs feel fully formed; in fact there's a vibrance and intensity that he barely ever managed to achieve on any of his other albums. Due to the lack of technical gimmicks, this album allows a glimpse at Beck's bare songwriting talent, and what I see is impressive and delightful. For me, One Foot in the Grave is easily his best album.

Country Blues, Revisited
A lazily simmering album's worth of bluesy folk-rock songs ranging from the haunted and moody to the melodic and upbeat, you will either love or hate this one. The soundtrack to a dry hot countryside far away from any highway. A big, broken, dusty window into what bored kids did while hanging around in the early 90s. It held up really well, in fact, it aged perfectly, like good oak wood. I found myself humming the songs every time I would listen to them.
"They just blow it out their nose and say 'okay'". I like that Beck's songwriting on here is often more focused on mood than story. Therefore, the songs manage to be both simple and complex at the same time, catchy and with a touch of Dada, melancholy and existential angst that you can only find in the music of teenagers trying to make sense of their lives.
The opening track is just sublimely beautiful and prepares you for the record's signature sound. My favorite song on here, "Fourteen Rivers Fourteen Floods", is truly one for the ages, a casual lo-fi homage to the earliest known blues recordings, a lifeline to another soul.
It is an incredibly rich and atmospheric album. These magical soundscapes created by unearthed and fried guitars are awe-inspiring, transporting you to another world altogether. Heavily recommended.

I tend to conflate slacker rock with lazy rock. That is to say, music that is lazily put together and lacking in real insight. The lyrics are really where you have to pay attention because they can often complement the music in ways that a surface-level listener can't really appreciate. I'm still trying to figure out whether the experience is at the level it should be or if I'm simply a surface-level listener. I'll leave it up to you to figure out. It slots nicely in his catalogue as a more low-key endeavour, so I can appreciate it for that at least. Nevertheless, I still can't believe Beck made an album with ImmortalHD (just look at him vibing on the album cover). The 90s were a crazy time.
