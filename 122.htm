Oh my Prog-Rock fantasy... I... love you...
Published
Terrorizer Mar 01 2013
+10 Vote downBookmarkVote up
To tell you truth from the very start, I'm not a passionate classic prog-rock lover as I find most of its famed chefs d'oeuvre to be too sappy, languid and pretentious. I always thought about Yes as of the perfect example of overblown cheesiness, laughed out at Genesis inoffensive pretensions (but at least they have some interesting moments), liked some Jethro Tull but never was infatuated with their music, never cared too much about Van der Graaf Generator or Gentle Giant yet have some mild sympathies towards both, especially the latter (mostly towards 'Octopus' because even with all its weirdness that beast had some balls), and even if I respect what King Crimson have done on some of their albums I never felt truly attached to their music on the pure personal level. I can listen to much of the prog-rock widely proclaimed masterpieces with moderate sense of enjoyment (but sometimes with not less articulate sense of annoyance) but rarely I find myself deeply moved by this music. Well, my personal Floyd history is entirely different but that blasphemous things I've just said are enough to make my prog-rock credentials terribly suspicious. Yet I was always a passionate Camel fan since the time I've heard them for the first time in my high school years (to make things even more strange I must note that I never felt the thirst for exploring Canterbury scene in deep as I found it rather dull and superficial). And this, imho, is their best album and definite high-water mark for all the 70s progressive rock music.

What was that main factor which made Camel so special and so brilliant? Maybe, it's their sense of song's flow. Maybe, it's the feeling of something deeply emotional and resonating to one's inner self, conveyed in the majestic sounding sometimes soothing, sometimes lush keyboard sounds of Pete Bardens. Maybe, it's the fact that even being that lucid and airy these guys can rock out forcefully wheт needed. Maybe, it's the fact that they mastered the art of composing and intertwining dainty melodies instead of experimenting with some weird sounds for the sake of experiment. Most probably, this unique place they have in my heart is due to combination of all these facts and many others which I haven't mentioned. I can't point out the sole dominant feature which make Camel such a grandiose animal but 'Mirage' is one of my absolutely favourite albums.

From the moment when creepy, eerie and even slightly disturbing sounds of the intro to 'Freefall' make way for some high-octane spiraling riffing which sounds so infectious and driving yet canorous and strangely serene, you know you're in for the ride. 'Freefall' is a really upbeat and uplifitng piece for most of its length but that doesn't make it less appealing or trivial. It already shows impressive technical proficiency of Camel's classic line-up and not only famous Latimer/Bardens creative duo. I must add that very underappreciated rhythm section of Doug Ferguson (bass) and Andy Ward (drums) formed a steady basis for Latimer's and Bardens' soloing to shine on and without it Camel would not be the same. Also this steadiness shouldn't be misunderstood for simplicity as music here is really rhythmically challenging (yet in a very non-showoffy manner) with surprisingly inventive bass lines complemented by pleasantly fat bass tone and intricate jazz-influenced drumming serving as a nice example of one of the best (especially if we consider quality to 'fuzz about' ratio) rhythm sections of the day. Maybe, Bardens was not the best vocalist out there and his tone is too optimistic and standard-sounding in this opening piece and there are some other minor minuses in this album like some parts of 'Supertwister' being too light-hearted and flippant for their own good, 'Earthrise' being slightly droning in couple of moments and maybe music here would become even better if it was more often packed with additional punch on those mostly instrumental compositions. But aside from such kind of minor nitpicking this album is brilliant. Heartfelt flute soloing by Andy Latimer in the same 'Supertwister', his simultaneously driving and touching guitar parts, prevalence of exquisite melodies, so bright and emotional yet far from being kitschy or catchy in a poppy way (and that, in fact, was a sin of many 'progressive' artists of that era including even some Canterbury scene proponents), moving and grand keyboard sounds of Pete Bardens which were also very varied as there are typical waves of Hammond grandeur, brilliant Minimoog solos, some slightly Baroque sounding insertions of classical instruments and a whole plethora of other heterogeneous keyboard sounds - all of this implies that this album is unrivaled in its sense of melodic brilliance. And I really like Latimer's vocals - they are far from being pretentious and overbearing as this type of tranquil and soft yet emotionally rich baritone really fits the style of music presented. Vocals are rather sparse (and it's good) but the beauty of some of these vocal lines in 'The White Rider' and 'Lady Fantasy' is astonishing.

'Superwister' and 'Earthrise' have some jazz/fusion leanings and overall sit much closer to Canterbury scene than other compositions here and in fact are less successful than so massively rocking and grooving 'Freefall', not to mention band's epic masterpieces 'Nimrodel/The Procession/The White Rider' and closing number 'Lady Fantasy', also segmented into three parts. But even these songs can be characterized as excellent in their own right, especially 'Supertwister' with its serene melodies and aforementioned magnificent flute sounds. But 'Earthrise' is also very far from being mediocre as it has slightly folkish beaming melodies, fast 'fusioned up' technical moments and really interesting drum fills. Even if I find some Hammond soloing being kinda overlong and not that impressive on this composition, overall it was carefully composed and has enough moments of brilliance and tender charm to be thoroughly enjoyable.

Yet (almost) nothing can compare with any of these three-parts epics. In fact, these two songs are one of the best I've heard in all rock/metal music. Words can't do them enough justice, so just listen for yourself and maybe you will be washed away by their combination of placid bliss with rocking groove segued into truly remarkable and beautifully varied instrumental jams. I know I do sound like a 13 y.o. fanboy but mentally I'm still that 13 y.o. fanboy when I'm listening to this album. I just love it that much.

Self-titled debut was already a great album and I won't be willing to argue about 'The Snow Goose' and 'Moonmadness' undisputable merits but it was 'Mirage' on which guys from Camel definitely peaked. Even putting aside their second-to-none mastery of elegant and touching melodies which so brilliantly evoke profound melancholic and doleful but not depressing or desperate atmosphere, one can be sure of the fact that their ability to combine still hard-rocking and brimming with energy mentality (kinda lacking on subsequent releases) with their already fully established immense skill of involving multidimensional textures while avoiding things that could ruin songs flow and not coming close even to slightest shades of feeling forced and contrived, was enough to make this one of the best albums of the classic prog-rock era. If not the very best alongside with Pink Floyd's highest achievements. I can't recommend 'Mirage' highly enough so I shall just stop here and proclaim that you can't get any better than this with your progressive rock.

Let's be clear: "Lady Fantasy" is the anthem of progressive rock.
Published
Mindcrimewithin Feb 06 2010
+9 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Judging by Red, Relayer, The Lamb Lies Down on Broadway or this can we state that 1974 was some of the top years of prog rock? Quite possibly.

Camel had a very unique style not focused on technical playing of extreme instrumental intricacy although they were capable of that. The melodic and catchy arrangements are the most important element here, especially the guitar and keyboards sound amazing. The absolutely impeccable sense of melody is the driving force of this band. Both mainmen were good composers and singer: both guitarist Andrew Latimer and keyboardist Pete Bardens contributed to this sound.

We start with the melodic hit-like Freefall, than we reach a short instrumental, Supertwister (with epic flute soloing and smooth keys). The three-part follower is the centrepiece of the album: with the double intro The White Rider rules the land with the bass and everything - White Rider maybe the best song on here with the flowing synth melody dominating the song. Lady Fantasy conquers with the vocals of Andrew Latimer.

The production is also very vivid, it leaves space for the bass and the very nicely integrated keys, too and doesn't favour anything, it is very well-balanced and everything is very audible.

This band is one of the main influences of the mellow period of Opeth and stands its ground individually, too. Somehow for me it is a measuring stick for emotional and non-shallow symphonic prog bands later to emerge.

Greatest songs: The White Rider, Lady Fantasy
Least great songs: -

I've listened to this album many times before, wanting so much to like it, because I'm a huge fan of a Camel track known as "Ice" from a later album. It is one of the most perfect pieces of music I've ever heard. I'm interested in exploring more work from the band who created it. Mirage is their most acclaimed and popular album, and I found it safe to assume that it is their best, and therefore my best option of really getting into this band.

This album starts out promising enough. "Freefall" gets off to a decent start. It's quite catchy for the first couple of minutes. I don't like the guitar solo segment. It doesn't feel like it belongs. It changed too suddenly and drastically for me, and didn't seem to work. The longer it goes, the more it becomes a nuisance. It finally returns to the way it was at the start, though by this stage I'm just wanting it to be over. When it is finally over, I'd have to say it's a failure. Good parts of a song does not equal a good song. [4/10]

"Supertwister" begins beautiful, but not for long... about 13 seconds. Then it becomes something that I suppose is alright. I don't like it personally, but can't fault it. Then there's a sudden change to something quite different. And a sudden change to something very different. I really wish they'd take an idea and develop it. I don't really enjoy any portion of this track other than the first few seconds. I find it meandering and pointless. It's the shortest track off the album, at about 3:20 minutes, and it went about 3 minutes longer than I would have liked. [1.5/10]

"Nimrodel/The Procession/The White Rider" has a very nice beginning. It merges okay into a marching band section, then becomes something quite smooth and remains pleasant as the vocals begin. It changes into something quite different at about 3:45, which I don't particularly like. I'd rather the whole segment wasn't there. Some unpleasant sounds going on. It eventually becomes the song I like again a couple of minutes later, though I don't like the way they decided to get back into it. Could have been done better I think. Around the 7 minute mark something magical begins. I assume it is now "The White Rider". It's my favourite segment of the album. It's some of the best sounding music I've ever heard. That is one of the best bass lines I've ever heard. It's very enjoyable. The instruments all work together creating a nice atmosphere. I want to give a song a decent rating. Mainly to justify to myself that it was somehow worth all the time I've invested in this album. [6.5/10]

"Earthrise" doesn't interest me. It's not that it's overly unpleasant. Just doesn't seem anything special. It drags on far too long. I do like the way it slows down with about a minute to go. It ended quite well actually. Just took too long to do it. There is no way I can substantiate why I don't like this. I simply don't like it. I'd much rather listen to most anything else. [2.5/10]

And it's all come down to this. This one last chance. "Lady Fantasy". It has to be one of the greatest songs I've ever heard, to gain the album a pass mark. I don't like the way it begins. I love the next little part though as soon as all the intro noise stops and it becomes something melodic. It doesn't quite lead to what I would hope it would from something that sounded so good. It becomes something I don't think is special, nor anything bad as the vocals do their thing. And, crikey. There goes one of those sudden changes again. It becomes something more upbeat to incorporate some soloing. Shit. It's become slow again, very suddenly. It refuses to settle into something. It's very frustrating. 6 minutes now. There's another drastic change. 6:30... And finally, it's sounding spectacular. This is what I want to hear. It's beautiful. It's building very nicely. Vocals come in without it having to become something drastically different, which is nice. The next change I like. It changes the mood of the music drastically, but it's been building up for something to happen for some time now, and I think it works this time. At the 10 minute mark, I quite like what I'm hearing. It goes on a bit too long though. Finally it begins moving again, and in a good way, before it becomes the very pleasant melody that made an appearance within the first minute of the song, which closes the song and the album. I reckon a really good song could be written around that melody. It's a really nice sound. I would have liked to hear them develop that idea into something special, similar to what they later did with the basic melody "Ice" is built on. I can't give this song more than 5 out of 10. If I only like half a song, then it's failed as a song. Too many ideas I don't think work together how they're all crammed in, and overall, it's too long. Feels like a chore listening to it every time. I do understand how with repetition and familiarity, all those sudden changes could seem perfect to someone. [3.5/10]

I'm not going to tally the scores to give a final mathematical score. That would be meaningless. Especially given that the tracks are of such different durations, and given that each score itself holds no significance. Overall I can conclude that the album is a failure in that I don't personally like it. That many people do like it, is also meaningless to me. My overall recollection of the album after letting it play though, is that I only liked portions of songs. There wasn't even one song that didn't disappoint me. An album with some songs that have good parts, does not equal a good album. It's rather typical of my experiences with Progressive Rock. I'd much rather a band focus on writing good songs, instead of just trying to be clever. I prefer some of their later albums where they did mature in that way. Everything I admire so much about the majestic simplicity and intensity of "Ice" is not found here. If all I had heard of this band was this album, I don't think I'd bother exploring them further.

A seminal moment in rock history. Singularly magnificent and criminally overlooked at the time of release, this is one of the finest albums ever recorded.
Published
CVoss Mar 14 2006
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Mirage is such a wonderful album from Camel, a band who was coming of age as a more "undergound" progressive rock band (not that prog was radio-ready) on their second release. Camel excels here with a tight variety of compositions, and you can see so much potential coming off this album that their debut only showed so much of. Even the vocals, though never the highlight of the band, are more accentuated and not as weak this time around. From the beginning, "Freefall" displays a more challenging direction, especially in the middle break. Again, Peter Bardens' keyboards and Andrew Latimer's guitars play off one another so well, and I cite Bardens' lead vocal as the first evidence of my case above about stronger singing, even if neither he nor Latimer are powerful singers. "Supertwister" introduces us to Latimer's flute playing, and Doug Ferguson has a nice thick bass part to go along with this beautiful interlude-style number. I especially like "Nimrodel," Camel's first try at true extended storytelling, with frequent time changes and musical soundscapes, ending with a guitar solo which sounds like Latimer played this from a mountain top. The first ever Latimer/Bardens collaboration (which would become the cornerstone of the band) is "Earthrise," another instrumental featuring Andy Ward doing some excellent speed drumming portions, but the keyboard/guitar exchange continues to highlight. However, the major piece is the 13-minute "Lady Fantasy" suite, but unlike Music Inspired By The Snow Goose, there were two lyrical sections, but the music provides that atmosphere, and Latimer has some excellent guitar that people can air out to, especially about nine minutes in. If you have the remastered edition, the original mix of "Lady Fantasy" is even better than the studio one, complete with effects on the final guitar and keyboard solos, and Ward's drums sound slightly more live. Even if they weren't quite as regarded or famous as Yes, Genesis, or ELP, Camel preserves the progressive roots on this album, and it's still my favorite in their catalog. Remember that if you own this album, it's essential to try out Music Inspired By The Snow Goose and Moonmadness to complete Camel's prime trilogy.
