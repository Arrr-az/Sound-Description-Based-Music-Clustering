Back to the Beck we thought we knew. Mutations was nothingness, an empty posturing fart, far as I'm concerned anyway bleh. If there's a reason Beck warrants my attention it's not for being a singer-songwriter, it's for his genre mashing strangeness and the interesting things that result from it. So forget Mutations even happened, this is the proper sequel to Mellow Gold and Odelay. It lacks a bit of focus though, because while Beck always seemed to go for everything and anything he could this never meant a total lack of keeping a centerpoint. Mellow Gold's material all was clearly related insomuch as it was stitched together from the same Frankenstein mix. Odelay had the Dust Brothers to keep track of the zany directions, who does Midnite Vultures have? What does it have? It has lots of flavor and color that much if for sure. See, I do remember the late 90's, very well in fact. It was around 97' that my young mind became aware of popular music for the first real time...oh the late 90's. I don't like them much. Hip-Hop was at it's weakest, and mainstream Rock was very...ugh. After the charming mainline trends of the rest of 90's rock (Grunge, Britpop) things settled into a disgusting swamp of Post-Grunge and Nu-Metal, culminating in the third and final Woodstock where things went to shit as if symbolizing the juvenile garbage people were listening to. But pop music from the late 90's? Well....it was goddamned stupid and silly, but charming it was! Boy Bands, Timbaland R&B, and all sorts of dance musics from Moby to Fatboy Slim. I don't even like it so much, but I can acknowledge it as awright. And Beck is participating in all that here, that swirling silly turn of the century techno stuff, and he's damn good at it I guess. I guess, it's such a mixed bag. Fun tunes but little in the way of really solid ones. The songs go in pleasant but they don't really stick, evaporating not long after. The staying power of this first two records is simply mixing. And once again Beck proves he has mixed results on doing folksy sounding stuff. When he dances around and is odd, he is good. His more ballad like stuff just....nothing man, does nothing for me. Thankfully this is far more of the former than the latter, and Beck even makes some good Hip-Hop tracks here! So while not quite up all the way, Vultures is worth getting for Odelay fans.

Rating: 3.5
Highlights: Sexx Laws, Mixed Bizness, Hollywood Freaks, Peaches and Cream

The best parties I’ve been to over the years have been relatively sedate affairs. A small and manageable group of us, clustered around a few tables in a restaurant, pub or in the relaxed surroundings of home, with any music being kept at a level where we don’t have to bellow over the sound system to be heard and allowing free flowing conversation with old friends, new friends and generally interesting characters.

Sure, I’m sure there’s much to be enjoyed from the no-holds barred, drink and dance until you collapse style shindig, but I’ve always felt somewhat out of place during those kind of festivities. To enjoy myself at a party I need to make a mental connection with someone and to do that we need verbal communication to be somewhat more sophisticated and personal than trying to desperately sign language that you’re off to the bar / lavatory / taxi rank / beat the DJ with a large piece of wood.

Maybe I’m not exciting enough, maybe I’m a little introverted sometimes (I’m certainly no dancer!) or maybe I just prefer to celebrate things in a much more relaxed manner to a lot of people. I like music, I like a drink, but what I like most of all is spending time with my friends and family. Maybe this approach to partying would have me labelled as a dullard, or uptight, but I’m set in my ways and I don’t like to be distracted from my primary partying purpose, to spend time with the people I love.

Apparently Midnight Vultures is Beck’s party album, but it’s not my kind of party album. It’s just too damn distracting.

Beck is great. We all know that. We also know that Midnite Vultures was planned as something to bridge the gap between The Beatles' white album and Metallica's black album. The artist himself referred to it in interviews etc as 'the beige album' and told us it would be 'a dumb-ass party album'. As I said earlier, we all know that Beck is great, and we can be pretty sure that when he sets out to make a party album, dumb-ass or not, said album will be well suited for the activity of partying. But what use is it as musical accompaniment to other activities? I've taken it upon myself to listen to Midnite Vultures while performing various tasks and evaluating how well the music and the activity are suited to each other.

*Doing the dishes: Very well suited. If Midnite Vultures didn't remove the tedium from the task, said tedium was certainly alleviated to no small degree, and I believe I finished sooner than I would have if this particular album hadn't been playing. The grime came off those pans like magic aided by these nocturnal scavenger birds.

*Manually sanding a small portion of my kitchen floor and applying varnish: Very well suited. Definitely a task that needs music, and of all Beck's albums, I think this is probably the one to choose if you ever need to manually sandpaper a small portion of your kitchen floor and apply a couple of coats of varnish. The first, faster part of the album makes the actual sandpapering a lot easier, and if you've finished that part by the ninth track, "Beautiful Way", you'll swear that the gently swaying steel guitar and slow rhythm section were made especially for swinging a paint brush.

*Trying to get an idea of what the French revolution was all about by reading a book (on the balcony): Not particularly good. In fact, even more distracting than I thought at the outset. Take it from me: Midnite Vultures is even less conducive to an understanding of the socioeconomic situation of late eighteenth century France than an understanding of the socioeconomic situation of late eighteenth century France is to an enjoyment of Beck's music.

*Folding diapers: So-so. This was the task I turned to when reading proved incompatible with Beck's ornithological album. In some ways like doing the dishes, in other ways not, folding diapers is probably too far away from the concept of partying to be a suitable activity while listening to Midnite Vultures. Maybe Mutations or Sea Change would be better choices.

*Working out: Well, duh.

Looking back from 1999, Beck's career path could never have been called normal. Five years earlier, Loser had skyrocketed him to alt-rock stardom with its playful mix of slacker Gen-X sarcasm and hip-hop beats that seemed, in some strange way, to preface the rap-rock revolution that would come later in the decade. The album it had emerged from - the predominantly anti-commercial, lo-fi Mellow Gold - had, on the back of Loser's success, gone platinum. Beck, ever the contrarian, had had a weirdo lo-fi folk record recorded with his friends in an Olympia, WA basement in his back pocket since before Mellow Gold had even come out, and decided to put One Foot in the Grave out as Mellow Gold's follow-up. Then, working with Beastie Boys producers the Dust Brothers (after abandoning sessions with his Mellow Gold producers Tom Rothrock and Rob Schnapf), he put out 1996's Odelay, a psychedelic tour-de-force which, while not a high-charting album, sold consistently well and ended up going platinum around the world. After this came Mutations, as much a seemingly retaliatory response to Odelay's success as One Foot in the Grave had been to Mellow Gold. Acoustic-led, mostly downbeat and low-key compared with Odelay's thick, dense productions, and produced by Radiohead producer Nigel Godrich, Mutations further cemented Beck's critical stature, receiving rave reviews from critics of the time.

From the moment you see the cover art for Midnite Vultures, you can see immediately that this is a step away from Mutations in every possible sense. While that album had a simple photo of Beck with the album's title on the side, Midnite Vultures, with art provided by all-around maverick and Boredoms frontman Yamantaka Eye, is, at first glance, potentially the worst album cover most viewers had ever seen in their lives. Featuring extremely garish neon colours, the cover art consists of a man in a strange and terrifying mask, a white jacket and hot pink leather trousers shooting lightning from his crotch towards a close-up of those same leather trousers, while the album's title - itself a laughable proposition - and Beck's name are shown in lights. This was not Mutations. What the fuck was Beck thinking?

However, the moment you put Midnite Vultures on, it's immediately apparently that Eye's artwork is actually perfectly representative of the sounds contained within. Sexx Laws, the album's opener and lead single, gleefuly takes refuge in the ridiculous, with Beck half-singing, half-rapping a kind of psychedelic word salad concerning various strange and sexual images. Sonically, the psychedelic patchwork continues, with hip-hop beats, horns, and a wild array of sound effects coming together to form something shockingly coherent. While I'm not going to go through this album in detail track-by-track, Sexx Laws is definitely a good introduction of Beck's persona is on this album - unabashedly sleazy, unashamed to be a weird horn-dog who wants to "defy the logic of all sexx laws". On Midnite Vultures, Beck is playing both Prince and Gene Ween at the same time to stunningly effective results. The rest of the album takes in piss-take rap (assisted by the return of the Dust Brothers), 80s techno, funk rock, and, on its stunning final track Debra, a tongue-in-cheek homage to ballads like Prince's Adore that manages to be both hilarious and yet still surprisingly heartfelt.

I don't see any major artist currently working who would choose to put out a record like Midnite Vultures today. Hopefully I'm wrong about this. Something this out-there, audacious, hilarious. Something which pokes fun at not only musical genres, but at the singer himself. Knowingly ridiculous and yet full-throatedly flying its weird, freaky flag. A glorious mess.

Beck's next album was Sea Change.

Just look at that cover art.
One of the more bizarre albums I've ever listened to for how good it is. Midnite Vultures sees Beck swing another direction into a sort of parody of the 70s that's performed completely straight-faced. Once again, the actual quality of the songwriting and performances here really help it rise above the concept, and his lyrics are just so off the wall it's sort of captivating.

I've always been a big sucker for 70s funk, so this album really grew on me as it nails that vibe while still being unique enough to not sound generic. Some of the interesting production and effects you saw on Odelay are back to give several songs a modern edge, though not nearly as overwhelming as it was on that album.

Lyrically, it's just a weird, often trashy stream of descriptions that don't have much to do with anything but still mesh together by sheer virtue of randomness.

Aside from the (relatively) straightforward 70s style songs, you have some weirder stuff like the feminine techno 'Get Real Paid', the absurd 90s rap sounding Hollywood Freaks, the odd southern Peaches & Cream, and the completely straight Beautiful Way that sounds straight off of Mutations. Every song on this is unique, and they all manage to hit the mark. He even manages to swing his baritone voice as high as it could possibly go on the closer Debra, based on Bowie's "Win".

The first time I heard this as a teenager, I think it was a bit too weird for me to really gel with, but in hindsight I really appreciate just how unique this album is, and how impressive it is Beck managed to both think up music like this and perform it so well. High 8/10.
