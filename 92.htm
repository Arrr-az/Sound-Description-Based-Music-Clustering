Bowie: stalwart music icon, pusher of boundaries, "genius". To tell the truth, I was kind of over his whole gender bending schtick and androgenous foray into glam long ago. Still am, too.

Before Bowie idolators everywhere go all huffy, don't get me wrong, I liked/like old Bowie, and there's a real gamut of soaring songs born out of that peak Bowie period. I have the Bowie songbook for guitar from way back whenever, featuring all those great songs. We all know them, a couple of which are on parade on Diamond Dogs here, "Rebel Rebel" and "Diamond Dogs". But for me though, the rest of the album is just kind of 'there', merely a wash through of too-clever intellectual cheese.

Yes, clever lyrics and great theatrical arrangements and all. Except this type of Bowieness was shoved saturately down my throat by the previous generation since just after I was but a sparkle in my old man's eye. It's now a problem for me to try objectively assessing an old "classic" from an era I can't relate to. I can only rate it based on how it has melded into my world, a very different one to that long-haired love-in of the 70s.

Maybe it's supposed to but it doesn't totally lift me up to a higher plane of consciousness or anything. With a wailing, howling Dave stitching it all together, there's at least some tougher roll-on to be had in "Sweet Thing (Reprise)", and "Rebel Rebel", that whole "not sure if you're a boy or a girl" stuff right there at the close of side one is a great old singalong. But often this album feels a bit wailing and whiney. The strings on "1984", sounding like a rip-off of the theme from "Shaft", are so kitch, crappy and awful that I do wonder if Dave must look back on it with a curdling cringe.

In defence of Bowie in that era, the 70s contained a huge suck factor of dribbly awful shit so we're quite lucky it also generated icons like Bowie. You and I probably both know of at least one gushing revisionist baby-boomer burnout who tends to filter out the terrible crap from his/her musical youth. Perhaps the ubiquitous, played-to-death anthems from 70s icons, like Bowie, were a blessing. Diamond Dogs however was not the greatest offering from Dave around the 70s. Its occasional sparks of greatness are padded out with a lot of not-entirely-awful but rather uninspiring stuff in-between.

BradL Jun 02 2006
+3 Vote downBookmarkVote up
This is, I think, where Bowie's ambition first outstrips his talent. It's not so much his dumping of the Spiders -- his own guitar playing is surprisingly strong (the warped riff of "Rebel Rebel" sounds like something Keith Richards would have given his dirty eye teeth for) -- as the silly concept he's forced upon himself, part George Orwell, part William S. Burroughs, part Lee & Kirby. Too many of the songs here are fragments, bibs 'n' bobs roped in to advance the "story" (such as it is). The end result is while there are several great songs -- "Rebel Rebel", the swaggering "Diamond Dogs", the terrific Philly soul paranoia of "1984" -- the album as a whole is a drab, dispiriting listen. (Although "The Ever Circling Chant Of The Skeletal Family" deserves a mention as the proto-Gothic anthem.) Bowie himself seems to have realised he'd reached a dead end and would soon be making some ch-ch-changes.

Honestly I don't see the criticism this album gets because I think among Bowie's glam era albums that aren't Hunky Dory and Ziggy Stardust, it easily has the best crop of songs and theming for me and yet it lags behind Aladdin Sane on RYM. I think that while yes, it may not be as succinct as Ziggy Stardust I think Diamond Dogs still does a great job with its story. The songs on this album by themselves are so good from "Rebel Rebel" to "1984" and especially the 3 track run of "Sweet Thing", "Candidate" and "Sweet Thing (Reprise)". I think it is easily his second most ambitious and cinematic album after Ziggy Stardust and I really appreciate that aspect of it. The only song I was not the biggest fan of was "We Are the Dead".

Overall, an album is made by how strong its tracks are and this album has a really good crop of songs. I also think that Bowie's guitar playing was really good on this. I think this album is probably a victim of being like a slightly worse version of Ziggy Stardust but I think if you don't compare it to one of the best albums of all time you can see it is a pretty great album on its own right. I think that Diamond Dogs and Aladdin Sane are both super similar in the fact that they both feel like leftovers of Ziggy Stardust but to me this album was just more creative and unique, as well as have a better crop of songs. 8.5-9/10.

While this is as a comeback from the awkward buffoonery that was Pinups, in my opinion, this album actually has less to offer overall: The bluesier rock sections meander and trudge along at an ugly pace and the more extravagant sections (ex: "1984" and "Big Brother") just come across as aggressively over-the-top to the point where I can't stomach it. At the end of the day, Diamond Dogs just isn't for me and I'll take that to the grave.

Comrade67 Oct 10 2023
Vote downBookmarkVote up
The last of Bowie's glam records finds him inspired by George Orwell's 1984 crafting a loose set of thematically linked songs. Plays darker and more mechanical than Bowie's other glam releases befitting of the subject matter. Of the Orwell numbers, the love letter of totalitarian devotion "Big Brother" is the standout feeling both grandiose and heartfelt. However, the best material on the record has nothing to do with 1984 as the scorching riff of LGBT rocker "Rebel Rebel" cooks and the theatrical fan service ballad "Rock n' Roll With Me" shines. Undoubtedly a lesser and more difficult entry in the glam triptych; Bowie still closes out the period in style as he plots his next stylistic change. Remember to be patient with this one; it doesn't really kick into gear until the B-side where all the real goodies can be found.
