Black Sabbath
This album is truly amazing. It is way ahead of its time, and very daring for its time too. Not many bands would have the balls to put a bass solo on their first album, and also not many bands would dare to sing about Satan and a wall that turns your body to a corpse in 1970. Some of Tony Iommi's guitar work is just mind blowing on this. There is not one song I don't like off of it but my favorite would have to be Behind The Wall Of Sleep. As a bassist myself you just have to appreciate Geezer Butlers bass work on this album it is phenomenal. The bass solo in the beginning of NIB, also known as Bassically, is one of my favorite bass solos ever, and I have heard it and played it 1,000 times haha! Arguably the first doom metal song was made on this album with the title track Black Sabbath. Though the sound is more of a, hmm how should I put this, "jazz" type of sound I could say this album is one of the most influential to the genre of metal. It is amazing, I HIGHLY suggest it. Plus, I was able to buy it for only $5.

Dark Side of the Doom

I suspect that this album's ridiculously high rating here on RYM (4.19 at the time of this review) has more to do with its seminal role in the development of heavy metal than with the actual quality of the music presented. That being said, Black Sabbath is an excellent record that belongs in any serious 70's rock collection: not as essential as upcoming classics like Paranoid or Master of Reality but still highly recommended. The most striking thing about this eponymous debut is how the band's trademark sound appears almost fully formed. There's a marked indebtedness to 60's blues rock here that is largely absent on subsequent releases (most obviously on the Aynsley Dunbar cover "Warning"), but Black Sabbath still sounds utterly unlike anything else from early-1970. The eerily spacious guitar lines, occultist lyrics, and gothic ambience- this record must have come as a complete shock to listeners back in the day. Those defining doom metal stylings are put to magnificent use on tracks like "Black Sabbath" and "N.I.B.", which are amongst the finest cuts of the group's career. The only place where the album really loses me is on the aforementioned "Warning", which is a serviceable foray into "standard" bluesy rock but isn't really what I'm looking for when I listen to a Sabbath album (it's also rather tedious at 10+ minutes). "Wicked World" and "Sleeping Village" are decent placeholders but also don't really stand out, making for a back half that's slightly underwhelming and preventing a rating of more than 4.0 stars.

Still, my only serious qualm about my particular copy of the record (the North American CD version) is how it annoyingly combines a bunch of unrelated songs into two extended suites. As with a number of Sabbath's early albums, I think that there's also some grossly incorrect track lengths listed on the album's back cover. How lazy!

As with each of Sabbath's first five records, strongly recommended to any fan of classic rock. Just don't expect any radio-ready megahits like "Paranoid" or "Iron Man" here.

Favorite tracks: "Black Sabbath", "N.I.B."

While the definitive Black Sabbath cuts--like "Paranoid," "Iron Man," "Sweet Leaf," "Children of the Grave," and "Sabbath Bloody Sabbath"--aren't represented on their eponymous debut album, it is, in the long run, their best album. Recorded cheaply and quickly, Black Sabbath not only had to show that they had something new to offer. They also had to prove they had the musical chops. Given the opportunity, each member of the group shines. Rodger Bain's production is crisp; the sound in the room is excellent (especially in Tony Iommi's extended solo on "Warning"), allowing each member to shine. Bill Ward's jazzy drum fills are expert throughout (especially on "The Wizard"), and even bassist Geezer Butler is allowed to shine (especially on "N.I.B."). With the opener, the emphaticly titled "Black Sabbath," the group creates an entire genre. The importance of this track cannot be overstated, with its powerful deployment of tritones (also known as diabolus in musica, "the devil in music") and Ozzy Osbourne's lyrical invocation of Satan. The track is easily the highlight of the album. That being said, all of Side One is roundly excellent, highlighted by "The Wizard" and "N.I.B." Side Two suffers slightly as a result, opening with the least effective (though still entertaining) track on the album in "Evil Woman." It seems that one of Black Sabbath's musical influences here is Ennio Morricone, as Osbourne's harmonica riff on "The Wizard" and Iommi's acoustic figure at the beginning of "Sleeping Village" bear a striking resemblance to the composer's work on Sergio Leone's classic "Man With No Name" trilogy of films. The influence of the latter figure can be felt in metal all the way to Metallica's "Unforgiven" (1991). The closer, "Warning," is essentially a showcase for Tony Iommi, and he does not disappoint. Though the album has more jazz and blues flourishes than their subsequent records (and almost all subsequent heavy metal music), it still sets the terms for most of the best heavy metal music that would follow. Black Sabbath is absolutely essential listening for all heavy metal fans.

Of all the things one can credit the mighty and ever-looming influence of Black Sabbath for, an inescapable fact should also be hammered into the texts of HM history. Upon their inception, the band didnt have squat. Their singer, Ozzy Osbourne, was from a desperately poor Birmingham, England family. Their guitarist, Tony Iommi, had learned to play despite accidentally hacking the tops of two of his fingers off in a sheet metal mishap. Their drummer, Bill Ward, soon would evince one of the more harrowing cases of alcoholism in rock history, while bassist Geezer Butler was a reluctant, but enthusiastic occult student who would learn to play his instrument only a short time before the bands first recording dates.

And so armed with $600 and a truckload of ambition, Black Sabbath recorded their first LP and subsequently changed the face of rock music. Nothing like having it all on your side, huh? But for their part, Sabbath pushed aside the blues roots that dominated the UK rock scene of the day, and forged a sound built on a somewhat new idea: the riff. Other bands (The Kinks, The Who, and to a lesser degree The Beatles) had played with the concept of a guitar riff being the central building block of a song, but Sabbath put the weight of the entire band behind it, all instruments present firing at full power at a small but imposing collection of chords. And believe it or not, that innovation is the essential component and differentiating factor of heavy metal music, especially the more extreme sub-genres.

But enough theory. The album itself is remarkable as presenting a relatively young band with a quite defined sound and very deft interplay between the players. The opening alone can freeze blood in veins, as the sound of rain and a soft, distant church bell chiming set up the mood, just in time for a loud thunderclap to sound and THAT RIFF appears. Only three chords, but the chromatic order of said chords was known during the Middle Ages as diablous in musica. Playing it or writing music containing it could mean youd find yourself in chains with a hooded dude aiming a branding iron at your privates. But the tune our heroes were using it for was their theme song, Black Sabbath, an ornery tale of demonic possession that cant help but leave an impression (of whatever type) on any perspective listener. The Wizard is moderately less sinister, but still blasts some wicked (and loud) riffs, and some damn inventive (almost jazz-like) drumming from Mr. Ward. NIB steps up after some instrumental noodling mostly bass fingering, and fires off another immortal, signature Sabbath riff. A mystery for some time, the songs title was interpreted by paranoid Bible-humpers to be an acronym for Nativity in Black, which has nothing to do with the lyrics (more demon possession stuff), and was in fact titled NIB after the appearance of Wards beard! Only in the wacky world of metal. Much of the albums remainder, especially Warning, relies on the bands jazz-blues base, and not for the last time in the bands music some very swing-type time signatures appear.

All in all, Sabbath may not have invented volume, riffs, and power chords, but they did combine these ingredients into their broth to a level mostly never heard before in rock. This is not their best album, but in heavy metal history it is a Cro-Magnon man taking its primitive tools in hand for the first time for forge the future to whatever end it would lead to. Brave, innovative and loud as a bomb, the Sabbath era had undeniably dawned. On our feet, or on our knees it would flourish amongst us, unstoppable.
