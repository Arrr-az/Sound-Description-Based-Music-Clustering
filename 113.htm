Without getting into the ontological/teleological/astronomical origin of the Planet Saturn and its satellites, suffice it to say that Saturn wasn't always as awesome as it became in its peak. But it pretty much was by the age of 19.*

I cannot believe Kate Bush wrote melodies this unique and sophisticated at the age of 19, on her first album. I admire her for having the will-power to persuade unwilling muzak execs to release Wutherings Heights as her first single, instead of the label's initial choice, the much less interesting James and the Cold Gun.

I remember in sixth grade, I saw the video for Wuthering Heights on TV. I wanted to repress the fact that I liked it, because I was artistically dishonest at the time. But I liked it. I was transfixed by her movement and the melody. Kate Bush is pure energy, sonically, visually, sensually, and many other words you can insert and it won't really say what you want it to say so fuck it.

The Kick Inside, I believe, refers to the baby that was in Kate Bush's womb. The baby being a latent spiritually-endowed vision, the kick being the spark inside, as Mark E. Smith puts it, the call to push out thy creation into the world and be as artist and deity. The womb being the soul and life-force.

In Moving, the first song, Kate Bush already seems sure of her intent, saying she digs your scene "as long as you're not afraid to feel." Do not be afraid to feel indeed, for without feeling you are a poorly sculpted, unexpressive statue of yourself.

*Kate Bush is the planet Saturn, so just think about it. Consult Spiritof77 for further info regarding Mrs. Bush's celestial status.

Right, let's see if I can get through this review without mentioning Kate's age.

In 1978, Kate Bush (19)... Oh sod it. Right, try again.

In 1978, Kate Bush (19)... Oh sod it, it can't be done. Here goes.

In 1978, Kate Bush (19) stunned the musical world with an album full of songs about sex, menstruation, pregnancy and other womanly things like dance instructor Lindsay Kemp. It was widely assumed in the late 1970's a time when egg whisks were still believed to encourage illicit desires that no-one below the age of 21 could possibly have knowledge of such things, far less of Dave Gilmour, the last presumed sighting of whom turned out to be a representation of numbness erected outside Roger Waters' stately home.

Bringing in her mates, the Whales, to provide a few backing vocals, Kate stood her ground against the wicked ogres at EMI and insisted that Wuthering Heights be her debut single rather than James and the Cold Gun. The bigwigs looked at each other, smirked, and decided to let the little lady have her way. She'd soon see!

Wuthering Heights - based on a film and a novel in which the main protagonists spring across moorland calling each others' names whilst in the grip of uncontrollable passion soon caused rich embarrassment at EMI as it shot to the top of the charts. It was, remember, only a year since EMI had sacked the most important band since a lot of other terribly important bands, Antichrist & the Love Guns.

With their incompetence now fully on display, EMI decided that the only thing for it was to brazenly rebrand it as a virtue. Determined to recover their masculine pride, the powers that be at EMI lumbered Kate with a bunch of hackneyed old prog-rockers in a blatant attempt to exert patriarchal authority over the recording process.

The wizened old wrinklies did all they could, but to no avail. In spite of their best efforts to reduce Kate's songs to the level of out-takes by Yellow Dog, the young (oh blast, I've done it again) Ms Bush summoned forth the spirit of Boudica and Joan of Arc to vanquish her foes. As a result, The Kick Inside sounds like one divinely inspired woman and a bunch of blokes who are still squirming from having been kicked in the balls.

Kate would go on to even more inspired work, living a reclusive life in a cottage in the woods, occasionally suspected of being a witch, but eventually declared the Living Embodiment of Peculiar Musical Urges in This the Year of Our Most Venerable Jubilee and Sky Diving Over the Olympic Stadium by Her Maj.

The whereabouts of Dave Gilmour remain uncertain. Some claim that Occult Powers transformed him alchemically into the town of Slough, but this seems implausible since Gilmour was never that much fun.

Meanwhile the good folks at EMI, noting how Kate was now on fire, became terrified that she might actually be the Burning Bush that spaketh unto Moses, and that God might indeed be a woman. Consequently they vowed never to sign any band that could be construed as masculine ever again, ending up with Frigid Games before being swallowed whole by the Great Universal Gorgonzola.

The end.

I should like Kate Bush a lot more than I do. But this is the only album of hers I've heard that I really like. It includes 2 classics with "The man with the child in his eyes" and "Wuthering heights". The rest of the album is very consistent, not as great as the aforementioned tracks but never bad. Bush is supported by fine musicians, these are all well written tunes and she's no doubt a fine singer. There's nothing wrong here, but there's not enough songs that are truly great in my opinion. So 4 stars because of "The man with the child in his eyes" and "Wuthering heights", because it is a very influential album and because a lot of people like it so much. Still it's not really my cup of tea. Recently I saw the critically acclaimed _Aerial_ very cheap several times. But listening back to this and _Hounds of love_ made me realize that my Kate Bush collection is already more than big enough.

The majority of KB devotees seem to be perfectly comfortable about her 'prog' associations with the likes of David Gilmour or Peter Gabriel but I'm not. It's always struck me as fascinating how someone so great can have such appalling taste. For example, I find a good song like 'Experiment IV' almost unlistenable due to the sonic debt it owes to Gabriel. Why should I bother when I'm so easily aesthetically challenged? Because I think that The Dreaming and Hounds of Love are two of the best albums ever made.

In 1978, I remember this "amazing" new singer being raved about a few weeks before I actually heard 'Wuthering Heights' and when I did I was as bewitched as everyone else. But even then I had some reservations. No-one ever mentions the truly rotten band behind her. On first listen, when in blissful shock at her unique voice, on the arrival of the chorus with its Gerry Rafferty's 'Baker Street' soft rock tones, I thought, "Lose the band." I saw her soon after live on T.V. and the musicians' appearance confirmed the worst. There was a very suspicious look in 1978 - that of the prog-rock musician trying to look 'new wave'. School tie & blazer, 'straight' as opposed to drainpipe trousers, feathered mullet and new 'pumps'. Though this disguise might have got them onstage without too much '78 ridicule, their horrible prog-playing is unmistakeable. I still feel the occasional need to hear selected songs from her first three albums. Without fail, I groan at some muso's tasteless virtuosity as much as I delight in Miss Bush's talents.

Grampus Mar 10 2005
+13 Vote downBookmarkVote up
It's hardly a rare occurrence for the music press to promote an obscure band or artist as the next big thing, but it is unusual for me to take a blind bit of notice, and you can count on the fingers of one foot the number of times they're actually proved right. But there's always an exception to any rule and The Kick Inside by Kate Bush was it. This album reminds me of my ex-wife who was introduced to me as being the image of Bush. Of course, like most men, I was infatuated with the singer and stated flatly that there was no comparison. She immediately took umbrage and our relationship progressed from there - hindsight is 20/20 vision!

It is not stretching things too far to say that, at the time, Kate Bush was unique. Her vocal threatened the survival of wine glasses everywhere, her lyrics were deeply personal and she enhanced performances with her own brand of dance and mime. Not afraid to wear her heart on her sleeve, The Kick Inside can claim to be one of the most passionate albums ever written; "The Man With The Child In His Eyes", "Feel It" and the title track are based on raw emotion to a depth that Bush would rarely equal again.

Most consider the Hounds Of Love to be Bush's finest hour but, for me, this will never be beaten.

    The cover is inspired by a scene from Disney's Pinocchio where Jiminy Cricket flies up to a whale's eyeball. Kate oddly alludes to this film a lot in her music. This includes the whale sounds in The Kick Inside, the "Wish Upon a Star" interpolation in Lionheart, and even the donkey braying in The Dreaming. Besides, the cover is also a literal depiction of the song "Kite," where a giant eyeball in the sky beckons Kate to become a kite. The Asian typography is just some classic orientalism.
