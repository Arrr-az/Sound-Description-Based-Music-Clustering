Yellow Sub is really a half Beatles half George Martin record. The Beatles side, the "A" side, comes as close as you can get to a 5.0 without actually being a 5.0, while the Martin "B" side is a forgettable set of instrumentals from the film. But man, that "A" side is incredible. Let's break it down:

Yellow Submarine just had to be here, and in an interesting twist of fate, is actually a better song here than it is on Revolver. I mean, come on, it's the title of the movie fer crissakes.

Only a Northern Song is a great psychedelic freak-out that I listen to nowadays a lot more often than their more stories tracks.

All Together Now might be a little silly, but it fits perfectly - sandwiched between the heavy Northern Song and the quirky...

Hey Bulldog, another apparently underrated track from the record. I think Bulldog stands firmly entrenched among their best latter day songs, however, it's quite a few steps behind...

It's All Too Much. Holy shit, stop the presses! This here's one of the best tracks the Beatles ever released - period, and could be the heaviest, most psychedelic thing Harrison ever did. Fuck Something and My Guitar Gently Weeps, this is George's Beatle-opus.

All You Need Is Love. This anthem of the hippies is perhaps more symbolic of the Love generation than any other tune of its time, and truly is a terrific track with eccentric instrumentation and a who's who of side characters.

So there you have it, a side of nearly perfect music that deserves every bit of a 4.5.

And that ain't no 'pinion neither!

"Beauregard, where are with with our Beatles reviews?", I asked the rockin' housecat. "We've reviewed up thru the White Album" replied the cat. "On deck is Yellow Submarine, the Band's 1969 soundtrack to the animated flick of the same name". "Hey, I saw that movie when it was released", I commented. "It was OK, but nothin' to write home to mom about. Put the disc in the changer and we'll give it spin".

Bobo inserted the disc into the changer, pushed play and after completion commented...."I donno about this album. One-half Beatles music, the other half incidental film music composed by George Martin. I've got nothin' against the latter, I jus' prefer not to listen to it when I'm not watchin' a movie. I'll jus' have to keep my comments to the Beatles tracks. First of all, two of these tracks, "Yellow Submarine", and "All You Need Is Love" were previously released on Revolver and Magical Mystery Tour, respectively. Two other tracks are Harrison's, "Only A Northern Song" and "It's All Too Much", none of which are especially memorable. That leaves "All Together Now" and "Hey Bulldog". Now, these are great tracks, especially "Hey Bulldog" a song that flat out rocks. One jus' knows that this is a Lennon composition and is probably the best track on the album".

"Bobo, what's the call on Yellow Submarine?", I asked. The rockin' housecat replied...."I'll give it three and one-half stars because it's a Beatles album. Every official Beatles album release should be in your rock-n-roll collection. However, it is recommended that young rockers new to the band save this album as the last purchase".

Y'all are overcompensating on the rating here at RYM. I mean, don't get me wrong, half of a BEATLES album not staring the fab four is certainly a let down, but let's not act like these orchestrations by the fifth Beatle George Martin are something to scoff at. The strings sweep you away, and the weird noises (those very wistful strings laughing at you) are on brand for the Beatles image. Its not a case where they're memorable, all-time greats, no, but they're still solid pieces that I'm happy to have to change up a Beatles binge.

Not to mention that every Beatles track that is here isn't some of their best. Yeah, there's two repeats, but Beatles fans are no strangers to compilations, and the songs remaining are all some of the best. Only A Northern Song feels like George taking those complaints of being too out there and running with them to make a cool, synth-like tune, All Together Now is fun, EXTREMELY catchy track I fall in love with again every time I hear it, Hey Bulldog is iconic for a reason and It's All Too Much would be George's "within Beatles" magnum opus were it not for Blue Jay Way.

I just find the utter betrayal against this album to be a slight over correction to what are, at worst, inoffensive orchestral movements, when there is still so much here to gourge yourself on. Me personally, I'd rather have all these tracks actually on an album, instead of wasting away as some unknown track no one remembers in one of the anthologies, and if I had to listen to a movie (a very good movie, actually) soundtrack to get them, so be it.
