You know what, I know it's cliché to be madly in love with this album. How the fuck could I not? It's number 3 on this site. I know I'm not a special snowflake for thinking this is one of the best albums ever made. If you don't think so, or you don't like the Beatles, then good luck to you. I don't understand you and I never will, but I appreciate your point of view.

I don't think that any genre or band is inherently inferior to the Beatles. I don't think that preferring ELO or ELP or Megadeth or Merzbow or Bull of Heaven or Katy Perry to the work of four Liverpudlians in the 60s makes you bad. I think it makes you not the same person as me. I think that's okay.

I don't think the Beatles are objectively good. This is the only album of them I have rated 5 stars. The rest run the gamut from 4.5 to 3. They've made mistakes. Who hasn't? I would be lying if I said I understood the cult of personality around them. Really, this is what happens when a boy band, massively popular with the world, suddenly turns around and starts appealing to the critics as well.

But here's my problem. Their “critical” albums are ones I can't really get behind. I think they tried too hard. Their songs, individually, great. Their albums, a different story. Abbey Road feels so much like an intended masterpiece that it's somehow trying too hard. Sgt. Pepper is trying so hard to invoke a sense of ease and childlike glee that it dances around not really doing very much interesting and, hey, let's not forget that their “concept album” is basically just an excuse for historians to attribute yet another invention to them. The White Album is...well that may be the exception in that it tried too little and just involved throwing everything in there.

This is the one. This is it. You want to listen to the band which holds all the records for anything and everything? This is it. Saying it's perfect would be a sign of hopeless naivete. Any album with “Here, There and Everywhere”, “Doctor Robert” and above all “Good Day Sunshine” can't be perfect. The rest of the songs barely need mentioning. It sounds like a band maturing. I honestly wish I could love what they matured into with more passion, but there's no need. This is their masterpiece. It's number 3 on RYM's list. It's number 3 on mine.

Since the dawn of time, or at least since the birth of the internet, mankind has been obsessed with arranging things in order of preference. Womankind has suffered these obsessions to a lesser extent on account of her brain structure or some such psychological hypothesising, and on account of her having better things to do with the precious little time we get on this planet, but us man folk simply can’t get enough of it. Cars, movies, books, boobs, terrorists, nothing is safe from the list makers, and there is nothing that hasn’t been listed. Naturally, some topics are hotter than others; the best pebbles on a particular beach would be a debate that rages less frequently and with significantly less ferocity than the best goal in a world cup final or the best shaped bottom, but ultimately all of these lists have one thing in common, and that is that they are effectively useless wastes of time and meaningless once subjectivity is applied to the results.

With that in mind it could be argued that the Beatles’ Revolver is perhaps the biggest waste of time that there has ever been in light of the fact that “Best Albums Ever Made Ever in the World Ever” lists are one of, if not the most hotly debated topic in the field of lists. Music, by its very nature, is immensely subjective, non-quantifiable and immeasurable in terms of what it means to you or I or to the average man on the street (the women, again, are interested in music, but they have lives beyond their earphones and iPods which take in shoes and make-up and other unknowables), yet Revolver regularly tops these “Best Album” lists and there seem to be few people who would argue with its claim to the highest echelons of music nerd respectability.

But why not argue with it? Why not tear the damned smug smile off its face and expose it for what it really is, which is an album made by superstars who are grumbling about having to get out of bed in the morning, having to pay their taxes and yellow submarines of all things? Why not mention that Ringo has used studio technology to enhance the sound of his drumming yet he still sounds like a child clanging enthusiastically on a tin pot? When are we going to admit that “Love You To” sounds like “The Flower People” as sung by an early incarnation of Spın̈al Tap?

Sadly, for those of us with little or no action left in our lives and who feel compelled to impress our own personal favourite record on our fellow man, it is undeniable that Revolver certainly holds up as a good solid album with no messing around, no filling up space and a number of excellent songs which have proven to be timeless since their creation. [Correction; it remains to be seen whether we will still be listening to the Beatles in another 40 years or more and in fact the “timeless” title can’t be deployed until the end of days, but Revolver’s songs have certainly stood up to a barrage of new and often brilliant material from successive bands all wanting a little taste of what it’s like all the way up there in the clouds.]

In fact, any criticism that I can think of to level at the record can be turned back around on me and used in a positive way to further raise it in my estimations. For instance, the “Yellow Submarine” nonsense hippy rubbish argument doesn’t take away from the fact that it is a great, catchy song that adds a bit of fun to proceedings. The sometimes poor use of stereo (check the chopped cymbal rush on the intro to “Got to Get You into My Life” (right speaker only)) is not such an issue when you realise that the group and George Martin were pioneers of the studio wizardry that we often take for granted today. Besides, such irregularities can be seen as adding to the overall charm of the record.

At only half an hour or so in length Revolver fits snugly into a brief respite from the busy hustle of the average working day and can provide an extra injection of sustenance to see it through to the end. It is light and breezy for the most part, three minute pop jingles and happy melodies combine with some sentimentality from McCartney than can tickle your soul and make the crowd of nobodies that pass you on the commute seem like significant somebodies for a fleeting moment. Most prominent of these moments, “Eleanor Rigby”, has one of the most innovative string parts I have heard in a pop song that gives it a touch of the classical whilst still remaining hip and cool enough to seduce the trendy youth of the sixties.

Oh, to have been a youngster in the sixties, eh? I can’t imagine hearing something that was unlike anything that I had heard before - it’s like trying to imagine a colour that nobody has ever seen before - but when “Tomorrow Never Knows” first flew - literally flew - out of speakers across the world, the noises and the sensations it evokes, even today, must have been mesmerising, and I pity the poor fool who was tripping the light fantastic when he first stumbled upon that particular song. "Tomorrow Never Knows" still has an otherworldly sound to it, and the tape loops sound like screaming banshees circling in the fires of Hell, albeit in a nice, approachable, Beatleseque style. Many more sounds and little touches here and there would have been completely mind blowing to an earlier generation but to my generation, who have been there, done that and don’t necessarily want to have to go any further if we can help it, they are old tricks and far from revolutionary. Yet Revolver still has a fresh, clean noise about it and is a good, textured listen.

They’re lovely aren’t they, the Beatles? You know where you are with the Beatles. They do exactly what they say on the tin; they are the Beatles. And you can’t argue with that. Although I don’t rate Revolver in my top however many records – I like my top albums to posses the ability to cripple me emotionally, give me goose pimples, adrenaline surges, heart flutters, that sort of thing – I can’t deny that it is a hugely entertaining listen and an excellent way to spend thirty minutes of your precious time.

Sin duda una recomendación demasiado obvia para vosotros, pero para mí no más obvia que cualquiera de las otras referencias de esta sección. Supongo que no puedo añadir nada nuevo a lo que todos conocéis de "Revolver", así que mejor aplicar la capacidad de síntesis.

"Revolver" es un adiós nada elegíaco a los días de la inocencia, a la inmediatez de un pop tan elemental como magnífico, a los tumultos protagonizados por teenagers histéricas en conciertos inaudibles. Pero como es un amable adiós y no (todavía) un violento portazo, "Revolver" conserva las fragacias más arrebatadoras del encanto yeyé mientras se abre a nuevos mundos de experimentación sonora y a supuestas inquietudes cívico-generacionales que supondrán la transformación de The Beatles de fenómeno juvenil a referencia cultural insoslayable.

Pero en "Revolver", tal vez, son mucho más importantes los "hola" que los "adioses": hola al ensanchamiento de fronteras sónicas (genialidades de Geoff Emerick y George Martin; sorprendentes ideas de Lennon y McCartney); hola al LSD psicodelizante y a los viajes de la conciencia, hola a una tercera mente compositora (George Harrison, con su sitar y su misticismo de ley)...

Pocas veces el proceso de grabación de un disco habrá supuesto una experiencia tan reveladora y apasionante para músicos entregados a un afán obsesivo de innovación. Ya no se trata de abrillantar y hacer irresistibles unas cuantas píldoras de pop infeccioso, como fue norma hasta "Rubber Soul" (aunque allí el cuarteto ya ofrecía pistas muy sabrosas de sus futuras inquietudes). Se trata de incorporar el latido de los tiempos, de dinamitar límites y convenciones, de acarrear referencias inequívocamente adultas al catálogo de las urgencias juveniles. Y todo ello con unas arrebatadoras reservas de ingenuidad que ahuyentan todo asomo pretencioso, que dotan de genuina audacia a lo que, en manos más escépticas o tramposas, hubiera devenido experimentalismo hueco. Predomina la excitación de transitar caminos inéditos sin renunciar a la exhibición de dotes portentosas para esculpir melodías sublimes.

A partir de "Revolver", como de "Pet Sounds" o de "Blonde on Blonde", ambos también cosecha del 66, las fronteras admisibles de la creación en pop y rock ya no pueden ser otras que los límites del talento artístico.

Overrated is overused. That is one of the things that strikes me most about this forum. If someone doesn't like a highly-rated album they seem to immediately reach the conclusion that everyone else is wrong in 'over-rating' an album. They on the other hand, as the representative of true good taste, have finally figured out that thousands of other people have been making a mistake for the last twenty years: Revolver, or Highway 61 Revisited, or OK Computer really is just a pile of stinking dog turds after all.

To call something overrated is often really a form of conceit. It's about thinking you know better. However, the musical canon is not a lucky dip. Revolver didn't get picked out of a hat to win the position of one of rock's greatest triumphs. It reached that position by having been consistently admired by some of the world's best musicians and rock critics for decades.

It appeals to both the mainstream and those with an ear for new, innovative sounds. Yellow Submarine is both a lovely ditty that children can song along to and an extended metaphor for the joys of hallucinogenic drug-taking. She Said She Said is a dark trip full of hazy guitars and surreally disturbing lyrics, but you can still sing along if you want to. The bright, brassy Got to Get You Into My Life has druggy under-currents and is followed by the exotic, otherworldly droning of Tomorrow Never Knows.

Chuck D of Public Enemy said he considered this the greatest album ever made. The Beatles would hardly seem an obvious influence on a militant hip-hop band, but when I think about the adventurous production on Public Enemy's best albums, full of all sorts of new sounds and textures, you can see some parallels with Revolver's embrace of the new. Perhaps, rather than immediately making the 'I don't like it yet = overrated' equation, it might pay at times to dig a little deeper and try and find out what other people are hearing and you aren't.

Old Review 03/02/11: spoiler: click to readWhat an overrated record.

First let me explain what about this album is good:

The Good

Taxman is a great rocker that lampoons presumably, the British government then.

Eleanor Rigby is an amazing song that everyone should listen to. Sad, yet almost upbeat with the strings, and its spare instrumentation (just a few stringed instruments and Paul and Lennon).

Tomorrow Never Knows is a trippy psychedelic rocker that makes full use of the experimental techniques the Beatles were using at the time, and the way Lennon makes you feel like you were on acid.

Sad that all the good stuff is about 10 minutes.

The Bad

So the bad will include average stuff here:

And Your Bird Can Sing, Good Day Sunshine, Got to Get You Into My Life, and I'm Only Sleeping are all average. The production is ok.

The bad parts? All the other songs. Especially Yellow Submarine. Yeah call me a soulless bastard, but I do not like this song. It's overrated, it's boring, hell there are some covers I like that are akin to this, but Ringo's voice just can not fit this song whatsoever. All the Asian parts (a type of music Harrison would not use correctly until Sgt. Pepper's) just seems pointless, and there's a lot of filler in what could have been a good album.

The Ugly

ESPECIALLY the filler. Doctor Robert seems like the most pointless song ever, and For No One is also just pointless.

Overall

I have committed RYM blasphemy, but yes people, this album IS overrated. There's too much filler, too little great songs, and more average songs than you can shake a stick at.

But of course, the almighty Beatles are perfect aren't they? That's why this fucker still has an average rating above 3.50, despite it being one of the most generic pop records ever, and you know damn well know it.

New Review 07/21/14: Have you ever tried being a contrarian just for the sake of being a contrarian? It's fun.

Reading my old review, I kind of cringed. Did I really find this album that underwhelming? It certainly wouldn't have hurt to listen to it again back then, and considering I'm going on a Beatles binge today, now is the perfect time to revisit.

First, I stand by a few statements from my old review: Taxman is a really fucking great rock song, Eleanor Rigby is beautiful with the orchestral arrangements suiting it perfectly, Yellow Submarine isn't all that good (though it's grown on me slightly; in particular, the orchestral section after Ringo sings "And the band begins to play" is lovely), and Tomorrow Never Knows is one of the most original songs I've heard from the band, let alone of all time. The tape loops, Harrison's backwards guitar solo, John's distorted and hard-to-understand vocals, Ringo's consistent drumming, and Paul's bass all work together to create something very hypnotic. And it's a pretty quick song too (even if it's the second-longest on the album).

I was probably a bit too harsh on some of the songs here. While I wasn't particularly so hard on Here, There and Everywhere, it's better than I originally thought. She Said She Said is definitely better than I originally remember it being. It's a good rocker, and Ringo's drumming is pretty damn good here and is especially noteworthy, and in fact is probably the biggest improvement from how I remember it being. Good Day Sunshine is jolly and upbeat without being annoying, but it just feels too short to make its point. For No One also carries as much as emotional weight as Eleanor Rigby does, just in a different way and with different instrumentation. In fact, it works very well.

However, some opinions I did hold originally for the not-as-good songs still stand. Doctor Robert still isn't all that great, and just feels kind of average and filler-y (though definitely a lot better than I remember it being), as does I Want to Tell You. The piano riff in particular just sounds super-odd with how dissonant and out-of-place it sounds. I don't dig the sitar and tabla use on Love You To; it just feels forced with not that strong of songwriting to back it up, even if it sounds a bit pretty. And Your Bird Can Sing is just... okay. Though the guitar licks throughout are some nice flavoring. And finally, Got to Get You Into My Life just isn't that great either. The forced yelling of the song title during the chorus, as well as the pompousness of the sax and trumpets (which don't add too much) sounds strange.

Still, I was definitely way too harsh on it from before. It's a better album than I remember, and I can definitely see all the ways it's considered a classic. It's just not THAT much of my style from The Beatles.
