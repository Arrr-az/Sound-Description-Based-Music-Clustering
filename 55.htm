It's been said (as has everything else about the Beatles), but the Beatles are somehow overrated as a commodity, while vast portions of their catalog and many of their strengths are underrated by their fans. What's the best song on Rubber Soul? Not the hammy "Drive My Car". Not the decent weeper "In My Life". It's actually "You Won't See Me," which enters on a sublime piano/drums/guitar construction and glides along McCartney's confident, gorgeous melody. Listen to how the middle eight begins dark and inches its way to hope. Structurally, it's a pop buckyball.

How many perfect pop songs did the Beatles cook up from Please Please Me through Rubber Soul? How about "Wait", with John putting weird delay (a symbol for impatience?) and a fatherly lyric to grand work. This refrain takes the opposite tack of the aforementioned middle eights: "Wait!" is ebullient, but quickly descends into gloom. The tension between hope and drear is incredible... just another remarkable Lennon/McCartney song.

Oh, and then we get "If I Needed Someone," George's flippant yet transcendant one-up of the Byrds. Those rough harmonies, insistent and unschooled, wouldn't have passed muster 10 years hence. Thank God for young instinct.

There's more, of course. "What Goes On" is a fine lil' skiffle/country number, "I'm Looking Through You" is another effortless Macca gem. Almost makes you forget the so-so numbers ("Girl", the dreary "Think for Yourself", "Run for Your Life"). After the next one, we lost the carefree, two-and-a-half-minute gems, which is a shame.

It sometimes gets quite impossible, juggling Beatles' albums in a quantitative list, trying to ascertain the merits of one over another. I really like Rubber Soul; in fact, I prefer it to Revolver, but I think Rubber Soul is more of a transitional album where the mop-top Fab Four turn into something extraordinary. Rubber Soul is indeed a departure point where one can look back (songs like "Michelle", "Wait", and "Girl" ), and look forward ("Drive My Car", "Nowhere Man" and "Norwegian Wood", for instance). The importance of this fundamental change in musical direction would not be fully recognized until their next album, Revolver, was released.

The dichotomy is particularly strong with John Lennon, where the maturity of his songwriting skills leap out in his paean to Dylan "Norwegian Wood" (with a nod to George Harrison for his first use of the sitar in a commercial rock recording) and especially the profound and beautiful "In My Life" (one of his greatest compositions); however, he is still mooning over a bitter teenage love affair in "Girl" and he sounds ludicrously immature in the oddly misogynistic and abusive "Run for Your Life". McCartney offers an acoustic based classic "I'm Looking Through You", but in one of the infuriating methods of crass record industry promotion, another great acoustic song "I've Just Seen a Face" appears on the U.S. release of Rubber Soul and not on the U.K. version. The intent for the U.S. was to give the album a more folk-bent in line with offerings from Dylan and the Byrds. McCartney also wrote the bright "You Won't See Me", and the album has one of my favorite Ringo Starr tracks "What Goes On" (with some great Nashville riffs). Not appearing on the album but released as a single, "We Can Work It Out" and "Day Tripper" clearly show The Beatles quickly entering the most creative phase of their career.

I was in HMV one day and a man approached me while I was in the queue waiting to purchase the remastered issues of A Hard Day's Night and Let it Be. He introduced himself with a nod before going on to state,
"My Papa bought Rubber Soul back in '65, and I'll tell you brother, that was the only Beatles record I ever heard for 30 years. But I'll tell you something else, it is was one of the best records I ever heard."

Dropping the doppelganger mop-tops for a somewhat hazier perspective of life, The Beatles burst through the hallucinogenic cloud to majesty a prodigious manifestation of psychedelic pop, soul and folk rock infused into their beloved signature pop/rock standard. Upon mellowing out in response to the heavy schedule that was harrowing down upon their soft machines, Rubber Soul was birthed. Rubber Soul earmarked the successful and final transition of folk music into the vein of the Beatles musical soundscape, as its successor will have its foundations solidly cemented in Psychedelic Pop; and from therein it's a mixed affair of flamboyant and varying influences and styles. Capable of spurring influence upon their contemporaries (Brian Wilson has stated this album was infinitely influential on Pet Sounds), Rubber Soul saw The Beatles iron out any creases that may have perpetrated their efforts since their debut in the music scene. The album was, by all means, flawless. The sound production was crisper, the arrangements were more sophisticated and The Beatles musical and lyrical creativity was really starting to flourish after their early successes served as the beneficial learning curve. Most bands may have scoffed up their success and flirted with their music in the same repetitive vein, but The Beatles were evidently explorers; they weren't prepared to settle on one fruitful oasis, they were willing to weave through the voids until they hit a planet of sound so majestic even a novice could be easily absorbed into the realm of The Beatles.

With Soul music booming in America, The Beatles, for the first time on record, incorporated the fabulous genre into the manifold, and as a result managed to spawn the bopping "The Word" and the cautious "Wait". The record was also notable for including, for what many claim was the first time on a pop record, a sitar in the gloriously charming "Norwegian Wood (This Bird Has Flown)", as played by an enthusiastic Harrison. A story birthed from an actual affair of Lennon's and Paul's observational imagination, the lyrics were Lennon's first real dive into more poet trances, as he details the story of an affair in a room strung out in Norwegian pine. The lyrics and music were penned while The Beatles were on holiday with George Martin and girlfriends/wives and after Martin injured his foot, Lennon would pamper him with an instrumental ditty that would become "Norwegian Wood", eventually developing a signature middle eight and a characteristic harmony. If possible, I highly recommend seeking the version of the song on Artifacts I - CD 2 - Beatlemania 1964-1965, which features the pristine sitar entrenched deeper into the sound. Stunning song.

Harrison plays as stridently formed as ever, producing two brilliant tracks, "Think For Yourself" and "If I Needed Someone", the latter boasting a particularly charming melody that just radiates brilliance; the chiming jangle of the opening chords (due to the key of A mixolydian) is a particular favourite of mine. While written by Harrison in response to The Byrds "The Bells of Rhymney", I can't help but hear the presence of "If I Needed Someone" in the Byrds "I'll Feel a Whole Lot Better". Either/or, I much prefer the absolutely breathtaking "If I Needed Someone" over both of the Byrds records, however "I'll Feel a Whole Lot Better" is rather brilliant (just to clarify appreciation here). "Think For Yourself" is a lot more brash with those zapping intro notes, and the short, sharp lyrics asserted by Harrison that really give the song a genuine wary characteristic. The two odes to females, "Michelle" and "Girl" are relatively gentle numbers, the latter very much superior to the former in my opinion. The deep breaths supposed to resemble inhaling in the chorus and the "dit dit dit" backing rhythm converted to "tit tit tit" marks an artistic turning point for The Beatles; enter the hidden messages and double meanings. While this may seem a bit immature, it's nonetheless a gem with it's German two-step interludes. It was also the last song recorded for the album. Great fact aye? "Michelle" on the other hand was composed in response to Lennon and McCartney's wavering nostalgia, as the two recalled a French-lyriced ditty Paul would sing at art school parties. The "I love you" located in the middle of the track are inspired by those in Nina Simone's "I Put a Spell On You".

"Drive My Car" is a groovy boisterous number that seems to roll stupendously of those chunky lead guitar notes as McCartney roars into life in this either simple crowd-pleaser or sexually connotative rollicking rock'n'roll. Heck, it even contains a choral closure of "Beep Beep, Beep Beep, Yeah!". How many artists have tried that in their career and pulled it off? Not many, well, at least that I haven't heard (whether it be by pure chance or over the sound of my laughter). McCartney's "You Won't See Me" is in my opinion the most underrated track on the album, those crashing beats before the conclusion of the chorus and the "La-La-La's" as McCartney switches from calm to contemplative is a truly mesmerising piece of art. The equally agitated and underrated "I'm Looking Through You" basically sums up McCartney's dissatisfaction at his relationship with Jane. Unfortunately for Macca, things would only go from bad to worse. But from anxious to relaxed, the absolutely beautiful "In My Life" seems to see all The Beatles shake any relationship issues as they reach sonic Nirvana in these peaceful nostalgic-contemporary see-saw through their exciting and entertaining lives. A stunning ode if their ever was one. Brilliant lyrics as well.
"There are places I remember
All my life, though some have changed
Some forever, not for better
Some have gone and some remain"

"Nowhere Man" is a hazy, soothing number which gently shifts you through the phases as Lennon tries to pinpoint this man in society. "What Goes On" is yet another Ringo venture into country rock, and most impressively he actually captures a glint of glory that completely eluded him on "Act Naturally". It's not great; but it's far better than what most of the miserable moaners make it out to be. "Run For Your Life" is perhaps my least favourite track on the record, and while it actually features Lennon at one his vocally finest, delivering an absolute pristine delivery, the rather bleak and blunt lyrics
"I'd rather see you dead, little girl,
Than to be with another man."
Very bitter disposition as Lennon arguably goes perhaps one step too many in a very jealously drenched direction, and it has one of the best choruses on the album. The theme might also be a bit repetitive, but I must admit I also enjoy Harrison's great playing on this track, very consistent; while Ringo's percussion is up to scratch as well. So, Rubber Soul isn't completely flawless, but it's a solid listen nonetheless, no matter what musical creed you debt yourself too. This is, by all means, one of the finest albums ever recorded. Well, at least it's the closest they ever got to recording, say, a Revolver ;)
