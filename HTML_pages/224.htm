Bob Dylan released Desire on January 5, 1976 and it would prove to be one of his best selling albums, reaching number 1 on the National charts while achieving double platinum status. The Basement Tapes had been released in mid 1975. Since this was an album of material recorded in 1967, Desire was the follow-up to the classic Blood On The Tracks. In many ways Desire was a more ambitious project as he would produce an album of rockers, story telling, and topical songs clothed in imagery and melodies. Jacques Levy would serve as a co-creator on many of the tracks. If there can be any criticism of this album, it would be the rotating group of musicians that never really coalesced into a tight knit working band.

Two long story songs appear on the album. “Hurricane” is a protest song about the incarceration of boxer Rubin “Hurricane” Carter. He had been convicted of murder in 1966 and quickly became a symbol of the civil rights movement. This song was very controversial when it was released. In 1976 Carter was re-tried and convicted a second time. Through a series of legal maneuverings a third trial became necessary in 1988 and when the state refused to prosecute, Carter was released. He remains an almost mythic figure. Dylan seemed committed to his cause but has not performed the song live since 1976. “Joey” was a biographical track about deceased gangster Joey Gallo. He is presented as an outlaw with morals. Noted writer Lester Bangs would take Dylan to task, line by line, for his praise of Gallo. Two wonderful love songs appear on the album. “Oh Sister” is about the fragility of love. Emmylou Harris provides some wonderful vocals on this track and many others as well. “Sara” is a personal song in the form of a tribute to his wife. It is basically Dylan in the confessional. She would file for divorce in March of 1977.

I have always been drawn to “Isis” which is a journey of imagery. “Black Diamond Bay” is almost a script waiting to be filmed. It explores the destruction of an Island from different perspectives including the Island itself. “Romance In Durango” is a return to the style of John Wesley Harding. It chronicles an outlaw and his lover on the run. “One More Cup Of Coffee” concerns a family of wandering gypsies. It is a moody and haunting tale set to music. “Mozambique” is a straight forward rocker with some lyrical imagery thrown in for good measure. Desire was an album of adventure. The songs are well crafted and Levy’s contributions fit Dylan’s style well. If Blood On The Tracks is rated 5 stars then Desire is very close at four to four and a half stars. It remains one of the important releases of the 1970s and is still worth seeking out three decades later.

I got a copy of this within a few days of its release: it was the time of my most intense teenage Dylanphilia. I had been listening to Dylan for a couple of years, since Planet Waves; since then there had been Blood On the Tracks, which quickly became my favourite Dylan album (and probably my favourite album); and I was spending my limited money on the back catalogue of 1960s Dylan albums. Of course I liked Desire – not as much as Blood On the Tracks, but that didn’t matter. Listening to it again now I can never be sure whether I am responding to the music or my teenage enthusiasm...this is problematic and means the objectivity of my views is dubious. Where most of the songs on Blood On the Tracks seemed to be about an internal world, here they are mostly external. (I have never read any of the mass of books on Dylan and don’t know anything about the role of co-writer Jacques Levy, but I am sure it has been well covered somewhere or other.) Many of the songs seem to be Dylan’s versions of Hollywood movies: Hurricane as Dylan’s Serpico, Romance in Durango as his Sam Peckinpah Western, Joey as The Godfather (I didn’t know about the controversy about this song for a while and if it is a glorification of a vicious mobster it undermines the moral force of Hurricane; at the time all I knew was that it was Dylan’s most tedious song since Ballad In Plain D); and there are those that seem to be adventures in exotic places, drawing on Joseph Conrad (the clue is on the back cover), Malcolm Lowry or B. Traven – my favourite of these is Black Diamond Bay, because it has a certain sense of humour and it finally exposes its own fantasy. But there are problems with Dylan’s famous lyrics: compare them to Joni Mitchell’s careful and artful lines or Randy Newman’s clever distillations, Dylan often sounds sloppy, as though they are the first thing that comes into his head and he never reworks them – of course, it could be argued that this is one of his strengths, it places him into the traditions of folk and blues where singers might well improvise words in performance or puts him among the artists of post-War American culture who strove for spontaneity, emotional directness and improvisation (e.g., Jackson Pollack, Charlie Parker, Marlon Brando and the Beat generation of writers) – the argument would be a little sloppiness is forgivable if it allows vitality. But listen to Sara (my favourite song on the album when I was a teenager): at times it describes Dylan’s love for his wife in vivid, concrete and vital images and details, but at other times it falls back on easy and lazy romantic generalisations...I’m inclined to describe them as clichés: the love expressed might be sincere but it is also hackneyed...which might explain their divorce the following year. But, ignoring the songs, Desire is one of Dylan’s most interesting sounding albums – it has none of the slick glibness that dominates his future work and has a wonderful roughness to it, the sense that the musicians have had a quick rehearsal and then throw themselves into it: I like the slightly ponderous bass and drums, and, most importantly, Scarlet Rivera’s violin constantly skipping around. And I like Dylan’s voice: whether it lifts in Hebraic cantillation, as Allen Ginsberg claims in the liner notes, I’m unsure, but I like the harsh strangeness of it.

"I rode back to find Isis, just to tell her I love her."

The follow-up to the epic and personal Blood on the Tracks has a sound that is the complete opposite of its predecessor. In just one year Bob's musical direction and songwriting topics turn a complete 180 degrees, he's back to being the voice of his generation and a love poet. He's not the bitter soul that he was on Blood on the Tracks. I personally am of the opinion that Dylan is at his best when he is a bitter soul, just look at Sad Eyed Lady, Idiot Wind, I Don't Believe You, etc etc. But this new sound is exciting and it is fuller.

A man who is all about the lyrics might find some disappointment on Desire, they are admittedly weaker than nearly anything Dylan had done to this point, but what Desire lacks in poetry it makes up for with its pure sound.

The chorus on Joey is abysmal, but the story is intoxicating and despite all my musical intuition telling me it's crap I can't help but be intrigued. I've heard the song more times than is necessary, or recommended for that matter.

It is impossible to write a review of Desire without mentioning Scarlet Rivera. Suffice to say, her work is superb and the way it melds right into Dylan's material is a tribute to her versatility.

Not every song is as good as Hurricane and Isis and any listener is weary by Romance in Durango. It would be a tiring affair if it wasn't for the wonderful sound of the record. Romance in Durango is one of the more forgettable songs of the record and of Dylan's entire career. If it wasn't for the weak finish this would be one of the great Dylan releases, but it just seems like the first half is really well done and the second side is completely half-assed.

Bob Dylan's _Blood on the Tracks_ album had served to re-ignite his career, and is widely regarded as the match of his mid-'60s pinnacle. Dylan rode this wave of new inspiration and artistic fervour with the follow-up, _Desire_, recorded during 1975 and released at the beginning of 1976.

Dylan wrote and recorded _Desire_ around the time of his Rolling Thunder Revue, and the album has a suitably ramshackle, gypsy-like vibe, with the rootsy, acoustic instrumentation and particularly the inspired choice of using Scarlet Rivera, literally picked up off the streets of Greenwich Village by Dylan in June 1975, on violin. The result is a collection of fine, melodic songs with their own unique sound among Dylan's catalogue.

"Hurricane" is the album's most famous piece, protesting the innocence of former middleweight boxer Rubin "Hurricane" Carter, who had been convicted of triple murder in 1966. Dylan's vocal delivery is impassioned and powerful, and the arrangement moves along at a brisk pace, Rivera's violin stabs adding a sense of urgency. The later "Joey" is a similar character study, as Dylan, joined on harmony vocals by Emmylou Harris, describes the life of deceased gangster Joey Gallo over a mammoth slow 11-minute epic.

The stunning "Isis" is an album highlight, Dylan uncharacteristically delivering his symbolic, engaging tale on piano. The arrangement is sleek and sensual, with Rivera's violin particularly enticing, and the melody is among the album's best. Dylan has been criticised as an unconventional and poor singer, but "Isis" is certainly one song in which, vocally, he shines. Other successful songs in this regard include the romantic, elegant "Oh, Sister," and the gypsy folk tale "One More Cup of Coffee," with its strange Middle Eastern-style melodic patterns, both of which feature effective harmony vocals by Emmylou Harris.

Other songs on _Desire_ have a distinctly exotic, travelogue feel. "Mozambique" is sunny and evocative, a pretty song that started as a game between Dylan and his _Desire_ co-writer Jacques Levy as to who could come up with the most "-ique" rhymes. The strong European flavour of "Romance in Durango" is a pastiche but an effective one, an unusually and suitably romantic and sensual sound for Dylan, while "Black Diamond Bay" is far speedier, another of Dylan's remarkable story-songs. The set closes with the wistful and personal "Sara," where the arrangement is stripped back to let Dylan's passionate, soulful performance shine. It's a beautiful, romantic, melancholy moment, with Dylan's wails of "Sara, oh Sara" among the album's most memorable hooks.

The music on _Desire_ is consistently entertaining, beautiful, melodic, and attractive. Many Bob Dylan records feature excellent lyrics but are sometimes let down by melodies that do not shine as brightly as the words; _Desire_ not only features imaginative and evocative lyrics, but the arrangements are unique and engaging and Dylan's vocals are well-judged and effective throughout. Credit must also go to Dylan's new collaborators here, to co-writer Jacques Levy, producer Don DeVito, to musicians Howard Wyeth and Rob Stoner, to the superb harmony vocals of Emmylou Harris and the standout violin playing from Scarlet Rivera, which lends _Desire_ much of its "travelling gypsy show" vibe. Today, _Desire_ is rightfully regarded as one of Bob Dylan's most interesting and unique albums.

Pyramids? Where the fuck ARE you, Bob?

Pyramids drops out of the sky two thirds of the way through "Isis," and stuns you with the thought that he was off doing stuff in the ten years since he last made albums with his eyes open. Mozambique, Durango and Savannah-la-Mar may have diluted some aspects of America's lightning rod, but all that was good is still there. Like for instance:

The tiny man bit the soldier's ear as the floor caved in and the boiler in the basement blew

The delivery of the lyrics vacuum-packed into each verse's third line in "Black Diamond bay" is one of those things that puts Dylan in his own universe. The song runs like an elaborate joke with a rambling set-up and a punchline that comes in from left field, in this case the wry closing cameo that casts a long shadow over the bottomless, hollow empathy available 24 hours a day on today's 'news' networks.

"Joey" is drudgery, despite the cute 'five to ten' lyric. The old gangster-with-a-heart thing seems a bit trite for someone with Dylan's credentials. It is all the worse for being in the company of "Hurricane," Dylan's last, isolated attempt to tell a story in the full armour of its political context. The barbs are more poisonous than ever - "you'll be doing society a favour/that son of a bitch is brave and getting braver" - and Scarlet Rivera's violin wheels around the edges, whipping up a madness completely out of step with the measured excoriations of Protest Bob circa 1964. Indeed, the music here may be the best since Mike Bloomfield's molten licks on Highway 61, and Emmylou Harris sings with Bob better than anyone else. Joan Baez never did have the reflexes, and came off like an oil tanker chasing a speedboat.

In all, too patchy to be compared to his high watermark, but better than anything he's put out since. File under 'just another anybody-else's-masterpiece.'
