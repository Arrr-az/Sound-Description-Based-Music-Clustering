Best Singer-Songwriter album ever
Surfjan Stevens is one of the best songwriters of all time, and he shows that in this record. The lyrics are amazingly profound and heart-touching. This LP talks about the death of the autor's mother in 2012 and the frequent trips Surfjan and her would have to Oregon in a way that really connects with the listener throughout all the songs of this record.

Apart from that, the instrumentation is really on point. The guitar melodies feel from another world and the vocals are superb. The production on this album is incredible also.

TheCunningStunt Feb 20 2024
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Fuck me, I'm falling apart
Since it was announced, Carrie & Lowell became arguably my most anticipated album in a year that's filled with albums that promise to be absolutely great. I love Sufjan Stevens and his music, Illinois stands as one of my favourite albums of all time. It's just one of those albums that I have a great amount of love and nostalgia for, the type of album that you've listened to so much that you've formed a profound connection with it and it reminds you of so many different times in your life, even times that were seemingly mundane and unimportant at the time. You know the one?

After the release of the sprawling, glitchy, electronic influenced The Age of Adz. I was incredibly interested to see where he'd go next musically. Somewhat surprisingly, he goes back to what he knows best. It's a musical shift where he goes back to his roots. That's often a phrase that often sets off alarm bells in my head and fills me with a certain amount of dread. So often when I hear stuff like that, I just assume that the artist or band in question are phoning it in and settling back into an aesthetic that they've already covered ground in. I loved that Sufjan Stevens had managed to incorporate different elements into his music and progress as an artist, but any such worries you may have about him returning to his folk roots aren't valid. He does it admirably and seamlessly.

Obvious statement here but usually with an album, the music is what's most important, but I actually connect with the thematic centre of the album maybe a little more. What the album is actually about is the thing that made me immediately connect to it as opposed to a melody, a lyric, a vocal inflection or any of the usual things. Prior to this albums release I read an article which I think should be a companion piece to the album itself. I believe this particular interview should be included in the album's packaging as the album's booklet. Now before I go any further, irrespective of personal opinions of the publication itself and although I personally don't really love it nor hate it, it should be ignored. It's about Sufjan Stevens, his story and his words. Click here to read it. I'm a little bit torn on when I'd recommend someone reading it because on the one hand I love experiencing a work and drawing my own conclusions, deciphering the thematic centre in my own mind and the more I read, listen to or watch a certain work the stronger the thoughts and feelings I have for it become. Whenever or if you decide to read it, either before or after listening to the album, I'd definitely recommend doing so.

Reading Sufjan Stevens talk about such personal things in such an open, honest and heartfelt way was incredibly moving. It really gives you an insight into Carrie and Lowell as an album and the man as a human being, as well as his upbringing. I imagine it'd be a good album irrespective of knowing the background of it, but I definitely believe it enhances it. Even though I recommend reading the article and Sufjan can say it better than I, I'll give you a brief history of the album and its conception. Carrie is Sufjan's mother, Lowell is his stepfather but he always felt closer to him than his own biological father. Carrie left him when he was only 1 years old and she suffered with alcoholism, schizophrenia and depression. His main and perhaps only real recollections of his mother were due to spending time in Oregon with Carrie & Lowell over three summers, between ages 5 and 8. She died in 2012 due to stomach cancer.

The album resonates with me personally due to my own fractured relationship with my dad who had always been emotionally distant, and selfish when he was at home. He left when I was very young, which even at a young age I could understand. I could understand people making mistakes and relationships dissolving, but the sadness, angerness and bitterness I have always felt no matter how much I have tried to suppress and ignore it involves what happened after. If you have children you should still have an obligation as a father and you should still want to be a father, irrespective of what happened between yourself and the mother. My emotions range from apathy, to a great sense of sadness, to melancholia and regret to outright anger and bitterness. I'm turning this review into a cathartic therapy session, but this is why I feel connected to the album.

Although the situations are certainly different, I can definitely relate to Sufjan Stevens and his experiences and feelings. I know that one day my father will die and I will only have very vague and esoteric memories of him. Much like Sufjan, the most vivid and pleasant would be through a few family holidays in Wales. Even though some of Carrie, Lowell and Sufjan Stevens' happiest memories are centred around their time together on those three summer holidays, this album isn't just an introverted, reflective homage to happiness. I can feel the full range of emotions including the upset, the hurt, the sadness, the feelings of nostalgia for any happy memories even if they're too sporadic. It's not sickly, sentimental or soppy. It honestly manages to capture the full range of emotions when faced with a troubled relationship and abandonment from a parent.

Though the lives of the people involved in the album's conception were much more tragic, Sufjan Stevens was able to say "It was in our best interest for our mother to abandon us. God bless her for doing that and knowing what she wasn't capable of" and that's a beautiful sentiment and have wished so many times it's something I could say. As long as somebody does their best, what more can you ask for? I hope when I'm 39 years of age I will be able to be as reflective, forgiving and mature enough to make peace with a relationship that fills me with such sadness. In the mean time though, this album is there and after only a few listens it already feels somewhat personal to me. The feeling of somebody else understanding, relating to and articulating how you feel about something so specific in such a beautiful and poetic way is an unbelievable form of catharsis.

Carrie and Lowell will be turning 9 years old very soon, and i have yet to find an album that expresses grief so profoundly, doing it in a way that's universal and yet incredibly personal. It's such an amazing mix of calmness and vulnerability put together that shows just how much that kind of pain is not linear.

Sufjan's lyricism always stood out to me, and on c&l it's far more hard hitting than on any other release of his. Paired with the melancholic instrumentals they create an intimate bubble that is able to completely move the listener. "Fourth of july" is probably the greatest example of said phenomena. You can't help but feel, however you define the atmosphere surrounding the song, you just feel, sucked into the tragic world it creates.

This album is without hesitation, a 5/5. There really is no replicating the unique experience of going through each track, it's just as beautiful as it is devastating.

Sufjan Stevens already released very intimate and personal albums in the 2000s, some quieter (Seven Swans) and some more playful and lush (Illinois), others experimental (The Age of Adz), but he never released an album as introverted and calm as Carrie & Lowell. It's his most melancholic album to date, and tells the story of him coming to terms with his mother's death. The songs can go from having a certain underlying sense of melancholy to being downright devastating, but it still ends up being very beautiful.

Lyrically this is already an incredible achievement, because the actual music is often not even that spectacular, the instrumentations are very minimalist, and the songs are carried by Sufjan's tender voice and his emotional lyrics. Even though these songs are so sparsely arranged, each element just works perfectly and guitar lines or small electronic elements come in exactly at the right time to create very immersive soundscapes despite being simple on the surface.

The best thing about the subtleties in the production is that it supports his very profound and personal lyrics. For many of them, I don't even feel comfortable writing about them yet, since there's just so much in there that I have yet to discover, but the one song that hits me the most is "Fourth of July", in which Sufjan brings the image he has of his mother into question, and there's a struggle throughout this song that's both painful and cathartic. In the end I maybe still love the production on Illinois a bit too much to prefer this, but it's still a near masterpiece of songwriting and one of the best album of the 2010s.

Highlights: Fourth of July, Blue Bucket of Gold, Death With Dignity

A Profound Journey Through Grief and Compassion: Sufjan Stevens’ “Carrie and Lowell”
My first encounter with Sufjan Stevens’ Carrie & Lowell was not long after its release in the spring of 2015, during a period of unemployment that I fondly remember. Back when I had the opportunity and the time to wander and contemplate life’s uncertainties.

The lead single 'Should Have Known Better' was what initially drew me in, and I decided to listen to the album in its entirety during a solitary walk. As I strolled through a nearby cemetery, the sixth track, 'Fourth of July' - a song which depicts a conversation between Stevens and his mother as she was dying in hospital - hit me like a tidal wave. Its haunting refrain, “We’re all gonna die”, shattered me, evoking a deep well of emotions that I could not contain. It was a raw, gut-wrenching experience, and I found myself sobbing amidst the gravestones.

Four years later, when my own mother passed away, Carrie & Lowell took on a completely new and profound significance. It was during that dreadful period of grief and personal loss that I realised the album’s astonishing ability to capture the essence of sorrow and longing.

My relationship with my mother was difficult, as is often the case with a single parent. She was an alcoholic, and that is what ultimately claimed her. At times, I felt let down by her, and our time together was marked by its fair share of hardships. But now, as I grow older, I just find myself consumed by sadness when I reflect on her life. The sort of sadness which stems from realisation.

Carrie & Lowell as an album has played a significant role in helping me come to terms with this complex interplay of emotions. Stevens’ introspective song writing and haunting melodies serve as a mirror to my journey towards compassion. In the album’s deeply personal narratives of family, addiction and loss, I see there my own experiences, and am reminded that life’s most profound struggles often see both love and pain woven tightly.

This is not just another release in an artist’s discography; it is a cathartic journey through the labyrinth of human emotions. It is a tribute to the power of music and how it connects with the self at its deepest and most vulnerable. Stevens’ delicate vocal delivery combined with his use of stripped-down instrumentation acts as a vessel for my own mourning, allowing me to confront the pain of my loss and the intricacy of my relationships both past and present head-on.

Eight years since that lonely stroll through the cemetery, Carrie & Lowell has evolved into far more than just a favourite album of mine. It has emerged as a companion that has helped me navigate the darkest corners of my heart. It has shown me the importance of understanding and forgiveness. And it now stands as a luminous beacon, reminding me whenever I return to it that beauty exists even in the most overwhelming sadness.
