p_q Jul 08 2007
+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
"You gotta have a problem if you want to mend the contraption"

I don't know why I'm writing this review, as it's become clear that those who appreciate the White Stripes brand of unreconstructed blues rock bluster feel that they can do little wrong, whereas the Cool Police will always sneer at them for having the temerity to actually be successful and selling more than half a dozen albums. Most of us that write reviews here on RYM are hoping on some level to influence whoever reads our random (and often ill-informed) ravings, but with the White Stripes the possibility of influencing the reader is greatly reduced.

Whatever the case, it's with no small amount of relief that the pessimism, marimbas and self-pitying whining of Get Behind Me Satan has thankfully been abandoned and that The White Stripes have returned with an album which reminds the listener what it was that made them great in the first place.

While Icky Thump (it's the first White Stripes album to have a title track fact fans!) isn't up there with White Blood Cells and Elephant, it's a solid return to blues, guitar heroics, willfully unorthodox drumming and a sense of fun. It kicks off well with the title track and continues The White Stripes tradition of having a killer opening track which sets the bar for the rest of the album, but like the band's lesser albums (De Stijl and the aforementioned Get Behind Me Satan), the rest of the album struggles to match the lead track. However Jack and Meg do give it their best shot and they do come close at points.

As has happened on their last couple of albums, Jack White has tried to reinforce his rock credentials by including a mini-epic on Icky Thump. Although "300 MPH Torrential Outpour Blues" isn't as tedious as "Ball And Biscuit", it underlines the failings of Icky Thump in that it's bitty, never seems to settles into any one mood and goes on a little longer than is strictly necessary.

It's not all bad news though, as "Little Cream Soda" and the deceptively brilliant "Catch Hell Blues" are strong tunes and although the Cool Police would happily write off "Conquest", "Prickly Thorn, But Sweetly Worn" and "Rag And Bone" as gimmicky novelty pieces (indeed they'd be happy to describe the band in the same terms), to my ears they're refreshingly enjoyable tunes that are paying no lip-service to indie credibility in order to score points with the band's doubters.

Actually it's possible to see Icky Thump as a concerted effort by Jack White to shake off the indie-rock tag. It's been given a fuller sound and a more lush production, with a number of overdubs bolstering the core of guitar, vocals and drums with various keyboards, bagpipes and trumpets. Meg White's drumming has significantly improved to the point where the sloppiness of her playing is no longer a talking point. Perhaps in all this increased sophistication, The White Stripes have lost some of the unique thrill of their sound (I for one yearn to hear the band once again stripped down and raw without studio overdubs), but after the disastrous Get Behind Me Satan, it's at least good to hear them back on reasonably good form.

Icky Thump is not The White Stripes' best album, it's bitty, loses focus too easily and perhaps just a little too much thought has gone into it, but it's still enjoyable and a better listen than what 90% of the same indie scene that Jack White has turned his back on will produce this year.

Perfect? No. A return to form? Not quite. An enjoyable thrash about? Oh yes. Better than long-time White Stripes peers The Strokes? The who?

"Who'd a thunk?"

hed skracha
For no apparent reason, over the past month, I've been hearing many good things about the song Icky Thump from a surprising variety of people in my life. It's a song that seems to be unequivocally one of their most popular. For some reason, I have been putting off listening to these guys in any capacity. Sure, I like the odd song here and there (Black Math is a certifiably ridiculous song) but an entire album? It just wasn't for me. Until last night! While perusing YouTube I see a video of Jack White performing the titular track of this album recently while on tour. I give it a spin, thinking "Fine, I'll give in." My reaction? Yeah, it's alright. Nothing particularly special and I can't quite understand why it's as big as it is, especially in the context of this album and the songs that can be found here.

There are songs here that are good, I will give this album that. Luckily, it's also a decent length, never overstaying its welcome. However, and it's a big however, this album is such a mixed bag. I think that it's more good than Meh overall, but man, it can be really Meh at times. I'm not a fan of the Tom Morello-esque wankery that can be found here and there. This might be a personal gripe but I just don't like Jack's guitar tone. It's so constrained and fake. When it works, however, it really works. So yeah, it's a decent album, with more hits than misses. I just don't think there's anything spectacular on here, unfortunately. A strange album to end a career on.

Rating - 6.5-7/10

MICTHESNARECORD SEPTEMBER DIVE: WHITE EPISODE 6: Icky Thump (2007)
This album proves that Get Behind Me Satan was just a hiccup. In comparison to that hot mess, this album really does feel like the natural progression for The White Stripes. It's loud, it's raw, but it has a lot more care put into song choice, lyrics and sequencing. Sadly, that doesn't save it from the fact that the songs are (at least slightly) worse than the best stuff off Elephant. I still enjoyed songs like "Conquest" and "Rag and Bone" a lot (Meg's voice can feel out of place in a lot of Stripes song but in this one she fits right in and it's great), but it just feels like they were kinda running out of ideas at this point? Like, it's a more organized album, but it's still their sixth album of doing the same thing in a row. I guess it would've been interesting to see where The White Stripes went from here, but this is their last album so I guess we're not gonna get to see that.

YAY: Conquest, Rag and Bone
NAY: St. Andrew (This Battle Is in the Air)

This is more like less like something like it I like it. I like this album.
I don't love it, but there’s a lot of good here. I know I opened my Get Behind Me Satan review by saying “everything feels like it’s been done before”, but that’s not entirely true of that album. Here, however, I don’t think it could be any truer. This is the most back-to-basics, garage-blues-rock banger mode White Stripes we’ve gotten since White Blood Cells, and that’s got easy appeal. It’s been well-established by now that Jack White can write that exact kind of song really well, and that he and Meg can play ‘em like a dream, and that’s exactly what we get here. Except for that one little twist, which is that these songs are long and slow, or at least they feel that way. Now I’ve liked plenty of the slower tracks on prior Stripes albums so don’t count that against me, but in this case some of these tracks just kinda drag on for no good reason. The worst offender, ironically, is 300 MPH Torrential Outpour Blues (try saying that five times fast), but the same curse plagues most of the album. The bat-out-of-hell energy of their earlier discography just kinda went away at some point, and they don’t seem to have been interested in recapturing that this time around, which is a shame because that’s one of the main selling points of their best work if you ask me. This project isn’t plodding per se, but at least their more plodding songs frequently amplify the punch to make up for it. This is somewhere in the middle of everything they’ve done before and it’s a disappointment for that. Even the big surprises like Conquest and the addition of bagpipes on a couple tracks don’t quite make up for the songwriting choices they’ve been paired with.

Honestly, as I’ve written this out, I’ve realised I don’t actually like the album that much, but I’m keeping that opening line anyway ‘cause it’s funny. There’s a bit of good here, but not enough for me.
