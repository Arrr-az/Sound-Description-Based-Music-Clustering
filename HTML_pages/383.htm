Favorite Roxy album by a mile and one of the great romantic sophisties, I love this album. I can't quite put my finger on why I think this, but Avalon strikes me as the type of album you put on really, really late. Like, so late it's early. Right before sunrise. An older bearded rocker guy I know told me that he's spun it zillions of times over the years after coming home from crazy gigs and parties. It just seems appropriate for the rising sun's beams to peek through the window and on to a trashed living room with a pile of ashes and cigarette butts in and around the ash tray as this album plays. I dunno where I was going with that. Hopefully you understand what I'm getting at. This is not for those late nights; this is for those seriously fucking late nights.

You gotta be in a very particular place to appreciate this album. Like, maybe it's late afternoon, maybe the sky has been overcast all day, maybe you don't know exactly what to do with yourself so you make yourself a cup of coffee and spread out on your sofa and think about the decisions that led you to the place you're in--

--or maybe it's the evening, you're waiting for the bus across town, it's kind of raining so everything's glistening but it's not raining enough for you to have to open your umbrella but you're still glad you brought it, you know you aren't going to enjoy the place you're going so you are resolved to enjoy the way there--

--or maybe it's late at night, you're on your third glass of red wine, you've turned all the lights on in your apartment so that you can fool yourself into thinking you aren't alone there, you're spinning around and you've forgotten that there's anything beyond the momentary, false, borrowed, euphoria you're feeling.

IDK, I wanted to give this another 4 because "While My Heart Is Still Beating" and "The Main Thing" are both kind of boring, but this is one of those rare album's that's more than the sum of its individual tracks, this is an album that captures something real and true in a way that cannot actually be attributed to anything concrete, and in a way that cannot be described--hence the vague gesturing above.

Roxy Music's enduring influence is underscored by their unwavering commitment to artistic experimentation, signature sound, and iconic album covers. The band's evolutionary journey unfolds in two distinctive chapters, with an initial period from 1972 to 1976 marked by a robust and rocking sound, followed by a transition to a smoother and more sophisticated style from 1978 to 1983. This metamorphosis finds its zenith in their final record, Avalon, released in 1982, a testament to the worthwhile nature of the dramatic shift.

The album's triumph lies not only in its compelling songwriting, exemplified by hits like "More Than This" and "Avalon," but also in the innovative contributions of producer Rhett Davies. The incorporation of electronic washes and reverb effects imparts a dreamlike quality to the songs, creating a confident and immersive experience that flows seamlessly. Instrumental interludes enhance the album's sensuous journey from beginning to end. Notably, the new sound allows Bryan Ferry to showcase his distinctive crooning style, adding an extra layer of allure to the immersive experience.

Despite achieving commercial success, Avalon transcends mere popularity, leaving an indelible mark on music and significantly influencing the birth of the sophisti-pop subgenre. What distinguishes Avalon from subsequent adult contemporary albums is its judicious use of electronics, ensuring the integral preservation of the spirit of the songs and the musicianship. This approach, along with Ferry's crooning prowess, contributes to Avalon's timeless appeal, steering clear of sounding dated in contrast to similar endeavors.
