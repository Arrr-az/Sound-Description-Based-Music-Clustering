The Arctic Monkeys new effort is a big step forward from their predecessors, although their new sound will probably prove to alienate many of the bands fans. The album itself is still quite strong although it does lack some of the quality found in the first two animals. Dangerous Animals is a lacklustre song that subtracts from the quality of the album while on the other hand Crying Lightning, Cornerstone and My propellor rank among some of the Arctics finest

Highlights: My propellor, Crying Lightning, the Fire and the thud, cornerstone, pretty visitors

EDIT: Since writing this review, I've found Humbug sitting in my stereo almost endlessly and the album has really grown on me. Apart from one bad song (Dangerious Animals) and a few bad lyrics here and there, this is a perfect album! The bass grumbles and moans and the guitars dance ethereally around the pounding drums, all supporting Alex Turners melodic croon. Josh Homme's production really helps accentuate the bands new sound, pitched somewhere in the middle ground of The Monkeys energetic beginnings, the sophisticated pop of Turner's side project the Last Shadow puppets and Homme's Queens of the Stone Age. If you don't like the album onh first listen, don't worry, it'll grow on you, just give it some time.

Highlights: My propellor, Crying Lightning, Potion Approaching, The Fire and the Thud, Cornerstone.

+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
If sophomore album Favourite Worst Nightmare was the Arctic Monkeys content to consolidate on the strength of the huge success of Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not, the so-called "difficult" third album Humbug sees the Sheffield band undergo the musical equivalent of gender reassignment. Hooking up with desert stoner Josh Homme proves much more than the simple recruitment of a "celebrity" producer, it signals a refusal to remain the darlings of the indie scene which, while not entirely successful here, augers well for the future.

Humbug is as much about the co-producer as the band. Arctic Monkeys haven't suddenly been transformed into Queens Of The Stone Age Mark II, but their different musical backgrounds meet and complement each other somewhere in the middle. On tracks like "Crying Lightning" and "Dangerous Animals" bass riffs rumble like stomach cramps, while "Dance Little Liar" sees Alex Turner languorously drone over a combination of lazy guitar twang and muddy drumbeat. The appearance of The Kills' Alison Mosshart has a whiff of inevitability about it, but the ghostly backing vocals she provides for "Fire And The Thud" are no less effective for it. Only the clumsy, rudderless "Pretty Visitors" and "Potion Approaching" sees the band falter and that's more through poor conception rather than overstretched ambition. Better is closer "The Jeweller's Hands" with Turner's effortless croon acting as the perfect counterpoint to the slower, pulsating backing.

Humbug is not all about Homme and the Monkeys. James Ford who produced their previous album, chips in with "My Propeller", "Cornerstone" and "Secret Door" and, to some extent, it's here the old band can be heard. Psychedelic leanings and a multi-faceted approach to the lyrics is readily apparent but, underneath, the same cheeky chappies who, in their earliest guise, prowled the dancefloor are still very much in evidence. I'm not sure why production duties have been split but it's hard to escape the feeling the band failed to commit totally to the new direction and sprinkled a few reminders of their old selves around to reassure the unconvinced it would take little effort to revert back to what was.

If an experiment Humbug was a brave move as there was no guarantee the switch would meet with approval. If a more determined effort to expand and develop Humbug is little more than a stepping stone. Showing promise and ambition is one thing, making a determined effort to carve out something new is a whole different ballgame.

If I hadn't already heard about this record a trillion times over from friends, critics, and fanatic forum posters, I would truly be blown back by this. So much heavier, lonelier sounding, darker/deranged... yup, Josh Homme's influence is very much felt here. It's almost like Britain's 2000s In Utero (okay, it's not nearly that ingeniusly anti-commercial, but still), in that regard: A massively popular band that had no issue putting its popularity on hold for side projects and sanity's sake (well, Dave Grohl was already plotting his exit, anyway...), and then they came back with something that's rather ugly. Sure, it's still heavily melodic, but the UK's always been allergic to distortion in their biggest bands, minus a possible few exceptions.

Truthfully, this album feels like much more of a drag... there's no, say, "Flourescent Adolescent" style bar song to switch up the mood--almost like The Beatles did with "Yellow Submarine" after "Here, There, and Everywhere", all those years ago--and the lyrics are much more direct, witty romantic imagery out and potentially down for the count. But this collection of ten never strays from Queens-meets-Stooges, so if that sounds like a party to you, go buy whatever brand of beer is in style over the pond, and pick this up from the local shop(pe) ASAP. You may have to have the liquor do some of the talking if you wanna perceive this as being as memorable as the first two LPs, though.

It's pretty much impossible to think of Humbug without referring back to Josh Homme's role as producer - his grubby fingerprints are all over this album, which is shot through with a psychedelic heaviness unseen before on an Arctic Monkeys album. This is no normal producer/artist relationship - this is a George Martin-Beatles democracy of ideas, a set-up where Homme assumes the role of Brian Eno and Alex Turner is David Byrne, or David Bowie, or Bono.

Now that SOUNDS like a good thing, but when it comes to things they haven't heard, most music fans are prone to assuming the best and not having any real perspective. After hearing the phrase 'Queens of the Stone Age meets Arctic Monkeys', they will automatically imagine Songs for the Deaf blended with Whatever People Say I Am... - it's what we do. But this is not the Josh Homme of 2002 and this is not the Arctic Monkeys of 2005. Both have been in decline since, Homme in particular - it's been five years since he put his name to anything good. Meanwhile, the Arctics showed on Favourite Worst Nightmare and Who the Fuck Are Arctic Monkeys? that while their good ideas remained as good as the ones on their debut, their bad ideas had been getting worse.

So in reality, it's not too surprising that Humbug is the weakest Arctic Monkeys album yet. What is surprising, though, is where the strengths and weaknesses of this album lie. You'd expect the songs to impress and the sound to disappoint, and yet, the exact opposite is true.

However regularly Homme has disappointed since Songs for the Deaf made him a star, and whatever stories have come out about his personal life, you simply can't fault what he does here - the sound is basically perfect. The Monkeys themselves may have been pictured wearing Black Sabbath t-shirts and talking excitedly about discovering heavy metal, but the primary influences here come not from Iommi and company, but instead from the heavy end of late '60s psychedelia - Iron Butterfly in particular, but traces of Cream and Blue Cheer are here too. How much of that influence comes from the Monkeys themselves and how much comes from Homme is up for debate, but regardless, as a way of moving the band's sound forward and away from the rapdily-declining indie scene that spawned them, it's bold and it's a huge success. It feels natural too, which is possibly the album's greatest strength - this is a world apart from their first two albums, but at times it just feels like they were meant to get to this point.

Sadly, that just makes the songwriting doubly disappointing.

Pointing out Alex Turner's lyrics as the reason people got interested in his band in the first place is hardly a great revelation, but that doesn't make it any less true. Whipsmart one-liners are his stock in trade and he's better at them than just about any of his contemporaries - lines like 'There's only music so that there's new ringtones' are memorable after just one listen. And yet, after the first listen to Humbug, I found myself struggling to remember anything apart from the admittedly brilliant "Cornerstone", one scream of 'Dickhead!', and a thinly-vieled line about propellers and erectile dysfunction. Erectile dysfunction is something of a theme to the album, in fact; Turner spends large portions of the album singing about sex, but he generally seems completely disinterested by it. Given how well he writes about love and tenderness - "505", "Despair in the Departure Lounge", and yes, "Cornerstone" - it's a shame to see him flounder when presented with a topic that, if rock history is to be believed, is a lot easier to write songs about. And even when he departs from the topic, the bad ideas continue to get worse - I'm fairly certain nobody needs to be told how "Dangerous Animals" is spelt, and who exactly convinced him that a Wollworths pick'n'mix counter would be good subject matter for "Crying Lightning"? Lord knows why this was chosen as the single - it's a pretty consistent album, but if I were forced to choose a worst song, I'd plump for this one. "Pretty Visitors" clangs too, its sudden tempo shifts making Turner sound almost exactly like George Formby.

Still, even with the band's star player letting his side down, this is a good album. Moreover, it offers tantalizing glimpses of where the band might go next. The biggest hurdle facing them was the need to develop, and they've cleared it with flying colours. If Turner can return to his best - and there's reason to suspect he can't - then the possibilities open to them are potentially limitless. Then, Humbug will be seen a stepping stone. That's certainly how it feels now.
