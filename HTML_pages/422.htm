talkjawking100 Oct 04 2009
+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Talking Heads display their most diverse talents in the studio representation of David Byrne's obscure comedy. From pop rock to latino, from African soul-tribe to even country, Talking Heads show they CAN play every style that's out there. But does it always pay off? ...sometimes.

The album's opener, "Love for Sale", is fun enough to listen to (with an even more enjoyable music video). "Puzzlin' Evidence", the gospel-style song, does make you want to get up and dance. Then there's "Hey Now", easily the band's least memorable song. I'm not sure where they were going with that.

"Papa Legba" is great, but even better in the re-release with the original actor's singing placed in. "Wild Wild Life" was fun when you first heard it, but is in all respects, a novelty song that wears off. "Radio Head", the song that the famous British band got their name from by removing a space, is another non-terrible song, but enjoyable enough.

Then there's "Dream Operator"...oh boy, Dream Operator. If there were ever a more underrated hit from the band, this was it. Some of Byrne's most beautiful guitar and vocal work mixed in with Harrison's wonderful keyboard rhythm, this song is undeniably wonderful. Then we move to "People Like Us," the Head's country ballad originally sung by John Goodman in the film, which is actually one of the highlights of the album. Finally, the album ends with another one of the band's best overlooked tracks, "City of Dreams", which pretty much speaks for itself.

All in all, diverse? Yes? Great? In waves.

dylanblues Jul 08 2008
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Right from the opening burst of laughter its clear that _True Stories_ is going to be a fun album. A few seconds later the tight rock riff of Love for Sale kicks in and it would seem that the groups transition to mainstream pop/rock was complete. However this opening track while excellent is hardly a clear indication as to the rest of the material on offer. At its heart _True Stories_ seeks to draw a parallel between wholesome old fashioned values and the emptiness of the modern media driven world. The fact that many of the lyrics hark back to childhood days only highlights the requisite innocence that the narrator is searching for throughout the albums duration.

The aforementioned rocker Love for Sale gets us off to a sizzling start, but despite its apparent lightweight nature the three major themes all knit together in the opening two lines I was born in a house with the television always on, guess I grew up too fast and I forgot my name. We then change tack completely and Springsteens recent hit Born in the USA certainly suggests itself as an influence. If this showed a surprising embracing of contemporary musical styles, then Wild Wild Life was reminiscent of the groups early track Uh-Oh, Love Comes to Town. Both had a childish, cartoon quality about them and successfully threw a couple of serious professions into the lyrics just to jumble things up a bit! Regardless of this similarity Wild Wild Life was enormous fun and its a measure of Talking Heads adaptability that they could sound so convincing in both stripped down and crisp pop/rock modes. Completing the immediately accessible trilogy was the danceable classic Puzzling Evidence. Despite its powerful attack on the media and money, this gospel flavoured diatribe remained musically upbeat throughout and was one of the key tracks in the development of the albums themes. Great lines like got what you wanted, lost what you had and now I am the gun and you are the bullet, I got the power and glory and the money to buy it brought into sharp context the skewered values of society, while comfortably avoiding the dreaded tag of protest song.

Unfortunately Puzzling Evidence is followed by two fairly weak efforts. At least Hey Now attempts to fit in with the materialistic vibe with various children demanding items from the mall, but overall the piece lacks musical drive and checks the albums early bounce. If this slowed the momentum a touch then the dull six minute pop song Papa Legba almost stopped it completely. Seeming to draw on folk rock imagery for its inspiration, this bizarre dragged out affair added nothing at all to the album and should clearly have been omitted. Thankfully the third minor work Radio Head was a lot more satisfying. Though little more than a short quirky love song, it did nevertheless capture the imagination of Thom Yorke enough for him to name his group after the track. In light of Radioheads largely experimental, oblique music it perhaps seems strange that they chose such an innocuous latter period offering, but it does seems fitting that they looked to the similarly innovative Talking Heads for inspiration.

Anyway back to the story and the albums narrative resumes in earnest with the beautiful ballad Dream Operator. Again harking back to childhood idealism, the lilting arrangement takes us away from the rush of modern life by skillfully empowering our dreams and letting us drift soothingly away into the clouds. Its a far cry from opening blitz of Love for Sale and the reflective mood continues with People Like Us, a more nostalgic look at the struggles for love and acceptance. The countrified arrangement adds a heartfelt ambience to the song making it far more personal than political. In this guise the phrase we dont want freedom, we dont want justice, we just want someone to love suggests a firm focus on the search for a loving relationship rather than a deflated acceptance of the vagaries of government. Finally the dignified closer City of Dreams signs off in fine style. Introducing a historical context at this late stage is a masterstroke crystallising the albums underlying message perfectly. The final verses are especially powerful underneath the concrete the dream is still alive, a hundred million lifetimes, a world that never dies. We live in the city of dreams, we drive on the highway of fire, should we awake and find it gone, remember this our favourite town.

Watching the film credits roll as this song is playing almost makes you sad that the group didnt split up immediately after this. With virtually a decade of fine work behind them including two live albums and two movies, this would have been a good time to call it a day. Perhaps the mixed reception to the _True Stories_ film persuaded them to return to their earlier musical leanings, but for many this is their last hurrah and for the most part its a fitting end to a varied and exciting career.

As with the movie by the same name True Stories, you either get the album or you dont. And having said that, if you get the album then you get the movie, or you may come at it from the other side, and if you dug the movie you will dig the album. Either way this is a step out of the box for the band, and a fine step theyve taken with this release.

"Puzzlin Evidence" sets the tone for the album where all that you hear in this song is a clue to the rest of the music; and I found it brilliant that the Heads took the time locate the key on the second track. This is undoubtedly the tightest Heads album to date, and probably the most confusing to most of the public, as most people are not sure if its a sound track or an album, nevertheless, once youre in, youre in with both feet and a smile as broad a your hat brim.

At points in "Papa Legba" David sings with a shaky voice, swirling back smokey memories of Dr. John, but then he smoothes out, causing you wondering if youre listening to the same song. If youve seen the band live youll understand this transposition, its a marvelous feature, never better used then here by Talking Heads than here.

The simplistic nature of "Dream Operator" with its extended introduction, bright and incredibly thought out, presented magically with a strain to Davids voice that lends a dream like quality to the song.

We, you and I, and those reading this, we are the people in the song "People Like Us." "We just want someone to love," people like us, loving music, taking the time to answer the phone, making the effort to connect.

No, theres no group hug coming here, this is just one really fine good natured album that doesnt demand anything of you other then to take the time to listen and feel the warmth of what we emotionally already know.

True Stories (1986)
This breaks my heart. The previous Talking Heads albums had been so filled with life and contagiously fun that I didn't think that they could make something that I couldn't at least enjoy a little bit.

However, I was proven wrong. 'True Stories' follows up a lesser effort in their catalogue in 'Little Creatures' which I found to be alienating in its experimentation but also pretty fun and endearing. There is nothing fun or endearing about this project here. Funnily enough, I do think the worst is put out of the way first with the grating 'Love For Sale'. This song nearly made my ears bleed, I was shocked. I didn't think that Talking Heads could produce something so sonically unappealing. It reminded me of the transition from Room on Fire to First Impressions of Earth. The horrible mixes and guitar tones here are truly repulsive. There is also a great deal of padding on this album. None of the songs with longer runtimes merit them, it just ruins it because it would clearly work in a movie but not in an album context. Something like 'Puzzlin' Evidence' brings a cool idea and harps on that same idea without the slightest variation for pretty much its whole duration which does not play well in Talking Heads' style.

An extremely bitter disappointment and one which I get no joy out of, similarly to the album listening experience.

FAVOURITE TRACK: PUZZLIN' EVIDENCE

WORST TRACK: LOVE FOR SALE

Yes, I am rating this equal to Fear of Music, and yes, I know that is ridiculous thing to do, but that's the problem with a five star ranking system, huh? Even with .5s it is still pretty limited in the nuances within each score. Just let it be known that this is no Fear of Music. No, not at all, in fact I'd argue there is some songs here like Hey Now that are actively bad, but I also think that dartboard-esque style for a project like this leads to a discography not getting stale even seven albums deep. Wild Wild Life proves to me that nothing actually changed quality wise in the creativity of the band, and in reality they just wanted to do something else, and this came from that, and I'm rocking out with it. Nothing mind blowing, but an enjoyable listen.
