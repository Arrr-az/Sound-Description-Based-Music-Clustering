A Mastery of Jazz-HipHop, the only album of its kind
A change in my life brought me to listen to music by the album - listen to albums for a journey, or a story.

Kendrick Lamar made it clear with good kid, m.A.A.d. city that he was one of the greatest story telling rappers to date, if not the greatest. To Pimp a Butterfly expands on this, with the jazz samples providing one of the most unique feels to any hip hop album.

Genuinely, this album has no holes. "Wesley's Theory" is a perfect introduction, and "For Free?" might be the most creative jazz-rap song ever created, despite being an interlude. Obviously, "King Kunta" and "Alright" are perpetually remembered by all hip hop fans, and modern music fans in general. He continues parallels from his previous projects (Revenge of friend murder in "These Walls" and alcoholism in "u" are the most notable continuities to me).

After the first half of the album establishes how Kendrick uses his talent (how Kendrick "pimps" himself) to make personal gain, the second half more clearly addresses the societal issues that terrify him - the issues that need changed. The acapella poem in the live version of "i" included on the album emphasizes his desire for change - he will tell his hood audience directly to their faces, they need to act to change.

Finally, "Mortal Man" reveals the transitionary poem that is gradually composed/revealed during the album is addressed to 2-pac. Kendrick strategically samples a 2-pac interview to invent a conversation between the two of them addressing the cultural issues present in black communities today, and the need for response. This is a conversation Kendrick wishes he could have had with 2-pac; but in the project, 2-pac acts as a symbol, and Kendrick has this conversation with the world instead.

The album is one of few perfect projects. It has a purpose, a message, and the slow development of that message creates a journey for the listener. Must listen 10/10.

EL MEJOR DISCO DE HIP-HOP QUE VOY A ESCUCHAR EN MI VIDA
No digo que es el mejor de la historia porque admito no haber escuchado los proyectos considerados escenciales para entender y saber acerca del Hip-Hop, pero este disco es el mejor proyecto que escuché del género en toda mi vida y dudo que algún proyecto lo llegué tan siquiera a alcanzar. Principalmente porque nunca escuché ningún proyecto como este.

En el año 2015, Kendrick Lamar saca la que sería su magnum opus, To Pimp A Butterfly. Un disco de un tamaño y valor inmenso que cuenta con miles de capas de profundidad tanto líricamente como instrumentalmente. Nunca escuché un proyecto como este en mi vida, es la primera vez que escucho esta fusión, donde Kendric une el Hip-Hop con el Jazz y el Funk, usando las trompetas, los sintetizadores y pianos tocando puro Jazz y los coros femeninos y masculinos dando ese toque también de Soul a las canciones, todo esto sumado a la ENORME cantidad de detalles que se sumergen a lo largo de todas las canciones, voces recortadas, diálogos que forman más a profundidad el concepto del álbum, el cuál gira entorno a los problemas raciales que existen en Estados Unidos, hablando además de la relación de Kendrick con su barrio, su fama, su plata y su pareja.

Es el disco más importante del género que he escuchado, debido a su concepto e increíble experimentación y producción. Es increíble lo que Kendrick logra en cada track, es fascinante al oído escuchar esa fusión de géneros aplicada de una manera exquisita y experimental. Canciones como Wesley's Theory, Institutionalized, These Walls, Alright, For Sale? - Interlude y Mortal Man aplican excelentemente este concepto, aunque en realidad todas lo hacen pero destaco esas.

Esta disco tiene la mejor producción, instrumentales, concepto y detalles jamás creados dentro del Hip-Hop y es uno de los mejores álbumes de la historia. 10/10.

Highlights: Wesley's Theory, These Walls, Alright.

Phenomenal in every aspect. #1 Favorite Album OAT
In quite literally every aspect I can think of, this album passes with flying colors. One of the best sounding albums I’ve ever heard, if not the best- the consistency of the track list is unfathomable; every single song is not only amazing but distinct and FEELS important and memorable. Even with this consistency, the high points on this record like Wesley’s Theory (My favorite song of all time) and The Blacker the Berry are so good, they break the limits of what I ever thought music could achieve to me personally. The lyrics on this thing are endlessly deep, the entire album can be looked at from so many different angles and it’s unreal how cleverly everything goes together from the themes to the sequencing to the tie-in poem.

The storytelling cuts are painting vivid pictures that you can really visualize and feel, the hype tracks are some of the biggest bops I’ve ever heard while also being incredibly meaningful to every theme this album dabbles in. The track list is so tightly constructed and sequenced that not a single second feels out of place on this album, out of order, or inessential. This is hands down one of the most fulfilling and satisfying listens in every single facet of music listening that makes me love the art form to death.

There’s only one song on it I wouldn’t consider absolutely perfect (Hood Politics), and it’s more of just personal sonic preference of it not clicking with me yet, if I had to guess. Even then, it contributes an essential piece of the album narratively and sequentially that is a monumentally better listen included in the track list.

This album manages to be a collection of some of the best music ever made, on one of the most creatively ambitious premises ever made, that on top of that manages to become significantly more than the sum of its parts.

Fucking Flawless. 10.0/10

With big expectations to follow the release of his critically acclaimed album, good kid, m.A.A.d city, Kendrick Lamar managed to outdo himself with a project more far reaching, more experimental, and more ambitious. A project as ambitious as To Pimp a Butterfly is not easy to pull off, but Kendrick did it perfectly. Masterfully blending motifs of racism, racial/social/class inequality, mental health, and varied relationships, Kendrick manages to incorporate the highest highs and the lowest lows of his human experience to create an emotionally explosive album. Continuing a primary theme of good kid, m.A.A.d city, Kendrick provides reflection on life in the hood, growing up surrounded by inescapable violence and poverty, while also expanding to capture the bigger picture of the African American experience, as well as personal relationships and situations that anyone can relate to. Sonically, To Pimp a Butterly maintains Kendrick’s iconic west coast style, but mixes in plentiful jazz and funk influence. Capturing a wide range of musical influences and lyrical themes, To Pimp a Butterfly is a near perfect album. Each track blends cohesively into the next, yet most of the tracks are still excellent on their own.

On September 23rd 2014 I came on to my computer and saw that Kendrick Lamar, one of my favourite current artists had released a new song, "i". I instantly logged on to this website to see what the general consensus was, because I value all of your opinions so very deeply. I fired up the website, immediately typed in Kendrick Lamar into the search box and went down to the singles section. Anger and disappointment. That was the general consensus, was everyone just being hysterical and overreacting or had Kendrick lost his magic? I listened immediately to find out what all the fuss was about and ultimately, it wasn't nearly as bad as the 2.60+ suggested, but I had an overriding feeling of disappointment. Is this it? was basically how I felt. I had been anticipating something from him ever since I fell in love with good kid, maad city and I got something that just failed to do anything for me. It was overly poppy, the production was slick, it had a catchy and enjoyable hook but ultimately it was just too poppy and commercial. Worst of all, it lacked the thematic weight and substance of previous songs. The worst criticism for it is that it just felt like a radio friendly pop rap song that was completely inoffensive. I started wondering if he was just a one album wonder (Although Section .80 was pretty good too), but I still held out hope for the album. Maybe it was just a forgettable song and the album itself would be better? Maybe it would fit better in context of the album? Maybe it won't even be on the album were my hopes.

First of all, I don't think a single from an album has been a bigger misrepresentation of the album's sound than "i". I think a lot of people felt "i" was Kendrick moving into a blander, pop rap, mainstream direction which would have been easy to succumb to because it's not like his beats weren't energetic and hooks lacked catchy accessibility. But the album itself is so far removed to that sound it's unbelievable. Second of all, the album version is so much better than the single version that I'm convinced Kendrick was just deliberately trolling everyone. Thirdly, it definitely does work a whole lot better in the context of the album. But at this point, I had no idea that this was the case and still had a feeling of excitement and nervous anxiety. Then come the song "Untitled" from The Colbert Report, which despite my love for To Pimp a Butterfly, I'm still devasted it isn't on the album. A part of me feels like conversation with Tupac for half of 'Mortal Man' could be cut to include "Untitled" instead, but then the ending of Mortal Man is incredibly memorable and it feels like it could be iconic, certainly a fitting way to finish the album. But I do miss that a studio version of "Untitled" isn't here but that was the song where I knew everything was going to be okay, Kendrick had not lost his intensity, his focus and what makes him great as both an artist but a writer. It's kind of strange how listening to the final product I had such concerns. Then the release of "Blacker the Berry" really stopped me worrying. This was the first single from the album, and any concerns about the album being a vapid, commercial pop rap affair were completely obliterated with the sheer levels of lyrical prowess and intensity of the song.

Those initial concerns seem absurd to me now because not only did Kendrick Lamar release something similar in quality to his acclaimed good kid, maad city he actively tries to better it. I still prefer the former, but I've had 3 years with that and I've only heard this a few times so that may change. But this feels like something huge and important. So often when an artist releases something to such acclaim, they're desperately trying to reach that level again, Kendrick sets his sights even higher and releases his longest, most daring, experimental album yet. To Pimp Butterfly's title is an obvious reference and play on words of the Harper Lee novel To Kill a Mockingbird and given the theme of racism, racial inequality and bigotry it's somewhat fitting. The album is political, socially conscious and has real thematic depth and much like on his last album the lyrics are just as integral to the album as the music. The lyrics are insightful and thought provoking, you can feel the passion and the anger in such a visceral way as Kendrick shifts from his childhood and growing up and focuses on modern day America, police brutality, racial injustice and profiling in such an incredible way from a lyrical point of view.

Musically however, Kendrick is on his game. Sometimes I think the danger could be putting so much into storytelling and lyrical content that the music itself could feel a little lazy and half arsed, but that's certainly not the case here. The instrumentation is beautifully intricate, rich and grandiose and Kendrick's flow is so on point, and the beats are just incredible. He has certainly never done anything like this before and the comparisons to Flying Lotus are definitely fitting, as the production does remind me an awful lot of his work at times. But musically, it's incredibly diverse and experimental to the point where it can't be pigeon holed. To me, the production is almost paradoxical. The beats simultaneously manage to feel raw and old school, where by the music is completely oblivious to what's currently happening in Hip Hop and the current trends whilst feeling fresh, new and innovative. He combines Hip Hop with Jazz, Funk, Soul and r&b in such a seamless way.

In summary, To Pimp A Butterfly is a culturally relevant and thought provoking album that's incredibly ambitious. It feels like an important album and it absolutely remands to be heard. Even though it's such a new album and my thoughts on it will hopefully be more fleshed out and insightful after many more listens, it does have the feel of something that will not only be one of the greatest albums of the year but also one of the greatest albums of the decade.
