atomicWedgie Mar 23 2006
+45 Vote downBookmarkVote up
There are all sorts of nerds, dorks and geeks in this world. Thank God. I often feel sorry for people who aren't nerdy, geeky or dorky in some way. Isn't is sad that there are people too cool, stale or dull to appreciate the things in life exalted by losers everywhere?

I believe that, given a scientific categorization system, all people, as either individuals or groups, can be given a dork rating. Let's try, shall we? I'll be testing three subjects:
A. atomicWedgie. That's me!
B. Frank Sinatra. The epitome of cool. I'll use his fifties-era persona as a guide.
C. Jethro Tull. I expect high marks for this band. Aqualung will be the reference point.
The scoring system will be from 0 to 10.

Categories:
Space Nerds: You know, trekkies, those nerds that dress up like Darth Vader when they go to see Star Wars, people like that.
A. atomicWedgie - 7 I haven't dressed up like Han Solo since I was eleven, but there was a time
B. Frank Sinatra - 0 Are you kidding?
C. Jethro Tull - 0 I don't think there's anything spacey about Aqualung.

Comic Book Nerds:
A. atomicWedgie - 6 I have an X-Men coffee mug at work, and I know far too much about Daredevil, Professor Xavier and Batman.
B. Frank Sinatra - 0 There's no way Ol' Blue Eyes ever touched a comic book.
C. Jethro Tull - 0 Nope. No caped crusaders on Aqualung either.

Band Geeks:
A. atomicWedgie - 5 I was a drummer in high school, and I played in the high school band. I hated it, though. I had to play in the big band so I could play in the school's jazz band.
B. Frank Sinatra - 3 Not really a band geek, but some of his closest friends were, so guilty by association.
C. Jethro Tull - 10 There's a damn flute player in this band who plays on one foot. Yeah, big time band geeks.

Drama Geeks:
A. atomicWedgie - 0 Never my thing.
B. Frank Sinatra - 8 Frank was in movies, including the brilliant Manchurian Candidate. He doesn't get a ten because it wasn't his main bag.
C. Jethro Tull - 4 I've seen movies with Jethro Tull playing live, but that doesn't really count. Look at the front and back cover of Aqualung. There's lots of drama going on there.

Computer/Math Nerds:
A. atomicWedgie - 10 I'm a software engineer. 'Nuff said.
B. Frank Sinatra - 0
C. Jethro Tull - 4 Their arrangements are fairly complex on Aqualung. Music and math are closely related arts (sure, math can be considered an art).

Prog/Metal Dorks: C'mon now, let's not get all sensitive about things.
A. atomicWedgie - 2
B. Frank Sinatra - 0
C. Jethro Tull - 8 Neither metal nor prog, but they certainly have elements of both. "Aqualung" alone contains elements of both generes.

Gamer Nerds: Dungeons and Dragons, anyone?
A. atomicWedgie - 4 I used to play D&D when I was in junior high. I can admit that.
B. Frank Sinatra - 2 Gambling in Vegas doesn't really count, does it?
C. Jethro Tull - 3 I dunno, I get a Magic vibe from these guys, but I can't really pin it down.

Renaissance Faire Freaks:
A. atomicWedgie - 0 Noooooooo . . .
B. Frank Sinatra - 1 Starred in Robin and the Seven Hoods, but that was a rat pack gangster movie.
C. Jethro Tull - 10 Aqualung is the ultimate Renaissance Faire rock album. I can see these dudes rocking down through the faire grounds, playing their flute and guitars and singing the songs from this record.

Summary
atomicWedgie - 34 Nerd Points
Frank Sinatra - 16 Nerd Points
Jethro Tull - 35 Nerd Points

Draw your own conclusions.

Aqualung - vinyl - 1971

It was the early 70s and the giants of rock were waking. Led Zeppelin had conquered America, Deep Purple were getting louder and slicker, The Who were at the top of their game, The Rolling Stones were still half-decent and Black Sabbath were still paranoid. Rock was loud, proud and officially huge. A good time then for Jethro Tull to make their contribution to the hard rocking rock pantheon by showing that Ian Anderson was a step across from (if not ahead of) the other lyricists of the time. Not for Anderson and Co the lustful pursuit of wenches, or the search for teenage wasteland. No, here we had intelligent social comment, songs of quiet reflection and (less welcome) gubbins about "Mother Goose".

Aqualung proved that Jethro Tull could rock with the best of them, but rather than an all out assault on the ears that less skillful bands would have favoured, the heavy rockers are punctuated by a series of delicate acoustic tracks that displayed Anderson's more sensitive side. In the title track and "Cross Eyed Mary" Tull showed that they could do intelligent riff-based rock and while tracks like the previously mentioned "Mother Goose" and the sub-par "Up To Me" weren't up to scratch, the acoustic numbers bolstered the first side of the album up very well.

As I've grown older listening to this album it's the second side that means the most to me, as it accurately reflects my approach to religion. Far from the tired cynicism displayed by the vast majority of my generation and those following it I do have faith, however I am utterly opposed to organised religion. As Anderson has apparently grown weary of explaining time and time again, Aqualung is not an attack on God in whatever form he / she / it may take, but it was an attack on organised religion and how narrow-minded and reactionary the people who sign up to it can be. Touched By An Angel it ain't.

Taken as just an album Aqualung is a fine listen, comprising of classic rock staples (the title track and the career-highlight "Locomotive Breath"), pretty acoustic numbers and stuff that made religious nutcases want to burn records. It may be one of the more straightforward Jethro Tull albums, but it's the one that most students of 70s rock will start with. Not the band's best in the grand scheme of things, but it's still one hell of a statement after all these years.

Aqualung [25th Anniversary Special Edition] - CD - 1996

"I don't believe you,
You got the whole damn thing all wrong."

Oh dear.

How did it come to this? That the best known and best selling album of one of rocks finest bands be treat with such a lack of care. Aqualung may not be Jethro Tull's best album, but it is the Tull album that most classic rock fans would be instantly drawn to, containing as it does two staples of classic rock radio in the awesome title track - hard rock being given a social conscious for the first time - and the brilliant "Locomotive Breath", with one of the most stunning piano intros in the history of rock music.

Aqualung was (quite rightly) the first Jethro Tull album to be remastered back in the mid-90s. The original vinyl always had some slightly muddy production and the original CD transfer only made it worse. The remaster was long overdue, as it offered the opportunity to clean up the original recording, correct some mistakes and add some choice outtakes from the album sessions.

Oh dear.

The first sign that something is very wrong is when you read the tracklisting, a long-winded interview with Ian Anderson is unceremoniously parked mid-way through the bonus material (if your going to include an interview, at least put it the end of the album, so we aren't reaching for the skip button every time!) and then the last three tracks are radio session recording from 1969, two years before Aqualung was even released! As a result these three tracks are stylisticly totally different to anything else on the album and were obviously a hasty after thought to fill up the space on the CD. What makes the entire thing even more insulting is that these three tracks are all on the 20 Years of Jethro Tull box set, so it's not as if they aren't available elsewhere!

Never mind, at least they'll have made a better job of mastering the CD this time. Put it in the CD player and press play.

Oh dear.

Well the good news is that they have improved on the original CD pressing. Just. Quite why they decided to master the album so quietly is a total mystery, but a tweak of the volume nob does reveal that it has indeed been improved slightly, but it still isn't quite up to the quality of the original vinyl release.

At least the inclusion of the first two bonus tracks is welcome. "Lick Your Fingers Clean" is a single that unforgivably got scrapped, as it was easily the hardest rocking Tull single to date, but at least the lyrics got recycled for "Two Fingers" on the War Child album. The quadrophonic version of "Wind Up" is sufficiently different to the original for it to be of interest.

At the end of the day this remastered version of Aqualung is an example of a brilliant album being made merely quite-good due to a lack of care. I for one hope that at some point in the near future they go back, get rid of the radio sessions and interview, increase the volume and tidy up some of the woollier production that was there on the original, just to bring it up to the standard of the other splendid Tull reissues.

Aqualung [40th Anniversary Special Edition] - CD - 2011

"Oh Jesus save me!"

There’s a part of me that genuinely hates needless remasters of albums. It’s as if the record executives feel that a tiny amount of sonic rejigging and a slip-case (where often ‘updated artwork’ means one blurry photo and a link to the act’s ‘official website’) justifies conning the act’s fans into buying an album they’ve bought at least once (if not twice) before. That said, many great albums sounded absolutely terrible the first time they were transfered on to CD, so many acts have rereleased their back catalogues multiple times to rectify this. In recent years though the sound quality has reached a capacity on CD, where new sonic upgrades are almost negligible in all but a few very rare cases.

If you’ve managed to get this far into the review, you’ve probably realised that rather than me disliking Aqualung as an album, but rather I have consistently dismayed at the lack of care remastering it and reissuing it on CD. While it is well documented that the original recording process was a fraught affair and Anderson’s memories of his disillusionment while recording this have echoed down the last four decades to the point where he still seems frustrated by the whole process. It is perhaps for this reason that while the last batch of Tull reissues were sweated over by Anderson and engineer Robin Black, the 40th anniversary resissue of Aqualung was overseen by Steve Wilson from Porcupine Tree, a band that misty-eyed prog-rock enthusiasts still see as the great white hope for the future, despite their advancing years.

Having an obvious love of the album, while still being emotionally removed enough from its creation perhaps emboldened Wilson to take a few liberties with the mix of the original album. While this may seem like heresy to some (and if I hadn’t heard the results, I would have been inclined to agree with them), Wilson has created a mix that stays very true to the original sound, but also improves it enough for it not to sound out of place compared to the other Tull remasters of the last decade. Sound-wise Aqualung has always been the worst of Tull’s 70s albums, a situation made even worse with the woeful original CD transfer and the subsequent 25th anniversary edition. With the re-release and all round improvement of Too Old to Rock 'n' Roll: Too Young to Die! a few years ago, it left Aqualung as the classic Tull album I listened to the least, simply because it sounded so bad on CD. This reissue goes a long way to remedying that sorry situation, as this new mix is much more punchy and has a lot more depth than any previous CD release issue of Aqualung that I have heard. Sure, there are things I would have liked to have been done slightly differently (personally I would have liked to have heard a remastered version of the superior mix of the title track as heard on the M.U. - The Best of Jethro Tull compilation, rather than the flatter sounding version on the original album), but it wasn’t me mixing the thing and I don’t know what the limitations of the original material were, so I think this is probably as good as Aqualung will ever sound.

Often the sticking point for me on reissues such as this, is that ‘bonus tracks’ are tacked on the end, without enough of a silence to indicate the end of the original album, or even worse, thoughtlessly dumped midway through the running order with dubious justification that was ‘what the artist originally intended’. Thankfully that is not the case here, as all bonus tracks are banished to the second CD, they were all vaguely contemporary with the original album (in contrast to some of the bonus tracks on the 25th anniversary addition which featured material released long before the original album) and they have all been given a sonic upgrade in line with the rest of the album. All in all, that’s the best that we can hope for on this type of release. If I do have a criticism it’s that the liner notes in the CD booklet are far too sycophantic, though to be honest you wouldn’t expect these to actually criticise the act or release in question would you?

If you’re as cynical as me when it comes to remasters and and ‘anniversary editions’ of albums and you’re in two minds about whether to splash out on this version of Aqualung, I’d recommend listening to a copy before you part with your hard-earned. While only an insanely obsessive super-fan could ever need the full-bananas vinyl / CD / 5.1 surround sound remaster, the stripped-down two CD release is a fine compromise in these times of greater austerity and, as long as you’re not slavish about re-releases being as close to the sound of the original vinyl release as possible, well worth splashing out on.

Aqualung [40th Anniversary Adapted Edition Remixed and Mastered by Steven Wilson] - CD / DVD - 2016

"The rainy season comes to pass"

Yeah. I know.

I spent years bellyaching about how the intentional scarcity of these Jethro Tull deluxe book box sets directly led to capitalists screwing over long term fans of the band by buying up multiple copies and the selling them second hand for vastly inflated prices. £250 for a copy of Songs From the Wood [40th Anniversary Edition: The Country Set]? Sure it's generally held as the jewel in the crown of this reissue series, but I'm not bankrupting myself to get all the deluxe book boxes in the series.

Yet here I am with a copy of the Adapted Edition of the 40th anniversary set for Aqualung. This is the fifth time I have purchased this album. Not only that, but it offers a minimal upgrade on the last version I picked up when it comes to the music. No, I bought this version of Aqualung because I am slowly but surely collecting all these deluxe editions. I'm not playing inflated prices for them, but if I see them for between £20 and £30, then yeah I'll pick them up, and being the monkey-brained completist I am, yeah, when I saw a seal copy of this version of Aqualung, I ordered it and crossed my fingers that eBay seller was genuine (turned out they were!).

When you consider what has been included in other deluxe editions in this series, this version of Aqualung doesn't seem tremendously generous in terms of additional content. Where's the Steven Wilson upgrade of the 1975 mix of the title track that found its way on to M.U. and Original Masters? Was that not worth including? Was there absolutely nothing in the vaults from radio sessions, or live recordings?

So yeah, in the grand scheme of this reissue series, this version of Aqualung is in a lovely format that the fans love, there's some nice extended essays in the book section, but when it comes to the music, if you already have the 40th anniversary 2 CD reissues, unless you're intent on collecting this deluxe book box series, then you might want to give this one a skip, as you can get the same music in the same mixes for far cheaper.

That said, it does seem that efforts are being made by the record label and Ian Anderson to prevent opportunists from gouging fans of the band by re-releasing these reissues at short notice and offering them at affordable prices again, so yeah, I picked up this version of Aqualung, because it was affordable again. And you'd better believe that I have a copy of the deluxe version of Songs from the Wood winging its way to me now it's available for £25 from legitimate sources.

"No way to slow down."

First, let's dispel a myth. There really was no full concept to this album. There were definite themes running through the separate sides, but if you're looking for the full story of the guy in the painting, forget it. He's gone by the end of the first side, and Ian Anderson is on to his rants against the Church of England on the second.

I have seen reviews that say Benefit was a transitional album, which I find untrue. Very little on Aqualung resembles that album, and the production on here is light years ahead. If anything it takes its cue from some of the singles they released in the late 1960s, although much of the emphasis here is on contrasting big guitar riffs and soft, introspective songs.

In addition, Anderson's lyrics start to come into full bloom, laying the groundwork for Thick As a Brick.

Aqualung is an essential album, and one of the greatest examples of 1970's rock, and Jethro Tull remains one of the few bands to create albums on their own terms, rather than the profligate whims of record corporations. Conformity was never one of Ian Anderson's strong suits, which is why you won't be seeing Tull in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame anytime soon, but you will see the Bee Gees (and ABBA, for Christ's sake! who the hell votes for these inanities?).

Critics have often tried to pigeonhole Aqualung into one of several genres: concept album, progressive rock, folk-rock, et cetera, ad nauseam, but the brilliance of the album is that it transcended what was termed 'hard-rock' back in 1971, and offered a refreshing synthesis of several styles and moods. The lyrics are dryly witty and sarcastic at times, particularly regarding Christian hypocrisy (like in 'My God', 'Hymn 43' and 'Wind Up'), whimsical ('Mother Goose'), reflective ('Wond'ring Aloud'), or vulgar ('Cross-eyed Mary'), but the overall effect is a seemless travelogue of England itself, a journey that includes both the pastoral landscapes of Hampstead and the gritty streets of London or Birmingham. We hobnob with whores, pedophiles, losers and bums, as well as schoolboys, nurses and bishops at tea.

The title song 'Aqualung' is a microcosmic mini-epic of the album itself, containing bits and pieces of the album's philosophy and irreverence (the lyric 'snot is running down his nose' made all middle school boys giggle with glee in '71 -- no one referred to snot on an album prior to this!), and it is still played daily on every classic rock station 'round the world, just like 'Stairway to Heaven' and the 'Bohemian Rhapsody'. The poetic allusiveness of the lyrics is exceptionally strong and evokes England to its very core ('feeling alone, the armies up the road, salvation a la mode, and a cup of tea'), while reminding us that the death of the homeless beggar, who snatches his last rattling breath with 'deep-sea diver sounds', is the reason the album is called 'Aqualung' in the first place (an 'aqualung' is a breathing apparatus used by deep-sea divers, consisting of a mouthpiece attached to air cylinders, causing the distinctive echoed gasping sounds as oxygen is breathed in).

The flute, that rock music novelty which has become indeliby identified with Tull, is not necessarily the focal point of this album; in fact, the album's greatest hit, the song 'Aqualung', contains not a hint of flute. Of course, Ian Anderson's virtuousity on the instrument is present on 'Locomotive Breath' and 'My God' where the flute becomes a weapon and not some effeminate, whimpering thing.

But it is Anderson's interludes on acoustic guitar that really sets this album apart from the run-of-the-mill 70's rock offerings. The acoustic guitar work on songs such as 'Aqualung', 'Mother Goose', 'My God' and 'Cheap Day Return' is integral to the overall effect of the album, sometimes subtle and nuanced, and at other times biting and inflected.

When Aqualung rocks, it really rocks, but it also presents other tonal dimensions that make it a unique and exceptionally strong album.

OK, we all know this was that album that made Tull a household name of '70s rock. Personally I find the album a tad overrated (where I found their previous album, _Benefit_ a tad underrated), but it's still full of great stuff. Some of the songs really received too much radio airplay, you couldn't listen to classic FM rock stations without hearing the title track, "Cross-Eyed Mary", "Locomotive Breath" and sometimes "Hymn 43". The album marked the departure of bassist Glenn Cornick (who formed Wild Turkey), and the arrival of Jeffrey Hammond-Hammond, who was Ian Anderson's buddy from a previous band. John Evan was now an official member (their previous album stated that he was added for "their benefit", get it? Benefit, as in the album?) The album was divided into two sides (if you own the LP), the first half (first five songs) were apparently about homeless people, as depicted on the album cover, while the second half were songs highly critical of organized religion. But Ian Anderson denied that this was a concept album. OK, the first two songs we all heard millions of times via FM radio. "Cross-Eyed Mary", along with "Witches Promise" and "Cup of Wonder" were the rare occasions of Mellotron being used in Tull. "Mother Goose" is a fantastic acoustic number with multi-tracked flutes. "My God" is the most progressive thing on the album with tons of heavy guitar work and an extended flute solo. Personally I was never big on "Locomotive Breath", I can't understand how it became such an FM rock staple. The album also has a few short interludes like "Cheap Day Return", "Wond'ring Aloud" and "Slipsteam" that goes between the regular songs.

I've also been very familiar with this album right from childhood when my dad bought a copy of this (on vinyl) back at the end of the '70s or beginning of the '80s. So I do have nostalgia attached to the album, but I never thought this was Tull's finest either.
