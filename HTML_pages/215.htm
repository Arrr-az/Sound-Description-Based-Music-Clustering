Who has had the worst influence on music over the last 50 years? My vote will go to Bob Dylan. This is not because I dislike Dylan: he was my hero when I was a teenager and will always be a part of my life. Listening to this album after many years I find I can still sing along to every song, know every word. There was a long period when I didn’t listen to my Dylan albums – so now, returning to them recently, I am partly trying to come to terms with them. So why do I think he has had such a malevolent influence? First, he has enabled us to judge the worth and seriousness of a song purely on its lyrics. Listen to A Hard Rain’s A-Gonna Fall: musically it has a very simple sequence of chord changes – the guitar becomes little more than a background drone whose purpose is to focus our attention on the lyrics. If you want to place it in a context, although there are Woody Guthrie’s songs, more pertinent is Allen Ginsberg’s Howl and the line of American literature going back to Walt Whitman. Learned professors have written books interpreting Dylan’s lyrics without making any mention of his music. The song has become a branch of literature and our responses are literary. Gone is the relationship between lyric and music that can be found in Schubert, the best of the Tin Pan Alley tradition or traditions of Brazilian and French songs. Secondly, the way we can take Dylan, or at least Dylan’s lyrics, very seriously (as I did when I was a teenager) is an important part of a shift in sensibility that allows us to have cultural kudos while not having to respond to music in any complex or challenging way. Easy music can be serious music, therefore let’s not bother with anything difficult. We constantly immerse ourselves in music, but much less often actually listen to it. This is not Dylan’s fault, but he has had his role. And this album? There are the two iconic songs: Blowin’ In the Wind and A Hard Rain’s A-Gonna Fall. Not just iconic Dylan songs but iconic for their times, for the young and politically attuned (and therefore for every generation that becomes politically attuned in its youth). And there are the other political songs, the protest songs: the bitter Masters of War, the amusing Oxford Town and Talkin’ World War III Blues. The songs of lost or failed love: Girl From the North Country (for me the strongest song on the album) and Don’t Think Twice It’s Alright (this line in Dylan’s work, leading up to Blood On the Tracks, is now the most important in my response to Dylan). The loose songs seemingly held together by a sensibility rather than any internal logic: Bob Dylan’s Blues and I Shall Be Free – although they are fun but minor stuff here, over the next few years they will become central to Dylan’s ambitions. As Dylan has securely aligned himself with the white folkie line of Woody Guthrie and Pete Seeger, although already showing a broadness of ambition that goes beyond them, this album, overall, has become musically simpler compared to the blues of his first album: it shows a startling but very limited sensibility.

In which the best singer does his best singing.

This is another one of those albums that lotsa younger people these days seem to avoid, at most approaching it with trepidation; as if it were some sort of educational exercise, an Important Historical Document that won't actually get the endorphins flowing. Let's be clear now: anyone with a functional cortex and a sense of humor can find this stuff not just educational - though it's certainly that - but damned fun! Entertaining! Heartfelt! Funny! Scary! Beautiful! And...well hell, I'll say it: life-affirming. I'm fucking overwhelmed!

Now, as a lyricist - and make no mistake, Dylan is without peer as a lyricist of the rock era - Bob hasn't worked into the Allen-Ginsberg-imagery-in-speed-mythos style yet, with which he'd do his best work. The party line on Freewheelin' is that the guy's still writing Protest Songs, which together with the fact that the music itself is still almost entirely just Bob with acoustic guitar and harmonica might be what (understandably) makes people bristle. You probably think you don't need to hear "Blowin' in the Wind" ever again. I know I don't. But that doesn't mean its directness isn't still worthy of tremendous respect. Too many people have died — I can't argue with that. And yet, is the answer blowing around in the wind? Blowing away with the wind? Blowing closer with the wind? Where's the wind, and where's it going? You decide. Similarly, "Masters of War" may set in like naïve cynicism ('A world war can be won/You want me to believe...'), it just gets more righteously confrontational, even shaping a would-be-typical blood metaphor into something bracing and (necessarily) ugly and culminating the song with some of the most damning and bitter indictments of another human being. (Or in this case, this certain kind of human being, i.e. evil.)

But y'know, those two are the only direct, obvious Protest Songs on the album...err, in the conventional sense of the term, anyway. What Freewheelin' signified for Dylan was the first dip into a vaguely surrealist strain of situational imagery - that's right, 'situational imagery' - previously unheard in such down-to-earthness; the words of someone who's studied his Rimbaud but reckons the guy could've been a little more colorful and moved a little brisker. Horror movie imagery like 'a white ladder all covered with water'; fragments like 'you hide from my eyes' or 'I met a young woman whose body was burning' that dare you to not take them literally; 'So it ain't no use in callin' out my name, girl/Like you never done before'; 'I don't have no sports car, and I don't even care to have one/I can walk anytime around the block' (a personal mantra of mine); 'Ten thousand dollars at the drop of a hat/I'd give it all gladly if our lives could be like that'; I could just quote individual lines for the rest of this review, but...well, that wouldn't be executing Maximum Fun Potential.

Nah, see, as great Bob's verse and prose is, the enjoyment of his music - especially in his early years and most especially on Freewheelin' - comes best served with the man's singing. Oft-heard suggestions that Dylan's songs are best sung by others is not only wrong (with a small handful of exceptions), but in most examples misses the whole point of why the guy wasn't quickly shoehorned into Poet Laureate status in the first place. The way he inflects words and phrases, leaping and bouncing and flattening-out into straight-talk monotone, hurling syllables like rocks and then skipping barefoot over them to get to the other side of the river, is agile and heavy and silly and poignant, often all at once.

Note how he inflects almost every line of "Girl from the North Country", its gorgeous melody paced like an English hymn, with a slight shivering breath, giving the 'memory of lost love' sentiment a crucial hint - just a hint - of urgency, desperation; like a last puff of a 'Wait...' as someone walks away from you. Note the stark way he sings 'In...the darkness of my night...' and then trembles gently toward the light with '...in the brightness of my day.' Note the laugh, which is good theatre. Note the one-note crescendo of the harmonica near the end.

Note the sad, knowing denouement sound of the beginning of each of "Blowin' in the Wind"'s choruses. ('The answer, my friend....')

Note the authoritative way he sings 'We thought we could sit forever in fun' in "Bob Dylan's Dream", like a dying teacher calling his class to attention for one final lesson. There's something very touching to me about the way he sings the words 'laughing and singing' — I'm not even sure what.

Note how his vocal in "Masters of War", as previously mentioned, just gets more withering and contemptuous as the song moves on, like a finger pointed at the bad people like they're sewer scum; a half-born cow lying dead with its mother in a pool of blood and piss and flesh; the low, empty, evil bursts of engineered viruses in the deepest black nothings. Note the bluesy stings of the guitar's downsteps, as under 'fast bullets fly.'

Note how he shapes the melody of "Oxford Town" (a quite poppy, quite catchy miniature even despite its subject of racial strife (quite offhand, albeit, which I'd say doesn't qualify it as one of the conventional Protest Songs)), working his lower register into a sober voice coming through an old Victrola. It's like he's running his voice in horizontal and vertical lines in unpredictable distances, like straight lines being drawn through a square maze or the grid of a Mondrian painting being painted without lifting the brush. ('...got their heads bowed down...'; '...t-Aahhxford town....')

Note the slight upward wavers in "Down the Highway", like a record running off its track just for a split-second. ('Lord, I really miss my babeeeee-a-eeeeee....') The toothless hillbilly howl near the end. ('Yes, I'm a-walkin' down your highwa-eeeeee-a-eeeeeee just as far as my eyes can see....') The way he touches the word 'Liberty' (as in, the Statue of Liberty) like a kitten somehow resisting itself from playing with a ball of yarn, laughing it off. Note how the guitar phrasing sounds like a car getting closer and closer to a highway hitchhiker and zooming past to nobody's surprise.

Note how he recites "Talkin' World War III Blues" (like all of his brilliant talking blues songs) as if he's describing it all to a psychiatrist; lines like 'It was a normal day' delivered in a hilariously plainspoken way, so that naturally there's nothing to do but fill the space with some jaunty harmonica. Note the very funny humility of 'I don't blame him too much, though, he didn't know me.' Note the unexpected prick of sad aimlessness of 'Good car to drive...after a war.'

Note how he sings the word 'allow' in "Honey, Just Allow Me One More Chance" with a delightful lap of the tongue with the 'l' sound, the song's perfectly-sequenced springiness only getting springier as it goes on. Note the last chorus, giggling as his mouth is meeting the harmonica just after proving that his rough side is still in the backseat if he needs it.

Note how he sings 'I'll love 'er 'til I'm dead' in "Bob Dylan's Blues", like he's imitating an outspoken kindergartener talking frankly about a doll. Note the off-the-cuff 'right now.'

Note the casual fare-thee-well of "Don't Think Twice, It's All Right", a song that rightly gets a lot of credit as one of the most brilliantly backhanded breakup songs ever written. Note how the fingerpicked progression imitates the lyrics: not overblown, not begging, not outright insulting; just stating the facts with a gentle, almost imperceptible melancholy, disappointed more for the lost time than anything else. (To quote an estimable follower: 'But now it's just another show/You leave 'em, laughing, when you go.')

Note his almost impossibly delicate touch on those high notes on the version of "Corrina, Corrina", with that gorgeous guitar figure from Bruce Langhorne inching the song along almost like a Spanish folk ditty.

Note how he sings "I Shall Be Free" to close it all out like the walk home after another long day, drained but happy. The 'whoooah!'-type noise he makes after name-dropping Willie Mays, Martin Luther King, and Olatunji could've been made into an affectation, a 'look at me!' move; instead, it just sounds like he's overwhelmed.

And let's all just consider "A Hard Rain's a-Gonna Fall" for a minute. Note the imagery, of course: hammers bleeding; the apocalyptic dread of 'a highway of diamonds with nobody on it'; all of it. Note the way it feels like you're being read a fairy tale, in the sense of being allowed to suspend your disbelief enough so that when Dylan sings that he - whoever 'he' is - has 'been out in front of a dozen dead oceans' (like, specifically a dozen), you feel like it's truth. Note the prophetic, scripture-like preclusion of the decade ahead in its most open-hearted highs ('I met a young girl, she gave me a rainbow') and its fearful lows ('I heard the roar of a wave that could drown the whole world', nuclear paranoia on the edge of the abyss).

But most of all, note how he sings it. Just listen to how he sings the word 'rain,' like he's pinching himself so as not to feel the pain of a needle. Or the way he sings 'where hunger is ugly...' like someone who's been there and done that, withered and disgusted at the prospect of it happening to another human being. Or the way he sings the word 'died.' Or the way he sings the third chorus to turn a young sailor yelling through a storm from the crow's nest ('It's a-haaard!') to an old witch foreseeing trouble ahead in a crystal ball ('...a-gonna faaaall...'). Or how he begins the 'What did you hear...' verse - 'Oh what did you hear, my blue-eyed son?/And what did you hear, my darling young one?' - delivering the first 'hear' like a stern father calling his son to place ('here!'), and the second like a subtle, sadistic crack of a whip. Or how he sings 'I heard one person starve, I heard many people laughing' in a way that doesn't bring to mind people laughing at a starving person, but rather a cut to an entirely different time and place; the long, slow, agonized scream of a dying man on a street jumped immediately to the feeling of passing a restaurant with a large table of laughing couples out front. Or the way he ever-so-slightly giggles under 'And who did you meet, my darling young one?', singing the former five syllables like a loving tease from a gossip-hound and the latter five with a warm stress on the 'daahhlin' young one' to feel like a father holding his daughter close in a time of great need. Goddamn it, I love that fucking song. It's a powerful, powerful piece of work. It gives me goosebumps every time, and yet it never, ever slows me down. Oh, and I love the way he pushes the final 'rain' in the fourth chorus harder than ever, and then breaks it with a tiny smile. Everything eventually breaks.

If there's a rebellion in Bob Dylan's music, it's offhand. Like he's in on a good joke, but empathetically taking it for granted that we the listeners can, or will eventually, understand it. He's not so much railing against evil as he's simply dissociating himself from it and letting it make itself impossible on its own silly terms. ('With the merciful thou wilt shew thyself merciful; with an upright man thou wilt shew thyself upright; With the pure thou wilt shew thyself pure; and with the froward thou wilt shew thyself froward.') As for Freewheelin' in particular, it's the best album of 1963, the best album of the early '60s, all 13 songs work, and 50 minutes - which was abnormally long for a studio album of its time (50 years ago) - can seem either lovingly generous or too short...depending on your mood. Blowin' down the road, and smart enough to know when he shouldn't be treated this way. I'll know my song well before I start singin'.

A+.

A good album with a masterpiece not on enough compilations...



Even though an online retailer says that the unremastered version of this album is discontinued, I bought it new a couple of weeks ago (this review was originally posted around this time last year) and will base this review on that, but make passing reference to the remastered version. Another aside...Rate Your Music lists a withdrawn version of this album, which has a slightly different track listing...interesting to know the story behind that!

Many years ago I bought the 3 cd compilation "Masterpieces" because I thought a Dylan album would be like broccoli-that is, 'good for you'. Can't say that I was overwhelmed with that introduction to Dylan. Really, I didn't want to explore his studio albums as I didn't think I'd get anything from them. However, watching a recentish documentary series on Dylan, I'd be intrigued by snatches of Dylan songs used on that. In coming up with a list of the best Dylan songs, I'd include some songs I heard from that series (I think the name of that docu was "No direction home"). Which is what prompted me to buy this album-it had songs I liked the sound of, but which weren't included on his compilations.



The out and out classic on this album is:



Masters of war: features an agitated, simple, accoustic guitar riff. The lyrics are simply stunning in their ferocity. Dylan is a hissing, spitting cobra on this song, squirting venom into the eyes of those with jackboots who cross his path and unsettle him (namely, politicians who take their country to war). The lyrics are blistering, searing, contemptuous and disdainful. The combination of such a simple accoustic arrangement and such withering lyrics is brilliant. Really, this song is timeless. As long as there are humans, there will be wars...pointless ones at that. You Tube no doubt has videos of this song which members have accompanied by pictures of the previous US president, George W. Bush. There will always be an era where a politician will be linked to this song, or could be.



Although I don't put any other songs from this album in the same class as "Masters of war", there are others that I like to a lesser degree:



I shall be free: this song grew on me. It's now probably my second favourite song from the album. Lyrically comical, it has an echo, to me at least, in a song from so called 'anti-folk' group The Moldy Peaches. The Moldy Peaches song which I have in mind is "Anyone else but you".

Down the highway: features a flittering accoustic guitar and bluesy style.

A hard rain's a gonna fall: an epic song, running at nearly seven minutes long. Lyrics seem biblical in tone. Not sure, but I think he may fluff a line, re "knowing my song". If that's right, it may be rectified in the remastered version.

Bob Dylan's dream: like most songs on this album, it features Dylan's harmonica.

Oxford town: about a racist town



Those songs are the ones I like best on this album, but some other songs should be pointed out:



Talking World War III blues: this song places Dylan specifically under the shadow of atomic bomb, in the midst of the 'cold war' between the US and the Soviet Union. Try You Tubing the phrase "Duck and cover" to show some of the absurd propoganda the US used to reassure its citizens around that time that they would be safe from a nuclear assault, so long as they would "Duck and cover" when the nuclear blast went off!
Seems to have some product placement in this song, like rappers are prone to do.

Honey, just allow me one more chance: Dylan in whimsical mode here. He uses a goofy voice and the lyrics are a bit silly too. It's a contrast to songs about racism and the end of the world, in any case.

Corrina, Corrina: the first appearance of 'Judas' Dylan. No doubt in a vain attempt to sweep the world with his music, The Bob goes extravagantly decadent...by utilising drums in this song! The song sounds a little bit country and bluesy.

Blowin' in the wind: a staple of his 'best of' collections. Can't say that this song is a favourite of mine...I tend to associate it with hippies who sound like singing nuns...well, ok, Joan Baez singing in her technically sound voice, but annoyingly nunny or school ma'am-ish voice. If some of the references are explained to you, there is political content to this song. When I was not that familiar with Dylan's music, I scoffed at the notion that he was political, and I especially scoffed at the notion that this song was political...it seemed more poetic than political to me, actually. Watching that documentary series on Bob Dylan, the political writer is re-claimed...with such stunning songs as "Masters of war".


Like I said, I bought the unremastered version, new, for $10 a couple of weeks ago. The sound quality was fine...no complaints, really, but it did sound sort of 'faux stereo', like early Beatles recordings were too. The guitar seemed to come out of one side of my headphones predominantly-maybe it was solely on one side, but I didn't confirm that.

Seeing as I had gotten rid of my receipt for that cd, when I saw the remastered version in the same chain, but a different outlet the following week, I took a chance on it, seeing as I liked the music. It cost me twice as much as the older version. The sound on that does sound more 'stereo-ey'-like the "wide" stereo mode on old portable cassette players. The harmonica definitely sounds more expansive on the remastered version. Occasionally you are aware of what the people doing the remaster are doing, which is a bad thing. It's not ideal to be listening to a song, and once in a while noticing something that could have been done better.

Have listened to the remastered version once only. Really, I can't say that the unremastered version is terrible...it's fine. Maybe you might want to buy the version which is the best value for you.

Overall, even though I don't identify as being a fan of folk music, I do like this album quite a lot. Actually, if you are like me, listening to studio albums by Dylan may be more rewarding than buying a compilation first up. The problem with the latter for me was that of the Dylan songs I knew, I knew them for superior cover versions. And songs that I would consider "essential" now, for having seen that documentary series on Dylan, don't appear on compilations with words like "Essential" in their title. E.g. how can "Masters of war" not be considered an essential song?

This is a charming album of acoustic guitar and harmonica music. Pretty much the only song I think would benefit the album by being removed would be "Girl from the north country" or, maybe "Corrina, Corrina", though that song might grow on me more.

This is an album of its time - a world of cold wars and racial divides. Dylan reflects on these issues, as well as having a bit of fun and lightening the mood.

Was thinking of putting "7/10" in the header for this album, but it grows on you. So, for that fact, I'll score this album 80/100 with a margin of error of +/- 5.

A very good starting point to discover The Bob.



Recommended Dylan:



Blood on the tracks. Mellow album.

The times they are a changin'. Quite the dour album.

Bob Dylan. Decent enough debut, which pays homage to his kindred spirits.

Much easier to digest than its successor The Times They Are A-Changin', here Bob Dylan cemented himself as the black knight of folk, skillfully wielding his guitar like a sword as he strummed his way through the political sphere with his witty and often on-point social commentaries. His vocals are much more refined that those on his debut, Bob Dylan, while the lyrics are completely sound here, and thankfully Dylan only continues to develop his lyrical mastery. Yet the musicianship is just stupendous. Just Dylan, his harmonica and his acoustic guitar. Yet he plays with such ability, his strumming completely fills the vacant voids that would usually be occupied by a bass line or a drum beat that Dylan later introduced in his later, more stupendous work. But here, this is noble Dylan. As I said, the Black Knight. A master of his trade, yet he seeks no reward; only ever trying to persuade the high and mighty to change from their wicked ways. The scathing critical attacks like "Masters of War", "Talking World War III Blues" and "A Hard Rain's A-Gonna Fall" not only showcases Dylan's fine playing ability, but the lyrics are so harsh, so brilliantly scripted; it's as if Dylan had really been there, done that, seen it all. Considering "A Hard Rain's A-Gonna Fall" was featured in a collection of poems, along the sides of works by Wordsworth, Coleridge, Thomas, Allen Poe, Joyce, Shelley, Keats, Blake, Byron etc. I had to study for TEE Literature, must certainly harrow a cry of excellence. I've never question Dylan's empathy, because it's simply something you DON'T DO. He knows all emotions, feels all emotions. Of the album isn't all deep and dark criticism of a corrupt political system, Dylan preaches of his love and affairs, such as the succulent "Girl From the North Country", which is bounding with musical creativity, the fun and toe-tap worthy "Honey, Just Allow Me One More Chance", perhaps being the silliest track on the album, before finally blending these two themes of politics and love in the fine closer "I Shall Be Free".

Then of course, you have the album stalwart, "Blowin' in the Wind". While I personally prefer "The Times They Are a-Changin'" (probably just), Blowin' in the Wind is perhaps more user friendly and features one of the best tunes scripted, including that highly memorable harmonica solo.

KEY TRACK(S): Blowin' in the Wind, Girl From the North Country, Masters of War, A Hard Rain's a-Gonna Fall, Don't Think Twice, It's All Right, I Shall Be Free

I often forget how much I like Bob Dylan. After all, I spent half of my eighth grade, all of my ninth grade, AND half of my tenth grade year on a virtually-nonstop Dylan binge. It got to the point where my notebook (if by "notebook" we mean "computer..." "notebook" just works better metaphorically) filled with rewrites of various Dylan songs. I had my own version of "Gates of Eden," "Brownsville Girl," "Just Like Tom Thumb's Blues," and a BUNCH of others, I'm sure. And it looks like I'm on the cusp of another one. I hope my roommate doesn't mind. Actually, seeing that he listens to Beyonce and whatnot, he probably will. Oh well.

Anyway, I played this one for the first time in months last Friday, and I forgot how much I liked it. I thought of busting out the five-star, but then I remembered that I don't like "Bob Dylan's Dream" much. Wonderfully poignant lyrics, don't get me wrong - I know exactly what he's talking about - but, musically, ass-boring. It does beat the shit out of anything from Down in the Groove (Sadly, Dylan isn't one of those - yeah, I'm gonna say it - musical geniuses who never put out a bad album (*coughJohnColtranecough*)), but in this context, it doesn't work so well.

But still, great stuff! It's easily the strongest of Dylan's acoustic albums, and it's loaded with fantastic songs. Of course, the main strength here is the lyrics. Because it's Bob Dylan, you know? And it's not all protests - while there are definitely protests here, one-dimensional this album is not. There are a couple brilliant love songs, some philosophizing (often mixed with the protest material), even a bit of humor here. Ha!

The one I'm sure you know is "Blowin' in the Wind," because it's been covered by just about everyone, features some extremely famous lyrics (you know, "How many roads must a man walk down?"), and showed up in The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy, which guarantees its brilliance. I don't think I really need to tell you about it, because it's "Blowin' in the Wind" and all. But it's fantastic. However, the one I like most here is "Don't Think Twice, It's All Right," one of Bob's many deconstructions of the classic love song. While Bob's musical ability often gets slagged on as being "limited" (from those who are willing to be charitable), the fingerpicking he does there is great. More importantly, the lyrics are... well, Dylan lyrics. Come on. "Girl from the North Country" is also gorgeous, although the shrill harmonica blasts kind of get to me. Whatever, it's still one of his classics.

Let's see, what else? Oh, I know! Protest songs! Well, "Masters of War" and especially "It's a Hard Rain's a-Gonna Fall" are still relevant and chilling today (Okay, so I wasn't alive in 1963, but hey). "Hard Rain" in particular is fantastic, loaded with such powerful imagery that it goes beyond "protest song" and crosses into "Biblical Fury," not to mention "nightmare fuel." I still don't like listening to that song after dark, just because the lyrics are downright frightening sometimes. "Talkin' World War III Blues" is also a sort of protest, but it also blends in elements of humor. It's a funny song, too.

And that brings me to category #3: the Humor Song! I think that segway deserves a bowl of nachos, even though I just ate one. Right before I usually go to bed when I actually have a class to get up for, no less. I will dream of nachos tonight. But this is not about nachos, this is about the funny songs. Some of the jokes on "I Shall Be Free" go over my head, specifically the ones where he references the women who I'm just guessing were big-deal sex symbols in '63 but are dead or whatever now. Other parts of it do crack me up, though, so it's all good. "Bob Dylan's Blues" works a lot better as far as that goes. I especially love the verse that closes it. "Well, lookit here, buddy, if you wanna be like me, pull out your six-shooter and rob every bank you see. Tell the judge I said it was all right." Come on. It's funny. When was the last time a folksinger from Greenwich Village who wrote about politics and dated Joan Baez and almost got his amps axed by Pete Seeger broke the fourth wall? that, and that alone, is enough to confirm that Bob was a badass.

Not all of it is up to that high standard, but even weak-for-this-album songs (the two covers and "Oxford Town" come to mind - I forgot to mention the desolate "Down the Highway" among the highlights, but I really like that one, too) sound good. Hell, even "Bob Dylan's Dream" is, as I mentioned, one of the truest songs ever written. Back when I was a freshman, I bought this very album with a friend while we were on one of our record store raids, and him and I drifted apart shortly thereafter, so I guess you can say I've lived this song. More than once, in fact. You probably have, too. It's just that Bob really skimps on the melody there, which in this case is a nice way of saying "didn't really write one." Brilliant lyrics, though.

I'm talking in circles by now, but dammit, that's not the point! The point is that if you have any interest whatsoever in Bob Dylan, and you somehow haven't heard this album, make it a priority. Tell me this: who else in '63 who wasn't a jazz musician (Don't forget, The Black Saint and the Sinner Lady, Monk's Dream, Impressions, and Midnight Blue came out that year) had their shit together the way Bob did? James Brown did put out Live at the Apollo that year, but he was still in brilliant singles and useless filler mode at that point (though he never really got out of that mode), The Beatles were just starting out, the Rolling Stones, Who, Kinks, Beach Boys etc. hadn't even put out albums yet, the Doors are overrated, a lot of iconic '60s acts (Hendrix, Janis Joplin, etc.) weren't even making music yet, and Motown never really thought very hard about studio albums until the '70s in the first place. So, yeah. Bob Dylan kicks ass. I'll never forget it again, I promise.

Apologies for the length of this thing.
