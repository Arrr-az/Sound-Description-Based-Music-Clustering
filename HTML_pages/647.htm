It's usually a tie between this and Loaded for least-regarded Velvets album; "Sister Ray" gets all the licks, but that's mostly it. Kids these days, they want the avant-garde shamans, or they want the cozy acoustic upper-crust lullabyes. But the thing is, the Velvets were fuckin' comedy rock. My favorite part of the whole record isn't even in the best song, it's the way Lou deadpans his way into Cale's fey narrative on "Lady Godiva's Operation". Changes the tone totally, as he worms his way in, wheezing like his own Lou Reed send-up. "White Light/White Heat" was the song that first gave me the idea of the sly freakout... all my moves were pop/rock and clean hooks and here comes this white-bread song from 1961 dressed up in shitface cymbals and off-beat chuggle. So there's that and "I Heard Her Call My Name," both joyous and full of pull-face moments from Reed on the mike - those white yelps. "The Gift" I can take or leave, but the resolution, musical 'n' narrative, makes it worth sticking around for. The only thing that really comes close to the otherworldly Velvets sound you kids seem to dig is "Here She Comes Now," which in the right universe functions as everyone's "Wonderful Tonight". Thank God Nico didn't get her hands on this one. My hunch is that like the first record, Cale got his prints all over this, cos the dude can slip on a peel when he wants to.

So I'm watching all this San Fran footage from the dawn of the Height/Owsley/Dead era, and I'm struck at how joyless it all seems. Like, even the trips to the universal consciousness are done so seriously... the Acid Tests, Leary somberly pronouncing "we are hoping to usher in a new era of spiritual perception" - I mean, thank God the Velvets got some shots in. The last thing we needed was 50,000 solipsistic hippies dictating rock 'n' roll policy for the next forty years. Didn't Leary ever realize the best thing acid ever did was H.P. Lovecraft's "Keeper of the Keys"?

My old review was a piece of shit, and I don't think it quite summed up how great White Light / White Heat. It's a definitive grower, and I now consider my favorite Velvet Underground album, though all three are just about equal in terms of quality. This is VU at its most avant-garde. Many of these songs twist the definition of art, rock music, and songs in general. Some of the songs here might be difficult to even consider songs. "The Gift, "I Heard Her Call My Name", and "Sister Ray" are all nearly unclassifiable. "The Gift" is an experimental spoken-word piece about a man who mails himself to a girl and gets murdered accidentally. There's a bit of dark, twisted humor in the song, and the backing track is very interesting. "Heard Her Call My Name" is literally ear-searing noise, with some of the most mind-blowing guitar solos I've ever heard. It's the noisiest song on the album, although "Sister Ray" is just as noisy and just as intense. "Sister Ray" is 17 minutes long ,which may seem overlong at first, but is worth every second. Throughout the song, every member of the VU are trying to out-noise each other, resulting in a beautifully successful mess.

After those, you have the three more accessible songs on the album, though it would be a stretch to call any of them accessible outside of the albums context. "White Light / White Heat" would be a normal rock n' roll song if not for the mountains of noise piled upon it. "Lady Godiva's Operation" takes a fairly nice-sounding melody and repeats it throughout the track, making it repetitive, but not monotonous. The song also has odd lyrics about a failed lobotomy, and occasional vocals from Lou Reed sang (or spoken) much louder than John Cale. The song, like "The Gift", is full of dark humor. It's the weakest song here, but I can see it growing on me, like the rest of the album. Lastly, "Here She Comes Now" is a simple, beautiful pop song, unlike anything else on the album. The lyrics and vocals have a weird, sexual edge to them that sets it apart.


Overall, this is an album that can't be missed. The best album by one of the best bands ever to come out of the druggy 60s.

I don't go here often. There are a few albums I like more than White Light/White Heat, but none of them demand so much of me as both a listener and a body, sending me into a bliss intense enough to make me self-conscious in the extreme. Why do these piercing squalls captivate me so? I've no idea. All I know is that when the back half kicks in, I'm there--hunched forward, head in speakers, and shaking some sort of tic out of my system, fears of irate neighbors and physical silliness being the only blocks keeping me tethered.

It's a cleansing experience, really. I'm seldom as far removed from my typical nervousness as I am here, and yet I'm seldom more connected with my gangly, otherwise distant frame.

So for this, I'm grateful. Grateful for the chance to escape myself, grateful for such searing textures and relentless rhythms--I'm even grateful for "The Gift", an adventure in stereo frenetic enough to reflect the internal chatter it helps me avoid. Perhaps above all, though, I'm grateful that something as mangled and convulsive as "I Heard Her Call My Name" or "Sister Ray" is able to touch me so deeply as to register as beauty.

From what I hear, that's not always the case.

As cliché as it may be to say, this album changed my life. I was 14 years old and only listened to three bands: Zeppelin, Floyd and Sabbath (Deep Purple were alright too). I regularly browsed lists of "greatest guitar solos" anywhere on the internet that I could find them, to either reaffirm my belief that Jimmy Page was the greatest guitarist ever, or to scoff at any list that didn't include him towards the top.

One day I found one that had I Heard Her Call My Name as #1. I couldn't believe it. I was disgusted. I even e-mailed the guy in protest, who laughed at my puny and juvenile argument (and rightly so). I downloaded the track using WinMX and thought it was decent enough, so I decided to hear the rest of the album. Piracy was extremely tedious seeing as I had a dial-up connection, so I had my stepmom buy me this album as well as Velvet Underground & Nico from Best Buy as my 15th birthday present.

A couple days after my birthday I turned all the lights off in my room, closed all the curtains, shut down everything except my stereo and put on my new curiosity. It was the most frightening experience I've ever had while listening to an album. Awkward pubescent kid living in a sheltered "dinosaur rock" world being first exposed to this murky avant-garde ode to heroin, sex changes and sleazy drug dealers. After that day it was at least a few years before I even considered listening to a Zeppelin or Floyd record.

I just adored everything about it: the title, the cover art, the production, the total rawness of it all; it was really the first truly ballsy record I ever heard. I got a t-shirt with the album cover on it and wore it on my first day of sophomore year of high school. I spent hours and hours and hours in my room practically ripping the strings off my shitty Washburn electric trying to replicate the distortion in the solo to I Heard Her Call My Name. It wasn't long before I was exploring the discographies of Sonic Youth, Dinosaur Jr., Butthole Surfers, etc.

I recall preaching to friends about how this was the most influential album of all time because it "instantly created like 5 genres and was at least two decades ahead of its time". Whether there's any truth to that is up for debate - I was naive and smitten. What I do know is that the moment I pressed play on that white Aiwa stereo I had, I began to slowly realize for the first time that there's a fuckload of totally valid and exciting music out there that isn't made by guys with crotch bulges and frilly symbols to represent their personas.
