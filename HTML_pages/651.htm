This sits right next to Illinois in the CD wallet of my car, but though both albums are named after states and were released by the same artist, there are more differences than you'd think.

Illinois is clean, it's bright. Even 'John Wayne Gacy Jr' is able to be perfectly heard and understood. Each second of the album jumps quickly from the speakers and into your ears. Colorful. Crisp.

But Michigan is a different animal. If you're familiar with Illinois and taking your first listen to Michigan, you'll immediately know what I'm talking about. This album is fragile, it's very light. It exists in a haze, hovering somewhere in a warm place. Each sound is lucky to reach the listener. Sufjan is also quieter here; there's more whispering than a library argument.

I'm not here to tell you that Michigan is superior to Illinois because it has a more lo-fi feel that allows you connect more organically with the songs. This is just one of the best batches of songs around. From the great opener ('Flint'), through the first half of the album ('For the Widows in Paradise...'), to the middle which Sufjan seems to have reprised in spirit on Illinois ('Detroit', 'Romulus'), all the way through the last three unbelievable tracks ('Oh God, Where Are You Now?', 'Redford', 'Vito's Ordination Song'), you won't be treated to a poor song along the way.

I enjoy this album most in the Summer, but it sounds like Winter to me. My fiancee hears it more as a Summer album, specifically the bit in 'Holland' about 'sleeping on Lake Michigan'. In my mind, they're sleeping on an iced over lake. In hers, they're in a small boat of some kind with a hat pushed over their eyes. Either way you slice it, that song and line are the centerpiece of the album and pretty much sum up everything as succinctly as possible.

This is not to be missed. Perhaps a heathly, developed love for Illinois might prohibit you from enjoying this in full due to the production, but I implore you to make the effort if you haven't already.

Don't let the Martha Stewart Living tourist brochure cover art mislead you, Michigan is an elegy for the state as it suffocates from its economic collapse (particularly in the automotive industry implosion of the 1970s), class disintegration and frozen climate. Even if it is currently rebounding from these troubles, Michigan is Sufjan Stevens' personal portrait of the state as it was when he was growing up there, when it hit such lows that Flint, Michigan had a reputation as one of the poorest, worst cities in America (when Michael Moore makes a documentary about you, things aren't going well).

Both in sound and theme, Michigan sits directly in between Seven Swans and Illinois. It has some of the Reichian big mathematical compositions, songs based around various local histories and civic slogans, and it was the kicking-off of the quasi-mythical 50 States Project, but it is much more subdued and personal than Illinois. This was where Sufjan grew up and it is an elegy for his lost childhood in equal measure as it is for the Michigan he grew up in. It is fiction drawn from hard personal experiences, and at times highly evocative and emotionally stirring.

The opening ode to Flint, dedicated to 'the Unemployed and Underpaid,' is a lament of loneliness and giving up, homeless and beaten down, 'I pretend to try, even if I tried alone.' It's a quiet tune with just the mournful horns hinting at the level of orchestration to come with the sharp-edged, slanted bustle of "All Good Naysayers! Speak Up! Or Forever Hold Your Peace!" and "Detroit, Lift Up Your Weary Head! (Rebuild! Restore! Reconsider!)" featuring layered levels of vocals and music interlocking in complex and unusual rhythms, although not quite so swelling and busy as what the metropoles of Illinois would bring. Much of Michigan is still based on banjo, even some of the larger, more orchestrated tracks swirl their compositions and 'dramatic cymbal swells' around the simple, pastoral sound of Sufjan picking the banjo. There are also some strange chiming vibes and glockenspiel pieces representing bodies of water.

This album means a lot to me for a lot of personal reasons and there are two songs in particular that never fail to break me down. "Holland" is a very subdued bit of doomed romance and I cloud up right from 'All the time we spent in bed, counting miles before we set.' Light acoustic guitar and the light sparkle of piano, even softer and more clutched to the breast than the songs on Seven Swans, building some momentum of sadness. Beautiful, utterly. The second is the stark "Romulus" about maternal estrangement, and the closing 'and I was ashamed of her' is devastating, with just the slightest tremor in his voice.

Of course, for all the tragedy and sadness of the album, it is tempered with light humour. The epic "Detroit, Lift Up Your Head!" is a cheerfully ridiculous series of gleeful pronouncements like 'Tigers game!' and 'Wolverine!' and 'Auto cars!' concluding with the aside 'hesistate to burn the buildings.' This song sprawls for eight minutes in between those two deep, dark emotional moments. Of course, the song titles throughout are mostly touristy nonsense of absurd cheer (especially "Say Yes! To M!ch!gan!"). However, this is much more downbeat than any of his other work, just the romance of tragedy writ large.

Excerpt from yesterday's Daily Mail

Last night the US tourist board issued the following statement:

"We, the US tourist board, would like to reassure potential visitors to our shores that America is in fact a diverse and multi-faceted country with a rich tapestry of flora and fauna, startling contrasts in landscape and scenery, and the differences between the people of one state and the next are often as radical as the differences between the peoples of two disparate continents.

"With this in mind, we have seen it necessary to relieve our musical ambassador, Mr. Sufjan Stevens, of his duties which were, until today, to provide a soundtrack to each of our 50 States which would be indicative of the lifestyle led by the people of each state, and sonically descriptive of the landscape present there.

"This decision came after the Board completed it's annual review of the work of each individual department and found that Mr, Stevens' work, to date incorporating the states of Michigan and Illinois, has been too similar and, we feel, too depressing to encourage substantial returns in the number of tourists to these destinations.

"The US tourist board would like to insist that neither Michigan nor Illinois are best described in Stevens' own idiosyncratic style, which for those of you who haven't been weighed down by the sometimes overbearing sadness of his work, can be frequently interpreted thus: dee de dee, dee de dee, wibble wobble wibble, dee de dee, dee de dee, wibble wobble wibble. Upon consultation with the people of these states, it has been ascertained that the sound best describing their home is tra la la, ta la laaa and tum titty tum titty tum for Michigan and Illinois respectively.

"The US tourist board is hoping to recruit a new ambassador for music but have not at this time appointed anybody, although David Hasslehoff has voiced an interest."

In response to his dismissal, Mr. Stevens commented that he felt he had seen into the hearts and souls of some of the characters he met on his travels through the states. Talking specifically about Michigan he admitted that, "It is a sad place, obviously not everyone is a manic depressive, but there is a stale culture there which is concerned with conservation and maintaining the status quo. I tried to capture the aspirations of those trapped souls and feel that I injected a degree of hope into the record which, sadly, the US tourist board failed to pick up on."

When asked why his two records to date were so similar, Stevens dismissed the accusation explaining that Illinois was a more upbeat affair. He went on to point out, "Obviously it is always me writing the music, so my own style is bound to shine through on each release, but I think each record has it's own unique sound. Michigan for example, is quite cold, almost like a perpetual winter, but we can hear the cracks of change as they begin to appear."

Asked what he thought of the rumour that David Hasslehoff may take on his old role, Mr. Stevens raised an eyebrow but declined to comment.

The US tourist board's loss is Rough Trade's gain, and our gain, as these two delightful records are now available for public consumption, and for those of you who like soft beauty in your music, that has to be a good thing.

Geez, Michigan must be a depressing place.

The tone of Michigan is defined by the first track, "Flint". It's a song about loneliness and unemployment. We've heard Flint described in similar terms before by Michael Moore in the film Roger and Me. Moore reacted with righteous anger to the suffering of his hometown, and his movie paints a picture of Flint as a city of sad, strange people abandoned by the company they helped build. Stevens uses Flint as the background for Michigan's first track, and he's created a song that is both sad and beautiful without a hint of anger or outrage.

Anger is easy. Finding beauty in suffering is hard.

"Holland" is another sad and beautiful song on Michigan, but not all songs on the album are depressing in nature. "Detroit" is a tune done in 9/8 time. The time signature is important because of the type of imagery that it helps to evoke. Triplet based signatures (6/8, 9/8, etc) tend to sound round and rolling (as opposed to something like 4/4 time, which marches unwaveringly forward). The 9/8 time on Detroit helps to evoke the images of wheels rotating, and it can be thought of as wheels on a car or the wheels of factory machinery turning as they build the cars. These rolling signatures are used on most of the more upbeat songs on the album.

Quite honestly, this is an album with depth that can stand up to a lot of analysis. If you're looking for music that is both challenging and pleasant to listen to, this may be the album for you.

I knew I would like this album when I first saw it back in February. I had just heard Sufjan Stevens' masterpiece track Chicago for the first time, looked him up on here, seen the incredible love Illinois had received, and took a look at the rest of his discography to see what I could find. But while several of Sufjan's albums have received more praise than it, it was Michigan that immediately stood out to me through its cover. While Illinois' cover features an industrial monstrosity staring you in the face, Michigan contrasts this, with soothing, light colours and a much greater emphasis on the natural. This all combines to create an invitingly cozy picture. Fast forward to now, and Javelin is one of the highest rated albums of the year, finally motivating me to start listening to the full Sufjan Stevens discography, which I have been meaning to do for a long time.

Stevens' has created the most unique and interesting records I've ever had the pleasure of hearing. Maybe not sonically, though of course it is no slouch in this area either, sounding beautiful acroos the board, but moreso through it's lyrics and structure. The album functions as a rough guide to Stevens' home state of Michigan, which I know very little about, but my knowledge of it (or at least, what it was like in the early 2000's) has definitely increased having listened to the album couple of times.

The journey starts off in Flint, a city which, like many others in the area, was slowly dying due to economic depression. Stevens portrays the role of a recently laid off worker, who has finally lost the ability to care despite knowing he should ("I pretend to cry"). The soft vocals are backed by a similarly soft piano and some low but slowly rising brass melodies, which really add to the feeling of hopelessness. The track is much slower than any of those that follow it, so in my eyes it was a bold but succesful choice to put this at the start of the project, as it really establishes the economic deterioration that much of urban Michigan suffered from.

After a pretty smooth transition, the instrumental picks up on All Good Naysayers, Speak Up! or Forever Hold Your Peace! (The first of many hilariously long titles to come). The album is an intentionally vague discussion of politics, inserted into the album more to establish Stevens' sound than to add substance, being the first appearance of most of his signature instruments. However, it is by no means just the average Sufjan Stevens instrumental, as the track is in 5/4. All Good Naysayers is a pretty great track instrumentally, and still very much competent lyrically, culminating in what essentially functions as a belated introductory track.

We are then transported to our next location, a small town called Paradise in the north of the state. Despite being the most popular song on the album as of writing, I don't have that much to say about it. Stevens' interpretation of the town is one he has admitted to making up, though the more general discussion of being a single parent is welcome, as are the religous connotations which fit the instrumental very well. I do get the popularity of the track, it sounds more airy than any other, and it has beautiful backing vocals, but I don't think it is anywhere near the albums best.

Following on from this, we get the closest thing to a titular track, Say Yes! to M!ch!gan!. This track is up there with Flint in terms of quality to me, explaining how Stevens' childhood in the state shaped him as a person and how much he would like to go back to it, over one of the more low-key instrumentals which is somewhat carried by a gorgeous trumpet. It's one of the shortest tracks on the album, yet it covers a surprising amount of ground, particular considering the slow delivery of Stevens' vocals across the entire project, and shapes up to be an amazing song. The outro with what I think is an electric piano briefly coming out of nowhere is also very enjoyable.

Next we take a trip to The Upper Peninsula, a less populated and more rural part of Michigan (though it is the location of the aforementioned town of Paradise). Stevens' once again takes the role of a different person, in this case an unemployed, divorced man who blames his losses on so called "Strange Ideas". These could either present him as somone whose identity is built on living rurally, and therefore these ideas would be related to urbanisation, or a conservative (for my sake I hope it is the former). At some point his son, who is fine with these ideas, leaves, possibly because of a dispute with the narrator. This results in the father driving around all night to find him, painting him in a more sypmathetic, caring light. The instrumental here is more similar to Flint than the other tracks covered so far, but only in that is is slower and more melancholy, still keeping the same instrumentation as the rest of the album. A short electric guitar segment ends the track, which is probably the most gorgeous part of the album so far. Another truly great track.

Staying in the Upper Peninsula will give us our next track, focused on Tahquamenon Falls which are located near the town of Paradise. The music is pretty simple, essentially a wall of soft bells which create a very idyllic feeling. Looking at some footage of the falls, it certainly fits the picture, as they are (relative to other waterfalls) quite calm and are surrounded by very lush woodland which instill the same feeling.

Stevens' then leaves the Upper Peninsula behind, taking us to the small Lakeside town of Holland, where he briefly reminisces about a past relationship. Only two short verses are found on the track, with Stevns' humming forming a bridge between them. The instrumental also becomes quite simplified, with only a piano and guitar until the end, but the polyphonic relationship between the instruments makes the music feel like it is constantly changing. Despite it's simplicity, Holland ends up being one of the most relaxing and enjoyable tracks on the entire album.

This leads into one of the largest and grandest tracks on the album, Oh Detroit, Lift Up Your Weary Head! (Rebuil! Restore! Reconsider!). This track is a whole 8 minutes long, and features quite a lot of ground, particularly when compared to any of the tracks before it. Of course, the album is about what is probably the US' most notorious city, Detroit, which similar to Flint, underwent an economic fall from grace starting towards the end of the 20th century. The track opens with a light instrumental, which quickly gives way to one fitting of a bustling city, with light, fast drums and powerful brass. Stevens' only begins to sing at around 1:30, giving plenty of time for this upbeat music to set into our minds, then immediately contrasting this with the line "Once a great place, now a prison". His singing remains upbeat for the most part, combining with the instrumental to represent the Detroit that once was, but the lyrics in the first verse are focused on the Detroit of now, a husk of what it once was. The instrumental then cuts back to the chorus, which does call back to the Detroit of old, but to emphasise that this Detroit is in the past through heavy vocal effects and Stevens' softening his voice. The instrumental then builds back up again before entering the second verse, which is much longer than the first and as such has room to reference many specificities about Detroit, such as its role as "Motor City" and the native Iroquois people. This leads into an instrumental section with a great sounding guitar solo. I think this part of the track is supposed to represent the biggest boom in Detroits economy, which is then abruptly cut away from with the second chorus. This chorus starts out the same as the first, but quickly builds up to create a picture of the more chaotic Detroit during its decline. After the chorus finishes the instrumental cuts back once more, this time to its most minimalist. It attempts to build itself up to sound like it did at the start of the track, but it sounds much more worn out, eventually giving up to become an ambient outro. This clearly is more reminiscent of Detroit now, unable to recreate its former glory. This section also goes well with notes Stevens' wrote about some of the tracks on this album, which open with the line "Oh Detroit, you complicated old man, nearly dead, with your shoulders arched over the river, polluted and grey, the threads of your shirt worn down with disease and car exhaust. You have grown fat and thin with industry, car factories, Motown music, riots, raids, transportation nightmares." (All the notes can be found here: https://fuckyeahsufjanstevens.tumblr.com/tagged/michigan%20stories - the notes on the Upper Peninsula are a particularly good read). Detroit is a fantastic song, and a great centerpiece for the album to be fixed to.

The next track, Romulus, is another masterpiece. The title to this track has two meanings, as Romulus is both a suburb of Detroit and one of the two brothers who founded Rome, who were both abandoned as babies by their mother. Stevens' had a somewhat similar experience as a child, as his mother moved to Oregon when he was 1 years old, and later remarried (I believe this is the subject of Carrie & Lowell which I am yet to listen to). The song starts from the perspective of Stevens' as a child, and how much he missed his mother to the point that he blamed himself for her leaving, but over the course of the track he ages and realises that mother wasn't all that great, as she didn't care about what is presumably the death of her own father. It's an extremely emotional and absolutely brilliant song, and absolutely my personal favourite from the entire album.

Another instrumental track follows, the relatively simple and short Alanson, Crooked River (Alanson is a town situated on the crooked river). While this track is nothing special on its own, it is really great within the context of the album, winding it down after two of the most intense tracks up to that point. It is also separates them from another intense track, Sleeping Bear, Sault Ste. Marie, which is my least favourite track. It sounds great, but I think it is a little too lacking lyrically, essentially just naming places in Michigan. They Also Mourn Who Do Not Wear Black (For the Homeless In Muskegon) is a big improvement from this however. This song is also pretty simple in it's lyrics, but it still gets it's point across much more effectively, and the irregular sounding instrumental really helps the lyrics more than the track prior.

After these tracks we have the longest track on the album, Oh God Where Are You Now? (In Pickerel Lake? Pigeon? Marquette? Mackinaw?). This track is an accumulation of the christian themes found on many of the previous tracks, with Stevens' asking God to reveal themselves to him. The slow piano-based instrumental fits the themes perfectly, invoking a feeling of this stereotypically rural form of christianity. I'm not big on Christian themes in music normally,as I think they come off as preachy, but this to me is the exception. The track ends similarly to Detroit with a more ambient outro, but this one feels more angelic and grander, which I believe is to link it to the lyrical themes of the final track.

The penultimate track is Redford (For Yia-Yia amd Pappou). It is yet another instrumental track, with a similar sound pallette to the main body of the track before. Yia-Yia and Pappou are clearly two of Stevens' grandparents, evidenced further by the track notes I mentioned earlier, which I quote in full "Bless our grandfathers and grandmothers. The work of their bodies, the work of their hands. The homes they built, the swimming pools and picture frames and wax candles and Christmas decorations put in the window. Bless their children and their grandchildren, the small faces, the hard faces, the frowns and eyebrows, the hair braids and aprons and gifts wrapped in newspaper. Bless their business and their sleep. Their labor, their watching of TV, their bowls of peanut shells and orange rinds and red grapes washed with water. Bless the omelettes and the cigarettes and the scotch in the freezer, the money given away. Put their hearts to rest, their spirits to rest. Give them sleep. Give them rest.". These grandparents are clearly two people Stevens' cared a lot about, and I think it fitting to put this track between the two most religion-heavy songs on the album, possibly indicating that this track is in reference to their passing (I'm kinda guessing with this so don't take my word for it!)

Finally, we reach Vito's Ordination Song. This song takes the idea of the narrator not being Stevens' to a new level, by having two narrators, of which he is neither. First, Stevens' sings from the perspective of Reverend Thomas Vito Aiuto from when he was ordained, who also provided backing vocals on the song. The first two verses are from this perspective and are pretty good. The second narrator is God themselves. "Vito" is trying to communicate with God during his part and God responds with just four lines repeated over and over, "Rest in my arms, Sleep in my bed, There's a design, To what I did and said". But that isn't the only person being responded to, as this is a direct response to the prayer from Oh God Where Are You Now?. Whether or not Stevens or Vito are able to hear God's response is unclear, but I personally don't think either of them are, and therefore this is actually just what Stevens' thinks god would respond with, which honestly makes the track feel more hopeful to me. The fade out at the end then really helps close the album up perfectly.

Michigan was the album that sold the world of Sufjan Stevens, but it did more than that for me. It sold me on the beauty inherent to the state of Michigan, even at it's lowest point. Unsurprisingly I would recommend this to everyone on the planet, even if I think they wouldn't like it.
