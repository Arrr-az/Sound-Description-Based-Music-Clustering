In my dreams I see a hand. The hand reaches out. I scream at it to stop - I want to grab it, stamp on it, hack it off at the wrist, but I cannot. It closes inexorably on its target. At the other end of the arm stands a man - no, a boy - badly clothed and badly haired, intoxicated by the thought of a CD for 3.99.

And what a majestic creation it is. Mysterious embossed symbols adorn the edge of the casing - what trickery is this? The monoliths on the cover do not tell... struck dumb yet open-mouthed, forever on the brink of divulgence.

The boy knows not the shame of trespass. The monoliths' secret is exposed by his clammy hands. His unfettered gaze falls on a disc of terrible beauty, surely hewn from some divine PVC variant, and sculpted by a being more god than man. He brushes those enchanting rivulets of red and green, defiling their virgin channels with muck and sweat.

The case is lined with a weighty tome, perversely restrained by just one pair of little plastic semicircles, not the conventional two. No room for orthodoxy in the universe of the Floyd.

The decision is made. _The Division Bell_ is to be the Third Album of the Collection. The neighbouring discs, with their bloodless images of men on fire and optical prisms, are shunned.

It smoulders in the boy's pocket until he is home. He heads for the solace of his room, and places his prize in its new home. The hand reaches out again, to press play, and the screams begin again. I cannot close my sleeping ears to the onrushing horror. It's too late...too late...too late...

I am awake. Silence laps at my unchained senses.


The dreams are less frequent now, and the terror has lost a little of its sting, but the lesson was learned well, and learned often. I have kept those mute statues, and the dread secret they keep. I know I cannot destroy them, so I content myself with shielding others from their power. I will seek out another suitable guardian before I die.

Do not pity me, reader. Pity is the preserve of the strong, and no man can have strength in the face of Pink Floyd's _The Division Bell_.

My parents played The Division Bell often when I was growing up, so before my ears even awoke to music this album was already a longtime resident of some little corner of my brain. Coming back to it as a young adult and a bigger fan of Pink Floyd's earlier work, I recognize that this album is pleasant but boring, with it's orchestration and constantly droning Gilmour. The guitar solos just kinda waft around like a lazy Sunday afternoon. "Marooned" is definitely my favorite track (incidentally, it is instrumental). And "High Hopes" is pretty good, but a lot of this is just nostalgia-triggering, like I was saying. "Take It Back" encapsulates that awful "late 80's early 90's production sound" where the drums sound really big and the guitars just fuzz around. I can't see how a person could develop strong feelings for this record. It's disappointingly benign and bland. The cover art, designed by Storm Thorgerson and created by Aden Hynes and John Robertson, is very cool. Always been a big fan of Easter Island (if that's even what they were going for).

The Dark Side Of The Moon for the next generation.
I could always see Pink Floyd as a half moon, Roger Waters' black dark, sad, anger at war and the loss of his father opposed to David Gilmour's white positive searching, inquisitive mind looking into the universe (and beyond?). (Ignoring Syd's Pink Floyd as a different band entirely).

Free from Roger Waters' darkness, we find David Gilmour guitar-in-holster flying us to the farthest reaches of the universe and leaving us there to absorb the wonder and beauty of it all, questioning our place in space and, clinging on to David's guitar lead, we return to Earth much the better for it.

Amongst the atmospheric sounds there are 4 songs to be added to Pink Floyd's folder of classic songs - What Do You Want From Me, Take It Back, Coming Back To Life and Keep Talking (ft. Stephen Hawking), all great classic rock songs (there are no poor tracks here).
Then, we have High Hopes, The Division Bell anthem, so powerful, perhaps a fine closure to the Pink Floyd saga.

If you only buy one Pink Floyd album from the 90's, buy this one (ha, ha).
Top 5 Pink Floyd albums:
1) Wish You Were Here
2) The Division Bell
3) Dark Side Of The Moon
4) Meddle
5) Piper At The Gates Of Dawn
(but 2 of their best songs are on The Wall).

A much maligned but, in my views, wonderful album, although I suspect that the haters mostly fall in the Waters fan-club. Freed of the burden of Waters' vitriolic doom on the last album he had been involved in ("The Final Cut"), Gilmour, Wright and Mason fly free of the shackles at last. Waters had been poisoning the well for years and years, dragging the others down and gradually losing all sense of enjoyment with his infatuation with angst. The previous outing was akin to a Gilmour solo album, although I still view it as a full Floyd outing, here we have a rich, melodic adventure with never a dull second. Every track is a success and what a cracking way to finish a career. Thank goodness Rick Wright was able to contribute, however much that actually was, before his sad, early death and whilst Pink Floyd was still a reality. His demise means that there will never be another Floyd album but they have left a legacy which is as great a body of work as any other group have managed in the modern era.

Magnificent! For me their 2nd best album in terms of artistic achievement and overall consistency.

I have often seen complaints that Gilmour "couldn't even write the lyrics", instead employing wife Polly to manage the task - as though that actually detracted from the music. I was about to get annoyed, if not angry at the user who requested a review of Division Bell from me, because I remember exactly the place and time when I wrote that review the first time. Little does it matter that I was at my history class and took the back pages of my notebook in order to write that review, and I remember I was quite happy with the result, but the users name was kinda cool (Captain_Slow), and it had been a while since the last time I had heard the album in mention. My surprise was when I realized I had actually tagged the album with a needs new review sign, so it was totally my mistake.

Division Bell is often despised as been one of the weaker links in Pink Floyds career. This concept is comprehensible when you have such gems as Dark Side of the Moon, Wish You Were Here, or Animals to compare with, and probably, being this the last album by the band, many people expected a crowning achievement which closed their career with a golden knot. Hard could that be achieved, when mastermind behind the three albums mentioned before, Roger Waters, had left after doing The Final Cut. Roger was a visionary man, not afraid to take risks; he created concept albums around life, offered tribute to his ex-band leader Syd Barret and created, possibly the greatest rock opera with The Wall. It is exactly that ambition which lacks on this album, its not really innovating much, something which the other albums at least, tried to accomplish. But we will be unfair if we didnt credit David Gilmour as been a very good singer, and one of the best guitar players ever, and Richard Wright a creator of great atmospheres.

Division Bell might not be the closing album all fans would have liked them to create, but it is definitely a good one. Most of it focuses on being a relaxing journey, very passive and moody, and not really varying much, but because of the musicians abilities, the listener is kept interested throughout its more than 70 minutes. In Poles Apart, there is some reminiscence of the Piper at the Gates of Dawn days, and if for those who miss Waters, we are constantly remembered of his existence through Gilmours satire lyrics which we cant help relate to him. We might not be happy for the way Floyd ended their career, we might be angry because they ended their career, we would have wished they would be together forever, but we cant blame Division Bell for not being another Dark Side of the Moon.
