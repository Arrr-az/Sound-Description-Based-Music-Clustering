pixiesfanyo Jun 14 2005
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
What else can really be said about the Pixies? Frank Black, Kim Deal, Joey Santiago, and David Lovering, four of the most talented, creative, and interesting musicians from the 80s up too present day. When the mainstream of the 1980s was littered with nonsensical hair metal and synth ridden pop tracks, this group of four oddballs basically helped carve out the genre of alternative rock. Sure, Husker Du and the Minutemen were competing against each other with their double LP battles, and Sonic Youth was ripping through extremely strange noise experiments, but the Pixies were able to do something different from these bands. With their two masterpieces Surfer Rosa and Doolittle, the Pixies showed the underground, they could still be dark and brooding but instill a very deep pop twist to the alternative music of the day. Husker Du had tampered with it, but the Pixies made it their full fledged purpose. While Im sure at the time Frank Black and his crew of misfits didnt think they were doing much, the music found on their two classic LPs would literally influence half of the music to come out of the next two decades. Like the Beatles before them, The Pixies had stumbled upon an extremely odd, yet accessible mesh of genres, and because of it would go down in history as a very very important band.

Surfer Rosa is my favorite album of all time. It has been for nearly seven years. While this certainly is going to affect my rating of the album, I think most people that have heard can agree it is perfect or at least pretty close to it. Surfer Rosa is important for an extremely large amount of reasons, but there are two very big ones that stick out in mind. Number one, it was one of the first albums that helped the underground alternative rock scene too start making its first inklings into the mainstream. While they certainly arent the first band to play this style of music, the Pixies additions to the genre helped it become very accessible and pop-driven, thus opening up a can of worms that would cause alternative rock to become the most popular genre in a few years. Another reason Surfer Rosa is important is because of how influential it was too the production career of former Big Black member, Steve Albini. Surfer Rosa basically cemented the Albiniesqueproduction style. Heavy drums, full band recordings, and odd snippets of various field recordings, are what all make this album so unique and timeless in my opinion. Where as the Beatles had George Martin who was largely responsible for their success, the Pixies possessed Steve Albini, for their debut LP.

The album, Surfer Rosa is basically fourteen straight amazing tracks. Blending Frank Blacks unique lyrics and odd falsetto, with the grinding power of Santiago and Loverings guitar and drum interplay, as well as the simple yet infectious vocals and bass lines of Kim Deal creates a sound very of itself. While I certainly have heard bands who have wanted to sound like the Pixies (Modest Mouse comes to mind) none of these imitators seem to find the exact balance of aggression, beauty and quirkness these quartet possessed. Whether its screaming about priest molestation over the pounding punk ridden sound of Bone Machine or the soft wails complementing what can only be described as the aural interpretation of swimming during an LSD trip in Where is My Mind?, the Pixies are an flawless team, of excellent songsmiths. The addition of Kim Deals soft vocals, to the rough scream of lead singer Frank Black on a few of the tracks, most notably Gigantic (the only Deal fronted track on the album) helps the album gain some diversity that most alternative rock albums dont possess. As well as the addition of two vocal styles, the actual change from quirkness to serious also helps the albums longevity be further increased. While the Pixies certainly can write some crazy song about a ball of string being taken away from them as a child (Im Amazed), they are also able to examine their inner demons or express their distain with a break up, (Break My Body, and Cactus respectively).

Musically this album is simple yet ingenious. Possessing that idea found in the punk scene involving the process that anyone could pick up a guitar and play the songs on the album, the connection to the listener is easy and apparent from first listen. Joey Santiagos unique guitar playing on tracks such as Vamos (which features a three minute feedback ridden solo) and Brick is Red really helps the Pixies stand out from their contemporaries due to the sheer originality of their members. Lovering and Deal certainly are not a virtuoso rhythm section, but their excellent sense of feel and time really helps the drum and bass performance on this album stick out. Deals ability to make extremely simple yet, infectious bass lines is something that few bassists possess, and certainly makes her stick out in my mind whenever Im asked about my favorite bassists.

Im sure some people could raise some complaints about the album, the rawness of its production, the strangeness of its singer and its sound, the sheer simplicity of the entire thing. But, these complaints in my mind are what make Surfer Rosa so endearing. It seems like this is music for music sake, the kind of music everyone writes in their garage but is too afraid to share with their friends, due to its strangeness, and thats where its timeless connection with listeners comes from. Anybody couldve released Surfer Rosa its basically a group of the stereotypical art-fags gathering together and making something strange and interesting. The thing is that the Pixies were one of the only bands, that honest and into their music, that they were able to release such a simple, yet honest record. Surfer Rosa is in a class all itself, even when compared to the rest of the Pixies discography. After releasing this album, they refined their sound and made it much more bland and uninspiring. While to criticize Doolittle as being a poor follow-up, it certainly is nowhere near in the same category as Surfer Rosa which is one of the most honest recordings of all time. Had The Pixies even tried to reproduce the sound they got on Surfer Rosa in my opinion they wouldve failed miserably. This album was a one shot deal, and only one group of individuals couldve made it, and luckily for the Pixies it was them.

Surfer Rosa is considered by many critics to be one of the top albums of the 80s and all time. I have to say I agree its flawless execution of the garage rock ethic is what has cemented its place in history. It is always the first album, I recommend to any friend I know who is trying to get into music, and it is the only album I have ever found (besides maybe maudlin of the Wells Bath) that I can listen to for an entire day and not get bored of. Surfer Rosa is basically the interpretation of surf rock, by a bunch of teenagers on acid, and while this may not seem like much, it certainly is.

Kate_McColl Oct 25 2011
+3 Vote downBookmarkVote up
After releasing a stellar debut EP in Come On Pilgrim the previous year, on March 21, 1988 the Pixies released their first full length LP. This is a fantastic album, and proved to be one of the monumental alternative rock albums of the 1980s, let alone of all time. It's no wonder why this album has been heralded by later rock legends like Nirvana and Radiohead. It was after the release of this record that the Pixies became a force to be reckoned with, finding success in America's indie circuit, and also having quite a bit of success in Britain.

Steve Albini was brought in to produce, and he does an excellent job. One thing I always love about Albini's production is the way the drums sound, they always sound so heavy and so clear. Steve has always said he likes to capture what bands sound like live, and I think he succeeded here. Listening to Surfer Rosa feels like you're sitting in on a Pixies rehearsal in a small practice space. With the little bit of studio banter in between songs and the raw production it gives a great intimate experience. Albini's mix is great, as there's not a lot of layers of instrumentation, you can hear every Pixie playing his or her part.

Now onto the Pixies themselves. There's such a cohesive nature to their playing on this record, as if the band are playing together, as opposed to recording separate tracks (this may be how it was recorded). They sound tight, but loose enough to just let go and have fun with these songs. Charles (Frank Black) Thompson's lyrics and vocal delivery is fantastic. It's very unique with lots of clever hooks, often going between talking, singing, singing in spanish, screaming, or a high pitched wail. Kim Deal's bass parts are simple but very catchy, and her backing vocals are mixed perfectly with Charles' creating the Pixies vocal sound. Joey Santiago's guitar playing is also great, nothing too fancy, but that's quite alright. His noise influence shines through with his well crafted leads, using his guitar as a color instrument, rather than relying on riffs. Keeping the pace going is drummer David Lovering, once again keeping it simple, but comes up with great beats to get his band going.

As for the songs, I could probably go through every track, but I'll just throw a few in here. Upon playing the album, immediately we're treated to the great drum sound of Steve Albini played by Lovering, "Bone Machine" is the first track. Featuring a great riff by Santiago and more of Charles' exceptional lyrics. "Gigantic" is one of my favorites, sung by the lovely Kim Deal. One of her few vocal leads in the Pixies discography, "Gigantic" tells the tale of a voyeur witnessing an interracial couple having sex. It's got a great bass riff, and Kim's voice is great (check out The Breeders for more of Kim). "Where is My Mind?" is the most talked about song on the record, and probably the most talked about Pixies track. Featuring a hypnotizing lead by Santiago backed by ambient vocals by Deal (which were recorded in a bathroom for a natural echo effect), the song has every right to be their most popular track. Charles' vocals and lyrics are superb, painting a surrealistic image of swimming in the Caribbean. Once again it's got the great drum sound pounding through.

Another thing that makes the Pixies so fantastic is their minimalist approach to songwriting. Much like early Wire, the band doesn't get indulgent with their songs. Eight of the thirteen tracks here are under three minutes, and the longest track is four minutes and eighteen seconds! I saw an interview with Kim Deal talking about how the Pixies would just end songs. No build ups to a finishing drum solo, arena rock end. They'd just end. Their songs hardly feature a guitar solo of any sort, if so they last a few seconds, unless you count "Vamos" with it's noise guitar break, but that's it. Their songs get to the point, and if they need to end, they will. See "Break My Body" for example. The last verse of the song sounds like a bridge, well is it? I'm not sure as after the vocals are done the song is as well, it could have gone back to another chorus, but why? Does it need to? No it doesn't. This is the genius of the Pixies, knowing what's needed and what's not, and making just great songs.

I can't give enough credit to the production and the overall feel this album gives off. It's so fun, but not in anyway immature or dumb, it's just fantastic. The band sounds like they're having the time of their lives, and Steve Albini captured this perfectly. The Pixies would go on to record yet another masterpiece the following year with Doolittle, thus further cementing them to their now god-like status. This is an album that you hear and immediately want to go pick up a guitar and start jamming, it's inspiring, and I can see why people like Kurt Cobain held it to such high regard. One of the finest albums ever created, by one of the finest bands ever formed.

Favorite Tracks - Bone Machine, Break My Body, Gigantic, Where is My Mind?, Cactus, Vamos

*Got my copy signed by Frank, Joey, and David on November 9, 2011!!

Call it reactionary, but somehow it seemed natural for most of the American college rock of the 80s to take a rather earnest tone to set itself up in opposition of the frothy frivolity of the British synthpop dominating the pop charts. R.E.M. and The Replacements mined small-town ennui to create genuinely moving compositions, while Minutemen and Mission Of Burma struck back against political apathy with short, sharp bursts of righteous anger. The likelihood of a band with a non-sarcastic sense of humorlet alone as purely goofy as the Pixiesto become the next darlings of the American indie underground seemed next to zero. But burst out they did, and Boston's finest delivered as their first LP proper (following an EP, Come On Pilgrim) Surfer Rosa, a 33-minute salvo of warped humor, playful perversion, and lots of rollicking noise pop. Joey Santiago's guitar was abrasive yet accessible, keening and wailing along in time with Black Francis' histrionic yet not-taking-himself-seriously-at-all vocals, with Kim Deal plonking along on her bass and teasing with her backing vocals like the girl going nah-nah-nah on the playground, and David Lovering just calmly playing along on his drums, yet still providing the proper splashes and crashes when called for. Even with the notorious Steve Albini behind the boards on Surfer Rosa, the Pixies still sound like they are having a hell of a ball throughout the record, and it shows.

From the start, this band has absolutely no pretense and Black Francis even calls out before launching into "Bone Machine," "This is a song for Carol!" Even as he sings about how a priest "bought me a soda and tried to molest me in the parking lot," it is presented in such a manner that seems so absurd that it's hard not to laugh. Such absurd little Francis moments are scattered throughout Surfer Rosa, such as "I'm a belly dancer" in "Break My Body" and "We'll have our sons/They will be all well-hung!" on "Vamos." On a similar note, although "Gigantic" is probably the most touching song on the recordand the only one Kim Deal singsas she talks about voyeuristically looking in on her older sister and her black boyfriend making love, during the chorus it's easy to imagine her suppressing giggles as she delivers the adjective that's both an affirmation of the couple's love and a rather obvious dick joke. "Where Is My Mind?", the other so-called single from Surfer Rosa, is similarly unconventional in that it really was inspired by Black Francis' snorkeling experiences in the Caribbean, looking at fish, with the chiming guitar representing waves and the "oooh-oooh" backing vocals simulating whalesong. But although "Gigantic" and "Where Is My Mind?" are two of the longest songs on Surfer Rosa and clear standouts for the many who love the album, the rest of the album is just great fun. "Something Against You" has always cracked me up since the contrast between the bouncy guitar and Francis' growling vocals make it seem like a bizarre hybrid of ska and death metal. "Tony's Theme" sounds like a couple of little kids making up songs about their favorite imaginary friend as they toy with guitars and make them squall and screech before going into boisterous chants of "TO-NY! TO-NY!", and by the end, Francis sounds rather canine with his little gruff yelps. "Cactus" is a little more slowed-down and subdued, but nonetheless works within the same sexy/creepy divide that the band has made for his niche: it's a simple tale of a horny guy wishing for his girlfriend to send him her dress that's covered in her bodily fluids, yet the seriousness of the fetish is somewhat skewered by the background chant of "P-I-X-I-E-S!" (This would be changed to "D-A-V-I-D" when fellow sexy/creepy enthusiast David Bowie covered it fourteen years later, in a louder fashion but keeping the song's spirit intact.) Some of the later songs have extended spoken-word parts, notably the "You fuckin' die!" part in "Oh My Golly" and the "There were rumors, he was into field hockey players..." intro to "I'm Amazed," showing that the band is unashamed to make its inside jokes public, yet it all seems to fit together and seem as if the listener's in on the joke as well. Even the closer, "Brick Is Red," seems like a classic anti-closer at first, with the vocals not beginning until halfway through the song, backed with fuzzy guitar and a variation on the "Be My Baby" beat, yet for a band that consciously eschews all notions of the "epic," it's so wrong that it's absolutely right.

Normally, when a band seeks out to create a silly album, it fails in one of two ways: it either is just so immature that it causes listeners to roll their eyes, or it's so stilted and orchestrated that it just doesn't come off as humorous at all. The Pixies avoid both of these traps on Surfer Rosa, and even 21 years after its release, it still sounds completely fresh and spontaneous, as if the four band members just went into the studio and recorded it in one wild shot of a jam session. This, my friends, is what makes a classic.

College rock! Weird feeling over transmitted meaning. Partly due to my peers elevating these guys to Stones-like status, I've always harbored a hatred for the popularity wake the Pixies left. But that ain't their fault; they may have cultivated the... er, cult, but someone has to plunk down the dollar.

But yeah, this is a catchy record. For a debut, it's fairly remarkable. Most of the poses struck achieve some sort of resonance (the false hesitation on "Broken Face," the bi-curiosity narrative of "I'm Amazed," the bilingual mythmaking of "Oh My Golly!"), and the playing combines the repetition of hardcore punk with the hooks of... well, I suppose the Pixies pioneered this sort of rock 'n' roll. Play-acting at weirdness is reduced to a venial sin when the songs are of a quality, and except for large chunks at the end, this is a quality LP.
