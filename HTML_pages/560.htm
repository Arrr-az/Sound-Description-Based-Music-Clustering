"Can it be that I don't want what you want?"

The White household, Detroit, early 2005.

Meg: Jack, have you written anything for that new album that you've promised everyone?
Jack: I've been writing loads of cool stuff for the new band that I've formed with Brendan.
M: Very good, but what about the stuff that we are supposed to be recording together?
J: Do I have to?
M: Well you did promise everybody and the last album sold a lot of copies...
J: But I'm bored of that now and I want to play with Brendan!
M: Well you can't yet, because you've got to write new stuff for us first.
J: But I've used up all my good ideas!
M: Haven't you got anything left over?
J: Well I've got one thing that sounds like our last album, a piano tune called "My Doorbell" and something we can sing together called "Little Ghost".
M: Are they any good?
J: They're alright.
M: Are they as good as what we've done before?
J: No.
M: Have you got anything else?
J: Yeah, but it's as rubbish as your drumming.
M: Well can you make it any better?
J: No. Don't want to. I want to play with Brendan!
M: But you've got to.
J: Why?
M: Because if you don't the cool kids will have won. Remember how much they picked on you after everyone else liked our last album?
J: Yeah.
M: Now we don't want to give them the satisfaction of watching you cry in a corner do we?
J: But what if my new stuff isn't good enough?
M: That doesn't matter, because plenty of people will buy it in the first week because our last record was so good.
J: Then can I play with Brendan?
M: Yes, as soon as we've finished recording this half-baked album.
J: Hooray! Can I have a cookie now?
M: Yes, But only one.



For me, Get Behind Me Satan is the White Stripes album when the bubble burst for me, not only on the bands career, but on Jack Whites broader career as well. That was a bit of a shame as I had picked the album up just after a long distance relationship had fallen apart and I was hoping that it would at least part-soundtrack the healing process of my bruised ego. It didnt.

The four White Stripes albums up to this point had been essays in stripped back blues rock. Lots of riffs. Deceptively simple rhythms. Choruses. And each album would be punctuated by a contrasting song or two which acted as something of a palette cleanser, contrasting the blues rock. Get Behind Me Satan inverts that, with Jack White seemingly taking the approach of deciding that the majority of the album should consist of the palette cleanser style songs, with the riffing rockers being few and far between. Oh okay, its just that thats not really what I was looking for.

Much of Get Behind Me Satan seems pre-occupied with the fact that Jack White didnt like the downsides of fame, which is something of a problem when your little two piece garage band has just become one of the biggest rock bands on the planet. Get Behind Me Satan therefore consists of White in particularly self-pitying mode, taking swings at former lovers and the modern obsession with celebrity. Again, not exactly what I was hoping for.

Having been a staunch defender of Jack Whites back to basics rock minimalism up to this point in his career, the fact that Get Behind Me Satan just wasnt what I was hoping for was a bit of a bitter pill to swallow, and t this day it remains my least favourite White Stripes album.

Blue Orchid - Not the biggest fan of the lyrics, but a big fan of the riff. 2.5/5

The Nurse - A bit of a reverse of Blue Orchid, the instrumental didn't really do anything for me (until the end), but I enjoy the lyrics of this song 3/5

My Doorbell - Much more catchy than the last 2, but seems to have much less meaning, to its detriment. A bit more enjoyable than some of the others though. 3/5

Forever for Her (Is Over for Me) - I do like this song more than the last few, but it still doesn't stand out as anything super special, the vocals and lyrics are pretty nice though. 3.5/5

Little Ghost - Didn't really like how this one sounded. 1.5/5

The Denial Twist - Good song. Better instrumental than a good bunch of songs so far, more catchy, and has good lyrics as well. 4/5

White Moon - Definitely the hardest hitting song on the album, great lyrics and vocals. The instrumental gets pretty good at some parts too, but is more or less just some piano really. 3/5

Instinct Blues - A pretty intense song, sounded really good. 4/5

Passive Manipulation - Great vocals, nothing much else to say really. 3/5

Take, Take, Take - Nice sounding song, not much else to it to me. 2.5/5

As Ugly as I Seem - A little boring, you can hear the influences in this song which is cool, but nothing else to this song really. 1.5/5

Red Rain - A much cooler song than the last few, I did enjoy the guitar and vocals on this track. 3.5/5

I'm Lonely (But I Ain't That Lonely Yet) - A bit more touching than the last few, nice vocals, and just a good song overall. 3/5

Overall, this album is nothing to write home about. I didn't see anything that stood out about it, although there were a few songs I enjoyed. I probably wouldn't recommend this one since it was more or less a bit of boredom for me, but if you're a fan of their music and want to check it out I don't think this review should stop you. 2/5

MICTHESNARECORD SEPTEMBER DIVE: WHITE EPISODE 5: Get Behind Me Satan (2005)
Um...
What the fuck happened?

Speaking of "firsts" in this review discography event thing, this is the first time we've had an album this bad having come out of nowhere. Post-Pinkerton Weezer was pretty clearly a ticking time-bomb towards Raditude, Modest Mouse getting more and more mainstream really forshadowed something like The Golden Casket happening, Volta... ok no maybe this album is pretty similar to Volta. The dip in quality just makes zero sense. They just released Elephant 2 years ago, what was preventing them from continuing down that road? Maybe make more stuff like their first three albums? Maybe go full-on commercial to try and capitalize on the success of "Seven Nation Army"? ...Instead of any of that, we got Get Behind Me Satan.

This album is a complete mess. In previous projects it could sound like the band didn't care a lot about some songs, but that was just because they were having fun in the studio and making music for the hell of it. In this record, it sounds like they not only didn't care about the songs, but they didn't care about the album or even the band. The best songs in this record, "Blue Orchid" and "The Denial Twist" stick to pretty regular White Stripes formulas, but there's some really weird experiments thrown in the mix like "The Nurse" featuring a marimba and random off-beats spurts of percussion, "Passive Manipulation" where Meg's already not great singing voice recites some truly embarassing lyrics, and "Red Rain" which features weird slide guitars and vocal effects, as well as having one of the worst choruses in The White Stripes' catalog.

About half of this album is passable by TWS standards. The rest is an incredibly large miss. Won't be coming back to this one much.

YAY: Blue Orchid, The Denial Twist
NAY: The Nurse, My Doorbell, Little Ghost, Passive Manipulation, Take, Take, Take, Red Rain
4/10

Everything on this album feels like it's been done before, which of course it has.
I shouldnt be surprised that The White Stripes fifth album feels a bit stale; their formula has been tried and true for the most part up to this point, but this time around it just feels tried. This couldve been the time to really switch things up, but instead the duo has elected to change nothing and persevere with whats already worked before. And that, unfortunately, is exactly what doesnt work about this album. Youve got Little Ghost repeating Hotel Yorbas bluegrass shuffle; Instinct Blues dirty, scratchy American classicalism bringing back the foundation of the bands debut; or the baffling choice to include Take, Take, Take, My Doorbell, and The Denial Twist, three far-too-similar piano-led bangers of which two were also released as singles for who knows why? And most of these songs are decent in a vacuum, but just unforgivably redundant amongst the bands oeuvre.

Sometimes it comes across in small ways, like White Moons lyrical self-reference to De Stijls Truth Doesnt Make a Noise. Sometimes its more medium, like Megs lead vocals on Passive Manipulation; the idea of letting Meg take lead was cool and novel before, but on this track the weakness in her voice stands out too strongly and it drags the interlude down significantly. But beyond that, this track list is laden with this same sin, making the project pretty much pointless to return to when I know everything heres been done better elsewhere. Again, these tracks are mostly decent, but I know the band has more to give than this.

And since Ive somewhat ironically repeated myself to emphasise that mostly decent point, allow me to hammer it home by getting into what falls outside that mostly. First, My Doorbells chorus annoys me, and it doesnt have anything exciting to offer beyond that hook so Im not a fan. And second, Take, Take, Take, which also has an annoying hook, though this time its mainly due to its overuse. That song also has other random flourishes, like a moment of choppily panned vocals, which really just make it painful to get through. Seriously, The Denial Twist does the same thing as the other two perfectly well, cutting them out wouldnt lose anything, just improve the record overall.

But its not all negative. Ive liked The Denial Twist for a long time and that track still holds up (Plus it has a great music video). Red Rain enters full White Blood Cells banger mode in a satisfying way. And The Nurse is shockingly fresh for this album, with a juxtaposition of chilled marimba with sudden garage-guitar-and-Meg-White-pounding stabs that make the song feel like its venturing into noise rock territory momentarily. Those two very different parts both reminded me of Bjrk; I specifically referred to the song as like if Venus as a Boy was getting punched in the mouth by Declare Independence in my notes, and thats a statement Ill stand by. And everything else on the album (in case you forgot from when I said it twice before) is decent.

If this album had come out much earlier, Id probably have very little to complain about and generally like this just fine. But it stands out poorly because of its place in the bands history, and isnt trying at all to move past that, so why should I bother trying with it any more than I already have? Something something repetition, something something redundant, something something decent, I think you get the point.

Four-on-the-floor opener "Blue Orchid," the album's first single, was merely a red herring. Get Behind Me Satan is not only the band's most experimental album, featuring an eclectic array of instrumentation (piano, timpani, bells, toy pianos, various other percussion) and compositional/arranging approaches, but also, in many ways, their most laid back album, with a new emphasis on acoustic instruments. The more rock oriented songs here ("Blue Orchid," "Instinct Blues," "Red Rain") are, by and large, the least interesting tracks. And while I appreciate the branching out, particularly the greatly increased use of piano, the experimental nature of this record can occasionally reveal the band's limitations, such as on the gospel inspired "Little Ghost," with its "pick a note, any note" mess of a vocal arrangement. (Then again, it's songs like this that also underscore my longheld belief that the White Stripes were always primarily a punk band at heart.)

Elsewhere, the blissful, rollicking, piano-led "My Doorbell" would certainly be in the running for the Stripes' best ever single (featuring a charming Little Rascals-inspired video clip to boot), and its sister song, "The Denial Twist," is almost as successful. Meg White's only vocal contribution to the album, the woefully short "Passive Manipulation," almost recalls They Might Be Giants in both its quirky (and adorable) approach and laser-focused commitment to a singular idea within a brief period of time. Closing piano ballad "I'm Lonely (But I Ain't That Lonely Yet)" illustrates, at least to my mind, not only the ability of Jack White to craft songs that sound like instant classics but also the enduring sadness and discreet interpersonal drama between Meg and Jack that always seemed to be simmering under the surface of so many of their songs, kind of like a more inscrutable, low-fi Fleetwood Mac.

While this shaggy dog record will most likely never be my favorite Stripes album, it may be the one that most heavily underscores just how special and unique they were...and just how sorely they are missed.
