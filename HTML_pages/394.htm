A stunning collection of brilliant pop tunes - timelessly classic...
A stunning collection of classic pop tunes and an absolute essential addition to any serious music collection.

At the time The Undertones appeared a little 'safe' for my liking but clearly their look was intended to demonstrate the very 'down to earth' nature of these group of lads from Northern Ireland. The quality of songs on this release and the singles of the same era (which did not feature here), really are self explanatory, this is an album of absolute genuine class and timelessness.

The Ramones of Northern Ireland? Anyone across the pond in the States who loves the Ramones, but isn't aware of the Undertones, must check this album out, immediately (please!).

Key Track: Here Comes The Summer

The Debut Album Project: Day 96

True confession: I like this album more without "Teenage Kicks" on it.

Wait. Hear me out...

Ever since I acquired The Undertones years ago, I've always known that song as part of it. In fact, it's the opening track on my copy of the LP.

And yeah, it's a no-doubt all-timer; I put it at #62 on my A Century of Song project, and still think I may have underrated it.

But "Teenage Kicks" was not part of the original tracklist of The Undertones. It was added to the American version, and has appeared as a bonus track on subsequent reissues.

Thing is, when listening to that American tracklisting, there's a bit of a "when are they gonna get to the fireworks factory?" effect in place. You know that song is coming, and both the anticipation of it, and the comedown from it make it loom large over everything else.

But take it away, and you have thirteen great pop songs (and one mostly-inconsequential lark) vying for your attention. Each shines more in this context, and you realize that this is an album of highlights, not a record with one dominant presence, and a bunch of runners-up.

Really, I absolutely treasure "Teenage Kicks." But The Undertones is better without it.

Rating: 9.1

This album may be the most straightforwardly pop take on the punk concept, even moreso than the Ramones or Buzzcocks. Why? Undertones lead vocalist Feargal Sharkey has a shaky sweetness to his tone - unrivalled. The record is both helped and hindered by consistency: there are no real troughs (though the strange keyboard-based pseudo-Krautrock True Confessions (B-3) may be a minor stylistic and musical misstep), but the peaks never pop. This is a great compilation of 8/10 pop tracks.

The choice cuts are all brief bursts of sound and infectious energy: Family Entertainment (A-1), Girls Dont Like It (A-2), I Gotta Getta (A-4), Wrong Way (A-5), Here Comes the Summer (A-7). See a pattern? The disc is a bit frontloaded. Regardless, Undertones is a very pleasant pop album, and buoyed by a certain self-aware adolescence. Not a brattish sort, but an earnest neo-Beach Boys-ism.

19 January 2022.

One of David Peels favourite singles early on graced the Undertones catalogue. It is not on here, but the band from Northern Ireland certainly delivers the same energy and playfulness on their debut album. One could accuse the Undertones of inventing that often-dreaded genre of pop punk, but here it is played with verve and cleverness that one simply has to enjoy it.

Family Entertainment storms through the gates, paving the way for 13 equally juvenile and joyous energy tracks. Guitar hooks galore, and theres hardly a song that is not catchier than the one before. The songs rush by so quickly that one can hardly find fault with their apparent one-dimensionality. In fact, the Undertones songwriting is quite clever and sticks out of the mass of punk bands from that era.
This debut is filled with anthems for eternal youth, and that alone makes it deserve a mention in the influential records of all time.

Highlights: Family Entertainment; Billys Third; Jimmy Jimmy; True Confessions;
