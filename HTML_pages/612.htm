Box sets like Nevermind: 20th Anniversary Edition usually never succeed the way they should. The vault-clearing nature of these releases tends to overwhelm the listeners with subpar demos, alternate takes, and shaky live versions of the album tracks. This set works because we see the creation of Nirvana's generation-defining second album as an ever-evolving process that took place over the course of a year and a half. The studio demos, boombox rehearsal tapes, and rough mixes culminate in the wonderful final cut of the album as well as the blistering Live at the Paramount set--performed about a month after the release of Nevermind--which was essentially Seattle's version of a coronation for the group after "Smells Like Teen Spirit" started its staggering, and wholly unexpected, ascent up the pop charts.

It is hard for me to believe that twenty years have passed since I was a sophomore in high school. At the time, I was undergoing a musical identity crisis. Two years earlier, I had submerged myself in heavy metal, especially Metallica, Anthrax, and Guns N' Roses. Then, my tastes started to shift toward punk rock, beginning with the Sex Pistols and Black Flag. One ever-constant source of musical joy for me through all these changes was The Beatles, who I really started to enjoy about four years earlier. So, during sophomore year, there were two bands all of my friends were talking about: Primus and Nirvana. For weeks I'd been hearing about these groups, especially from my musically-inclined buddies. A friend of mine lent me a cassette tape that had both of Primus' first two albums on it. I listened to it, and about halfway through the first side, I fell asleep. Primus was boring, and dreadfully so. Afterward, I couldn't even say "Primus Sucks" around friends because that is the band's slogan. Clever chaps, those guys.

About a week later, I was doing homework in my living room, and MTV was on in the background. I turned up to see, on the TV screen, a left foot with a black Converse shoe on it, tapping the grimy bleacher in a smoke-filled gymnasium. Next came the sound of ten thunderous cannon-blasts and a monstrous four-chord progression, which immediately caught my attention. I watched, listened, transfixed, for the four-and-a-half minute duration of the "Smells Like Teen Spirit" video, and instantly concluded--like the sixteen year-old that I was--"this is the best song I've ever heard." From that moment, I was an instant convert, an acolyte to the ways of Nirvana. It wasn't until the holiday season that year that I finally received Nevermind (along with Bleach) on CD as a gift. (Sadly, I got one of the early copies that didn't have "Endless, Nameless" on it.) After a year or so of buying all their obscure CD singles and EPs, I came to the realization I really loved Nirvana. Once In Utero came out, I quickly forgot about Nevermind. Consequently, I felt the album was way too slick, especially when compared with the primal, Steve Albini-produced beast that was In Utero or the raw bluesy emotion of MTV Unplugged in New York. Only in recent years have I returned to Nevermind. I have come to realize that it perfectly blends all those things I was looking for in music at the time: that glossy sheen of 80s heavy metal, that punk rock angst, and those Beatlesque melodies. It is one of those rare records with no missteps. Even the weakest song on the record, "Lounge Act," has a sturdy groove and a rousing conclusion.

Nevermind arrived on the scene during a transitional period for popular music. Hip-hop was about to become pop music's most popular genre. Glossy, pre-packaged pop acts like New Kids on the Block, and childish hair metal acts like Poison and Motley Crue were exhausting their excessive personae, and "alternative" acts like R.E.M. and Jane's Addiction were beginning to get major MTV airplay. The four-chord blast of "Smells Like Teen Spirit" would give off the impression that things were about to change in a big way. Whether this is true or not--as historians like to simplify things for the masses--the song (and, subsequently, Nevermind) definitely caught people's attention. Enigmatic frontman Kurt Cobain yelped out practically unintelligible lyrics with so much conviction it was hard to believe. When the CD single for "Lithium" came out--containing the lyrics for Nevermind (in these pre-World Wide Web days, this was the only way to obtain the lyrics)--it became apparent that Cobain's words were actually pretty intriguing. Furthermore, Butch Vig's sturdy production--combined with Andy Wallace's sugar-coated mix-job--made the record a throw-back to the days of Cheap Trick. The guitar tracks crackled, Chris (later Krist) Novoselic's rudimentary yet groovy bass lines throbbed, and Dave Grohl's Bonham-meets-Ringo drums sounded like battlefield explosions. All of this combined to make the punk rock qualities of Nirvana more accessible to a populace weaned on Mr. Mister and Whitney Houston.

"Smells Like Teen Spirit" is now an iconic number, with its "thesis statement of a generation" bromides--like "Here we are now, entertain us" and "oh well, whatever, never mind"--its irresistible vocal melody, and its quiet/loud flaccid/erect dynamic. The song displays a mastery of dramatic tension. It is followed by "In Bloom," a song which lyrically mocks its own listeners ("he's the one / who likes all the pretty songs / and he likes to sing along / and he likes to shoot his gun / but he knows not what it means") who are undoubtedly singing along to it, oblivious of its meaning. The song is exciting right out of the gate, just like "Smells Like Teen Spirit," and similarly masters its songwriting formula. "Come As You Are" follows, and changes the dynamic ever so subtly. (The album sequence itself replicates the dynamic structure of their songs.) The chorus effect on the guitar gives off a watery sound, as Cobain's lyrical protagonist completely befuddles his audience ("Come as you are / as you were / as I want you to be"). It also reveals a less flattering quality of Cobain's: his propensity to nick from established sources (like, say, Killing Joke's "Decades"). "Breed" returns to punk textures, and is the hardest rocking number on the album yet. Things soften a bit with "Lithium," another song filled with brilliant, self-loathing lyrics ("I'm so ugly / that's okay 'cause so are you") and an irresistible chorus. The quirky chord progression is representative of the influence of the more abrasive new wave acts on Cobain's songwriting. Side One culminates with one of two acoustic numbers on the album in "Polly." The song, about a rape victim who wins the sympathy of her tormentor to secure her escape, is completely harrowing. Not only is the first side filled with wry humor, surrealism, aggression, and restraint, it is overwhelmingly human and empathetic, especially to those who are considered underdogs. Cobain and the group's feminism and Queer-friendly politics stand in stark contrast to the hairspray rockers who were popular at the time, and soon to be relegated to roadhouse dives.

Side Two is nowhere near as brilliant as its counterpart. However, there are no missteps to be found at all. The side opens with the most explicitly punk-inspired number on the album in "Territorial Pissings" (a song which borrows liberally from Lard's "The Power of Lard"). The fast, three-chord number is pure energy. From there, Nirvana delivers solid performances in "Drain You," "Lounge Act," "Stay Away," and "On a Plain," all of which feature brilliant non sequiturs and ironic inversions (like "with eyes so dilated / I've become your pupil" or "I'll start this off / without any words"). The album closes with its other acoustic number, "Something in the Way." Quietly performed by Cobain on acoustic guitar, he expresses pure desolation in his lyric ("I'm living off of grass / and the drippings from the ceiling / it's okay to eat fish / 'cause they don't have any feelings") which is matched by his voice and session musician Kurt Canning's cello. It's another haunting moment on an record generally lacking in them, revealing Nirvana's ability to stray from the confines of a punk rock sensibility. Nevermind is ultimately a masterpiece of sequencing, matched with damned-fine, rock-solid tunes. Its success instantly rendered acts like Guns N' Roses, [MC] Hammer, Van Halen, New Kids on the Block, Extreme, Mr. Big, and even Michael Jackson irrelevant.

Here we are now, twenty years later. Entertain us!

Nevermind: 20th Anniversary Edition presents the proper album in a pristinely remastered form. As a bonus, "Endless, Nameless" is tacked onto "Something in the Way," replicating its status as a hidden track on the second pressings of the CD version. (This time, there is only a few second gap between the two songs to allow room for more material; on the original, there is a ten-minute gap.) The rest of the first disc collects all of the B-sides associated with Nevermind. Highlights include "Even in His Youth" (featuring one of Cobain's more honest lyrics from the Nevermind era), the original studio version of "Aneurysm," the catchy but unengaging "Curmudgeon," and great live versions of "Drain You" and "Been a Son"--superior to their studio-recorded counterparts--from the Paramount Theater set (more of which I'll talk about later).

The first eight tracks on the second disc focus on Nirvana's Smart Studio sessions (manned by Butch Vig) from April, 1990, long before they were signed to DGC Records. The performances are closer to demos in quality than they are to finished takes. A few exceptions include "Dive" (which would be the B-side to "Sliver") and "Polly," the only track from the sessions to make it onto Nevermind. These tracks are followed by some crudely recorded rehearsal demos, recorded about one year later. This is clearly the roughest patch on the box set, though there are a few highlights (like a decent version of "Verse Chorus Verse," an early arrangement of "Smells Like Teen Spirit," and a practically fully-formed "Territorial Pissings"). The disc ends with two previously unreleased BBC sessions performances, "Drain You" and a decent full-band arrangement of "Something in the Way." The third disc would seem to be the most irrelevant of the set, as it features essentially the same performances as the officially released version of Nevermind. These tracks, known as "The Devonshire Mixes" in the bootleg community (because producer Butch Vig mixed them at Devonshire Studios in Los Angeles in the days following the recording of Nevermind in May, 1991), show that the album would have been much different--and probably far less successful--if it had been released this way. Nirvana was unsatisfied with the mix, and brought in Slayer engineer Andy Wallace to remix the tapes. Vig, still a fairly green producer at this point in his career, puts all the emphasis on Cobain's guitar parts. The result is a rawer sound, but a less dynamic one. Wallace raises Grohl's drums up significantly in the mix as well as Cobain's vocals. The only version here that really benefits from Vig's approach is "On a Plain."

The box set culminates with Nirvana's triumphant return to Seattle in the wake of Nevermind's release. The Halloween, 1991 show at the Paramount Theater is fantastic. It is a shame that this show has had to wait this long to be released, as it is far more riveting as an audio document than the Live at Reading set (which, of course, features more human drama). It should be no surprise that four tracks from this set ("Drain You," "School," "Been a Son," and "Negative Creep") had been previously released. It helps that Andy Wallace produced these live recordings. It includes a few nuggets, like an early cover of The Vaselines' "Jesus Doesn't Want Me for a Sunbeam" and an early rendition of "Rape Me." They tear ass through the set; it's clear they're excited to be back on home turf after spending months on the road promoting the album. The concert concludes wonderfully with the instrument-destroying mayhem of "Endless, Nameless." This live set alone makes the 20th Anniversary Edition worth purchasing. The box set also comes with a DVD of the concert performance (the only Nirvana concert shot on film), as well as the four music videos released to promote Nevermind, including the brilliant, aforementioned "Smells Like Teen Spirit" and The Ed Sullivan Show parody "In Bloom."

Clearly, only diehard fans should spend the $100+ to purchase this. But, if they do, they will be rewarded with great music and a worthwhile document of how this monumental album came to be. As a bonus, the box set comes with a fully illustrated 90 page hardcover book, and two replica magazine posters from the Nevermind era. Like I said earlier: box sets like this rarely work. Fortunately, Nevermind: 20th Anniversary Edition totally delivers.

You're In High School Again...
So today, September 26th 2011, it's both the 20th anniversary of Nirvana's Nevermind album, and also the day that Pink Floyd reissued their entire back catalogue on CD. It got me thinking, when I was in high school, everyone around me was obsessed with Nirvana, always wearing their shirts and badges. It seems teenagehood is when you connect with Nirvana the most, but I actually missed out on this. I listened to them, it was hard to avoid them. But I didn't like them. I'd rather listen to Soundgarden and Pearl Jam when it came to grunge. I was also obsessed with Pink Floyd in high school. That was the band I connected to the most, Nirvana seemed trivial to me, and it stayed that way for a good 8 years or so. Nevermind was too polished and clean sounding, Smells Like Teen Spirit was stalking me on the radio and music channels to the point where I despised it, and I just didn't see Kurt as an inspiration or connect with his lyrics and backstory.

It wasn't until the release of Live at Reading Festival in 2009 when I heard this rawer, punkier more intense side of Nirvana that the shiny polished production of Nevermind didn't quite hint at. For the first time in my life I became obsessed with a Nirvana album. I listened to it over and over that Christmas, and eventually owned the CD and DVD. I consider it my favourite live album I've heard. My good friend alexdrennen then started his Nirvana obsession, and a year later he introduced me to With the Lights Out and Incesticide, and I fell in love with this rawer, punkier, lo-fi side of Nirvana that I just never knew existed back in my teenage years. And at this point all my Pink Floyd albums have been gathering dust and barely listened to at all!

And now it's September 26th 2011. Perhaps it's some bizarre coincidence that Floyd and Nirvana would reissue albums on the same day. Perhaps it was a test of my loyalty. But I couldn't disguise it. As a teenager I was obsessed with Pink Floyd and scoffed at Nirvana. But now it is very clear that whilst I still like and appreciate all the Floyd have given me, I connect way more with Nirvana now. And I get the chance to hear even more of those raw demos that I love. I could have bought the Dark Side remaster, but I consciously chose to buy the Nevermind reissue.

As for my thoughts on Nevermind, I never liked it as much as Bleach and In Utero because the production on it was too clean and commercial sounding, and it had Smells Like Teen Spirit on it which is aural poison to me. But listening right now to Nevermind. I FUCKING LOVE EVERY MINUTE OF IT!!! All these songs are fucking genius, even if I do prefer their live and demo incarnations. Nirvana are doing for me now what Pink Floyd did for me back then. I guess it's never too late to find a new appreciation for something you never liked or understood. Nevermind has never sounded so good to my ears...

Band:

Kurt Cobain: Vocals, Guitar

Krist Novolesic: Bass

Dave Grohl: Drums

True, when this album was released in 1991, the music world was in pretty awful shape. Hair metal ruled the airwaves on both radio and television, and the alternative scene wasn't very promising either; Bono's Jesus-complex made most punk-rockers sick. So when Nirvana's first major label album, along with its landslide hit single "Smells Like Teen Spirit" hit the streets, America changed overnight. In one sweep, the rock landscape went from bloated solos and teased hair to no solos and uncombed hair. It also went from bad to worse.

Throughout this album, one understands that Kurt Cobain, singer, guitarist and songwriter, has a problem writing lyrics that makes sense. Read any mainstream review of this band and you'll see descriptions like "angst-filled," "angry," "depressing," "bored," etc. and yet not a single example from the album to support these descriptions. Why? Because the truth is that this album says nothing. I could give a bunch of excerpts proving this, but the point is that none of it makes sense, so giving examples of some songs that make no sense is a nil point indeed. Fans waste hours and indeed, lifetimes trying to determine what exactly Kurt Cobain was trying to tell us all. I can say I've already figured it out. Hint: nothing, because he couldn't articulate a thing.

Of course, lyrics are only half the music, so you must weigh the musicianship behind the album as well. And instrumentally, it's pretty clear that these guys suck. Dave Grohl can play fast; so what? It's generally the same beat, with same lame, generic surfer-rock fills as intros from time to time. Krist Novolesic's bass lines are annoying and whimsical sounding, as if a kid had just got an effects pedal for his bass he never learned how to play and made some cool new sounds that he put behind a song. Kurt's singing could be good but unfortunately he wastes it, opting to usually either shout or scream instead of hold a proper note. The guitar playing is pathetic as well; the solo to "Smells Like Teen Spirit" is as good as this guy's playing ever got, and that is a 100% true statement. Let the laughter begin.

The sad part is that you get the sense that these guys could play well, they just choose not to because they're too busy trying to be all "punked out." Dave Grohl can play drums; so why doesn't he do it? Krist Novolesic can play bass; why doesn't he do it. Kurt can sing; why doesn't he do it? Kurt can play gui-- well, never mind.

It's interesting that an album that says nothing and means nothing became the cornerstone of an entire generation.

No wonder the 90's was so terrible musically.

True, Nirvana started a revolution with this; a revolution of incredibly crappy music. Like Creed? Thank Nirvana. Like Nickelback? Thank Nirvana. Like Tool, one of the worst rock bands today? Thank Nirvana.

Anyone who can stand listening to this Godforsaken album all the way through deserves a reward. And anyone still under the wrong impression that this band was anything more than stoned out piss-poor musicians and a guy who liked to mix up random lines out of his "poetry books" to make pathetic excuses for lyrics is naive.

"Fashion shits,
Fashion style"

It should go without saying, but sometimes its okay not to like something as much as people tell you that you should do. This applies many multiple times over for music.

So why am I pointing out the obvious? Because since the widespread use of the internet, there are more and more opinion pieces on why you should love album A by act B, I know, because Ive contributed to the ever growing pile myself. The thing is, there are certain albums by certain acts which it seems that you are not allowed to criticise, and in doing so you are crossing some sort of cultural line from which there is no return. Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band, The Velvet Underground & Nico, Astral Weeks, What's Going On, Dark Side of the Moon,Horses, London Calling, Thriller, The Queen Is Dead, OK Computer these are all albums which numerous lists, TV shows and general popular opinion tells us are the towering achievements in terms of album-length musical statements, theyre all hugely important in regards to the evolution of popular music and any article questioning just how good they are will raise at least a few eyebrows, if not leave you open to severe criticism. But do you have to like them?

This month sees the 25th anniversary of the release of Nevermind, the second album by grunge power-trio Nirvana. It was an album which had a seismic effect on teenagers at the time, as lead single, Smells Like Teen Spirit, engaged my generation in a way that little in the way of rock music had done previously, and it has since become considered to be one of the key albums in the evolution of popular music. From its iconic artwork, to the immortal lead single (still one of the most thrilling examples of rock music you are likely to hear), to Dave Grohl, Krist Novoselic and Kurt Cobains dragged-of-the-street appearance, to the unarguable angst-rock anthems, to the hidden track at the end of the album (something that would later become a staple of 90s rock albums, but still a novelty at this point), Nevermind is from top to bottom a classic album. Its just one Ive never connected with.

Is Nevermind a great album? Yes, undoubtedly. Do I have a decades long appreciation of rock music? Yes, I certainly do. So why dont I like Nevermind? I actually do like Nevermind, I just dont like it as much as other people do. Maybe its because it was a favourite album of the chest-beating alpha-male guys I didnt get along with particularly well in school (the irony being of course is that Kurt Cobain later went on record to say that hed rather have socially awkward quiet kids in his audience, than chest-beating alpha-male types), maybe its because that Cobains angst-fuelled howl made it difficult to make out the lyrics, maybe its because Nirvana only released three studio albums during their career, of which Nevermind was by far and away the most accessible, causing it to unfairly overshadow the other two, or maybe its because, by Nirvanas own admission, sometimes they were just ripping off the Pixies.

What you cannot take away from Nevermind is how it is now part of the DNA of rock music. There are hardly any acts featuring loud guitars of the last quarter of a century who dont owe something to Nirvana. Its a great angst-rock statement and it altered the course of louder rock music and became the shiny and polished spearhead of the grunge movement. In the decades since its release, it has become common knowledge that Cobain himself didnt like Nevermind, feeling that the remix and production polish that record label Geffen demanded effectively neutered the album, rendering what he and his bandmates intended to be a savage and uncompromising album far too commercial and accessible to their ears. Whether Nevermind would have dragged Nirvana to mainstream attention if it hadnt been given a lair of candy ass production (Cobains own description), is debatable, but it certainly would have taken a longer time to do so. For all its polish, its still a raw album that combines the strands of alternative, punk and hard rock that really hadnt crossed over that much in the mainstream before. It gave a lot of teenagers a soundtrack as they tried to negotiate the perilous, and often miserable, pathway to adulthood, and for that reason alone, its considered to be a classic album.

If Nevermind had never existed, then someone would eventually have released an album that connected with my generation as teenagers in the same way, but the evolution of rock music would have pretty much stalled until that point. Its unarguably an important and massively influential album, and should be rightly celebrated as such, but is it impervious to criticism? No. Because no album should be.

04. Nirvana - Nevermind
I remember it clearly. I knew it from the opening chords. The big hair, flashy videos, and convoluted compositions were dead. We had been sick of them for quite some time without even realising it. And Nevermind showed that to us. It demolished everything with a syringe into the mainstream, contaminating the overground fashion with a destructive simplicity, overpowering all trends by popularising depression and drug addiction, inventing the grunge genre and defining a generation. It was a divine intervention, immediately exploding from the arms of its makers and running away without them, an accidental success galvanised by lost teenagers discovering a rebellious power within the angsty anthems, free of gimmicks and frills, just straightforward permission to stop-giving-a-fuck. And it changed everything for me. It was the first real thing I ever felt a part of, and is still unchallenged as the album which made the biggest impact on my musical life. So, yes, perhaps its not the cool Nirvana album to choose. And, yes, perhaps it has suffered from overplaying and accusations of overproduction. But now that these decades have passed, we can finally accurately and objectively appreciate this release for what it really was. It was the 90s record, and you fucking know it.
