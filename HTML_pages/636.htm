This is what I wrote originally:

"I was never much interested in punk rock until recently. Usually, like other angry music, it is youth that picks up on music of this genre, but it seems to me that I enjoy it much more now in my thirties, then I did in my late teens early 20's. Billy Idol and The Clash we're my introduction, but I listened to them only peripherally at that time.

Then _Smash_ back in the mid 90's peaked my interest and I began to look at punk again. I can't figure how a band that came out with "Self Esteem" and "Come Out And Play" (both on this album) ended up putting out a song like in the genre of "Get A Job" later but anyways I'll get back to this CD.

This is a great CD the first 20 times you listen to it, then it kind of gets old. "Come Out And Play" loses its catch and ends up sounding like a teen bitch song (and I guess that's what it is). I still like "Self Esteem", It reminds me of many of the f...ed up relationships of my early 20's. It is probably the best song that describes relationships in the 90's that I've heard.

My favourite song on the CD is "Bad Habit", a description of the ultimate road rage scenario, it's got attitude and you really feel like what's described in the song when some asshole, through his f...ing impatience, decides to throw the rules of the road out the window and cuts you off.

Later releases by the band move them towards a pop hip hop feel that is quite dumbfounding. So this release is probably their best before that change."

Then a funny thing happened. I decided to take the album out of storage and give it another listen. When I first bought the album back in '94, I listened to it continuously for approximately 3 months. It wore out it's welcome after that time and I moved on to other things.

But now that I have listened to it again for the last couple of weeks, I realize that my original review missed the most important reason the album lasted in my CD player for a quarter of a year; and probably the reason I went back in search of it again.

The most important reason I liked this album is also the most cerebral and complicated. Realizing it has this nature, it is not surprising that almost ten years later that my review would miss it. The music was great, the singing was stellar and the songwriting was top notch. But what made this album, in my mind, now that I have listened to it again, has to do with the overall feeling the whole album produces in me.

In order to explain this feeling I must lead you through a Coles version of history and myth. After reading what I have to say, if you have the patience to stay, you can decide what is actual history and what is a subtle form of myth-making.

Anyways, if we go back in history to the end of WWII, all the GIs return home (the ones who aren't dead anyways) and we have the creation of the largest generation of people the western world has ever seen. Nick-named the Baby Boom generation, it has dominated market and cultural forces throughout the rest of the century and continues through this one.

When the Baby Boomers decided to have children, they were in the middle of one the most flamboyant and shocking time in western culture and attitudes. The west adopted the ideas of love and tolerance. The civil rights movement was in full swing. The pill was invented. LSD was introduced. The Vietnam War was being protested against.

The Boomers carved out a whole new society and attitude that spread through the western world. Where it had its positives, it also had its negatives. Freedom and Individualism was the mantra that all the Boomers espoused. But with increased choice came an attack on traditional values that the war veterans espoused. The results for the Boomers children were not everything their parents thought all their fights, protests and love groupings were made for.

What did the Boomer's children receive. These Generation Xers were the first generation that were brought up without God. They were faced with seeing their fathers on week-ends or not at all. The invention of the single mother as a lifestyle was what the Xers experienced. AIDS not gonorrhea was the STD of the day.

The Xers took the brunt of what was negative about their parent's actions. What does all this have to do with _Smash_? If you have listened to the album you will understand what I am saying.

"Our generation sees the world
Not the same as before
We might as well just throw it all
And live like there's no tomorrow"

These first words yelled out after the intro by Holland's angry voice tells you exactly where the band is coming from. They continue:

"The official view of the world has changed
Live fast cuz if you don't take it
You'll never make it..."

As you can see they are espousing what my generation feels about the world. The generation of love our parents tried to create has resulted in a world where my generation see our parents leave a world where, in order to live, we must take and take again without concern for our fellow men. The band expands on their view with songs like "Genocide" (with lyrics saying "Dog eat dog everyday, on our fellow man we prey...").

A generation of people brought up without God, raised by single moms in poverty, or raised by strangers while mom works. A generation of people who must fight everyone else in order to get anywhere. This anger is crystal clear in songs like "Come Out And Play", that show how we were in high school, and "Bad Habit" when we later get our own cars.

"Smash" tells you what we think of trying to fit in. "It'll Be Along Time" tells of the respect we have for the Boomers in charge of it all. "Self Esteem" tells you of our relationships with women. And "What Happened To You" tells you what really happens when a friend decides to get into the drug scene, unlike how it was portrayed as harmless during our parent's time.

This is The Offspring's breakthrough release. It took them 10 years to get their voice out to a large audience. Otherwise, you could have heard this in the late 80s. You do hear it in some of their earlier stuff but not as well put together and prophetic as this.

Basically, the album hit a chord. It sold 4 million copies off an independent label (Epitath) which is unheard of this side of _Dookie_. Like the book by Alan Bloom _The Closing Of The American Mind_, _Smash_ shows the results of what a generation of free sex, love without responsibility, and a major expansion of freedom without morality or ethics or God even, gave western culture and my generation in particular.

I wonder where my children's voice will come from. _Smash_ is one, although coming pretty late (I was 28 when it came out), from mine.

So just how did this album sell as many copies as it did?

For all the scorn people have poured upon The Offspring in the years since, Smash might be one of the best ways to win an argument about the early '90s being a great time for independent music. To go top 5, to sell 4 million copies, is unreal for an album released on an indie label; it remains the biggest-selling album ever on an independent and I can't imagine it happening again for a rock band. But why The Offspring, and why Smash? I suppose this sound just had to happen at this point, really; Nirvana and Metallica had both kicked the doors wide open for melodic hard rock in the mainstream, but both were still a little too much for some. Too introverted, in "Smells Like Teen Spirit" and "Lithium", or too heavy, in the case of "Sad But True", or maybe too silly, in the case of "Enter Sandman" - whatever the case, neither band were truly all-inclusive. And that's where The Offspring and Green Day came in.

Green Day sold a shitload of albums in 1994 too, with Dookie, but they had the headstart of a major label backing them. Their album came out two months before Smash, though, and that in itself contributed to this record's success - because when The Offspring sauntered into view, they came off like equal parts Metallica, Nirvana, and Green Day. They had the heaviness necessary to satisfy all the people that bought The Black Album (some of the riffs on the first half of this album come very close to crossover thrash), the dynamic control and the outward veneer of rebellion of Nevermind, and crucially, the all-inclusive pop nous of Dookie. The sound had been developed at an arms length from commercial cynicism, but to an AR man, this must have sounded like heaven. I don't mean to imply that The Offspring wrote the album with the deliberate intention of becoming famous or shifting truckloads of units, but it was perfect for the time and for the audience - a generation of kids had heard Nirvana and Metallica (and perhaps even Korn, depending on their age) a little too early to really understand it, but had been sucked in by the melodies anyway, and Smash was an album that offered the same base thrill along with content that was more at their level.

Really, rock music needed to come to this kind of point in the '90s, and Smash was as necessary in that as Jagged Little Pill was. Hearing this album is an important part of understanding the journey the genre took as the decade grew on, not just for all the bands that it influenced, but for understanding how the audience for rock shifted and mutated over time.

Oh, and the album itself? It's as good as I remember it being, which is a nice surprise, but on the other hand, I don't remember it being anything more than dumb fun. Tails off a bit after "Self Esteem", but not drastically. It's about as good as I'd expect an Offspring greatest hits set to be, only a touch more consistent, so I guess if at some point in the future I get the sudden urge to hear them I'll turn to this. Not sure that'll ever happen, mind.

Smash is the pinnacle of The Offspring's career. It combines the punkish attitude of their early material with the pop smarts of their future records and has the most consistently engrossing tracklist. The spoken intro sets the atmosphere for some devlish fun and the CD kicks off with "Nitro," one of the Offspring's finest songs. Everything on this CD works. From the road rage of "Bad Habit" to the penetrating isolation of "So Alone." "What Happened to You?" Can truly be seen as the precursor to what would become Americana due to it's reggae influenced pop. Anyway, did any band in the 90's write a more kickass single than "Self Esteem?" "Come Out and Play" is a teenage anthem...and Killboy Powerhead is a steroidal punch. If you're only gonna pick up one Offspring album, make it this one.

Por aplastamiento
L.A. PUNK. Smash fue el pelotazo definitivo de Offspring. No creo que les fuera fácil asimilarlo. De repente, una banda con una cierta repercusión en los ámbitos más alternativos, y con un cierto reconocimiento gracias a ese Ignition (1992) que los puso en el mapa, empezó a vender discos a mansalva y a verse a todas horas en la MTV gracias al videoclip de "Come Out and Play". "Self Esteem" no hizo sino amplificar el efecto un poco más para que este álbum fuera, junto a Dookie (Green Day, 1994), el responsable directo de la reactivación del rock alternativo más allá del grunge y de la popularización de un punk rock que no estaba en su mejor momento.

A partir de este disco la banda tocó un estrellato que no supo cuidar, todo hay que decirlo, pero que le permitió fichar por una multinacional, Columbia Records, y ganar pasta a espuertas. Su onda expansiva continuó varios años influyendo en la forma de construir sus siguientes trabajos, Ixnay on the Hombre (1997) a la cabeza, y les granjeó una popularidad que no podemos decir que no merecieran.

Al fin y al cabo dieron con un estilo que huía de lo pacato y que logró reactivar una fiereza que con la muerte de Kurt Cobain y la institucionalización de la escena de Seattle y sus satélites corría serio peligro de extinción. Lo que manda en este disco es una rítmica inexorable, un galope que deja sin resuello y que acompañan de ese estilo gritado de Dexter Holland al micro, el cual no se guarda nada desde que empieza la canción. También han aprendido a jugar con los matices en medios tiempos más currados, que son la auténtica novedad en su forma de escribir. De hecho, los tres singles extraídos del disco se ajustarían a esta descripción, ya sean las dos canciones mencionadas arriba o una "Gotta Get Away" que no por parar un poco el metrónomo deja de ser contundente.

Lo mejor de este disco, aquello por lo que siempre apetece ponérselo, es ese vértigo inextinguible al que nos somete. Esa sensación de que estamos en medio del vórtice sin poder salir. No hay solos en él. Eso es algo de lo que siempre han huido los californianos. Simplemente canciones bien construidas y mejor despachadas por parte de un grupo absolutamente on fire. Lo malo es que ni acabo de entender dónde está lo bueno de esos interludios con esa voz en off que acaba por irritarme, ni logran mantener mi atención durante todo el metraje. Sí, al final todo se acaba desinflando un poco. Algo que se podía haber corregido con una ligera poda. Por eso este clásico, para mí, lo es un poco menos de lo que debería.
> Dookie

Is it malicious of me to retroactively wish that Dexter Holland stayed in academe?

There are a plethora of things I don't like here, which is shocking because I used to revere this band in my teens. I suppose I've only got myself to blame for sticking to the hits at that time in my life. I'm all but positive that my favorite album when I was 14 was Rise and Fall, Rage and Grace, then newly released. And, judging by my sour reaction to Smash, I feel confident in declaring that I've no reason to spin that CD again.

I think the Offspring got lucky, honestly. Their timing was just right. Riding on the coattails of grunge and contemporaries of mainstream pop punk a la Dookie, I don't believe for a second that this would have held up in the slightest had this been released ten years later. It's extremely unconfident in its execution. Dexter's vocals are abysmal by punk standards. He's so off-key that I'd argue his doors are best kept locked. Hell, I nearly switched the album off after physically recoiling at his irksome "La-la-lala-laaaaaa" at the beginning of "Self-Esteem". The guitars sound flat, highly neutered and sterile. The percussion is weak. The songwriting is massively inconsistent. I could go on, but I think I've covered every base I can think of.

I do have to acknowledge, in spite of it all, that this does contain the slightest, most tenuous thread of nostalgia I've ever encountered, and that's the sole thing keeping this from a sub 2.
