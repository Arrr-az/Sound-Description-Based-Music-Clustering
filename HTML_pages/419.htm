barlights Apr 09 2006
+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
After Nick Lowe broke up with his country-rock/pub band, Brinsley Schwarz, in 1975, he spent a couple of years mainly producing other artists' work, most notably Elvis Costello's debut album (he'd go on to produce Costello's first five albums).

In 1978, Lowe returned to performing with his debut record, also the first album released on the new Radar label. Apparently the title was too much for American audiences, so on this side of the pond, the album was called Pure Pop for Now People. Although that title is pretty lame, it IS somewhat of an apt description for this album. Marking a sharp departure from his work with Brinsley Schwarz, this actually does sound like a missing Costello record.

Man, this is what pop music should sound like. Songs like "Shake and Pop" should be played in every dance club in America. The sound is fresh and could be released today to equal acclaim. I swear I've heard "I Love the Sound of Breaking Glass" sampled in some hip-hop song, but I just can't place it. If someone knows, please tell me! And if it isn't, it's ripe for the picking.

This album is long out-of-print for some unknown reason - along with Buckingham/Nicks, it's surely a sin for this not to be readily available. Unlike that album, it DID receive a cd issue in 1989 - that includes a terrific b-side not released on the original vinyl - "Rollers Show" is a power-pop parody of the music charts at the time - A who-know-how-many-tracked cacophony of Lowe voices singing about going to a Bay City Rollers show could not be more ironic.

Very highly recommended.

The Debut Album Project: Day 65

The American version of Nick Lowe's debut, Jesus of Cool, Pure Pop for Now People arguably offers up a superior track listing and sequence. But either way, it is - as promised - a great pop record.

Lowe is in fine form as a songwriter, and each of the twelve tracks presented here possesses its own unique sound and arrangement. And while the opening four-song stretch outclasses the rest of the set, lyrical and musical gems appear throughout.

Due in part to it's variety, this is not an easily classifiable record ("power pop" comes the closest), but any fan of hooky rock music is bound to find plenty to enjoy here.

Rating: 8.7

LunarCrooner Jan 19 2021
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
There’s an unwritten rule in cinema: never remind the audience of a better film. If you’re making a middling action or sci fi movie, the worst thing you can do is reference The Terminator or Aliens because that will get the audience thinking about them and comparing them unfavourably to your film.

That’s really how I feel about this album. Pretty much every song reminded me of a better one by a different artist.

The verse of So It Goes is almost a note-for-note cover of Steely Dan’s Reelin In The Years. I Love the Sound of Breaking Glass is reminiscent of Low-era Bowie. No Reason is pretty standard white guy reggae that had been à la mode since Mother and Child Reunion came out.

I suppose the pastiche in kind of the point - Nick Lowe clearly has a sense of humour about himself and his music and aping (if not outright spoofing) different musical clichés is his schtick.

But the problem is that the competition at this time was so (no pun intended) stiff and the late 70s was a time of such musical innovation
that what might have sounded like incisive parody at the time now sounds a bit insipid in comparison to its contemporaries.

I will say that the production is fantastic, not too polished and with warm, crunchy tube amp tone galore, and the musicianship is excellent throughout. Nor does the album outstay its welcome, at a crisp 33:17.

Overall it’s an enjoyable record, but for me there isn’t quite enough originality here to put it in the saturated top tiers of late 70s releases.

Highlights: I Love the Sound of Breaking Glass, No Reason, Nutted by Reality
