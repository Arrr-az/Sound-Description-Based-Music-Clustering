It's a crime that this album gets overlooked in the Floyd canon.
Yes it's another soundtrack, but much more structured into proper songs than "More" was. It's as straight forward rock and roll as you're gonna get with The Floyd, especially with the tracks "The Gold it's in the...", and "Childhood's End" which is widely regarded as Gilmours' finest solo composition within the band. There are some soundscapes such as the "title track/When You're In", and "Mudmen", my favorite track on the album. "Stay" is a cheesy little number by Rick Wright, but he makes up for it with brilliant organ work on "Absolutley Curtains".

Obscured By Clouds is one of Pink Floyd's lesser known albums, and being a soundtrack to an obscure film, that's rather understandable. However it must also be said that it's just simply one of their weaker efforts. Musically it sounds a lot like the albums it was released between, with some rootsy elements of Meddle, as well as some of the spacey rock that made Dark Side of the Moon so popular, but at the same time, it sounds more like a collection of outtakes and jams than a proper album. A good portion of the album is instrumental, which is natural being a soundtrack, and while these songs are good, particularly Mudmen, they seem a little unfinished. Where Obscured by Clouds shines is on its vocal tracks. On the curiously titled The Gold It's in the... Pink Floyd rocks out sounding almost like Zeppelin, while Free Four is one of the band's forgotten classics, a strange combination of folk and hard rock that muses about growing old. Other highlights include Childhood's End, which would have fit well on Dark Side of the Moon, and the album doesn't really have any glaring weak points. Obscured by Clouds is one of the least of Pink Floyd's 70s work, mainly due to a lack of focus, but for fans that have grown tired of the classics, it's certainly an interesting and worthwhile listen that grows over time.

FloydWright Apr 21 2004
+4 Vote downBookmarkVote up
This is a CD that can hold its own with many of Pink Floyd's greats--do not pass it up simply because it is a "non-concept" album. It is, in fact, the last non-concept album that Pink Floyd would produce until _A Momentary Lapse of Reason_. Therefore, what the listener ought to focus upon is the power of the music. This disc really does a nice job of forecasting some of the beautiful music that will later appear on _The Dark Side of the Moon_. The entire CD is underrated--and that is why I have chosen to open this review with ObC's most underrated track.

That, in my opinion, is "Absolutely Curtains". To be honest, it is beyond my understanding why this song seems to bother so many people. It's quite heartwrenchingly played by Richard Wright...there's something mournful and mysterious about it that his fans would recognize from his solo song "Interlude" (from _Broken China_)--that same feeling of standing frozen upon the edge of _something_ that's about to happen, not quite knowing exactly what it is. I know the song is credited to the entire band--but its _execution_ is largely down to Mr. Wright: the song, aside from percussion and tape effects, consists entirely of a synth (VCS3?), Farfisa and Hammond organs, Rhodes and acoustic pianos. While Pink Floyd obviously did not know what was to come, it's quite chilling to listen to this song bearing in mind that they _did_ in fact stand upon the edge of an era that, while filled with great promise, would also result in great pain. This is the very side of the music that, over time, was forced out of the music and only regained in later years. As for the chanting, I personally find it mesmerising.

Another historical rarity on this album, for which it should be treasured, is the appearance of the powerful Wright-Waters songwriting team--something seen only on this album and Dark Side of the Moon. Had Roger Waters had more respect for his opposite, this could have gone even further, as these tracks prove how perfectly they complemented each other. Look at "Burning Bridges", the best of these songs: here you have a gorgeous, memorable, and _innovative_ tune, along with enchanting (not overbearing...not yet!) lyrics. I should also point out that the mixing is the boldest for Mr. Wright's vocals to ever appear in a Pink Floyd album before the song "Wearing the Inside Out", making it quite a treat on the ears. "Mudmen", the instrumental companion track to "Burning Bridges", adds to Mr. Wright's gorgeous chord sequence some guitar work that might remind the listener of "Time" although with a backing more like "Us and Them". There is no doubt that both David Gilmour and Richard Wright were now ready for The Dark Side of the Moon. (A bit of trivia--someone pointed out that the time signature is changed between "Burning Bridges" and "Mudmen", a very interesting subtle sign of musical innovation.)

Perhaps the most _chilling_ of all the predictors on this album is the lyrics to "Free Four", put on a backdrop of music that is entirely too happy for the subject matter (intentional sarcasm on Mr. Waters part, I believe, similar to "Corporal Clegg" on _A Saucerful of Secrets_). The lyrics, in a way, are a precursor to every theme that Roger Waters will dwell on in future works, particularly _The Wall_. His fans will probably see this as a welcome introduction to his more serious approach to music--others may see this song as hailing the future destruction of the band.

"When You're In"/"Obscured By Clouds" is a quick rock instrumental opening that shows Mr. Wright's Hammond technique in fine form. "The Gold, It's In the..." is perhaps the album's weak point--a track that sounds less like Pink Floyd and more like the Doobie Brothers: basically a simple rock tune, but still enjoyable enough. "Wot's uh the Deal" is a very pleasant "Pillow of Winds"-like tune with interesting lyrics, nice vocals by David Gilmour...not THE best of the album, but very soothing and likeable. "Childhood's End" is written solely by Mr. Gilmour, and while musically (except in the intro) doesn't really deviate from standard rock, it's lyrically one of his better outings, just under "Sorrow" and "High Hopes". "Stay" is the other Wright/Waters outing, and my suspicion based on the ordering of credits is that the lyrics, similar in theme to "Summer '68" (describing the emptiness of a one-night stand) were _mostly_ written by Rick Wright himself. My personal guess based on intuition is that the chorus is Roger Waters--the rest is Rick Wright, and his vocals are very pleasant to listen to.

Overall, this is a disc that all Pink Floyd fans should make sure to pick up, both for its own intrinsic qualities and for the almost prophetic way in which it indicates the future direction of the band.

The Bridge To The Best
Not many people talk about this album or even acknowledge it due to the fact its sandwiched between two even better albums with more importance to rock history and to the band, and it's really a shame it gets overlooked. This album is easily one of their strongest despite it being done as a soundtrack to a film, it features some of their greatest songs and a lot of the songs on it sound like much of what came after on albums like "Wish You Were Here" and "The Dark Side of the Moon". "Childhood's End" is one of Gilmour's greatest lyrical works and for example has the same concept and idea of reflecting on youth that's present in many songs after with a similar music style to "Have a Cigar", "Free Four" has similar lyrical themes to "Time" and is quite brilliant.

The rest of the album is all quite great, with the title track and "When You're In..." being some of Pink Floyd's heaviest songs, "The Gold It's In The..." being another fantastic song with amazing instrumental work, "Wot's... Uh the deal?" and "Burning Bridges" each having an elegant instrumental that's nothing short of pretty. "Stay" is alright.

The only downsides to this album are "Mudmen" which is too similar to "Burning Bridges" and sounds like a clone, and "Absolutely Curtains" being a sorry attempt for an atmospheric track by the band.

The final stepping stone
This is Pink Floyd's last hurrah as an underground band. It has a lot of great tracks, and feels like a bit of a callback to their early 60's music, with prog rock injected into it.

A lot of songs in this album have lyrics relating to age, and this album really feels like the band aging into something more commercial. The sound of their popular era was already established, but the mentality of maturity was not. This is them gaining that mentality.

The production, and mix are the best in their discography to this point, and the album is all around enjoyable on that side of things. Nothing special, however.

The big issue with this album is that there is a lot of filler, instrumental tracks. Nothing as bad as the pointless songs filling More, but they aren't great either.

Great album all around.
