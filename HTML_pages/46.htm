After reading that Adam Yauch had died, I tried a couple times to write a review for Hello Nasty, but everything came out in turns unnecessarily sappy, transparently insincere, or myopically nostalgic. So instead of trying to write some epic love letter/eulogy, I've decided to go the simple route and just point out why I love this album: it's the lyrics.

Sure, the music is fantastic--aside from the canonical Paul's Boutique, it's the best music in the Beasties' discography. But there are so many funny lines, sly references, and clever turns of phrase on Hello Nasty, that a reviewer who doesn't mention the lyrics is positively negligent. Thus, I present for you my Top Five favorite lines from Hello Nasty:

1. I don't mean to brag, I don't mean to boast, but I'm intercontinental when I eat French toast. ("The Move") Positively hilarious. Positively brilliant.
2. I'm the king of Boggle, there is none higher; I get eleven points with the word "quagmire." ("Putting Shame in Your Game") Obviously, a reference to Run-D.M.C.'s "King of Rock," this line doubles as a goofy tribute to the Beasties' golden age compatriots and a clever boast about the group's own lyrical skills.
3. Dogs love me 'cuz I'm crazy sniffable! ("The Move") Yes, I suppose they do.
4. Beastie Boys, known to let the beat, mmmmmm . . . drop? ("Intergalactic") A sly reference to their own "The New Style" from twelve years earlier.
5. This one's for Ondo, Bombay, Espaa, Upper Tasmania, Scandinavia . . . . To all the people in the Dead Sea! ("Dedication") Positively Ol' Dirty Bastard-ian in the realm of bizarre-yet-awesome rambling nonsense.

I can't get over how live this record sounds. The mixing is a marvel, info-dense but still readable. To me, this sounded like the future when it was released, although it's important to note I was a moron. Hello Nasty is 60% banging tracks, mostly shuffle and hi-hat instead of their earlier fuzzed-out funk (or their later regressive break-bap). In places like "Body Movin'" or "Remote Control", it sounds like Big Beat.

And one of the instrumentals, as usual, plates a couple runs. "Sneakin' Out the Hospital" melds wah guitar, Eastern percussion, and Mix Master Mike's deft turntable touch.

But the Beasties can't pull everything off; for example, their stabs at exotica/Latin funk don't bring anything to the table. "The Negotiation Limerick File" is a game attempt to switch the stale delivery somewhat (and has one of the best hip-hop intro samples ever), but the rote let's-let-one-guy-flow-solid tactic flops on "Flowin' Prose". Really, these tracks are good, but the best-deployed Stravinsky samples can't counteract an ossified rhyme scheme. So the best songs do the usual, but on another level of passion: "Electrify," "Remote Control".

From the first time I heard it, "Intergalactic" became a top-ten all-time song for me, and it carried its ranking until some time last year. Now it sounds like the work of a parallel-universe late-90s one-hit wonder: bad flow, great spirit, self-consciously quirky sampling. Now the honor seems to be "Instant Death" - without parallel in the Boys' catalog: a hushed electric piano/acoustic ditty, sung in unrhyming, metrically-hexed phrases, that gives way to an industrial-meets-muzak drumloop stairway to purgatory (or Radiohead's "No Surprises", which is probably where I stuck it on mixes back in the day). Oh, and the song's sung by a kid missing his mom and thinking about his friend's suicide. No way the Beasties could've made a record out of that sound, but it's a credit to Hello Nasty that you can understand why it's the closer.

Wow.

It's been a couple of years since I've listened to this album. I forgot how good it is. Hello Nasty is so confident, so diverse, so full of amazing shifts and turns that its almost impossible to settle into. Instead, you ride it like an uncontrollable bronco.

Grab the reigns and hold on.

Hello Nasty is the home of "Intergalactic". How do I love thee, "Intergalactic"? Let me count the ways. Well, never mind. Great song is all, and it defines the musical and lyrical themes for the first half of album.

"I Don't Know" is what solidifies Hello Nasty as one of the great albums of the nineties. The nice bossa nova rhythm and guitars along with lovely Miho Hatori's backing vocals (she was born to sing bossa nova songs) give the song an organic feel. The fact that this gem is nestled in amongst a bunch of tracks that are grounded in electronic and digital sounds might seem jarring, except that it's set up nicely by the preceding "Song for Junior" which sounds like an outtake from a Santana album minus the guitar solo, as well as snippets here and there spread throughout the album that suggest that the album might just go anywhere at any given moment. So, when it does make a drastic turn here or there, the listener is ready. When "Picture This" comes along a couple of tracks later and ties the bossa nova vocals, the Santana groove and the electronic sounds together into a cohesive unit it makes perfect sense. It's where the bronco was going the whole time, right?

Space rap, yes, but there's more going on in Hello Nasty, as side two reveals. How do we resolve our organic natures with scientific and technological advancements? That seems the be the question being asked, and by the end of the album the boys seem to have figured it out.

Their wildest LP
Today, July 14, 2023, as I am writing this review, is the 25th anniversary of this classic fifth LP by Beastie Boys! So today I thought I should take a closer look into what I think is easily the wildest, most interesting LP the group put out.

First can I just say how consistent of a discography these guys have. Like c'mon that stretch from Paul's Boutique to this record is definitely one of the best ever, and even outside of that I don't think they even have one bad LP! Anyways, this record isn't in my top 3 Beasties records but it definitely deserves to be given the title of the most interesting.

First some context: this is the third and final of their 90s LPs which basically forms a trilogy of three genre bending, live instrument focused LPs. These three albums, Check Your Head, Ill Communication, and this one are all great, but I think this one is the weakest. Still, it is a great record and easily out of the three the most sonically dynamic featuring sci fi production, rap focused tracks, acoustic tracks, lounge music tracks, funk inspired instrumentals, and more. One of the most sonically diverse records I have ever heard.

Anyways let's first get to the highlights: first let me just say side one of this album is one of the greatest album sides in their catalog. Anyways Side 1 begins with "Super Disco Breakin'", one of the best Beasties openers ever. This track is a great introduction with infectious energy, and catchy chorus, and is short yet powerful! A great opener and introduces you to the really cool sound of this LP which I love so much. One of my favorite Beasties tracks ever too. "The Move" follows and again is a short yet powerful hip hop track, with a simple yet great beat, great energy, fun beat switches (I love that one with that goofy organ near the middle), and so many quotables (I especially love "But I'm intercontinental when I eat French toast"). The ending with the sample is a bit odd but it works well to segue to the next track, "Remote Control". I may be wrong but I believe this is a Mike D solo track and it's great! His energy is through the roof on this cut, with great production that feels clustered yet so spaced out at the same time. I like the guitar in the song too. We then get "Song for the Man" which is definitely one of the wildest cuts on the LP, with mesmerizing production, great drums, guitars, and even horns show up on here, this song just SOUNDS so cool I love it. I also love the deadpan delivery too it works so well with this strange instrumental. Good lyrics too. This is followed by "Just a Test", which is a more straightforward hip hop cut, I love the catchy chorus on this track, another great produced song with some great bass licks and sound effects. "Body Movin'" follows which is simply just perfection. Great chorus, fun lyrics and energy, great quotables such as "I'm so sweet like a nice bon bon", some of my favorite production on the entire record, and the fitness samples are funny and work well on here too. I do have to shout out the Fatboy Slim remix it's also really good. Side 2 starts with the absolute classic "Intergalactic" which is probably the best track on the LP. One of their best singles ever in my opinion, this track has so many great quotables, an infectious hook, amazing sci fi production, and just such great energy. What else can you say about that track? Something cool about my personal history with the song was that it was the first Beasties song I heard when I watched the first live action Diary of Wimpy Kid movie, anyone else have that experience? Another great track is "Putting Shame in Your Game", the beat on here is one of the most interesting on the album, and the chorus and energy of the track is great. We also get the title drop of the album too on this track. "And Me" comes soon after and it is probably one of the weirdest cuts on here with that trippy distorted production and digital sound effects, and some really interesting technology focused lyrics that are helped by the robotic sounding vocals. "Three MC's and One DJ" ends the first half of the LP and it is a really fun cut with a great chorus and energy, and while the beat on here is one of the more bare ones it works well with the energy of the track. Later on Side 3 is "Song for Junior", which is the first of two lounge inspired cuts on the LP and while I prefer the other one this track is still a lot of fun, sounding immaculately produced. I love the flute and keyboards on this track and Jill Cunniffs sweet vocals work really well with the instrumental. I like the faux live sound effects too here. "I Don't Know" follows and is one of my favorite Beasties tracks of all time and is one of the best on this LP. MCA's voice on here is so sweet it makes me wish he sang more on tracks, and I love Miho Hatori's vocals on here, making it sound even more sweet. I love how acoustic this track sounds yet it also has those pounding drums and low mixed strings which work really well. A great track, it sounds so different from the rest of the LP yet it still works really well and this is one of those tracks that makes me miss MCA even more. Good lyrics too. "The Negotiation Limerick File" is another hip hop track and it is a fun little track, I like how they wrote a song using limerick its a cool touch, not one of my favorites but still a good track with some great production like usual. Later we get to Side 4 which starts with "Picture This", my favorite out of the two lounge tracks. Brooke Williams' vocals work really well with the instrumental, and with those abstract lyrics and kind of unnerving instrumental this song always takes me to another place, what a track. A few tracks later is "Dr. Lee, PhD" which definitely sticks out among the bunch but it is still a fun reggae track and is really unique for the group. It's great that they were able to work with the late Lee "Scratch" Perry, I like his feature and it's just a fun little track. Could've been a bit shorter though in my opinion. The album closes with "Instant Death" which is a really haunting Ad-Rock solo cut dedicated to two people close to him that had died, it's definitely a sad track but with its mechanical production it's really unique and it ends off the album in a really odd way.

Some other great things about this album: Like I said prior this album is so sonically and stylistically diverse its remarkable. It's easily their least punk influenced album out of the trilogy but it doesn't really need to be as Check Your Head and Ill Communication already have punk tracks on them. I love the production on this LP, I brought it up a bunch but seriously if Paul's Boutique never existed this would be their best sounding LP, the psychedelic sci fi sound of this LP is just so good and quirky. And let's talk about this artwork! Great album cover, with the front and back emphasizing that sci fi theme. I also like the inner sleeves and the record labels as they have images of the moon and the earth's crust which is a cool touch. All in all a really well presented LP.

Finally the lowlights: First on Side 2 is "Sneakin' Out the Hospital" which while is a great produced instrumental doesn't really do anything for me. It's definitely one of the more odd cuts on the record but it fits in nice, however I think it could've been sequenced better in the tracklist. Not really a favorite instrumental for me by the group. Second is "Flowin' Prose", and while I like the flow on here it just doesn't do much for me. The beat and production again is good but it's just not a standout. The opener on Side 3 "The Grasshopper Movement" is next and it's a fun one but I feel like it's missing something. Well produced like usual. Next, the Side 3 closer "Electrify" which admittedly has great production and samples it doesn't really do anything for me. I do like that Posdonous sample from "Stakes Is High" used for the hook. Then, two cuts on Side 4, first is "Unite". Again, another great produced song and I love the sound effects but I just think there's better tracks on this record. Second we also have "Dedication", and while I think it's a nice tribute to their fans and friends and the production's good, again it just doesn't do much for me. It really feels just like filler and not one of my favorites. I think an issue with this record is that while it is so sonically diverse it is a bit repetitive. I think that's really just something about their rapping style, but like some of the rap cuts near the end just don't hit as strong because they get a bit repetitive delivery wise. Second this album is pretty bloated, and while I love a lot on here I would've preferred a shorter LP. It's great to see them put out basically anything they wanted though, as you can really feel the creative freedom here. Third, this album is not sequenced the best for me as a lot of the best stuff is in the first half, I mean you got 2 of the biggest singles, "Body Movin'" and "Intergalactic", literally right next to each other. Thankfully there's good stuff in the second half to make up for that.

Overall, this is a really really good Beasties LP. While I think that the other two LP's in the trilogy and Paul's Boutique are better, this is still a really good record. Like I said prior production wise this album sounds so good and this record is just a lot of fun to listen to. If you are listening to this group I can't recommend this one enough, a really fun and stylistically diverse LP.

8/10

My copy: I own a 2009 EU vinyl press of this record which sounds pretty good! I think some of their other reissues sound better but this one sounds pretty good. I mentioned it earlier the packaging is nice and while I would've preferred better material inner sleeves I do love the art on the printed inners. You really can't go wrong with a Beastie Boys vinyl reissue, I've never gotten a bad one.

Hello Nasty - Beastie Boys
Beastie Boys final studio outing for the 90s. Hello Nasty marked the trio shifting their sound and style once more. Their previous albums Ill Communication and Check Your Head were both very eclectic and original genre-hoppers, although execution on the latter was lacking a bit quality-wise. Here the group is as eclectic and eccentric as ever, but they veer into uncharted and experimental territories. Its less funk and more electronic, less punk and more psychedelic. Its also quite a bit longer, at nearly 70 minutes compared to the sub-hour runtimes of their previous projects. The result? In my opinion the groups strongest showing since their magnum opus Paul's Boutique nearly a decade prior.

Boom Bap beats, electro, and turntable scratches accompany the groups tried and true performance style. Sometimes they switch things up and go for singing, occasionally they dont even appear on a song at all, but its the standard stuff youd come to expect at this point. If you like their performances on prior albums, youll like it here, and vice versa if you dont. Personally, I enjoy it, and when they switch things up here, its on par with their regular stuff. Its a pretty fun record, and despite its length and fluff, the project is surprisingly consistent. From the start with the fun Super Disco Breakin to the end with the more melancholic and low-key Instant Death the album has plenty of strong showings, such as the iconic Intergalactic, catchy as hell The Move and Unite, and the pretty nice I Dont Know.

8.3/10
