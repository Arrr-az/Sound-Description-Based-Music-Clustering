There has never been a Power Trio quite like Cream. All three members are virtuosic masters of their instruments. And Jack Bruce is in fact so good on Bass, and His bassline's take up so much of Cream's songs that it pushed Guitarist Eric Clapton to his absolute limits and beyond just finding holes to play through. And because of that, in his entire long and storied career, Eric Clapton never was better or more of a Guitar God, than he is on Disraeli Gears. I mean, the man invented Wah for God's sake. And I haven't even mentioned Ginger Baker. one of the five greatest Drummers that ever lived. With all that it's no wonder this album is a epic of the highest order. And that just scratches the surface of the album's brilliance. Tales Of Brave Ulysses is possibly the greatest Psychadelic Guitar song ever written. And the overwrought, brain beating, primal stomp of Sunshine Of Your Love is very nearly Heavy Metal thuggery. This album is just glorious.

Those goddamned hipsters named this album after a gaff one of them made describing the "disraeli gears" on a bicycle. If that isn't self-indulgent and rather pretentious. . .then again those two qualities are a good part of why this album is so great. They really don't hold back with expressing their grandiose stoner ideas at all. Outside Woman Blues is kind of sucky and stilted, but then again Eric Clapton covered it in his solo stuff so what else would you expect? It's the worst song on here.

Swlabr, Tales of Brave Ulysses, World of Pain, Sunshine of Your Love, and Strange Brew are all totally badass. Mother's Lament, Blue Condition, and Dance the Night Away are good. We're Going Wrong, Take It Back, and Outside Woman Blues are not really so good. Overall, the good songs have more win than many albums have in the whole thing, so it totally deserves 4.5 stars.

This review is about my first encounter with trying to analyze something, and coming up with hardly anything based on an unchangeable incompatibility. I'm gonna go ahead and leave this discombobulated diatribe the way it occurred; I think it serves the exact feeling of said incompatibility: that of being on the total outside of something and being totally unable to get in.

I'm sorry. Okay. Here:

Supergroups fall into two categories: those made up of songwriters focusing on songs, and those made up of renowned instrumentalists focusing on the incredible power that can be created when they unite. Now, if your one stipulation is that you must use Eric Clapton, what category would you put him into? A master of his instrument, an occasional provider for fairly decent licks, exempli gratia 'Layla,' never much more asked of him from his fanbase. Well, you don't need much more from him, and therefore you don't get much more from him. Not now, not ever.

And while he's a master of his instrument, he's never strapped you to the bed and done unspeakable things to you with his guitar work, like Hendrix or someone along those lines. His guitaring could much better be likened to a generally great experience, one where the van's rocking (and warding off all potential knockers, given your warning, written or vocally announced) and everything's terrific and you'll ask for it again and again, but it's not something where you're gasping for air afterward, wondering how much humanity you've just lost, and you're clutching at the sheets to balance yourself with this particular latitude and longitude. Okay, I'm going to stop talking about Eric Clapton having sex via shredding, because that's not appealing to me.

The point is, he's not an overwhelming guitarist, and his stripped-down songwriting skill is like eating Planters when you could be eating gourmet home-roasted.

Jack Bruce and Ginger Baker seem, gauging from this record, like they are also not foudroyant musicians; it's not like they can't, it's just that it's not their style, man. This isn't Hendrix and Mitch Mitchell, here. No one's plooping jasm in their Lee's. And I'm not entirely familiar with the other groups Bruce and Baker are from, but taking a gander at their Member Of: sections, I take it they're well and sought-after on the studio and touring circuit.

Maybe I had to be there. At the time of this review, I'm freshly 25 years old. This type of music doesn't hold frequent purchase in my recreational listening, there's something unsettling to my sober Generation Y mind about the flower power era and its outward dependence on acid et al to make something incredible, and god damn it, I'll just say it, I fucking can't stand Eric Clapton on a general stance. So maybe I had to be there on the streets as Disraeli Gears hit the stores to retain the full effect of it. Because this album has classic masterpiece written all over it, just in another language that I'll never understand.

Not much stands out from this album except that one song that's in every Beginner-to-Intermediate Guitar Sheet Music Compilation: Classic Rock Hits collection I've ever seen. That song, which any idiot could dun-dun-dun and air-guitar to on command, is pretty agelessly badass. It's one of those exceptions that you most likely enjoy unless you have a sweeping negative opinion on everything that came out before 1999. Or '89. Ish. Point is, if you like to hear the classics from time to time, 'Sunshine of Your Love' is undoubtedly on your mixtape. The guitar line is perfect, the one-three drums are perfect, the melody is perfect. Great song, shut up, listen to it, just shut up. This song gets great mileage in many different areas of life.

1. You and your high school buddies, no matter what generation you're from, hate modern culture and you want to get high. Get an .mp3 of this, put it on a playlist with some Hendrix, 'Acid Queen,' Pink Floyd, and you'll feel right at home in your misplaced era, and you can talk about how the Republicans can suck it.

2. EXT. HOUSE - FRONT YARD - DAY

A pathetic dumbass, DARREN with a boombox approaches the front door, presses the doorbell. KAYLA opens the door.

KAYLA
Darren, what are you--?

DARREN
Kayla, you are the sunshine of my love.

Darren presses play on the boombox and the opening guitar riff plays once, almost twice before he abruptly hits the stop button with an intrusive click. He stands there in awkward waiting for a response, which happens to be Kayla shutting the door in his face. Fade to black.

3. You're learning guitar and you need to show your friends that you've been learning guitar. Whip this sucker out, and they'll be like, 'Whoa!' unless one of them has also learned guitar, and so happens to be good at it, and thinks you're an unimaginative novice. Noobcake. What's next, 'Stairway,' or 'Iron Man?' Perhaps 'Smoke on the Water?'

It's just one of those songs. Very few people who have ears and are not steeped in impoverished culture will die without having heard it. And most will get it stuck in their head for at least an allotted week and a half, if not more. Census, can we get these types of questions to deathbeds? I'd be interested how many times this and 'I Would Walk 500 Miles' and 'Surfin USA' have gotten ingrained.

Anyway, once you get past this song, you can pretty much get to your philosophical discussions and let the tunes rest in the background. 'Strange Brew' and 'World of Pain,' placed conveniently at the beginning of the album in a cute little menage a trois with 'Sunshine,' are minutely noteworthy. Everything beyond this is three musicians jamming on a variety of blues heads, though none of them sound like one another; it's all pretty distinct. 'Tales of Brave Ulysses' is alright, so is 'Take It Back.'

But, listen, I never thought I'd concede to the hitherto ridiculous notion that I might be too young for something to be appreciated fully, but you know, I think I am in this case. It’s 3:43 in the morning as I begin this review, my PTSD has kicked in full time, the anniversary of Woodstock was a week or so ago, I purchased this album at Tower Records in Los Angeles at just about this time in 1967, a city I’d be returning to in a year or so as I caught my flight for Vietnam, bringing me full circle ... right back to tonight and my inability to sleep. I’m not listening to “Disraeli Gears,” though it is echoing in my head; this is one of those albums you only need to hear once, perhaps twice, and it will be forever locked in your ears.

I’m not going to tell you that Cream was the perfect band, they were certainly a power trio, though in my book the greatest trio prior to them was The Nat King Cole Trio, who broke as much new ground as did Cream, and suffered some of the same trials and tribulations. Before I go on, I must say that I find it totally amazing that a group such as Cream ever managed to get together to begin with. Ginger Baker has to be one of the greatest drummers of all time, and an equally miserable person ... this is a band who seemed to be at odds with each other from the very beginning, and if you ask most people who laid down the vocals, I bet the majority of folks would snap back with "Eric Clapton," having completely forgotten Jack Bruce. Cream were not as smooth as their name would indicate. Cream infused jazz, rock, and blues in a unique fashion, becoming a “Pop” Psychedelic sensation, but their music was anything but easy to listen to. Of course there were a handful of songs from Cream's portfolio that everyone loves, but to this day no one walks around humming “Sunshine Of Your Love,” or the Homer on acid influenced “Tales Of Brave Ulysses,” a song that always seems much much longer than it’s two minute and forty-nine second running time, yet alone “We’re Going Wrong,” a number that is so disjointed and full of polyrhythms that it always leaves me hurting, because Cream were actually going wrong ... or at least imploding.

Some have said that Disraeli Gears, Cream’s second album, fixed, polished, and discarded any mistakes that were made on their first release Fresh Cream. Though in actuality for me, they seemed to create even more disjointed sounds than on Fresh Cream, leaving me totally captivated by a splendid cover, one of those album jackets that stands as a visual beacon for the Summer of Love, but without much substance within the package. It wouldn’t be 'til the formation of Blind Faith and the addition of additional members that the essence of Cream would finally strike gold with stunning harmonies and less acid drenched, and more blues orientated musical meanderings ... and for my money, even with that being said, Blind Faith was a much more satisfying psychedelic release.

Now I’m not gonna try to tell you that Eric Clapton didn’t lay down some legendary riffs, because he certainly did ... but they were only air guitar moments that live on as just that, and then disappear into the ether until someone decides to spring them at some inopportune moment while way too stoned. Eric’s playing doesn’t so much balance, or carry the songs, as it rises above, like a huge flag over some Fire-Base on a jungle hillside, calling attention to itself and drawing flack. Equally, Baker’s drumming sets its own course, leaving Jack Bruce out there in the rain trying to pull it all together with his vocals. If this album had been made by anyone other than these three great musicians it would be laughable, or perhaps as forgettable as Iron Butterfly ... because man, they put on the paisley shirts, the striped bellbottomed paints, wide belts, floppy hats, fury jackets, trippy vests, high heeled boots, and all the turquoise jewelry they owned. And while it may have made for a great album cover, it certainly didn’t make for an easy walk-around look; nor was it all that original.

“Sunshine Of Your Love,” probably one of Clapton’s best known songs actually takes its solo from “Blue Moon” by Billie Holiday, and the whole track is based on a Jimi Hendrix performance. I have to laugh when I hear people say that Clapton out-did himself on this release, because [smiling] Eric seemed to want to out-do himself on every track, an impossible task, less of course one’s drug induced haze makes them think they can lay dynamic music over what they consider to be Dylan meets the Grateful Dead lyrics ... which they aren’t. “Strange Brew” was originally to be called “Lawdy Mama” and would never have seen the light of day had it not been for Felix Pappalardi, who rewrote the guitar tracks and the lyrics. And even that tale of Brave Ulysses, that was based on a poem by artist Martin Sharp [yes, the man who designed the album jacket], so even that song did not wholly these three bolts of lightening.

Disraeli Gears is rather boring to listen to, nearly impossible to get through from end to end, full of imperfections, and rather creepy as a whole ... I remember standing there in front of the stereo thinking, “Man, I just wasted five bucks.” But it was a cool album cover, one that looked great under a black-light, and was certainly in fashion with my bellbottom paints, my paisley shirt, my floppy hat, vest, hippy beads, and that incense that I kept trying to like ... but only gave me a headache.

And now it’s 4:46 in the morning ...

Some fun facts: As to the album's title, Eric Clapton had been thinking of buying a racing bicycle and was discussing it with Ginger Baker, when a roadie named Mick Turner commented, "it's got them Disraeli Gears", meaning to say "derailleur gears", but instead alluding to 19th Century British Prime Minister, Benjamin Disraeli.

As to the album cover: The front cover consists of a psychedelic collage with the album's title centred and the band name below, surrounded by a floral arrangement. Martin Sharp was attempting to capture the sound of the music in the cover, which he describes as a "warm fluorescent sound". The cover art was created by Australian artist Martin Sharp who lived in the same building as Clapton, The Pheasantry in Chelsea.

Disraeli Gears: Deluxe Edition (Polydor 2004)

The Deluxe Edition of Cream’s Disraeli Gears expands the original single LP to a double-disc set. Packaged in a digi-pack gatefold, it is comprised of the stereo and mono mix of the original album, outtakes, associated demos, and BBC recordings.

Rest assured, Disraeli Gears is still the same lumpy stew of psychedelia and blues it always has been. The added outtakes are bland as are the BBC sessions. On the other hand, the demos included here are a welcome alternative to the versions that made the record. They are a little more straight forward and represent Cream’s sound more accurately. Some interesting lost tracks as well.

This deluxe edition includes the original mono and stereo mixes of the album. Albums were released in both mono and stereo during the ‘60s. Initially, the mono versions were considered to be the primary product while stereo was regarded a gimmick released to appease a niche market. Like any technological discovery, interest in this magical two-channelled wizardry increased and the market for stereo mixes expanded. Yes, gadgetry shadowed the fact that the mixes were often unpleasant and quite simply inferior (where does this sound familiar?). Whichever mix was considered the primary when this album was originally released I couldn’t tell you; however, it appears that time has rendered the stereo mix the standard. Although the mono is much more powerful.

Disc One

Tracks 1 - 11: Stereo Mix
Tracks 12 & 13: Stereo Out-takes
Tracks 14 - 16: Stereo Demos (recorded at Ryemuse Studios March 15, 1967)

Disc Two

Tracks 1 - 11: Mono Mix
Tracks: 12 & 13: Mono Out-takes
Tracks: 14 - 16: BBC Session recorded May 30, 1967
Tracks: 17 - 19: BBC Session recorded October 24, 1967
Tracks: 20 - 22: BBC Session recorded January 9, 1968
