A debatable 5, as there are some questionable moments on Imagine. "I Don't Want to Be a Soldier" just goes on for too long, when it didn't have all that much going for it in the first place. Also, "Oh My Love" is slightly unmemorable. But the rest...

It is because of these other songs that Imagine sneaks up to a 5 star rating. Obviously the title track is an all time classic that really needs no introduction, a message of optimism that remains unmatched in music. "Jealous Guy" happens to be my favorite solo Lennon tune, beautiful and somber, Lennon's sincere vocal, and a great whistling section to boot! The closing "Oh Yoko!" is so damn catchy too, and there's that cool harmonica present as well.

Imagine is usually remembered for being a 'softer' and more accessible album than the previous John Lennon/Plastic Ono Band, but its not all lightweight. The stinging guitar of "It's So Hard" is great fun, and the biting "Give Me Some Truth" is one of Lennon's very best. Then there is a rant slagging former Lennon bandmate Paul McCartney in "How Do You Sleep?", another great track that shows this is not all 'chocalate coated for public consumption', as Lennon once said about the album.

So, as a followup to John Lennon/Plastic Ono Band, Imagine loses a little something. On its own it is a fine album however, and those wanting a somewhat more 'accessible' Lennon should check this one out first.

Problems abound - starting with the first song, it's pretty obvious to me that we're a long way down from John Lennon / Plastic Ono Band (which is in turn a long way down from the Beatles, but that's another story for another day). I don't care how famous "Imagine" is, I don't care that it's been covered by everyone from Ray Charles to Chris Cornell to Average White Band to Lady Gaga, I don't care how many of my friends love it, I don't care that I used to love it in high school, I don't care that it gets all sorts of radio play, I don't care that John Lennon wrote it, and I certainly don't care that it's Rolling Stone's third-favorite song of all time. "Imagine" is boring, "Imagine" is sappy, "Imagine" is ironically unimaginative, and "Imagine" is, as Elvis Costello pointed out, disgustingly hypocritical. If "Stairway to Heaven" didn't exist (memo to "Stairway to Heaven" - stop existing), it would be the single most grossly overrated song of all time. Because it's basically what happens when you drink coffee with too much artificial sweetener, except the coffee is cliched on top of that. The only thing I even vaguely like about the song, other than the fact that it can be blocked out a lot more easily than many of McCartney's contemporary hits, is the socialistic undertone, but even then, it's not like I imagine John actually living like a socialist.

Steeeee-rike one.

Ah, but the problems continue long after the for some reason famous title track fades out. For as interesting as I find the strings on "How Do You Sleep," the lyrics are utterly repellent, with Mr. "And the wo-o-o-orrllld will live as one" rapidly correcting himself and saying "okay, so the parts of the wo-o-orrld that aren't named Paul McCartney will live as one." "Give Me Some Truth" was probably something that needed saying at the time, but there really isn't anything of interest going on there musically, and the same can be said for the endlessly repetitive "I Don't Want to be a Soldier," the only other song besides the for some reason legendary title track that truly bothers me - the monotony of the vocal section is broken only by an equally monotonous, cheesy, "aggressive" sax solo. Meanwhile, "It's So Hard," "Oh My Love," "How?" and "Oh Yoko!" bore more than boring has never bored before. Lennon's downhill slide from pseudo-genius to one of the most boring musicians ever pretty much began here.

So I am thankful for the existence of the ironically jaunty "Crippled Inside," which for me is exactly what "Give Me Some Truth" wanted to be, and especially for the heartfelt, honestly beautiful "Jealous Guy," which is to me what "Imagine" wanted to be. These two songs appear as an oasis of quality on an album that can otherwise best be described as a barren desert where all things interesting about music go to die.

Track picks: "Crippled Inside," "Jealous Guy"

Q: How pretty is "Jealous Guy"?

A: So damn pretty.

That song, and not the universe-swallowing title track, is the one that steals the show here by being one of the best songs he's ever written. This is obviously a much easier listen than the primal scream therapy that was Lennon's first solo outing. Don't make the mistake of thinking it's easy-listening however. Sure, half the tracks here are tender and melodic; the afforementioned beauty, the little boy lost in "How?" and the hushed idealism of the opener and "Oh My Love". But Lennon has a way of making you see past the naivety and the hypocrisy of his grand statements and just enjoying them for what they are. It's almost frustrating, like the troubled child who keeps letting you down but who you can't give up on because they have a way of making you believe in them that one more time.

That troubled child is evident on the other half of this album, the bitter, angry stuff that is so much a part of Lennon's songwriting. "How Do You Sleep?" directs its fury at Macca, "I Don't Want To Be A Soldier" at war and "Gimme Some Truth" at, well, everyone. The latter is the venting highlight for me. "Crippled Inside", with it's country vibe, and "Oh Yoko!" put a big smile on my face for being funny and lovely respectively. Really, everything here has something going for it and as an album, a listen from start to finish, I think it might be his best.

+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
While Imagine was not hailed as a classic upon released, rock 'historians' now routinely give the album 'masterpiece' status. Perhaps they need to give it a real listen again. The album is often stiff and most of the arrangements lack any real fire. The title track, of course, is considered a great anthem of peace and humanity, but if one really reads the lyrics, some troubling issues come up. That a multimillionaire who later moved into the Dakotas would attack people for clinging to their possessions smacks more than a little of hypocrisy. And it's a good bet a large number of people who view this song as a great anthem actually are people of faith who just don't get the attack against that faith.
The second song, "Crippled Inside", is supposedly a response to "Three Legs" from Paul & Linda McCartney's Ram LP. Lyrically, the song could easily have been about Lennon's own issues as much as McCartney's, but the song suffers from a stiff arrangement. Whereas "Three Legs" was funky even with numerous tempo changes, "Crippled Inside" falls flat musically.
"Jealous Guy" is probably the high point of the album although it is a re-casting of a song Lennon wrote in 1968 called "Child of Nature". Lyrically, "It's So Hard" is interesting, but the arrangement is nothing short of grating. The last song on side one is "I Don't Want To Be A Soldier" is more experimental with percussion, echo and lyrical repetitions (and includes Tom Evans and Joey Molland from Badfinger on guitars). Whether this anti war mantra works is for each listener to decide.
The second half begins with "Gimme Some Truth" which has a fierce arrangement, great guitar from George Harrison, and a great vocal from Lennon. "Oh My Love" could have been one of the standout tracks. The melody is beautiful with a pretty arrangement. However, the vocal reflects no passion and seems more to be read rather than sung from the heart.
Except for the title track, the track most often singled out for praise is the attack on Paul McCartney, "How Do You Sleep". While the song is often cited as being a response to some perceived digs from McCartney on RAM, it should be pointed out that Lennon sniped at McCartney on the PLASTIC ONO BAND lp ("I've seen religion from Jesus to Paul".) As someone pointed out all those years ago, only someone who truly loved another could harbor this much anger when the relationship ends.
"How" harkens back to the POB album, except is sugar coated with strings. After the purging of the previous work, "How" comes across as a piece of self pity.
The closing track, "Oh Yoko" is one of the worst pieces of music/lyrics Lennon ever laid down. What a laugh so many who snipe about McCartney's 'silly love songs' could praise this sugary--and barely listenable-- ode to Ms. Ono.
The sad part is that for all his anger toward McCartney, Paul's input could have probably helped make most of these songs true gems.

If you truly love music to the extent that your life would be irreparably damaged without it, then it can be a fickle mentor. It can send you soaring on an exhilarating high so joyous you heart feels like it could break its fibrous shackles. It can plummet you into such despair there seems little point in carrying on. It can induce frustration, anger, empathy, love, disdain, indifference and laughter in equal measures. Music lives and breathes and, even though Im renowned as a cold fish, music is where I get my emotional fix.

However it is abnormal, perhaps even unique, to receive a shot of every feeling at one sitting but Imagine delivers that shot. The first time I heard "Jealous Guy" I cried. When listening to "How Do You Sleep" I was appalled. It was akin to Lennon spilling the beans on McCartney to the News Of The World. "Oh My Love" is a naive paean to the happiness to be found in a loving relationship. "How?" is a raw cry of pain from a lost soul who appears to have everything yet is both insecure and helpless. And "Imagine", whose familiarity can lead to it being ignored, is a simple prayer for peace whose message has never and will never be bettered.

My first experience of this album was so draining I didnt immediately play it again as would be my normal practice. I simply sat in silence until my sense of equilibrium was restored. Imagine still retains that unsettling quality.
