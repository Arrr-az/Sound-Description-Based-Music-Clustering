The Clash's debut album The Clash was initially released as a british only release, but after being imported in extremely large quantities, it was re-released in the US with some changes. many of the songs were taken out and replaced by others, and the version of white riot is much different than the version found on the British release. The songs that replaced the old ones, i find, were much stronger than the originals. though The Clash may not be as vulgar or agressive as the Sex pistols or many of the other punk bands from that time, they do make some great music. All of these songs come together to make a near perfect album, which, i believe rivals London calling in quality, delivering a bunch of great punk rock songs, with the occaisional reggae track, which make this album really memorable.

The original and not the best. 72.5/100


Too late I discovered that the album I bought was not in fact the original, but the US version with a slightly different track listing. So, soon after, I bought the original UK release online. Listening to the UK version so soon after hearing the US version, I have to say that my initial impression of the UK release was not that positive. Would have given it 3/5. On second listen, however, I was more positive towards it and gave it a score not that far off the US version. My preference is for the US version of this album, but I'm guessing that when people name this album one of the greatest of all time, they have in mind the UK release, which was the main reason I thought I had better listen to it. Probably the main reason I like the US version of this album better is because it has the terrific song "Clash City rockers" on it. In fact, given that I like three songs (two songs on the UK version) on this album more than the Clash's big hits "London calling" and "Rock the Casbah", it's perhaps surprising that this album doesn't score more with me. I would put that down to me not rating the songs I like as amongst the greatest songs of all time. They're all decent punk rock tracks in any case. Anyway, the 1999 release of this album on cd has generally excellent sound quality, but a few songs have unintended distortion. The sound is bassy and features the chalk and cheese vocal stylings of Joe Strummer and...the other guy...who both sing lead or as a team.


Best tracks:


London's burning - catchy laddish/geezer vocals which are hard to understand at times. Emphatic, thumping drums. Backing vocals. Lead guitar work.

Police & thieves - 6:03 minutes long song. Sort of familiar intro. Laid back track. A bit reggae. Bassy...double bass features? You do get two different bass sounds at times. Has repetitive lyrics. Effete backing vocals (i.e. the other guy who sings in the band). Studio effects are utilised.



Next best tracks:


Career opportunities - catchy chorus bit which has some thrashy elements to it (think Metallica's "Battery" for a comparison). You get some "Oi!"s thrown in at times. Not sure if that qualifies this song as "Oi!" punk though! Bassy, with good emphatic drum parts.

What's my name - nice guitar and drum intro to this song. Song has an interesting tone to it. Nice and bassy. Vocals are rhythmic and backing vocals are effete...combination works well. Vocals and drums are catchy. Sounds sort of familiar at times.


Best of the rest:


48 hours - not on the US version of this album. intro a bit different...not punk. Seems to me that this song references musically the classic 1960s Australian song "Friday on my mind" by The Easybeats. Has backing vocals, and the main vocal was catchier second time around. Nice and bassy track...featuring the double bass? Lead guitar has a 1950s rock'n'roll vibe to it. Lyrics are hard to understand. Joe strummer was apparently notorious on this front.

Cheat - not on the US version of this album. Perhaps this was left off the US release due to the "f" word being used? Bassy, with spacey effects at times. Lead guitar not bad near the end...has an interesting tone to it as well. Song has some drug references too, I think. There seems to be a glitch on one syllable 24 seconds in.

Janie Jones - interesting drum intro. Some humour in the lyrics. A good strong bass line in the left channel. Grinding guitar on the right hand channel. Sort of catchy.

Remote control - nice and bassy tracky. Effete pop vocal style. Backing vocals. Frequent unintentional distortion. Vocal melody is sort of catchy.

Deny - not on the US version of this album. Lacks the classic guitar sound but has some nice ethereal lead guitar note picking (playing a nice-ish melody). Vocal stylings reminiscent of the Ramones at times.


The rest (in track order):


I'm so bored with the USA - guitar intro reminds me of The Sex Pistols..."Liar"? Nice and bassy track. Has backing vocals.

White riot - energetic, laddish punk song with guitar lead guitar work, including solo. Has backing vocals. I think that this song is about being afraid to exercise mob power...perhaps mob power is viewed as a good thing, sometimes?

Hate & war - mid-tempo song with effete pop vocals. Backing vocals are more manly sounding. Some unintentional distortion.

Protex blue - the other guy sings lead here. Intro is sort of thrashy sounding at times. Lead guitar has a 1950s rock'n'roll vibe to it. Guitar and drums have a different tone to them. Vocals have a rock'n'roll vibe to them as well.

Garageland - riff at the start is The Rolling Stone's "Brown sugar". Chalk and cheese vocal pairing. Harmonica features. Nice thumping two beats on the drums, a la "Baby it's you" by Promises. I like the rhythm guitar outro...the last 45 seconds or so...reminds me of another song, which I just can't place. Good lyric in "The truth is only known by guttersnipes".



Recommendations:



The Clash - London calling; Combat rock; Sandanista! These three albums see The Clash move beyond punk and get a bit more arty and experimental.

Wire - Pink flag; Chairs missing. If you like arty punk, this band's first two albums are really good examples of it...as opposed, in my view, to Television's "Marquee moon", which does have its fans though. Don't really view that album as punk though. Wire's 3rd album, "154" is their best but isn't punk at all.

The Saints - (I'm) Stranded; Eternally yours. Debut is The seminal punk album...they invented the genre...along with Ramones. Follow up album has the greatest punk song ever, in "Know your product", as well as many cool, slower songs. "Prehistoric sounds" is good post-punk too.

Stiff Little Fingers - Inflammable material. Along with Eternally Yours, this is one of only two 5/5 punk albums for me.

Radio Birdman - Radios appear. Pioneering punk from Australia, by Saints contemporaries. Has some jazzy and jammy tracks...a Stooges influence there.

Johnny Thunders & The Heartbreakers - L.A.M.F. Good ol' fashioned punk rock, from one of the earliest practioners of it in the US.

New York Dolls - New York Dolls - proto-punk to punk-ish, at times. Johnny Thunders formed The Heartbreakers after this band stopped.

* The Clash - Give 'em enough rope. Not a fan of this album, but it's their second and last punk album. Found it pretty 'meh'. The best song on it is "Guns on the roof" which basically recycles "Clash City rockers"...if you get the US version of "The Clash", which has that song, then I don't really see there being any point to buying this album.

I am one of the few remaining listeners who is more of a Sex Pistols guy than a Clash guy. As a result, I've been a bit rough on them over the years. As I've gotten older, I'm still not convinced London Calling is that good of an album, but their other work is generally solid (save for parts of the overlong but adventurous Sandinista! and the forgettable Cut the Crap). Their first album, while far from being their most interesting one, is definitely their best. The Clash packs quite a punch at the beginning, featuring at least four of the best punk songs ever made ("Janie Jones," "Remote Control," "I'm So Bored with the U.S.A.," and the best available version of "White Riot"). It is hard to sustain this kind of brilliance though. "Deny," "London's Burning," "Cheat," "Protex Blue," and "48 Hours" are punk-by-numbers cuts. There are a few remaining highlights, however: "Career Opportunities," which foreshadows Oi!, "Police & Thieves," the best of their many (subsequent) stabs at reggae, and the closer, "Garageland," which is probably the most sentimental track on the record (and this is to be taken as a compliment!). So, it's a bit uneven, but the aforementioned highlights result in some of the best music to come out of the '77 punk movement.

This is one addictive album. From the moment I bought The Clash's self titled debut album (US version), I have never stopped playing it. All of the songs present are of incredible quality in their own little ways, be it great guitar riffs (the obvious example of that being "Clash City Rockers", and many others as well), excellent basslines ("Police & Thieves", anybody?), making cover songs their own ("I Fought the Law" and "Police & Thieves") or just the ferocious, pounding sound of the songs. Another highlight is the reggae influenced "White Man in Hamersmith Palais", which shows The Clash starting to branch out into other styles, which set them apart from all the other big punk acts.

Again, I love all of these songs, but here are the ones I think are the best:
"Clash City Rockers"
"I'm So Bored With the U.S.A."
"Complete Control"
"White Riot"
"White Man in Hamersmith Palais"
"London's Burning"
"I Fought the Law"
"Janie Jones"
"Career Opportunities" (probably my favorite)
"What's My Name"
"Hate & War"
"Police & Thieves"
"Garageland"

See? Thirteen out of fifteen are absolutely perfect, and the other two ("Remote Control" and "Jail Guitar Doors") aren't far off either. I just love this album. I haven't heard many punk rock albums, but this is easily the best one I've heard. Job well done Clash.

One of the best debut albums of all time that captures the ambience and attitude of the times in which it was created. The Clash went on to bigger, more succesful projects but this remains at the heart of what made them what they were. This is an angy, prolonged diatribe from street level. Inspired partially by the Pistols, reggae and life in highrises and squats, this is folk music at its source. There were other records from the same period that came close to this but nothing that really surpassed its anger or lyrical venom. By the time of its release the punk scene in london was already starting to fold in on itself, but this album remains as a powerful statement of intent and a war cry that will live longer than anything else the band would come to record- even the mighty London Calling suffers from its lumbering size and somewhat overstated ambition when compared to this one.

While _London Calling_ is often hailed as the finest effort by these guys, my tastes has always made me favor their debut above the rest. It really stands out as the best representation of what the Clash were capable of and why, at one point, they were considered the only band that mattered. Unlike _London Calling_, there isn't a single bad song within the bunch. On this particular release (US version - which in my opinion is a more improved edition of the UK release) you get punk classics such as: "Clash City Rockers," "I'm So Bored With the USA," "White Man in Hammersmith Palais" (which just might be my favorite Clash song), "London's Burning," "I Fought the Law," "Janie Jones," and so on. Every last song is a stone cold classic and it just sounds so exciting no matter how many times it has been played. This is about as good as it get's when referring to punk rock and it has my vote as the finest from the "Brit-punk" era. Actually, make that from any era.
