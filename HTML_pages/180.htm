Post-punk, much like the original punk movement, is frequently described as a musical revolution. Such language correctly identifies the sudden emergence of early standard-bearers like Wire and Joy Division, while emphasizing the radical aesthetic reorientation spearheaded by those bands. Yet distinctly unlike first-wave punk, much of the mystique surrounding post-punk appears to have been retroactively applied. In particular, there's a large number of quintessentially post-punkish artists who required a couple of years to "figure out" their trademark sound. This is was certainly the case with the Cure, whose 1979's debut (Three Imaginary Boys) was relatively successful but has aged rather poorly. Enter Seventeen Seconds- the 1980 sophomore release that set the table for the remainder of the band's career. A stone-cold classic who mixture of atmospheric sounds epitomized the rapidly-maturing nature of post-punk during the early-80's.

Seventeen Seconds was one of the first gothic rock records to achieve mainstream success. In my mind, it was also the single most important release in the development of melodic gothic rock, as well as a key forerunner of both Coldwave and Darkwave. A true touchstone of its era. The album's overall sound presents a surprisingly stripped-down variation of the classic Cure formula, almost entirely ditching the bouncier New Wave-isms of Three Imaginary Boys. In particular, Robert Smith inaugurates the depressive crooning that would become his primary calling card. The difference here is that most of Smith's vocal melodies feel downright supplementary, with the album placing a consistent emphasis upon echoing grooves and piercing guitar lines. "Cold" is the first word that comes to mind when describing the overall production style. There are also a handful of tracks that descend into experimental soundscaping ("A Reflection", "Three"), whereas other tracks have a distinct Minimal Synth vibe ("In Your House", "At Night"). On the other hand, what Seventeen Seconds entirely avoids is noisy walls-of-sound, placing it in direct opposition to 1982's Pornography. An "anti-Pornography" that is still immediately identifiable as the work of the same band.

I'm obviously a huge fan of Seventeen Seconds. That's fairly common among lovers of classic post-punk, but I appear to be an even higher opinion of the record than your typical Cure fan. In fact, I'd rank the record as the second-best in the entire Cure catalogue, behind only Disintegration and ahead of its (marginally) most acclaimed successors, Faith and Pornography. What I really like about Seventeen Seconds is how the record marries hypnotic arrangements with melodies that are catchy in spite of their impressionistic nature. I also appreciate the record's sparse arrangements, which grant the music a distinctly "arty" vibe. The bottom line is that, if you prefer the atmospheric, slower-burning side of the Cure but still prefer a healthy dose of hooks, Seventeen Seconds is likely to become a personal favorite. Alternatively, lovers of claustrophobic, guitar-driven gothic rock may find the record to be frustratingly slight, and those listeners will probably have a much better time with Pornography. I'd still be surprised if a genuine fan of Pornography was outright disappointed by the release.

On a track-by-track basis, Seventeen Seconds is also one of the best albums in the Cure discography. An impressive feet, seeing as the record was obviously constructed as a cohesive whole, with only one promoted single ("A Forest") and zero tracks that compromise in the name of mainstream acceptability. I'm particularly impressed by the record's back half, which rivals side-A of Disintegration as my favorite side off any Cure release. Included here is the aforementioned "A Forest"- an effective mix of haunting ambience and an almost Krautrock-esque groove. "Seventeen Seconds" is another personal favorite, with a soaring climax that is arguably the catchiest moment from the band's entire "Gothic rock trilogy". The rest of Seventeen Seconds is loaded with excellent deep cuts that should appeal to any fan of gothic post-punk: "Play for Today", "Secrets", "In Your House", "M", and "At Night" all fall into this category. That leaves the album's more adventurous instrumental cuts, which could be dismissed as filler but are short enough that they don't detract from the overall flow.

All things considered, my only significant complaint about Seventeen Seconds is that it runs fairly short, barely breaking the 35-minute mark. I'm still happy to trade lots of content for consistent excellence, and I never feel dissatisfied when the record comes to a close. Seventeen Seconds is essential listening for any fan of early-80's post-punk and gothic rock. It's also the early Cure album that I recommend to newcomers of its era: Disintegration and Wish may be better starting points for the band overall, but if you're already familiar with "peak", pop-era Cure and are looking to explore their earlier years, Seventeen Seconds is a relatively accessible entry point.

8.9/10

 ArtificialEnergy Apr 30 2013
+3 Vote downBookmarkVote up
So, entering the 1980s, just two albums and as many years into their recording career, this is the album where The Cure are commonly said to have arrived and established themselves - a new line-up including a keyboardist and more creative control, resulting in a wholly new, fully formed and instantly identifiable sound, that no matter how many stylistic twists and turns the band would go through for the rest of their career, would imbue everything they made, for better and for worse (you decide!). Gone are all traces of their punk and the more poppier aspects of their New Wave roots; Joy Division, Siouxshie and the Banshees and post-Pink Flag Wire are the paramount influences, yet all seamlessly fused together into a very personal and groundbreaking style.

And what are the vital ingredients that make up this new sound? Firstly, purely on a songwriting basis, there's a radical shift in aesthetic and intent, musically as well as lyrically; the emphasis is now more on atmosphere and creating an artistic and unified album-length statement rather than a succession of commercially friendly hooks (not that that many of these songs lack memorability, but that's done to the band's own innate musicality, rather than their desire to produce a big seller). While apart from A Forest and At Night, the songs aren't that much longer than their Three Imaginary Boys counterparts (the longest on that album was Another Day at 3:44, and none of the others on Seventeen Seconds much exceed four minutes), their more expansive and multi-layered sound and the inclination towards slower tempos, gives it a greater depth and a visionary suspension of time and space much belied by their modest track times (lest we forget, this is still post-punk we're talking about, and not prog rock!). The songs continue the darker and more minimalist lyric writing of its predecessor, this time pinning it to a more vague, melancholy and more predominantly introspective mood; often the lyrics are more like vignettes, enigmatic and half-finished sketches, subservient to the music rather than the other way round as is usually the case in popular music. And sonically, this album exemplifies the band's remarkable knack of suggesting so much with so little - few actual chords are used, with the harmonies implied rather than overtly stated. The arrangements stick to essentially the same formula, with subtle and ingenious variants of course - a backing track of drums, bass, keyboard and guitar, with vocals, overdubs (like an additional guitar line), reverb and various other effects added on top. The guitars and keyboards play mostly single lines, with any chords restricted on the guitar to little more than bare fourths and fifths. (With Matthieu Hartley's predisposition towards complexity and relative distaste for depressed and sombre music, it's no wonder that he and Smith didn't get on for long, and Smith found himself looking for yet another replacement after this album's release.) Thus the emphasis is primarily on rhythm, timbre, studio effects that are integral to the music rather than being flash over substance, instrumental beauty and vocal expressivity and on this album's best songs it all combines remarkably well, being often disturbing, dark and gloomy, yet also imaginative, haunting, highly compelling and never overly depressing.

But, there's something about this album that doesn't quite make it come together as a whole. The presence of three instrumentals that still only push the album's length to 35 minutes are the chief culprit, giving an impression of padding and a slightly rushed feel to it, which is perhaps not surprising given that with budgetary restraints, the band needed to work intensively for sixteeen to seventeen hours a day over just a week to complete the album - more time and more sleep would definitely have benefited. And while A Reflection makes for an effectively stark opener, with the spacious eery eastern-flavoured guitar and synthesized piano melody succinctly setting the downbeat mood that pervades the album, the more obvious Three and The Final Sound in their attempts to provide a spooky atmosphere define route of the least resistance writing. The ticktock interplay between chiming high and and resounding low keyboard notes interspersed with the fuzzy electric guitar is undeniably inventive in itself, but little is done with it as it just repeats itself over a robotic drum beat, transposed up and down a semitone. The Final Sound, if intended as an intro to A Forest, fails in its mission, being a totally unrelated atonal random assortment of pitches that sounds like an unintentional horror B-movie soundtrack parody - the problem is well documented that the band ran out of tape while recording it, so the 52 seconds we have here is all they were able to put on record, meaning that no time is given for it to develop as we hear it, yet at even less than a minute it feels like a waste of tape. Finally, but thankfully dealing with the negatives for the last time, the limp title track makes for a really anticlimactic and inconsequential closer, with no melody to speak of over the same two chords repeated throughout, where for the first time in their vocal numbers the band stumble over the always flimsy dividing line between austere yet captivating to merely dull and charmless introspection.

But the quality of the rest is very high, ranging from very good to simply outstanding. The best of it all by some distance is The Cure's breakthrough single, the astonishing A Forest, which yielded their first video and got to #31 on the UK chart. Edgy, daring, frightening, yet totally thrilling and extremely catchy and danceable, it practically defines the genre gothic rock in the best possible sense (even if it's a term that Smith himself despised), and is as quintessential Cure as other classic album singles like In Between Days, Just Like Heaven, Pictures of You and Fascination Street. Anyone who's still only familiar with this song through the more techno-styled Mixed Up reincarnation (which apart from having more synthesizer layers and reverb, is essentially unchanged) will be astounded to know how far the original and, needless to say, the definitive version, dates back to in The Cure's career and just how many years ahead of its time it is. This was the song that the band spent the longest out of their limited studio time on, and this is evident from its incredible attention to detail - the echoed pitch black long synthesizer lines depicting the scariness of the forest at night, the hallucinatory and unsettling chimes of Smith's flanged guitar, the obsessive rhythm section evoking a sweaty rush through the trees, the startling and evil harmonic progressions of the bridge, Smith's powerfully emotive vocals which rise from a moderately stated desperation to neurotic despair, climaxing in the unforgettable looped "again and again", its haunting coda where just the bass guitar has the final word, like the sound of distant footsteps, and many more to which a single paragraph like this can scarcely do justice.

Following A Reflection, the relatively uptempo Play for Today announces the new Cure in earnest, with its characteristically bright, melodic yet brooding lead guitar line and warm synthesizer counter-melody offset by Gallup's propulsive bass and the crisp attack of Tolhurst's drumming. Lyrically, while this song comes closest to the more conventional themes of Three Imaginary Boys, with its reflections on romantic ennui, the musical treatment is quite different with the lyrics only occupying a comparatively small portion of the song, allowing the mesmerizing instrumental sounds and slowly moving harmonies to envelop the listener - the increasingly melancholy and slowing down of the coda reinforces the impression, in case it wasn't already obvious, that this is a much deeper and more serious album than its predecessor, but a rewarding one if the listener can meet it on his or her own terms. Secrets, with its lower and still more pensive guitar lines, carries on this introspection, accentuated by Smith's faraway barely audible double-tracked vocals, with some strikingly beautiful washes of keyboard to prevent it from lapsing into monotony. The best track on side one, In Your House, which combines the slow tempo of Secrets with the more extrovert nature of Play for Today, achieves its emotional impact chiefly through its memorably ethereal horn-like, almost vocal sounding, synthesizer line, elaborately crafted and constantly arresting bass, wonderfully spacey, grandiose and never intrusive keyboard effects, and poignant chord changes (particularly the spine-tingling one that occurs just after a minute in and near the end). All in all, a very solid beginning, which sadly couldn't be sustained, but there's plenty more fine stuff to come after the central hiccup, which isn't just restricted to the magnificence of A Forest...

...which includes the second best track on the album, At Night, which could be seen as a more reflective but no less disturbing and gripping sequel to A Forest, while simultaneously being a more mature successor lyrically to the song Three Imaginary Boys, portraying the same night time fears as the former and the inherent if often imagined dangers of night of the latter, juxtaposed with the supposed safety of the indoors; alternatively the night outside could be a metaphor for Smith's troubled state of mind, with the peaceful sleep representing his girlfriend's blissful obliviousness to his acute anxiety. The heavily accented snare, menacingly dissonant and driving combination of fuzz bass and flanged rhythm guitar, another memorable synthesizer hook, quiet yet majestic, and Smith's paranoid but muffled vocals, like a desperate plea for help falling on deaf ears, all contribute to another mini-masterpiece. The more straightforward M, in a more similar vein to Play for Today, while not among the best, serves its purpose nicely in providing a relaxation in intensity between its more feverish songs either side, while maintaining the sombre yet resonant feel.

So all in all, while the band can't be said to be quite the finished article in terms of marrying a new style to a wholly coherent and consistent LP, there's much to be excited and moved by here, and if not essential to Cure beginners, is still strongly recommended for those, especially avid post-punk fans, who are only familiar with their output starting with or succeeding The Head on the Door. They would undoubtedly get better from here, but the first significant and truly promising steps had been taken with this release. Just short of a 4/5

Somewhere along the way, Robert Smith decided he was a morose son of a bitch with a knack for articulating it. Whether it is in lyrical form or musical, Smith has found his way into our grey hearts through song. The Cure, known for their bleak and dark sound and lyrical content, weren't necessarily The Cure on the first release. In the span of a year, The Cure changed their sound entirely for the sophomoric release, Seventeen Seconds.

The first few notes of the record foreshadow the future that The Cure would build for themselves. "A Reflection" is just that, reflecting on the woes of life without the aid of words. "Play for Today" is a good introduction into the new Cure sound; a giddy, yet dark, Post-Punk cut, the song is accentuated by Smith's pained vocals and the gloominess of the arrangements. Its energy is only matched by its pristine musicianship. "Secrets" slows down the album's tempo, but this is a good thing. The song offers a nice build-up, with a gorgeous bass track and irresistible guitar manipulations; throw in a little piano for added dramatic effect, it is the Cure's calling card, after all. (Adding dramatic effect.)

Most songs on the album seem so very familiar, not because we may have heard them before, but because The Cure's influence is simply too omnipresent in contemporary artists. A song like "In Your House" brings to mind a sound that could be associated with Nirvana. Smith dredges along, laying down a vocal track that lingers in its misery in the background, while the gorgeous guitar track carries us across the misery sea. "Three" showcases the band experimenting in the studio, adding a layer to their sound by sampling studio sounds and adding it at the beginning of the track. The track then devolved into a drab cut worth noting for its dark and brooding sound, akin to that found in Pornography, although sounding callow in comparison to the rich sound in the aforementioned album. A great way to end the A side.

On the flip, we have "The Final Sound," yet another dark and scary track, sounding like a haunted carnival ride, wallowing in its destitute. Sadly, due to a limited budget, the band wasn't able to lay down the complete track on tape as it ran out and there wasn't anytime to rerecord it. However, I feel it adds to the album's bleak feeling. There is something so telling in the band deciding to put an incomplete track on a record, as if they accept their own incompleteness."A Forest" is without a doubt the album's magnum opus. Already here we can clearly here the true Cure sound which would permeate all their future releases. The guitar once again takes center stage, leaving a dirge in its wake. The bleakness is only matched by the song's overall unadulterated energy. Clocking in at 6 minutes, the band experiments and dilly-dallies around with a perfect Post-Punk pitch. It is classic Cure at its finest.

"M" once again adds another memorable guitar track from the album. Perhaps the word to best describe the sound in "At Night" would be askew; it adheres to Post-Punk ideals, creating a working song out of unmelodious elements. For fans of the band, this is nothing new, but in 1980, it was new to the band. Once again we have the title track for the closer. "Seventeen Seconds" offers no new ground to tread, once again relying on a good guitar track to stick to, although the bass track may be more prominent. Nonetheless, it succeeds in concluding the dark album by adding its own tinge of darkness to the mix.

Seventeen Seconds is of course widely seen as the turning point in The Cure's famed history. It is one of three of the first trilogy within the band's discography (along with Faith and Pornography.) And like in any successful trilogy, Seventeen Seconds manages to lay the story down while presenting a sound exclusive to this record, although slightly altered in the next. A true Post-Punk classic, Seventeen Seconds should be heard by all fans of the genre. Even without all these facets taken into consideration, the album is a huge step-up from the lackadaisical debut, professing the musical maturity of those three imaginary boys.

This is the point where the Cure went from being a miserable pop band to being just all round miserable bastards.
