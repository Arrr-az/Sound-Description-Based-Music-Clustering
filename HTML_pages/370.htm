They're dirtbags, they're creeps, they're dangerous, they're sad and they're mad—just look at those scowls on the front (and back) cover—but if you like mean and noisy rock, few bands are more huggable than The Stooges. Listen to this album in the middle of a lot of 60s garage rock and, as sweet as all that Nuggets and Pebbles fare is, The Stooges instantly sound more real than most of it. This LP sounds like the culmination of it all. It's one of the best influences in rock and it grooves brutally from start to finish. Not even the ten-minute pagan pothead chant "We Will Fall" hurts it too badly. It's a nice comedown from the face-pummeling openers.

Well it's 1969 okay
All across the USA
It's another year
For me and you
Another year
With nothing to do...

1969... in the aftermath of the Detroit riots of '67, and the Tigers hard fought '68 World Series seven game test versus the St. Louis Cardinals, the Motor City became one mean fucking metro area. The American epicenter for rubber burnin' street muscle also became known as the murder capitol of the states. Far removed from the laid back, flowers-in-their-hair California scene, Detroit was a cracked concrete battleground, where bad ass guitar distortion provided the soundtrack for a night of molotov cocktails and looting.

With a tough as nails reputation, piston-poundin' Detroit was all about survival of the fittest. There wasn't a peace symbol spray painted within miles of the mighty Motor City. The Stooges, bent on self-destruction, drugs and decadence, debuted at the end of the wartorn '60s with the release of their self-titled album, pushing rustbelt garage rock with a punk attitude to a new high, or low... it's all based on your own personal take of what rock 'n' fuckin' roll is all about.

While Woodstock played out over several mud-caked and dazed days/nights in upstate New York during the hot summer of '69, and the leather clad Hells Angels took over Altamont in Northern California, assembly-line Detroit was blowin' gaskets as Iggy and the boys banged out chemical-induced raw rawk with wah-wah fills, with a no-future vibe. Suck on that belchin' rusted tailpipe, West coast hippies!

Aptly introduced by the four-minute "1969", the loose eight song recording peaks with the bitchin' "I Wanna Be Your Dog", while adding "No Fun", and the short, but to-the-point "Not Right". No points awarded for the ten-minute-plus "We Will Fall" buzzkill.

Primal, The Stooges is a reflection of the apocalyptic surroundings that was smokestack Detroit, Michigan, in '69. If you can't stand the heat, get out of the fuckin' kitchen.

KNOCK IT BACK!

"We Will Fall" gets such a bad rap. It's a shame because I think it's the best song on here and it just might sum up what the early un-recorded Psychedelic Stooges were all about; fucking with people. Fucking with their audience that was more than likely made up of mostly hippy college students there to groove on Ten Years After or whoever the band were opening up for at the Grande Ballroom. People coming to a show looking to have a good time and 'kick back' with a few joints and a dirty leather satchel of Boones' Farm Strawberry Hill and The Stooges come out and wreck that good time by having a band play one note over and over at a deafening volume while the 'drummer' bangs out a rudimentary Bo Diddley beat on 50-gallon oil drums and a 'singer' who spazzes out all over the stage, spits on them, hits them, antagonizes them and stares them down and leaves them confused, angry and downright upset for what they have just gone through while waiting to see the latest re-tread of the 'blooze' from England. That's why I love "We Will Fall". Because it fucks with you and it no doubt confuses you and upsets the order that the already fucked-up album has laid down.


(not to mention that the idea of Dave Alexander, Scott &Ron Asheton and Iggy having a sit-down strike in the studio and that being what brought about "We Will Fall"' in the first place, is hilarious)

atomicWedgie May 04 2008
+3 Vote downBookmarkVote up
I'm not sure where to even begin with The Stooges. So, let's begin at the beginning.

This album came out in 1969, the hangover of the summer of love. The sixties were coming to an end. People were still wearing flowers in their hair, but the trips were turning bad. The Beatles were falling apart. The Rolling Stones were finding their groove. Led Zeppelin was on the rise. Bob Dylan was in seclusion in New York. The Stooges were in Michigan creating chaos.

The sound the Stooges were producing was fairly unique at the time. It had an extremely hard edge to it. There's a harshness of tone that the Stooges shared with their fellow Michigan scenesters MC5, but where the Motor City Five were angry and political, the Stooges were more primal, more carnal. Today, the sound of some of the tracks on their first album The Stooges is recognizably punk, but there was no such thing as punk rock yet. "1969" is a great example:
It's another year for me and you,
another year with nothing to do
That's a punk attitude. I can't think of another example like it from the sixties. That's classic Repo Man attitude, but way ahead of it's time. "Now I Wanna Be Your Dog", well, I mean . . . you know? One of the greatest punk songs of all time except, of course, there was no such thing yet.

As quintessentially punk rock as "Now I Wanna Be Your Dog" and "No Fun" are, some of the tracks on The Stooges are firmly rooted in the sixties, and more specifically in the shadow of Jim Morrison and The Doors. Iggy Pop's persona at the time was that of a less articulate, more dangerous version of Jim Morrison that was turned up to eleven. The ten minute closer to side one, "We Will Fall", explores the same dark open spaces that the Doors explored in "The End". Iggy delivers the same type of harrowing performance that Morrison did but without a lot of the poetic imagery.

The Stooges is a great album. It's absolutely essential for anyone trying to come to some kind of understanding about rock in the sixties, seventies and eighties.

The Stooges debut is a simple, direct, dark record with a juvenile delinquent's swagger and pure nihilistic abandon. In opening track "1969", Iggy sings about boredom and dread...this ain't no flower-power hippy shit. There's no flashy musicianship here, most songs are two or three chords played over and over, but the lack of complexity is implicit to this record's appeal; if you are looking for simple music that transcends it's limitations by creating a sonic atmosphere that is completely unique, The Stooges debut has it in spades - this is a seminal punk record to say the least. "I Wanna Be Your Dog" is perhaps the albums highlight. The Doors-ish "Ann" is great too, and the album's closing track "Little Doll" ends in an incredible howl of wah-wah, some of the best wah ever recorded, it just sounds so....apocalyptic. The dirge-like "We Will Fall" is a distraction, but I happen to love it - the albums producer, John Cale of VU fame, plays his droning viola whilst the band chants a menacing mantra. This is THE soundtrack to the death of the sixties.....
