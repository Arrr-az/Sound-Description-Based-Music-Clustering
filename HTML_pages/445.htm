There are only a few albums for which I cannot understand the mass appeal. Colossal Youth is one of them. The music is very minimal with simple baselines, muffled guitars, and synthesizers backing a dull female voice. Each instrument is perfectly audible yet the overall sound seems empty and vacant.

For the most part, I agree with the RYM crowd, but not here. I find it hard to feel any sort of emotion while listening to this. I know it’s minimalism, but I have felt emotion bursting out of other minimalist music. Is it originality and influence that make it as great as most say? Possibly. I’ll try this one again in the future. Maybe I’ll eventually get it.

I also own a vinyl pressing of this, a U.S. Rough Trade press. The only area where the Domino CD issue pales is that the front cover is too dark on this 3CD set. You can clearly see Alison Statton's hair, and all three members' visages plainly articulated on the vinyl copy.

Colossal Youth is a landmark album from the post-punk era, circa 1980. It's certainly minimal, yet retains its charms and can fascinate and enchant the listener after the umpteenth time listening. The instruments credited are voice, bass, guitar and organ, yet the fifth element imperative to the proceedings is all of the negative space between the notes. (A quick aside, am I the only one that gets completely sucked into the Bruce Willis/Milla Jojovich/Gary Oldman/Chris Tucker movie whenever it comes on cable? It's captivating in a different way, namely by being goofy. Back to our story).

The Collected Works therein accumulate the Testcard EP, the "Final Day" single, "Ode to Booker T" from the compilation Is the War Over?, scads of demos and a Peel Session. The discs come packaged neatly in black and white sleeves that slide into a glossy cardboard box. The set also includes extensive liner notes from British music critic Simon Reynolds, who penned the indispensable Rip It Up and Start Again, an exceptional touch stone covering post-punk and new pop from 1978 - 1984.

I think it's imperative to point out that anything that influences Hole (which covered "Credit in the Straight World" when Courtney Love wasn't considered a complete joke), Pants Yell! and the xx (for the aforementioned negative space + monochromatic style) is a authoritative document that requires your attention. If you want further proof that it's something special that everyone should at least peek in at, I am the third different RYM user to review a version of this album today. Any album that would compel three different people to review it thirty years after it was released (and not be something facile like a Led Zeppelin album) again is something unique and laudatory.

 Grampus Nov 14 2008

It must be twenty years ago since I first heard Colossal Youth by Young Marble Giants. I borrowed the album from a mate, fell in love with it and have spent the time in between trying to search out a copy. Okay, I may not have been trying too hard, but hunting down Colossal Youth was not as easy as you'd think. The problem was, by the time I got around to hearing the album, the band had been defunct for seven years.

The trio consisting of brothers Stuart and Phillip Moxham and vocalist Alison Statton formed in Cardiff at the back end of 1978. They arrived out of nowhere, blew the socks off the majority of music journalists, recorded an intensely minimalistic album, were touted as one of the best new bands by the New Musical Express and fell apart amidst a flurry of bad feelings and irreconcilable differences. By early 1981 they had returned to relative obscurity leaving behind a small but perfectly formed body of work.

The liner notes accompanying the album claim, because Young Marble Giants didn't hang around for long, they were unable to sully their work with inferior product. There may be a kernel of truth in that but it does tend to detract from the fact what makes the statement true is the material they did produce had to be damn near perfect to begin with.

Where to begin describing the music of Young Marble Giants? There may be something clichéd and ho-hum about the statement but Colossal Youth is verging on the unique. Stepping outside music, imagine a wireframe designed to be the foundation on which layers of papier-mâché will be pasted to create a landscape. The music of Colossal Youth is the wireframe. It's as if the band went into the studio, laid down the most basic of backing tracks, had an initial stab at a vocal and then made the astonishing decision that that was enough.

The music is beyond minimal; at times just a few clipped notes on the guitar, a muted beat and a wispy vocal. The whole thing could be blown into oblivion by an interloping tinkle of a triangle. It's incredible how so much substance and depth can be drawn from something so skeletal. There isn't a wasted note on the whole album because there simply aren't enough notes to waste.

The band eventually folded due to a combination of the breakdown of the personal relationship between Phillip Moxham and Statton and the fact Stuart Moxham – the principal songwriter – had always wanted to sing his own material but had been persuaded by his brother to let Statton join. Eventually the cracks became too divisive to ignore and the Young Marble Giants went their separate ways.

In terms of this release, Domino have done a fabulous job. Young Marble Giant's output is spread over three disks with Colossal Youth retained in its pristine state on the first of these. CD2 collects together various EPs and alternate takes, while the third CD contains the Giants' only session for John Peel. It's a memorable document of a band whose star shone very brightly very quickly before just as swiftly burning itself out. Young Marble Giants were a band who exemplified the saying less is more – in their case a hell of a lot more.

It was only one album, a single, an EP and a John Peel session (all of it is included on the 2007 3-disc reissue of this album) that the Young Marble Giants left behind before they disappeared. But over the years the band's cult following has grown, REM's Peter Buck and Kurt Cobain have both mentioned to have been influenced by this trio from Cardiff. Although I had known their name, but not their music, for years I didn't actually listen to their music until summer 2007 after I'd read a Record Collector article about a one off live performance in Hay-on-Wye and the reissue of this album.

What I heard was something that was completely new to me, it left me stunned. The minimal instrumentation of just guitar, bass and the occasional drum machine and strange organ or synth created a scary yet beautiful mood. On top of all that we have Allison Stratton's voice which reminds me of Maureen Tucker singing on "The murder mystery" and "After hours" on the Velvet underground's untitled 3rd album. Songs like "Include me out", "Eating Noddemix", "N.I.T.A." or "Music for evenings" have melodies that could have come from pop songs. The guitar riffs and bass lines of the brothers Philip and Stuart Moxham sound like the basic structures for an alternative guitar rock song. The organ and synths sounds are not too far removed from Barry Andrews' work with early XTC. But with all the elements put together the finished results doesn't have anything to do with any of this. It really hasn't got anything to do with anything at all. It's so unique and years ahead of its time that anyone listening to this for the first time in 2007 will be just as knocked out by it as anybody listening in 1980 was. A one of a kind, influential minor masterpiece.

That was the original album. This reissue is all that and more. Disc 2 starts with the Testcard EP. Completely instrumental it shows a different side of the band. With no contributions from Allison Statton it features the band as a duo. Some bits even sound happy. The "Final day" single follows, the song itself being one of the absolute highlights of the set. Its B-sides would also be nice additions the album. "Ode to Booker T" is great as well, originally a track from a various artists compilation album. It deserves its place here on a Young Marble Giants release. The Salad days rarities collection completes disc 2. It is probably the set's weakest moment, but still alright. It features tracks from aforementioned releases in demo form and the odd outtake such as the nice "Zebra trucks". Fortunately the set goes out with a bang, disc 3 features a John Peel session featuring several of the band's best songs in excellent renditions. The only problem is that's it's ove after only 12 minutes.

If you missed this band the first time round like I did (I wasn't even born yet, can you really blame me?), be sure to give this album a listen this time. No doubt one of the classic bands from the late 70s/early 80s post punk era they deserve to become a cult favourite like XTC or Joy Division. This all their essential stuff, beautifully repackaged and fortunately easily available again. The only thing I'm missing is the Live at the Hurrah CD/DVD. A bit of live work would have been more than welcome and disc 3 was far from full. Apart from this strange omission a wonderful release. Well done folks.
