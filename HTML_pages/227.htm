Echo And The Bunnymen evolved a lot with Heaven Up Here.

It's evident right from the start, with an impressive showcase of early 80's gloom-rock ("Show Of Strength"), that shifts gears towards the end with an epilogue of sound-effects that "paint" a picture of howling winds splashing against a solemn rhythmic scenery. That is the greatest quality of the Bunnymen here; how they're able to convey not just a mood, but landscapes, pictures, colours within their songs, with just the humble means of the rock-band. At the fore is Will Sergeant, whose guitar is the primary "painting-brush", escalating in stellar crescendos, or crashing to steep cliffs; expanding to vast panoramas, or contracting to brisk snapshots. Closely behind, the vocals of Ian McCulloch represent a mystic in trance that laments for the new-dark-age. The model was the Doors at their most transcendental (circa 1967), updated for the post-punk era.

The music acquired a myriad guises. Funky guitars prevail in "With A Hip", though interposed with an epic melodic phrase, leading to "Over The Wall" (a career-highlight), that's driven by a hypnotic double electronic-rhythm, crashing drums, thunderous guitars, and a dramatic chorus. Midway through the song, a majestic wall of tribal drums and psychedelic guitars raises the speed slightly, while McCulloch sings with the urgency of a clergyman performing a ritual.

Another highlight, "A Promise", basically showcases a monotonous rhythm, which makes it all the more dramatic when Sergeant blasts into a spectral guitar-part in the chorus, raising impressions of a rainbow forming amidst a grey landscape. "Heaven Up Here" contrasts a frenetic acid-rock verse with a subdued and somber chorus, as well as the usual spectral guitar solo.

After so many intense songs, the relaxed atmosphere of "No Dark Things" surprises, but not really. Actually, the song depicts a smooth progression from the relaxed to the neurotic, and from the neurotic again to the relaxed, though slightly depressed in the end. A stunning eloquence. "Turquoise Days" goes one step further, with a shift of tempos and density, though always introspective and somber, preparing the way for the emotional apotheosis of "All I Want".

"Show of Strength" is the sound of demented dreams. Dark and bass-heavy, the sparse guitars and baritone vocals add to the tone of despair. One of Echo and the Bunnymen's best tracks, it's a song I rarely see mentioned when discussing the virtues of this band. As an opening track it sets an incredible tone and raises the expectation for what's to come.

Welcome to Heaven Up Here.

If this album had been released in 1983, there wouldn't be much to say about it. A couple of years make a big difference, though. In 1981, there weren't too many bands putting out records as dark, atmospheric and intelligent as Heaven Up Here.

Side one is solid all the way through. Side two begins great with "The Disease" and "All My Colours", but it's a pretty steep dropoff from there. That's my main complaint about the record, it falls off quickly on the flip side, ending with a dull thud that I rarely hear because by the time I should be getting to the end I've lost interest and moved on.

A weirdly shocking success in England, despite the increasingly dark, depressing and non-commercial songs. "A Promise" is the only track here that sounds like it would have any mass appeal, and would later appear unaccompanied on their compilation Songs To Learn & Sing.

Side One of Heaven Up Here is actually quite strong, especially the primal "Over The Wall," which features the late Pete De Freitas going ape shit on the drums. Ever notice how most great drummers are "late?" I gave the album only three and a half stars because there are too many weak tracks (if only about 3), and not necessarily the slow, dark ones. Also, the album sounds far less consistent than Crocodiles. Still, it is not hard to see why some fans view this as their favorite.

I actually get kind of pissed off that early Echo often doesn't get included in discussions about the best UK post-punk bands or albums. What more were they supposed to do that was within their control? Only the Damned and the Stranglers got this much disrespect.

Within a year the leap from Crocodiles to Heaven Up Here by the Liverpool lads was striking. Their quaint beginnings as Eric's club regulars is being pushed over for newer more psychedelic stylistics. While their sibling band The Teardrop Explodes is nearing their last leg, Echo and The Bunnymen's creative writing streak is on the ascent. A lot of what makes The Bunnymen such a unique specimen begins to manifest on Heaven Up Here. The determined climbing synths on Over The Wall (possibly my favourite) and the frantic guitar riffs of Heaven Up Here. While All My Colours foreshadows what The Bunnymen would do later on records like Ocean Rain.

Not an album people can immediately grapple on, and honestly this isn't a band I can say people can on average immediately grapple onto. But when things sink in it becomes wildly apparent the genius and appeal of Echo. Clever song titles, and even more clever lyricism from McCulloch is maybe slightly paled by Siousxie and the Banshee's music and fame of the time, but it is safe to say that The Bunnymen as a whole or even just Will Sergeant can give Siousxie's lineup (ie John McGeoch) a run for their money when they want.

Insofar as I considered myself a fan of post-punk, my preferences lean toward the moodier and more atmospheric side of the genre. In fact, many of my favorite post-punk albums could just as easily be described as gothic rock, Neo-psychedelia, or dream pop. It then comes as little surprise that my opinion of Echo & the Bunnymen improves as you progress from the early-80's to the mid-80's. More specifically, whereas I'm significantly less impressed by 1980's Crocodiles than your typical Bunnymen fan, my appreciation for (most of) their next four records broadly mirrors consensus. This makes 1981's Heaven Up Here the earliest Bunnymen album that I significantly enjoy. The record might not be an all-time favorite, but it's a quintessential example of melodic post-punk that I can recommend to any fan of the style. Alongside Ocean Rain, one of the two best records in the Bunnymen catalogue.

Heaven Up Here epitomizes the darker side of the Bunnymen's classic sound, nicely complementing the artier and more psychedelic style of Ocean Rain. Overall, the album is equal parts post-punk and early gothic rock, albeit without the noisier aspects of other goth trailblazers like Bauhaus. More specifically, the record ditches the more "conventional", post-punk style of Crocodiles for slower-burning arrangements that emphasize layered soundscapes over propulsive grooves. These changes are especially evident in the guitar-playing of Will Sergeant, who leans into his (then-innovative) fondness for winding, riff-averse lines. If you want to hear a forerunner of the "textural" guitar approach that the Edge perfected with late-80's U2, Heaven Up Here is the place to look. As for the vocal melodies, Ian McCulloch is even more maudlin than usual, with a heavy focus on meandering verses and a lack of discernible choruses. Heaven Up Here is clearly a product of the early-80's, but it's remarkably innovative for 1981.

Although Ocean Rain might feature a stronger collection of tracks, Heaven Up Here has long been my favorite Bunnymen album. It's definitely the first record I return to after a period of time away from the band. Heaven Up Here is the classic example of an album that's greater than the sum of its parts, with a cohesive mood and wonderful flow from track to track. In terms of individual ingredients, I'm particularly impressed by the Sergeant's guitar parts, which are the best of the band's career. McCulloch's vocal performances also represent a big leap forward from Crocodiles. It's amazing how McCulloch can deliver so many memorable lines without resorting to obvious, singalong choruses. That being said, Heaven Up Here still features a handful of tracks where the melody feels oddly supplementary. Every Bunnymen album from the era features cuts that completely fail to move me and, although Heaven Up Here is more consistent than the competition, it's no exception to the rule.

Heaven Up Here is full of impressive tracks, but the stylistic cohesion of the record means that I have difficulty picking individual favorites. In particular, nearly every track from the album's front half is equally great. On the more atmospheric end of the spectrum you have "Show of Strength" and "Over the Wall", whereas "With a Hip" provides the best example of Will Sergeant's proto-Edge-esque guitars and (lead single) "A Promise" should satisfy anyone looking for hypnotic, goth-tinged psychedelia. The B-side of Heaven Up Here is where the band begins to throw in a bit of filler, even if everything apart from "The Disease" is good or better. Easily the strongest track off the album's back half is "Turquoise Days", although the angular "All I Want" may also appeal to devoted fans of Crocodiles. Apart from several tracks that sacrifice melody for mood ("The Disease", "All My Colours", title track), my only major complaint about the track-listing is that all of the least engaging tracks are clustered in the middle third of the album.

Ultimately, Heaven Up Heaven has more than enough great moments to warrant an "excellent", 4.0-star rating, even if it lacks the truly phenomenal tracks necessary to earn anything higher. A great entry point to the Bunnymen catalogue for all listeners. I just wish the band would've stuck with this overall sound for at least one more album, as opposed to the artier (and ultimately less engaging) sound that they would adopt on Porcupine.

7.8/10

Sort of impressed, but sort of underwhelmed. Echo and the Bunnymen are clearly an artist I can vibe with. It’s steadfastly post-punk with soaring, angular guitars all over the joint. Ian McCulloch is either hopefully despairing or despairingly hopeful, depending on your mode of interpretation. And I love that. But perhaps there are other Echo and the Bunnymen albums for me.

“It’s hard to dig it all too happily” goes the line on the rather unspectacular opener Show of Strength, which is indicative of how some of the songs weigh down on my overall experience of the album. I’ve devoted a fair chunk of listening time to get into this one, but it falls fairly flat at times - It Was a Pleasure and Heaven Up Here are the most rambling of the bunch.

But the less compelling tracks are offset by some moments of magic. Over The Wall feels exceptionally realised and like a most organic jam; somehow blending a sort of busy, cheery rhythm against an abrupt, brutally exclaimed chorus, all while guitars twinkle in and out of prominence. Another winner is the “Zimbo” song, All My Colours, it’s intense, tribal feel. recalling Joy Division’s Atmosphere or I Remember Nothing in the best possible way. It’s pretty gorgeous. A Promise offers a fair bit of rapture, while I also love All I Want which has this fabulously assembled groove. It sounds like it would be hard to execute, but Echo and the Bunnymen find a way to make it weirdly resonant, even if it’s a sort of jarring way to end an album on.

There are songs to love and songs to skip in Heaven Up Here. Hits and misses. But Echo and the Bunnymen come out of this commanding further exploration and attention.

Top 3: Over the Wall, All My Colours, All I Want
Weakest 1: Heaven Up Here
