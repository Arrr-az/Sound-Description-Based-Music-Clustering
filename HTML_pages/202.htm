Irritating.

Can't decide what's worse: is it the piss-poor vocals wailing the lyrics that drip with pretension: is it the inept and nonsensical use of strings throughout the album at seemingly random points: or is it the general half-assed nature of the songwriting that makes each track shuffle along with what seems to be the minimum effort possible and keeps interest levels low?

That said, "Useful Chamber" is six-and-a-half minutes of competence: the slow beat and the female backing vocals work in the band's favour here, and the acoustic guitar melody is decent and contrasts well with the bursts of electric guitar. The most positive thing I have to say about the rest of the album is that sometimes it's forgettable rather than annoying.

troutmask Aug 23 2021
+3 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Sometimes I like to think of Bitte Orca as Dirty Projectors' debut. I have no issue with the five (really six since New Attitude isn't that much shorter than the rest of the discography and I'm just gonna start counting EPs of fully new material as part of a band's main discography now anyway) albums that Dave Longstreth released under the Dirty Projectors name prior to this, hell catch me in the right mood and I'll stand up for The Getty Address as one of the most interesting lo-fi projects of the 2000s, but circumstantially it kind of is the first Dirty Projectors album. Up until 2007 the band was really just a Dave Longstreth solo project - he had collaborators but no real consistent collaborators - and while Rise Above is the debut of Dirty Projectors as a band the nature of that album as a tribute to the half remembered version of Black Flag's Damaged in Longstreth's head doesn't really make it feel like their debut. Bitte Orca, however, acts as the first album to be conceived from the beginning as a full band album, with a firm set of collaborators involved throughout the process. That reads more like a debut ot me, and in that light it's probably one of the best debut albums of the 2000s despite the technicality of being the seventh album under this band name.

There's also a big sonic shift that accompanies the transition from solo project to full band that helps to accentuate the idea of this as a new project rather than a continuation. Previous Dirty Projectors albums were defiantly lo-fi and super-insular combinations of math rock guitars, hip-hop beats and Longstreth's...shall we say divisive vocals. Bitte Orca, on the other hand, feels like the closest anyone's come to updating Remain in Light for the then current generation, fusing ideas from African tribal chants, intricate guitar hockets and alternative R 'n' B into a novel strain of art-pop descended from post-punk. I feel like that doesn't quite do the album justice while also making it seem like too much all at once, but...well you try summing up something like "Temecula Sunrise" neatly. Art pop verses punctuated by fragmented guitar breaks, three-part harmony choruses and the sort of seasick meter-fuckery you might expect anywhere but in the context of what is ostensibly a pop song? Or "Stillness Is the Move," the most straightforward cut on the album which sitll throws guitar arpeggios that sound like Vini Reilly on amphetamine, weirdly processed percussion breaks and truly otherworldly vocals into a song so fundamentally R 'n' B that Solange sounded right at home covering it? The songs here are all seemingly untenable combinations of genres that manage to coalesce into great songs on their own and also into an overall sound that manages to sound coherent across the whole album. The degree to which this should not work is off the charts, but even considering my own affinity for albums like this that don't quite pull it off it's truly glorious to see it realized so fully.

The thing is that, for all of my theorizing about this being the true debut of Dirty Projectors this is still an album that is fully in Dave Longstreth's control, and over the years that he'd been recording the dude became something of a savant in the realm of making things like this work. I mean, he pulled off The Getty Address, a concept album about The Eagles, Aztecs and 9/11 (so you know, light material,) so the idea that he'd be able to fuse Talking Heads-style new wave with all the weirdness inherent to the Dirty Projectors brand as it were wasn't too far beyond the realm of possibility. Having a defined band to work with probably helped with this though; I'm going to spend a lot of time on the vocalists here so I feel the need to give bassist Nat Baldwin and drummer Brian McOmber some love right off the bat since they have the not-so-simple task of anchoring songs that are just a second away from going off in a completely different direction, and staying on top of that speaks as much to their skill as a rhythm section as it does to Longstreth's skill as a composer. I think a big part of what made this work as an introduction to the band was Longstreth's decison to share vocal duties with Amber Coffman and Angel Deradoorian, who were present on Rise Above but mostly as background vocalists. Not only does Bitte Orca offer both of them a chance to act as de facto frontwoman for the band - Coffman gets the slinky, R'n'B groove of "Stillness Is the Move" and Deradoorian handles the more downbeat acoustic ballad "Two Doves" - Longstreth takes full advantage of having two other lead vocalists to take his vocal arrangements to a different level. The number of songs on the album that are elevated by some very judicious uses of vocal hocket - a term for the splitting of an single melody between two or more instruments leading to a sort of interplay effect witin a singular melodic idea - in either relatively simple forms (the wordless punctuation for each couplet in "Cannibal Resource") or more complex ones (the bridge of "Remade Horizon" where the three vocalists and separated guitar tracks hocket simultaneously leading to sensory overload in my math-rock pleasure centres,) and having a ready made vector for three-part harmony at all times allows for even more depth to the vocal arrangements. It's also worth noting that Coffman and Deradoorian are just great singers in general; neither are virtuosos but within their respective ranges they know how to work wonders. I'm thinking of the way that Coffman weaponizes the vulnerability in her upper range during the wordless suffix to the chorus on "Stillness Is the Move" or the huskiness of Deradoorian lending more of a 60s vibe to "Two Doves." Having them around also lessens the impact of Longstreth's aforementioned divisive vocals; he's still front and center for most of the album but he's not the only vocalist on hand and even if you've become attumed to his quirks as a singer (and as someone who has heard most of the stuff made back when DP wasa solo project I was definitely attuned to those quirks) it's nice to have other, more traditional vocalists sharing the spotlight.

It's remarkable that Longstreth and co. managed to make it all work so well on their first official go 'round as a firm unit. The balance they strike between truly off-the-wall elements and a firm base in some strain of pop music without selling either end of that spectrum short is a remarkable achievement that I wish they'd been able to replicate again (I have some level of affection for Swing Lo Magellan that is 100% not tied to seeing them live on that tour, but it's very decidedly a step down and while I'm skeptical that Lamp Lit Prose is actually as bad as its rating here makes it out to be - the recent backlash towards any band that was big in the indie sphere around the mid-to-late 00sseems more performative than anything - given how little I got out of their s/t it's not exactly a high priority to investigate that fully,) Somehow, David Longstreth managed to make a pop album that was equal parts addictive and confounding, equal parts beautiful and jagged, equal parts Talking Heads and Richard Bishop and somehow that combination worked for enough people in 2009 that Dirty Projectors was, to the best of my recollaection, second only to AnimalCollective in terms of larger indie-sphere talking points. It's the sort of thing that it's hard to understand in retrospective, but one listen to Bitte Orca clarifies exactly how that could happen.

benmoot Jul 11 2012  
+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Dirty Projectors, a five piece group from Brooklyn, had put out two years before this release a cover of Black Flag’s Damaged. The source material is a far cry from the group’s traditional sound which could hardly be even joked about as punk in any way, but yet it’s palatable with the direction the group takes with it. Even the album before that didn’t seem to really hint toward what could be of Bitte Orca. Yet, here we are, and for me this is where the band gets it. The sound, the lyrics, the atmosphere of the instruments and swelling of the background, at times foreground, vocals of Amber Coffman and Angel Deradoorian are all in just the right places at the right moments to make this an incredible experience.

Bitte meaning “please” in German and Orca meaning exactly what it sounds like, a species of whale, are two words that mean absolutely nothing to front man and lead vocalist David Longstreth. He just thought they sounded good together. And they do, and he is sure to shout it through out the explosive chorus of “Useful Chamber” until the whole song just comes crashing down in a short jam session. It’s undoubtably one of the highlight moments of the album where it feels like literally there is this swelling of madness going on in these meaningless words. If they did have some sort of story backing them, it could only make it a more powerful moment, but it brings what it needs to without overdoing it.

Equally on the playing field is “Stillness Is The Move”, a track featuring Amber Coffman on lead vocals and she really nails this track so effortlessly. The huge thumping bass drum and infectious guitar lick throughout the duration of the song just dance around Coffman’s vocals, and the overdubbed backing vocal hummings and singing she does really root this track deep into the album’s core. I would be pressed to say that this track is, if anything, the track that would initially get a new comer to the group interested. It screams accessibility while being enjoyable by those looking to get a deeper experience out of the entire album, as the song fades into “Two Doves”, which is also sung by Coffman and is another really great track featuring an acoustic guitar and a violin as the backing instruments. Even without being as large sounding as “Stillness” is, it gets the point across in a minimal way, and I’ve yet to discover when that was a bad thing.

Bitte Orca closes out with a very ambient and hypnotic “Fluorescent Half Dome”. This is a spotlight point for Longstreth. His vocals are so strong and the instrumentation doesn’t get in the way of focusing on that key element to this song. There are no crazy guitar solos or a really rough jam, instead the violins do their own thing, Coffman and Deradoorian join Longstreth in vocalizing just over the strings and the album until there is nothing left but the pad sounds of the synth. It’s a beautiful way to end a hell of a record, and it’s no doubt one of my favorites of 2009.

I know a lot of people are calling this hipster garbage, but to me this is clearly one of the most brilliant albums in recent memory. I also know that some people say it's a grower, but unlike other 2009 indie favorites Merriweather Post Pavilion and Veckatimest (which it took me quite some time to actually enjoy), I must say that Bitte Orca hooked me on the very first listen. And another clear evidence of how compelling this album is would be that, the more I listen to it, the more I like it, even years after I got it. One could think that, after the initial feel of sheer excitement, its effect would eventually fade; yet, I feel exactly the opposite way. Every single time I listen to this is a bliss. What strikes me the most about Bitte Orca, apart from the fact that I love every song on it, is its sound. The crisp recording, the arrangements, Dave's guitar playing (!), the way all the instruments blend together, the masterful use of Amber and Angel's voices, all of it works. Yes, it's twisted and fractured, but it's also downright beautiful. Dismissing Bitte Orca is easy: weird for the sake of weirdness, affected male vocals, soulless female vocals, senseless songwriting, etc. But loving it is so much easier!
