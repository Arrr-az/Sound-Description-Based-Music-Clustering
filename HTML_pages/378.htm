Ah, the good old days, remember them? The days when it was always hot, except for Christmas when there was twelve feet of snow into which were pressed the light trace of reindeer footprints and what looked like the trail left by a heavy sack. Back then, ladies ate muffins in the afternoon and never burnt a single tea cake as it was impossible to do so because when you bought a household appliance such as a toaster or a grill, you knew darned well that it was built to last, not like any of the new stuff they make which is all mass produced and almost inevitably destined to break within a year.

People would leave their doors on the latch and nip out on holiday for a week, safe in the knowledge that the streets were safe and the home was a temple to the individual and consequently respected as such by those who may, in today's society, be of a criminal bent. In fact it was more likely that you would return from holiday to find that somebody had let themselves into your house and brushed away the dust that had settled in your absence so that, upon your return, you could relax rather than go about the housework.

In those days you could take a shiny pound coin (they were all shiny) and go for a day at the races by bus, gamble overzealously and lose everything, buy a three course meal for yourself and the wife, finish the day off at the cinema and travel back in a hackney cab and still have change to feed the kids for a week.

The Kinks, or specifically, The Village Green Preservation Society belongs to that time. However, this record is not merely a nostalgic trip down memory lane, it holds answers for those of us unfortunate enough to have been born outside of the good old days as to why those days were so good. Yes, it's because less drugs were criminalized, more specifically, LSD was still legal. Memories can be interfered with over the passing of time but good old reliable LSD kept those good ol' days good in the memories of many acid casualties still making a life for themselves today.

This is like a walk down Blackpool Pier after a mouthful of some dubious punch handed out by a well meaning old dear, or a game of bingo in which two fat ladies, two ducks and 66 snippity snip could become terrifying ordeals, or like a donkey ride along the sea front without a donkey. There is a psychedelic presence here that battles against the English niceties that seem to dominate the album upon first listen. And it really works, it lends weight to the belief that us Brits are eccentric and interesting people and not just grey and balding jobsworths with an unusual sense of humour.

The good old days were better than today, but thanks to this album I now know why; those crazy kids were off their boxes on perfectly acceptable, legal highs that we will never know.

Beatles water stones fire who air...but gimme the kinks babe...earth
You see this a lot...four British invasion acts of consequence ordered the beatles, the stones, the who, and right there at the bottom of a digitally stoned journalists chart....the kinks.

Why are the kinks different? Let me answer that first with a sort of I know what I'm saying professor type vibe, a smug adjustment of the idealized bow tie, and with a learned rhetoric I intend to persuade you that the kinks were the most earthen, the most brotherly, the least political. The least ideological. The warmest of the four. If they were a beatle they would be none of them. The beatles would be McCartney. The stones Lennon. The who would be Lennon too. So would the beatles actually.

But the kinks are on the ground. Townshend is the seeker. Lennon asks you to imagine, to give peace a chance. Mick Jagger will eventually sue sex for copying his act. They all stood for grand sweeping concepts...love independence freedom truth revolution sex rock n roll itself drugs. The kinks were no metaphor. The kinks were no sign of the times nor were they heady. They were feet on soil with a whiff of the south and the old physical touchies of a grammar college.

Their ambition was checked by a gentle reconciliation of an England of grace and an England on LSD. They understood where they came from. Half their heart goes to their satirical depictions of English tradition, and half goes towards the cunning, subtle satire. The kinks made no grand gestures towards a future. They very humbly mocked what they loved and Walter is definitely dead by now, if he was as haggard in 68 as Davies suggests...and pictures...they're the finest substitute for memory since this search engine.

Digressions on a Village Green
From my own experience, and that of people I've spoken to about this, I would say that nothing makes you feel a greater kinship with your native land than living abroad. This has nothing to do with liking or disliking, respecting or disrespecting other peoples; but it does have everything to do with realising how many of those instinctive responses we have to things are firmly set very early in life.

There are different theories about the Kinks. One influential school of thought suggests that being banned from playing in the USA forced the band, and Ray Davies more specifically, into focusing on and exploring England. This is an attractive idea, but it doesn't explain why English bands like The Beatles, The Rolling Stones or The Animals were obsessed with the USA long before any of them had been there; rather it's a case of the grass always being greener on the other side, which makes the Kinks' achievements all the more remarkable.

The truth it is that rock music as it came to be following The Beatles, The Stones, et al, was the result of a curious transatlantic hybrid, with Howlin' Wolf, Chuck Berry, Little Richard, Buddy Holly, et al filtered through a peculiarly British (or English) sensibility; the sound of a brash, optimistic, vast country brimming with self-confidence, rammed through the filter of an understated, conservative, cramped nation reeling from the loss of Empire, in need of renewal but with no clear idea of how to go about it. The attractiveness of the USA (and of rock 'n' roll) to young Britons had much to do with a slightly rosy picture of the USA, which was still possible at the time, as a land of almost unlimited opportunity.

And certainly this was the Kinks' modus operandi to begin with; debut single a cover of Long Tall Sally, breakthrough with a (brilliant) take on the garage ethic of The Kingsmen's Louie Louie; a debut LP featuring covers of luminaries like Chuck Berry, Bo Diddley and Slim Harpo, along with original compositions by Ray Davies where he stuck pretty close to his influences.

It would, I think, have been easy for Ray Davies to continue a mythological romance with the USA; instead he suddenly seems to have realised that he actually quite liked his native land, a land changing radically as the sixties swung, a process his band was very much a part of; like a man who only realises what he's got when he risks losing it.

An odd thing about Britain (or England), is how passionately it pursues a romance with its own past; consider the incredible popularity of all those costume dramas, more recently of Downton Abbey. Yet the only people in Britain who use impeccable upper class accents these days (apart from the Queen and the occasional unreconstructed Tory Toff) are immigrants eager to establish their desire to belong. England continues to live its past in serials like Midsomer Murders, while at the same time castigating anyone not enamoured of its new, real-life multiculturalism; thus Brian True-May was ousted as the producer of Midsomer Murders for stating that there was no racial diversity in the series because it was a bastion of Englishness. Clumsily put, no doubt, but in the furore the point was utterly missed that (quite aside from ethnic diversity being a largely urban phenomenon) the series never was about a real, modern England, but about recreating an old England with certain modern trappings; essentially the Miss Marple mysteries with mobile phones. Morrissey, who could easily pass for a reclusive author in an Agatha Christie adaptation himself, is routinely attacked for his supposed racism, when really his only crime is his romantic attachment to an England that no longer exists, much the same as any of the millions who revel in Downton Abbey.

The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society is one of those records that would appear to have been made for Morrissey to play endlessly (am I the only one who thinks Have-a-Go Merchant is the dysfunctional shadow of Picture Book?). It is a portrait of a country where Mr Toad still lords it at Toad Hall, and where youngsters in Enid Blyton novels assure genial policemen that they will only take up smoking once they've passed the age of 21.

Of course, it's easy to feel dissatisfaction with modern life the most vital driving force of nostalgia. What makes Village Green so astonishing is that it was recorded between 1966 and 1968, when things seemed to be improving in leaps and bounds, when people had it even better than they had in 1959, when Harold Macmillan could, in good conscience, say that the British people had never had it so good. So much is made of the sixties as a counter-cultural decade that it's often overlooked that it was a decade that aided and abetted the spread, the coming domination of the American concept of consumerism as ideology; of office blocks and skyscrapers, the onward march of mall culture, of uniform high streets, of gloss replacing idiosyncrasy, of the convergence of the words home, speculation and investment (an Englishman's home no longer being his castle, more a casino chip). But while Mick Jagger insisted on his preposterous drawling approximation of something American, the Kinks remained the English Speaking Vernacular.

Village Green is actually an extraordinary chronicle of a way of life that was in the process of being lost, rather than one which had already been lost. It would still be inaccurate to say that Village Green even now describes a lost land; there are still B&B's in Southend. Cultures are more resistant than that; to this day you can find enclaves of London with greens that, on a Sunday evening, are not that far removed from the ambience this LP conjures up. Continuity and change exist in equal measure, just as the differences between cultures can be both exaggerated and underestimated, depending on how you choose to view them.

Equally, in parts Village Green serves to remind us that not all change is bad. We may feel a certain nostalgia for steam engines, but does anyone seriously long to have the soot and the grime back?

And of course, ultimately Village Green, for all of its Englishness, is a hybrid. However many the music hall, or the pastoral or tudoresque inflections; however many references to draught beer, cricket, oak trees, church steeples and, well, village greens, without Elvis, Lead Belly and Little Richard, this record would never have been made. The first thing for God to bless here (alongside vaudeville and variety) is Donald Duck.

Ultimately memory is selective. If it's easy to feel nostalgia for the sense of England conjured up here, that's because it's a comforting version of England, populated with ordinary people enjoying good times; people who may enjoy a tipple, who go to seaside towns for their holidays, who are cheeky but never vulgar, and who honestly believe that cleanliness is next to godliness. What it isn't, is the England of sadistic headmasters, of starched collars and overbearing managerial types who learned their leadership skills at the Battle of the Somme, of rigid hierarchies and required obsequiousness in the presence of people who assume they're better than everyone else thanks to the silver spoon still dangling next to their lolling tongue. But then Ray Davies had already proved he could satirise elements of England; Village Green is the affectionate side of the coin, but interestingly no less dependent on an old class structure that the Swinging Sixties did much to reorder.

No-one loves their country unconditionally, and in order to be able to luxuriate in a sense of belonging, we pick and choose those parts of past and present that please us the most (incidentally just as we pick and choose what to like and dislike about other cultures). Village Green has travelled as well as it has because its universal air of nostalgic yearning transcends its English specificity.

Yet if Village Green is in part a memorial, equally it is of its time. You could never make this record today, not only because times are different, but because the changes that allowed it have reached full maturity, and the pigs have become the new humans. The changes of the sixties were changes in the very fabric of the social composition of the nation, changes in power and the way power is exercised. Today we like to talk about the pace of change, but the pace of change is very much a technological construct (faster broadband), less a sociological reality (social mobility at a post-war low).

But none of this would matter much if Village Green wasn't such an elegantly constructed record; lyrically dexterous, musically playful, executed with a stunning deftness of touch, melodically delicious. As concept albums go, it beats the living daylights out of most of them, thematically taut, yet never pompous or overbearing. The Kinks invite you to share in their vision of England by chaperoning you around their favourite bits of it. Love it or hate it, that's your prerogative, but it's doubtful if any band has succeeded in expressing its fondness for England and Englishness with quite the same sensitivity and affection. Village Green remains an exquisite ode to a core essence that changes only superficially in response to time's fluidity.

A year or so down the line, the Kinks were admitted to the US and made up for lost time, becoming lost to their native land. In the meantime Arthur or the Decline and Fall of the British Empire had provided yet another take on the English experience; one less rose-tinted, less comforting, but no less sympathetic to the ordinary, everyday England that continues to create a love-hate tension that occasionally erupts as great, great music. And there are few greater than Village Green.

I've never been to England. I'm more than a decade removed from having any first-hand knowledge of the 1960s. I like modern gadgets and big cities. Yet somehow, The Kinks are able to make me not only understand the bucolic charm of the halcyon days revered in The Kinks Are Village Green Preservation Society, but feel like I belong there. It is this transformative power that makes listening to this album such a rich experience.

The Kinks Are Village Green Preservation Society presents itself as a sort of sequence of brief daydreams, each one providing just enough detail to convey a particular feeling. "Village Green Preservation Society" serves as the prologue, calling for the preservation of various cultural touchstones of the then-recent English past. Thereafter the album opens up like a pop-up book of scrapbook memorabilia from past, present and future perspectives in jumbled order. There are childhood fantasies and horror stories ("Phenomenal Cat" and "Wicked Annabella"), wide-eyed memories of young adulthood ("Do You Remember Walter?", "Village Green"), abstract contemplations about life's transition ("The Last of the Steam-Powered Trains") and purpose ("Big Sky"), allusions to awestruck idol worship from both sides ("Johnny Thunder", "Starstruck"), blissful escape ("Sitting by the Riverside", "Animal Farm") and frustration about the inability of pictures to truly capture the past ("Picture Book", "People Take Pictures of Each Other") covering all sides of human relationships in under 40 minutes. Baroque instrumental drapery gives the entire project a cinematic feel to enhance each feeling, making for a potent emotional tug that never has the chance to overstay its welcome in any particular setting.

While each song is worthy of its own lengthy description, I'll only focus on a few of the most striking moments. The dichotomy of doughy-eyed nostalgia and jaded reality is perhaps best captured in "Do You Remember Walter?". It is clear that Davies looked up to his long lost friend as he fondly recalls how popular Walter was with girls, the fun times they spent smoking and playing cricket, and their plans to change the world with the surety of youth, but all the while he laments how the world has changed and surmises that Walter now would likely be a tubby, dutiful husband who would not care about such past endeavors. It serves as a powerful statement about the change of perspective, as youthful dreams give way to tough reality in a way that can warp the illusion of both the past and the present. Elsewhere, the humor in what I'll call gnome singing on the childhood myth of "Phenomenal Cat", the way that the murky "Wicked Annabella" is presented as an exaggerated warning from parents about a social outcast to keep their kids in line and the nightmare of hyperbolic public embarrassment in front of all of your friends in "All of My Friends Were There" ensures that the album has its light moments, too. I'm no fan of country music, but the dirty swing of "The Last of Steam-Powered Trains" is nonetheless a favorite for its anthropomorphic depiction of the last steam train's final hurrah and the way its sound is mimicked by three falsetto voices converging in an elongated "steeeeeam!". "Starstruck" and "Picture Book", meanwhile, are perfectly hummable pop songs that get me moving beyond what I thought 60s pop/rock was capable. The unquestioned highlight, though, is the song which inspired the entire project. At just over 2 minutes, "Village Green" is the most lushly orchestral track present, with harpsichords, horns and an oboe unraveling the picturesque beauty of the humble countryside. Davies narrates from the perspective of broken disillusionment after the call of the big city proves unfulfilling, focusing on just the right elements to make the song universal by highlighting the things he misses: morning dew, fresh air, a church, an old oak tree. He then reveals the loved-her-left-her fate of his relationship with Daisy in a mere two lines. Things quickly turn sour as he mocks tourists marveling at the very same quaintness he remembers so fondly before he nonchalantly states that Daisy married a local grocer. Although the song finishes with the image that upon his return he and Daisy will "sip tea, laugh and talk about the village green", the wistful way in which the song is performed and sung gives the direct resignation of the lyrics its own twist, as if he doesn't want to say too much in order to prevent himself from completely losing it. In the end, you get the feeling that the narrator will be dying on the inside as he feigns a smile and a laugh drinking tea with Daisy.

Only one other artist I've encountered has demonstrated this ability to leapfrog over my musical prejudices to transport me into a world of which I know nothing and color in the lines so vividly - Sufjan Stevens. And as with Illinois, The Kinks Are Village Green Preservation Society has proven to be such a thoroughly invigorating and informative listen that it feels like a personal milestone. Adjacent albums Something Else by The Kinks and Arthur (Or the Decline and Fall of the British Empire) likewise chronicle this sort of baroque photo gallery through the mundane but important lives of John and Jane Doe, but it is at its most well-developed here.

Nostalgia is a potent thing. The past is easy to idealize, we got through it the first time after all, and a lot of people tend to believe in a sort of cultural and social entropy, especially as far as society's ills and urbanization. Particularly in the sixties, when all the social upheaval made the older and younger generations alike want to escape into a woodsy, folksy sugarcoated and idealized past. It should be no surprise that The Kinks, who were constantly talking about escape and wanting to get away from the city and about the grand old days of the British Empire (albeit usually in a somewhat satirical way), would be especially keen on retreating into the sunshine of some nostalgic village community where the grass is green, the birds sing, and everything is just so, like the pictures in the old family photo album. This however is not the fey yearning to escape the frightening workaday world that you might find in many of Brian Wilson's songs before he succumbed to anxiety and became a shut-in, but something a little bit more knowing and satirical.

The Davies know that this dream of time traveling back to a pastoral wonderland is a bit unrealistic. It was practically the plot of one of their other concept albums, Arthur or the Decline and Fall of the British Empire in which the title character ends up depressed in quasi-exile to Australia. This is why in the title track they equate the mythical Village Green with Disneyana, comic books, pulp novels, and virginity. The sort of things that adults lose track of over the years, essentially. They also add in there 'strawberry jam and all the different varieties,' which are of course preserves. Sly gag. Throughout the album they also note the obsession with documenting and cataloging the past ('Picture book of people with each other - to prove they loved one another' for instance or 'People take pictures of the summer just in case someone thought they had missed it') and how that simultaneously living in the past and future subtracts from living in the present. Why should we preserve the past for the future at the cost of living in the present?

This album also notably discards any notions of them being associated with the mods, allying themselves with nostalgists and the empire, although a certain psychedelic element certainly lingers. This is mostly in the sound which, although folkier in bent, still bears the fuzziness and muscle of psych-rock. "Last of the Steam-Powered Trains" is highly prescient of Grateful Dead's folk period, and the whole album has something of an acid ambiance, an extrasensory appreciation of the natural world (see in particular the wonky "Sitting by the Riverside").

There's a bajillion deluxe editions of Village Green, this particular one comes with the stereo version of the album which had "Days" and "Mr. Songbird" and subtracts a handful of other tracks, which is a cool thing to have if only to just listen to most of the album a second time over in a different mix/fidelity.

"This world is big and wild and half insane"

There's something very wrong with the world these days....

I'm a member of the Now Generation, a generation of Westerners who have had life relatively easy up to now, a generation that has become obsessed with lifestyle, possessions and image. We're not a patient generation, we like to think that we're ambitious, but very few among us are willing to take the time to achieve that ambition - we've seen the lifestyle we want and we want it now. We don't want to spend long years gaining experience and recognition, slowly but surely edging ourselves to our ultimate goal. We want the money now and we don't care who we have to walk over or stab in the back to get to it. We're in such a rush to make more money and achieve these allegedly perfect lifestyles that we never take time out to care about what matters - our friends, our family, our roots and heritage.

I take a look at my generation now and the vast majority of us are hurtling towards mental, emotional and spiritual burnout, as we are so determined to get what we want, we never actually take time out to appreciate what we actually have. Our parents called it 'the rat race', but it's no longer a race, as a race suggests some kind of sportsmanship - this is more a case of selling our souls in order to have everything we want, and as we've been told many times before, what we want and what we need are usually two very different things.

I see myself as lucky, circumstances over the last decade of my life have led me to avoid some (but not all) of the pitfalls that many of my generation have fallen foul of. I bought my first home at a very young age, much younger than most of contemporaries anyway, so when everyone else was scrabbling to get on the property ladder three or four years later, inevitably pushing up property prices to insane levels, I was already there. Sure, I've had to make sacrifices over the years - I've not had the experience of traveling like most people my age have and I've never learned to drive, but the upshot of that is now a part of me can feel smug and self-righteous as I walk my non-carbon footprint creating route into work every day, passing my peers sat in traffic jams in their over-priced, natural-resource guzzling, dick-compensators. Not for me the over-priced and ugly 'lifestyle apartments' either (call it what you like, a flat is still a flat), no, my home is a humble little two-up two-down terraced house, the long-time traditional home of the UK working class. My parents owned one and their parents before them and they'll still be standing long after the hideous loft apartments they're erecting these days have been bulldozed into the ground.

This desire for a simpler and less hectic life is the theme at the heart of The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society. Away from the hustle and bustle of the soulless big city, it is a heartwarming reminder of a simpler life. Time is taken to reminisce about friends and family ("Do You Remember Walter?", "Picture Book"), days gone by ("Last of the Steam-Powered Trains") and life in the countryside (the near title track, "Sitting by the Riverside"). When the scene does shift to the bright lights of the big city, it is accompanied with words of warning, as "Starstruck" inevitably reminds me of those friends of mine that have headed to London to further their careers, only for most of them to have their characters and souls crushed as they struggle to come to the terms with the realisation that money isn't everything and the fact that they can have a career or a family, but not both.

If there is a lesson to be learned from The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society, it's that it doesn't hurt to be out of step with your generation. While The Kink's contemporaries were expanding their minds and releasing albums that now sound badly dated (Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band, Their Satanic Majesties Request, The Who Sell Out, etc), Ray Davies' manifesto was considerably more less ambitious, but ultimately more robust and timeless.

Consider that while you're sat in front of your 40 inch HD television this evening...
