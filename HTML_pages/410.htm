I understand it, like: he tries to tell me about the racism, slavery and social injustice concerning our black fellows. It ends up being very conceptual and, yeah, creative in the means of composition and sounds used. And, frankly, it works as I can get the message. But it's so cold and unfriendly, I can understand, but some kind of "spiritual" (oh, metaphysics!) connection is entirely lost. I never thought of jazz as some kind of high art, as I always imagined it more like honest and unbound sound of the bottom of the society. This seems to want to be both... and somehow it fails to be free.

Revisited 18.1.2009: I agree that it's bullshit what I've written up-there. The objection remains, though, but it's not that black and white (no pun intended).
I don't know if it's lack of tradition, social background, education or mental set-up that refrain me from fully appreciating this on emotional level, though I can fully accept Mingus' endeavors. There is plenty of things I enjoy here, at first it simply sounds good as hell. I like how Mingus himself steps into background, like a nearly-silent conductor, which seems to be a sign of confidence in his players abilities - something that is fantastic in this context. I like his love for the masters of the past and thus for jazz music at all, that is displayed in countless references. And it's not impersonal, as I thought at first time, there's enough pain which obviously comes from Mingus himself, not from those he stands up for.
But it still has this "composition" feeling. All things are set there to like they are - the pain there is set there, it doesn't arise by itself. That's a bit hazy statement, yes. But the struggles with composition are not the struggles I originally expect in jazz, I mean I don't prohibit jazz-men from making their composition - but then it seems to be transition to another tradition: that of Western classical music. I think this point is partly true, it's "ballet" after all, isn't it?
And then also different criteria may apply, those of classical music, though not entirely. It would be stupid to expect Mingus to re-work the classical music, and such ambition would be laughable and irrelevant to him, obviously. But it's not essentially problem of criteria, it's the problem of approach - that is: how to listen to it. I like the manic banging of piano or screaming horns, but when for example the Spanish guitar enters, it's something that's seems to be so deliberately included there that it's just too confusing to me, making this strange hybrid. There are moments like that - it's not that I'm just making this up or thinking more than listening, it's based on perception. When I hear them playing their hearts out, though in a controlled - but say: fascinatingly humble - manner, it's gorgeous, but in other places, when things too openly reminds me that it's actually a composition, with some concept, purpose, message - it just don't work. There is no inner struggle inherent to composition and it can't be, but that's what makes my favorite classical compositions great, and that's what Mingus' composition necessarily lacks. It's great but it's also naive, and done by anyone else or probably performed by anyone else it would be a failure. It is not performed by anyone else, of course. But it is naive.
Free-jazz is in my ears a logical and most fruitful step-forward for jazz. Mingus' version of jazz is an isolated weird branch which worked thanks to his immense genius, but died with him. And Black Saint & Sinner Lady is probably a culmination of his art, but it would be for me impossible to really love Mingus for this record, without hearing some e.g. Pithecantropus Erectus or Blues & Roots, too.
Simply: I can see it but I can't feel it. Take this as my apology. I'll never be a jazz person after all. I don't think that's something wrong. My preferences and expectations are elsewhere. Maybe I am fucking pseudo-intellectual snob. Get used to it. Rating increased to 4.5*, though.

+12 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Serious jazz fans cite _The Black Saint and the Sinner Lady_ as a monumental album that can be appreciated on multiple levels. Of all Charles Mingus’ works, it will probably endure the longest. From a production standpoint, it was one of the first jazz records to make extensive use of studio overdubs for added texture and colour, even though the eleven musicians who played on it were free to improvise off of Mingus’ demanding score. Among the musicians whom bass player Mingus assembled for this album were Richard Williams on trumpet, Jerome Richardson on flute and baritone saxophone, Booker Ervin on tenor sax, Jaki Byard on piano, and longtime Mingus regulars Dannie Richmond on drums and Charlie Mariano on alto sax, who is arguably the star of the show.

The pieces on this album owe a tremendous debt to Duke Ellington, but as a whole they sound less like a jazz work than a classical tone poem or a modern ballet. The album is a series of dances not unlike Antonín Dvořák’s or Béla Bartók’s folk songs and dances. In fact, Mingus had referred to his collective as a folk band, possibly as a way to signify a break with the traditional jazz output then on the market.

The album appears daunting given the fact that there are two sets of written material designed to aid the listener's experience. Mingus wrote secondary titles for each piece in an attempt to tell a story all by themselves. In addition, the liner notes were partly written by Mingus’ psychologist as a guide to what might have been going through Mingus’ mind as he wrote this work. But if you have difficulty like I do catching the shrink's meaning (I fail to recognize the struggle between races as the album's suggested concept, and reading Mingus' own didactic essay doesn’t help), there is always the music and listeners can provide their own interpretation to it.

I’m not going to try to impart a meaning to every detail of the album, but I thought it would be fun to sit down and spontaneously write a paragraph on each piece as I listen to it. After all, that appears to be the way the liner notes are presented. So here goes.

It was Mingus' intention that the album play as one continuous piece, but the limitations of the LP made that difficult. Thus _The Black Saint and the Sinner Lady_ is broken into six parts, three shorter pieces on Side 1 and three more on the flip side that form a side-long suite.

The first part is entitled “Solo Dancer” with a secondary title of “Stop! Look! And Listen, Sinner Jim Whitney.” It opens with a drum march followed by a brass-led funeral dirge before moving into more soulful, swinging territory. Mellow and seductive in spots, it reverts back to the theme before ending with Charlie Mariano’s alto sax solo chased by the brass section.

The second part is of the “Duet Solo Dancers,” with a secondary title of “Hearts’ Beat and Shades in Physical Embraces.” It begins with an Ellingtonian piano solo over the orchestra's dreamy, slow-paced melody. The orchestra becomes louder after the first stanza until it drowns out the piano, ending on a minor note after which a baritone sax and a tuba clash with a trombone, trumpet and saxophone. The beat picks up speed, slows down, then picks up speed again in an orgasmic frenzy. By this point, the dancers are panting, and a drum and muted trombone growl out a duet. The other instruments fall in behind a lead trombone in an ebb and flow motion. A return to the opening theme is briefly heard before the part ends, again in a minor key.

The third part highlights the “Group Dancers.” For an alternate title, Mingus wrote “(Soul Fusion) Freewoman and Oh, This Freedom’s Slave Cries.” Like the second part, it also opens with a piano solo, this time performed by Mingus himself. It is interspersed with a belch of brass and later a quick waltz. Then the music shifts gears to feature a flamenco guitar break before erupting into an orgy of sound with flute and saxophone trading riffs against a matador’s drum beat. The music shifts again to a sensuous, swaying groove that grows ever faster until it fades to a lone saxophone blast.

The side-long suite incorporates elements of the three previous pieces. The section “Trio and Group Dancers” begins with the mellow feeling of the first part combined with a lengthy flamenco guitar solo of the Group Dancers. This is followed by a cacophony of instruments playing at various tempos as the dancers come together. Mingus titled this section “Stop! Look! and Sing Songs of Revolutions!” and one wonders if by “revolutions” Mingus meant a political upheaval or a basic definition of spinning around while dancing.

Another piano solo followed by the sensuous group dance as the music carries us to the “Single Solos and Group Dance” section of the suite. For this section, Mingus subtitled it “Saint and Sinner Join in Merriment on Battle Front.” Again, the flamenco guitar representing the Group Dancers enters and is joined by saxophone. The music fades into what I would describe as a bullfight scene, as a mariachi trumpet makes its cry above all the woodwinds and brass.

The finale, “Group and Solo Dances,” takes the slow, swaying movement from the Duet Solo Dancers and plays it faster and faster until it is at near breakneck speed. Then it abruptly slows down only to speed up, slow down, and speed up again. This echoes the third piece, the Group Dances, in concept. Finally, the Solo Dancer’s opening theme returns, followed by the lone saxophone of the Group Dancers as the two parts have bonded. It is a passionate finish to the piece, befitting the subtitle “Of Love, Pain, and Passioned Revolt, then Farewell, my Beloved, ‘til it’s Freedom Day.”

_The Black Saint and the Sinner Lady_ is not an easy album to get into right away but it is a rich, rewarding album for listeners who exercise patience. Though at times intense, the music works like a strong dance partner whirling you around the room. Give it a few twirls and see what everyone here is talking about.

I think my jazz collection improved tenfold when I moved from music by heroin addicts to music by crazy people.

This one gets a five. You know, people can’t stop talking about how great Eric Dolphy is, and how Andrew Hill is amazing, and Albert Ayler is unbelievable...and I listen to their music and yes, they are all that, and yes they're all woefully all under-appreciated, and yes I believe that it’s important to hear them and understand that's there's more to jazz than Love Supreme and Kind of Blue…

...but THIS... this record, Black Saint and Sinner Lady...this is exactly what I’ve been looking for for my whole life.

Mingus’ jazz is inventive, strange, and jarring, AND it swings, AND it makes me get up and shout "holy shit!", AND it makes me want to play it way too loud and not give a fuck whether the old people next door are asleep. AND it makes me want to drink wine with my lady and make out til 2AM. It is great. By the mid 60’s (this one was recorded in ‘63) many artists were giving up playing jazz, in my opinion, in the name of reinvention and playing an entirely different game. It would be as if LeBron James decided that Chamberlain and Magic and Jordan had already done everything there was to do in basketball, so instead of bothering to try to score he thought it would be more important to just throw the ball at the crowd, instead of the hoop. Which would be great, really. Especially at a Laker game. I would love to see that. That spirit of reinvention is Ayler and Sanders and Roscoe Mitchell...Sun Ra took the game out of the arena and moved it to Saturn for Chrissakes.

But what I think Mingus does on this album is take jazz in a different direction without becoming intentionally abrasive. His compositions argue that music should be beautiful, even when they're totally fucking weird. And for that I love him. This album is orchestrated and it's spontaneous. There's nothing masturbatory about it. It gets better with every listen. It's beautiful and heartbreaking and inspiring and cacophonous and loud and sad and drunk and crazy.

Mingus stepped up and played jazz, and dunked on Bird and Ellington and Jelly Roll and everyone else.

Listening to this makes me feel like I could dunk on Jordan...

...and that would be amazing.

I hate listening to this album sitting down.

It is, by far, my most walk-able jazz record. I'm heading to the library downtown, huge jazz selection there, fresh out of Blues & Roots and A Love Supreme, ready to immerse myself in another Mingus record, well my third or fourth jazz record to date. I just start walking down the street with the album playing in my CD Player. For albums heavy on soloing I prefer sitting, and for Free Jazz I prefer darkness and solitude, but for Mingus? He’s moving. He’s going somewhere. Mingus molds and shapes his own thoughts, but every time I listen to his records I can’t help thinking that maybe he’s keeping his listeners in mind. His psychologist latched onto the idea that Mingus wants to be heard. This isn’t “Here’s something I made, tell me what you think”. I agree. I agree to the ends of the earth.

I wasn't blown away by this record. I wasn't stunned, jaw-dropping to the floor. I was actually reading the linear notes throughout my first listen. Waiting at a bus stop, I pulled out the little booklet that was slipped into the album and just started reading. I remember several paragraphs about chord changes and all these almost scientific-esque observations (which come from Mingus himself surprisingly), and then one of those moments hit me. Sometimes a melody just strikes me as familiar; a musical Deja-Vu. The exact moment was that group blast of "WAAA! Wa-Wa. Wa-Wa Waaaaa, Waaaa, Waaaaaaa..." during Track A's final solo. I just let the booklet rest in my hand and listened to that repeat itself, the soprano sax (I'm horrible at identifying instruments) dancing on top of it. I sort of drifted into the next track.

I had several more Deja-Vu type moments. I know for sure one of them involves that repeated flute medley in "Mode D, E, F", but after that it's just a blur. Almost every section of the album is now catchy, poppy almost. It's like hearing [Insert frequently-played radio song here]. I dunno how i've come define Black Saint as a collection of very catchy melodies.

(Come to think of it...)
Why do I like Black Saint and the Sinner Lady?
I have no clue.

It's very up-front. That’s my general answer. Once you hear the opening drum struts and the wailing saxes, you’ll think it’s “up-front” too.

I love Jazz that occupies a un-identifiable gray area where "timid", "abrasive", and "care-free" sorta meld into one. Again, I wasn't blown away by this record. I wasn't stunned, jaw-dropping to the floor. I was feeling it, toe-tapping to every melody (and being forced to re-think my toe-taps as they became off-beat more than once), eyes focused, but clearly un-aware of what they were seeing. I was in my groove, especially when re-listening to it. Everything sort of pops out at once on this record. There're plenty of simultaneous conjunctions of emotion; the climax of the last track is the best example I think: The lazy, lower-register Sax-a-ma-phoning counter melody, the sharp shrills of the muted trumpets, and a mood-shifting solo, all converging and accelerating into a Mingus Implosion!

Mingus. That’s all I can ever think of when I hear this album. Mingus. There’re a couple of distinct melodies, all explored on the first three tracks (Side 1) which are then clearly fused together on the last three tracks. Everything is really cut and pasted; you can clearly hear when a melody shifts from one to another. A haze organic thought and grueling construction, I say. I’m sure if I was some sort of jazz purist (I don't know the first thing about jazz purity!) I’d be mentioning, in a not-so-nice/positive/kind/caring kinda way, that “overdubbing was used!”

I’d then, in this jazz purist character, spout off a pampered down version of the following:

“ Black Saint isn’t in the moment at all! Mingus is a back-stabbing traitor! Jazz is supposed to be about what you feel! About the soul expressing itself and nothing standing in it’s way! You pretentious, classical-composition-loving piece of stoic sh*t! Take your indecipherable title and go discuss the “true” meaning of music with all those upper-class Europeans you obviously adore!”

This is a composition (I can say that for sure), but the soloing on the album isn’t overshadowed in the least. I mean, the only soul expressed here is not simply Mr. Mingus’:

I tune into Turner Classic Movies on cable once in a while, (which airs a handful of good films once in a while and a plethora of Star-Power Cheese-Fests every day) and at this particular moment of intrigue I was watching a violinist “solo” with an orchestra. The extremely versatile playing stopped me from changing the channel. He possessed a precision that left me stone cold however. The perfect “response” of the orchestra behind him at the “right” spots had me wondering (And smiling. I usually smile when I ask myself rhetorical questions) “Why? Why does music have to be so tightly knit like that? Are you free Mr. Violinist? Why aren’t you showing any emotion on your face? Dance! Why are you staring intently at that music stand? Is that where the music is? From the paper?” The scene switched to an upper-class woman talking to some senior. I changed the channel.

I imagine each soloist on Black Saint as I always imagine jazz soloists: Eyes closed, or eyes staring intently at something, and the definite must: Body movement. Even as they’re concentrating on the most complicated four-bar passage they’ve ever seen, they’re moving. It’s impossible for me not to imagine the band jamming without those individual wild shakes and tussles. Putting out as much movement as they possibly can without severely hindering their performance. This adds to this universal mental studio image I have of Mingus and Co. (Not to mention Jazz performers in general).

I have a clue now: Movement.
This composition isn’t aligned in proper posture. It’s moving. It’s surging. It’s blasting through every feeling out there. It’s Mingus. It’s alive!

This is Mingus's most ambitious album and is often regarded as the culmination of everything he had done hitherto. One can't argue with that but does it necessarily follow that it is also his best work? Personally I don't think so. It certainly takes us to places we've never been before and it's an impressive journey, has any other jazz composer been as successful in attempting such a large scale work as Mingus has here?....i'm not sure.
There is some truth in the argument that jazz lends itself more to the shorter piece than to the multi-part composition. The orchestral palette is richer than that of the jazz ensemble and can expand on themes more easily, perhaps a wider range of tempos and rhythms allow a greater and more extreme variety of moods. Above all the improvisatory nature of jazz is at odds with the written form.
None of this bothers Mingus though who's insatiable appetite for experimentation and love of the classical form were almost bound to lead him towards ever bigger structures. This wasn't a one-off either as he would later record Meditations and still later Cumbia And Jazz Fusion. The Black Saint And The Sinner Lady, though, is surely the most daring and complex.The titles Mingus gives the separate parts may be a little pretentious but then so is the music itself. It's as grand as opera at times, poised and refined, but it's gut-bucket too. It has Latin America stamped right through it but it doesn't totally neglect the blues. It does seem to by-pass bop and swing though which somewhat restricts the musical variety available to Mingus and leaves him with a strange combination of styles employing Spanish guitar and classical piano passages to lend character to what is essentially Mingus's version of the Ellington tone poem expanded and modernized. Much of it tends to rest on a two chord vamp over which Mingus feels free to super-impose seemingly unrelated rhythms and riffs that eventually force themselves to meld into a whole. At times there are so many things happening it almost goes atonal.
About four minutes into the first part it emerges from the dense undergrowth into a bop rhythm and a sax solo but before too long more riffing and a tuba solo thicken things up again....heavy stuff.
Part two picks up on the two chord vamp again with surprising little staccato run-downs and then settles on a see-saw motion that speeds up, stops and then slows down again. One trombone is like a fog-horn another is muted. The saxes play repeated lines. Mingus and Richmond wade through the mud trying to keep their footing.
A piano opens part three, the same chords used at the end of Passions Of A Man, played by Mingus himself I believe. Out of it flows a 3/4 idea which is then taken over by the Spanish thing that Mingus is so fond of and this time with a real Spanish guitar! There's quite a lot of editing here to create juxtapositions of sound textures. At times it is similar to Ysabels Table Dance. A slow beat takes over with a four note descending line (similar to one on Harlem by Ellington) and some collective improvisation. It starts to accelerate again, so much is going on! Mingus and Richmond double the tempo before a drum roll brings it all to an end leaving a sax soloing on its' own and wondering where everyone has gone.
Part four and we are back with the two chord vamp again, this time with some harmonic variations. Some slower lines, flamenco guitar, a latin rhythm with soar-away Tijuana trumpets once again accelerating and this time to a sudden stop. Piano stuff follows then the 3/4 idea again, in fact a bit of everything! Even when we think we've arrived at the finale and we're into the home run that insistent Spanish guitar, that sax, those fanfares!
It takes the mighty blues to finally boot them out and the chase is on. The music accelerates like a run-away train that only a strategically placed edit can halt. But it's a down-hill slope and we're soon off again, even faster this time, only Dannie knows how to apply the breaks. But this lumbering locomotive is just too heavy and a crash seems inevitable.....
To spare us this Mingus re-introduces the opening theme and much to everyone's relief we arrive at our destination dizzy and exhausted but still alive.
Perhaps this is Mingus's greatest recording, perhaps it's a little over the top, perhaps it depends on how you feel. Compositionally there's a lack of variety and probably too much repetition but there's a lot of detail to compensate. There are countless edits throughout the recording, they are easy to identify and crucial to the overall result, they are, for Mingus, a tool of composition and the creative way in which he uses them (rather than just correctively) is as important as the ideas themselves.
This is Mingus pushing the envelope until it bursts open. Check out what's inside and judge for yourself.
