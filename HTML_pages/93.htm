Bowie fans tend to be very divided on the merits and demerits of this album. This is understandable, for it is unlike any other release in Bowie's catalog, being extremely diverse or inconsistent depending on your view. The allegorical opener, Space Oddity,signals Bowie's future role as an alienated explorer of sorts, but in a sweet and lush pop setting replete with orchestral arrangements (courtesy of Paul Buckmaster). However, the dreamy aesthetic tone of that pop gem is quickly dashed by the rocking Bo Diddley jam-out, Unwashed and Slightly Dazed. Sex and class-conflict come to the fore as Bowie depicts a pretty but snobby girl "spying" on him from her rich father's house ( "don't turn your nose up", he sings, and "I know what a louse like me in his house could do for you.") Letter To Hermione finds Bowie in a rare confessional mood pining for a lover that has spurned him. The song, I think, is stirring and beautiful. It is all the more confessional since Hermione is a very real character with whom young David Jones fell in love, but by whom he either felt or was rejected. As far as I'm aware, Bowie would never be so intimate or vulnerable again on record. In Cygnet Committee, the personal turns political as Bowie breaks into another autobiographical piece. The 10 minute long progressive rock ballad chronicles his participation in, and ambivalence about a counter-cultural artists' collective in the late 60s. He does not conceal his disappointment with this collective, and treats it as a microcosm of the utopian hippie culture of that era. In this fascinating screed, Bowie rips into the hippies for their hypocrisy, laziness and reliance on the money and labor of others, shallow slogans and platitudes, and ultimately their lack of empathy for anyone who does not share their ill-defined , pie-in-the-sky agenda.The song ends with a fictional, dystopian regime in which the hippies run the State "plowing down man, woman and not hearing anymore", all " in the name of "slogans such as wish you could hear, all you need is love, kick out the jams, kick out your mother, cut up your friend, screw up your brother or he'll get you in the end." This may be the strongest track on the album, lyrically and musically. It succeeds in passionately and angrily critiquing 60s youth culture from an insider's perspective. It also reveals Bowie as an experimental songwriter, capable of dramatic and somewhat complex song-structures The chord progressions, dynamics and melody hint at Bowie the future art-rocker. Here Bowie trashes the hollow promises of the hippies of the 60s, and it is well to remember that Bowie would have much to do with putting 70s rock on an entirely new footing in coming years. The next tune, Janine, is a fairly standard piece of folk-rock circa 1969--agreeable but none too interesting. There are some intriguing baroque folk tunes (Occasional Dream, Wild Eyed Boy) in which Buckmaster's orchestral arrangements (of which I am a fan) figure prominently. Some will, doubtless, find the orchestra overbearing, but the arrangements are at least inventive, if a bit ornate. God Knows I'm Good is a pointless tale of a shoplifter with a guilty conscience set to unremarkable folk music. The closing track, Memory of a Free Festival, revisits the theme of 1960s counterculture.The song memorializes an outdoor music festival at which Bowie had played. Again his ambivalence toward the 60s counterculture emerges as he compares the music festival to "Satori", but ends by quoting "Hey Jude" in pessimistic fashion, proclaiming that "The Sun Machine is going down, and we're gonna have a party." As the Sun sets on the 1960s, Bowie looks back sentimentally, but with a clear sense of finality.

Overall, this is a tough album to "rate" because it functions more as a sampler of various styles than it does an album with an aesthetic arc. Bowie would continue exploring different styles with mixed results until he learned to communicate through characters (Ziggy Stardust, A Lad Insane, Halloween Jack, Thin White Duke, et.al.) But in 1969, all of that was future history. What strikes me here is that (Major Tom not withstanding) Bowie is not occupying any theatrical role here. The album is full of musings on his actual life experiences and feelings. In sum, an interesting, and at times, rewarding record of this artist as a young man.

Kind of a comfort thing for me
It's mostly a collection of stories on isolation and heartbreak, and it feels like sitting around a campfire every time. I've grown to love every song here, from the vivid epics to the touching laid-back ballads. And the rockers are super fun.

However, the production is kind of hazy to say the least, and it sells the music a bit short. The '19 mix is not without faults either - not at all -, but it's punchier and more robust, and made the whole thing really click for me. I'd say it's the way to go for a more fulfilling experience.

Space Oddity is very different from his Self-titled debut. That one was more of a folky and baroque pop-type record. While this one seems to start to lean more towards the early Glam Rock Bowie was widely known for and would mature into.

Space Oddity is much better than the debut and I think its pretty underrated. I was pleasantly surprised. Other than that, I dont have much to say about this one.

Anyone who got on the Bowie Bandwagon after Hunky Dory is likely to be a little disappointed with this one, he is still in his full singer songwriter mode occasionally channelling his inner Anthony Newley. It does take a little head adjustment and a number of plays for these songs to get into the zone with them but indicators to the future are all there.

Of course it does have Space Oddity the song that put him on the map, which while not having the aura it did fifty years ago it is still one of his best known and best loved songs.

Accompanied by that interesting assemblage of musicians this is not the fully developed Bowie familiar to all, here he is following the familiar singer songwriter tropes of the time arguably without the magic and familiarity that Ronson and Co would bring on later when everything would crystallize with Hunky Dory.

This is definitely one of those albums where familiarity improves the listening experience, a bit of a let down initially. To varying degrees Unwashed and Slightly Dazed, Letter to Hermione, Memory of a Free Festival with its Hey Jude like coda, God Knows I'm Good and the gentle An Occasional Dream are ones to strike an immediate chord. The nine minute thirty Cygnet Committee is a tougher one but gives us an eye into the future, while Wild Eyed Boy From Freecloud is reminiscent of the lush orchestrated style of early Al Stewart or is it his homage to the Musical genre, not sure which.

I have to admit I was pretty disappointed with this initially, because it wasn't a Bowie I was familiar with, unjustifiably so as it turns out, as this is one of those occasions where familiarity breeds appreciation.

Hard pushed to give this 3 stars initially, now a solid 3.5 bar a couple of tracks pushing 4 stars.
