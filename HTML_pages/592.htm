This I believe is one of my favorite top 3 XTC albums. Very underrated, but most XTC fans agree that this one has some amazing songs. This is the first of their albums that is really enjoyable all the way through, or is way more than just a curiosity at least. I do enjoy White Music and Go 2, but I'm not sure how many people would care about their existence if they weren't released by this band. Drums & Wires takes their quirky new wave or "post punk" type of sound from their last 2 records and uses it for some of the catchiest, most fun, clever, original and groundbreaking type of songs this band had yet to put out at this point.

Classics include their first big hit (in the UK at least) "Making Plans For Nigel". Also "Helicopter", "Scissor Man", "When You're Near Me..." and "Ten Feet Tall". All of these are so infectious and are like nothing I've ever heard anywhere else. Then you also have "Outside World", which is very punk rock, has some amazing guitar work and is unlike anything else they've ever done before or since, and the creepy "Complicated Game". In fact, this whole album sounds like nothing they've done before or since. It's a very unique record with lots of quirkiness, complex chord structures and hooks galore, and after this they haven't been this good since The Big Express as far as I'm concerned.

There's also some awesome bonus tracks as well like the universally loved "Life Begins At The Hop". Also "Limelight" and "Chain Of Command", which are just as great as any other upbeat catchy rocker that has made the original album.

It's hard to believe there was ever a time when Andy Partridge's personality wasn't so dominant in the XTC sound. Drums and Wires preceded both that era and their era of Sixties Beatles/psychedelic tributes. At this time, they were still playing solidly in the mo(u)ld of then-current British new wave; the kind that derived much of its sound from reggae, then sped up and given a harder drum and guitar kick.

Some of the original songs are somewhat weak, but they don't really interrupt the general flow of their quirky sound. The bonus tracks include two songs that are as good as many of the proper cuts, as well as a somewhat forgettable dub experiment.

The third XTC record was a considerable improvement over Go 2. Keyboardist Barry Andrews had been replaced by guitarist Dave Gregory, so the sound had been completely revamped. It was still new wave, but guitar new wave more than keyboard new wave. The common 1979 British New Wave band use of reggae accents in some of the beats and guitars was still there, though.

But this is something more than new wave. Even hit "Making Plans for Nigel" showed something special was underway. "Roads Girdle the Globe" is the best delight for me. The album isn't quite as sophisticated as the pre-psychedelia XTC albums Black Sea or English Settlement would be, but it is just as timeless, because it contains elements that are lacking on their later, greater albums. Like humor. And there's a quirky, jammy appeal to most of the songs that they would never quite revisit again. Even the weaker cuts still make me smile sometimes, because of that fast lost-forever interplay between guitar, bass, drums and whatever little other tricks they felt they needed.

The closest thing to this band's sound from 1979 would probably be Fischer-Z.

Grampus Jun 15 2008
+3 Vote downBookmarkVote up
After the staccato punk of White Music and the mechanical beat of Go 2 XTC finally settled for Drums And Wires quirky intelligent pop which would stand them in good stead for the remainder of their career. I think it's fair to say no other British band has come close to emulating the skewed world inhabited by Andy Partridge and Colin Moulding (The Kinks?) and I can think of only They Might Be Giants and, possibly, Devo world-wide. Like the Beatles' loopy cousins sending messages from a parallel universe, Drums And Wires is an absolute revelation. Covering the drudgery of factory work ("Day In Day Out"), the relentless domination of the automobile ("Roads Girdle The Globe"), the lack of privacy ("Real By Reel") and the overwhelming emotion of being in love ("When You're Near Me I Have Difficulty", "Ten Feet Tall") as well as other strangely off-centre subjects, the album is the epitome of wacky pop.

Between Go 2 and Drums And Wires the band had undergone a personnel change. Out of the door went keyboard player Barry Andrews and into his shoes stepped Dave Gregory not a straight swap because Gregory was more of a guitarist who could, if need be, play the keyboards. The result is a more stable, rounded sound providing songwriters Partridge and Moulding the foundation on which to build their weird and wonderful creations.

The thing I love about this band the most is the way they play with the English language; manufacturing words to suit the occasion; using alliteration to emphasise their point; choosing their words as much for their sound as their meaning. Take "Helicopter" the story of a girl let loose on the world after a sheltered upbringing (I think) the line "she a laughing giggly whirlybird. She got to be obscene to be obheard" shouldn't work but just seems to capture the mood perfectly.

Another fact to be aware of is neither Partridge nor, especially, Moulding can really sing. They reach for notes and sometimes fail to find them. They speak rather than sing, voices sounding like, during puberty, they failed to break completely leaving them in a halfway house where both highs and lows are a stretch. The beauty of the situation being it suits the band perfectly. Not only are the songs bizarrely brilliant but so are the vocalists.

The other point of note about Drums And Wires is the way Moulding has stepped up to the plate in terms of songwriting. No longer is his work secondary to Partridge. "Ten Feet Tall", "Day In Day Out", "Complicated Love" and, of course, "Making Plans For Nigel" are all memorable. As a result - more so than on their earlier work - XTC are now more of a band.

Drums And Wires isn't XTC's greatest work that was still to come but, as a signal of intent, it sent out a clear message that a major player had arrived in the world of pop. That they never achieved their potential was as much their fault as it was their prospective audience.

Very good, also very 80s in a good way. It sounds like they were very aware of and picked up what went on musically around that time (The Police, Talking Heads, Public Image Ltd, The Specials all come to mind) and make a new, original yet referential pop record out of all of it. If you listen closely, you'll even hear the roots of what a lot of bands, from British 80s outfits like Savage Progress up to current bands such as Clap Your Hands Say Yeah or Franz Ferdinand are building their music on. Don't think that "Making Plans for Nigel" is the best song, because it isn't. There is a lot of great stuff on this album.
