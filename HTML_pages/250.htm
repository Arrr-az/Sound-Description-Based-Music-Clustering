Before I start this, I would like to take a moment to discuss something that's been bugging me, and that is the number of users who spend just as much time talking politics in their reviews, lists, profile pictures, etc. as they do music. I have no objection to the occasional passing reference to your personal politics - I think I myself, for instance, have made it pretty clear that I'm pretty far to the left - but there are users here who let this get out of control. I don't care about the fact that some of these users express views that differ from mine - it's just that I don't feel reviews, lists, and whatnot are the proper place to express these concerns. That's what the political boards are for.

Anyway, this is THE classic P-Funk release. It's often called the best album Jimi Hendrix never made, but I think that really undersells the originality here. I mean, for all the genre-busting that Hendrix was getting into later in his career, I can't see him pulling off my favorite song on this album. Which, believe it or not, isn't even that title track: it's the one after, "Can You Get to That," a downhome country-folk-blues with Motown-inspired vocal harmonies and the catchiest intro EVER. And while most of the rest is in acid rock mode, the band's roots in Motown are pretty strong (check out the bridge on the thudding, piano-driven "You and Your Folks, Me and My Folks"), as is the garage rock influence. For instance, "Hit it and Quit It" (which totally fucking RULES) is based on an organ that could've escaped from an Iron Butterfly song. Oh, and the guitar solo? Cool as hell!

But there's no doubt that Eddie Hazel was channeling Hendrix when he sat down to record "Maggot Brain." Even then, though, he still brings plenty of Eddie Hazelness to the proceedings. I'm not a big Wilson and Alroy fan, but I think the phrase they used to describe the song - "Wild tenderness" - fits it perfectly. We've all heard the "play like your mama just died" story. Both it and the resulting guitar solo were probably the result of a lot of acid, but hey, it's cool! And the solo, of course, is pure genius. Eddie runs through the full emotional spectrum, from the depressing beginning to the triumphant end. It really is something to hear.

Actually, the entire first side is brilliant. The whole album is, in its own way, but especially that first side. "Can You Get to That," as I already mentioned is arguably even better than "Maggot Brain," and that's saying something. Then you've got "Hit it and Quit It," which I also mentioned above, but I'd like to emphasize just how great that riff is, how awesome Eddie's soloing sounds, and how much FUN it is. Pure chaos? Kind of. But FUN chaos, the kind of chaos this band does best. And "You and Your Folks" just rules all over the place, a classic psychedelic soul number with awesome echoey piano.

Side two is when things get really weird. The guys ease you into it with the big, stomping, completely insane rocker "Super Stupid," which is incredibly loud and really, really cool. Eddie sings! And, in an alternate universe, it might even be considered a proto-metal kinda thing. Oh yeah, and it's about a guy who buys the wrong drug by accident. Don't think too hard about the lyrics But then it takes an entertaining left turn. "Back in Our Minds" has a hilarious trombone solo and a twanging... well, I don't even know what that instrument is, but it twangs, that's for sure. After that comes the album's most universally hated song, "Wars of Armageddon." Personally, I like it. Sound collages usually aren't my thing, but you've got a cow mooing and a cuckoo clock here. So it's not an overly serious, "Revolution 9," higher art type thing. It's just a bunch of guys getting zonked and having fun.

Actually, that's this whole album. A bunch of really talented musicians get really, really high and play whatever they damn well please. This kind of thing obviously isn't the most reliable way to get great results, but here, it fucking works, and results in one of the all-time classic funk albums. Come on, maggot brain. Go on, maggot brain.

Or: How that title track will grab onto your mind, knead it, bend it backwards, stretch it, use it as a hackeysack, and give it back to you when it's done.

Track picks: Plenty can be said for all seven, actually.

This is the one perfect, totally essential release of the always entertaining but often unfocused P-Funk collective. The famous title track shows off some electric guitar virtuosity that really rivals Hendrix, and for a full 10 minutes, really wringing out a surprising amount of emotion and drama from the electric guitar... it's deservedly entered the canon of amateur teen guitarist gods. "Can You Get to That" is an anthem, and "You and Your Folks, Me and My Folks" shows a knack for combining thoughtful social critique with irresistible, booty-shakin' rhythms on par with Sly Stone. Wars of Armageddon is the least interesting track; it's unnecessarily long and may suffer a bit in comparison to the fantastic jam that opens the album, but it's still a good listen. Overall, this is one of those rare albums that can provide a party soundtrack no one would object to, as well as some deep headphone analysis.

George Clinton was so fried on drugs his mind probably wasn't capable of much foresight, but you can't help but feel he knew Maggot Brain would be his finest achievement. Why else would he get the fantastic Eddie Hazel to play a 10 minute guitar solo? Why else would he tell Eddie to play the first half as if he'd just found out his mother had died, and the second as if he'd found out she was alive after all? And why else would he then put it as the first track on the album, making it completely unavoidable to any newcomers approaching the record? The whole thing smacks of inspired insanity.

The title track ins't the only thing on Maggot Brain, but it can feel that way a lot of the time. It is, after all, a damn good contender for the best guitar solo of all time; an emotional rollercoaster soaring over a minimalist, almost post-rock backing. And Clinton obviously wanted it to be the focal point of the record.

Still, there is more to Maggot Brain. Every other track is a raucous, psychedelic, funky classic, with "Hit It & Quit It" and the blistering "Super Stupid" being the highlights. The spirit of the 60s is very much alive in this record, but it's fused to Clinton's interstellar preoccupations ('I have tasted the maggots in the mind of the universe....') and innate funk. You could say it brought psychedelia right up to date for the 70s, but it still sounds futuristic today.

The greatest funk album of all time? Quite possibly.

"Mother Earth is pregnant for the third time. For y'all have knocked her up."

Funkadelic is a band with a very unique sound and great versatility. They're so unique, I have a difficult time describing their genre to people who haven't heard them. Funk, rock, psychedelic/experimental? Whatever they are, they are original above anything else.

_Maggot Brain_ is an album I basically neglected after I first got it. I was bored by the really long songs and the short ones weren't that great, except "Can You Get to That" which I have always loved. The reason that I wasn't impressed was that I didn't really know what to think. I was expecting James Brown-esque funk based on the band's name. After listening more to _Maggot Brain_, I still don't absolutely love this album but it has grown on me quite a bit.

It's hard to deny that the title song isn't pretty fuckin' cool. The stereo delay on the guitar and the unbelievably talented Eddie Hazel make this one of the better epic guitar solos I've heard. Generally, ten minutes of guitar solo bores me, but I do enjoy "Maggot Brain." As I said before, I have always been a fan of "Can You Get to That." Acoustic guitar is so unexpected after the first song and suddenly we have a happy song. The lyrics are great in this one, not to mention Clinton's voice in the cellar.

"Hit It and Quit It" is the song that introduces the awesome organ in the Funkadelic sound. The song is probably the most funky on the album and features another amazing Eddie Hazel solo. "You and Your Folks, Me and My Folks" is okay but rather repetitive. I like the weird drum sound and the minimalist piano, but they could have made it more varied. "Super Stupid" reminds me of Jimi Hendrix a lot, which is definitely a good thing. Again, the organ just makes every song awesome. I'd imagine hearing this song live would be incredible.

"Back In Our Minds" sounds like the guys weren't quite back in their minds yet. It's kind of a joke song that follows the story of a guys who got the wrong drug. You can actually hear some lyric mess ups if you listen carefully to this song. "Wars of Armageddon" is the way Frank Zappa would end an album. It's quite weird and rather funny. "More power to the pussy, more pussy to the power" followed by a cat's meow. That must have been the pussy they were talking about!

The bonus tracks aren't that great other than "Maggot Brain" of course. Nothing to be amazed about. _Maggot Brain_ is about half and half with me. Half is great and half is only okay. I'm glad I had to review this album for now I understand what Funkadelic is, at least more than I did.
