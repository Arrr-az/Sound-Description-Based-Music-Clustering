I'm glad to see this is rated so high on RYM. I've seen reviewers give this the cold shoulder because it's not one of the Beatles' original British releases - it was cobbled together for the American market. Regardless, it's now considered part of the official canon (I believe it's the only "American" release to be included) and has been released on CD along with the Beatles other albums.

The fact is this is a much better album than Sgt. Peppers. I know, the historical significance of this release is comparatively negligible, but if you just compare the music on the two albums, this one is hands down better. I think it's appropriate to directly compare the two albums because they're from the exact same period in the band's career (both were released in 1967) and have a very similar style. Several of the songs here are actually outtakes from the Pepper sessions - "Strawberry Fields Forever" and "Penny Lane". In fact, Sgt. Pepper would have been improved if they had included either of these songs in place of some of the weaker tracks on that album. There really are only two weak songs on this - "Flying" and "Baby You're a Rich Man". These aren't bad, just nothing special. Everything else on this is fantastic. You have some of Paul's best sweet songs that are beautiful but still retain enough edge to avoid being totally saccharine - "The Fool on the Hill" and "Your Mother Should Know". Many people have commented on Paul's melodic gifts. Give these two songs a listen and you'll know why. They're just astonishing. Another example, take the first song on this - "Magical Mystery Tour". Knowing that this album was released right after Sgt. Pepper, you could dismiss this as Paul trying to re-capture the feel of the prior album by starting off with a similar title track. But this song is far superior to "Sgt. Peppers Lonely Hearts Club Band" and is probably one of the very best psychedelic pop songs ever made.

Side two (my copy is on vinyl) just goes from one amazing song to the next - you'd think this is a greatest hits album! It closes with "All You Need Is Love" which, although perhaps not John's very best song, could certainly be seen as the perfect summation of 1967 in popular music. It even has a bunch of other musicians (including Mick Jagger) singing backing vocals and when Paul starts into the chorus from "She Loves You" near the end, it fits perfectly - reaching back to the Band's earlier period - and sounds absolutely genuine, intuitive and spontaneous.

Overall, this album is full of some of the Beatles very best work from their middle period. I would say this has to stand as one of the absolute best psychedelic pop albums ever made.

Following the roaring success of Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band, it seemed unlikely that they could reach those heights again. Not long after its release, their manager, Brian Epstein, died, leaving the group despondent and rather aimless in the short term. To try to regroup, Paul McCartney came up with the idea to make a film, a psychedelic bus trip, to be titled Magical Mystery Tour. The film ended up being as aimless as the group was after Epstein's death, and was deemed to be the first flop of their impeccable career to date. Fortunately, the film was accompanied by a nice little set of songs.

Magical Mystery Tour has always been a strange entry in The Beatles' discography, and, as such, is never given the full regard it deserves. It was first released in the United States as an eleven song LP, consisting of seven songs from the film and four songs that had previously been released as singles (Penny Lane / Strawberry Fields Forever and All You Need Is Love / Baby You're a Rich Man -- crammed at the end of the record). The EP, consisting of six songs from the film (minus "Hello Goodbye," which plays only during the credits), was released in England two weeks later. The eleven song version became so popular in England that it reached the lower end of the charts in 1968. By 1976, the US version was finally available in England, and would subsequently be viewed as an official entry in the British discography.

Compared to their three previous albums, Magical Mystery Tour seems shockingly out of step. Rubber Soul, Revolver, and Sgt. Pepper's--with a few minor exceptions--were masterpieces of sequencing. The tracks on these albums flow so smoothly between each other they resemble classically composed suites. Magical Mystery Tour completely lacks this sense of unity. With the exception of the transition between the instrumental track "Flying" (the first officially released instrumental by the group, and the first to have all four Beatles share a songwriting credit) and George Harrison's proto-ambient "Blue Jay Way," there's little sense of what the group had achieved on these previous records. Also, the fact that the last six songs consist of couplings of recently-released 45s (with the Penny Lane / Strawberry Fields Forever single dating back to before the release of Sgt. Pepper's) suggests a general laziness in this regard. As such, Magical Mystery Tour is practically an anti-album.

But it works. Why? In large part, it works because so much of this material is brilliant. Side One, which consists of songs from the film, at first glance appears like a significant drop-off in quality when compared to Sgt. Pepper's. The half-baked "Magical Mystery Tour" theme or concept never really emerges, but the tune is catchy and has drive. "The Fool on the Hill" is a soppy McCartney number that is saved by a nice lyric and occasional rhythmic punctuations. The aforementioned "Flying"/"Blue Jay Way" coupling would suggest The Beatles are on autopilot. Though they lack the complexity of the Sgt. Pepper's material, they are more atmospheric than anything on that album. "Your Mother Should Know" is another McCartney number harkening back to "When I'm Sixty Four." This one, though, has a bit of an edge, especially when Lennon sings "Your mother should ..." during the chorus, which suggests the song is as nostalgic as it is a maternal insult.

Side One ends with "I Am the Walrus." With this track, the album begins an unprecedented string of songs, four to be exact, and arguably four of the best songs of the Twentieth Century. This Lennon composition is rather minimal compared with some of the Sgt. Pepper's material, though it does feature strings and an excerpt from a BBC radio production of William Shakespeare's King Lear. The lyric is Lennon's most explicitly Carroll-esque to date, and obliquely captures the beneficent madness of The Summer of Love. Few songs from the period outside of Bob Dylan have this many memorable, striking couplets. Side Two begins with another McCartney pop masterpiece in "Hello Goodbye." Despite its slight lyric, the song's fusion of rock and classical sounds is absolutely infectious. Try getting this out of your head. The next, "Strawberry Fields Forever," is arguably the group's greatest recorded achievement (and, for the record, my favorite song of all time). Lennon's ambivalent, practically inarticulate lyric ("I think I mean ah yes but it's all wrong / That is I think I disagree"), coupled with McCartney's spooky Mellotron lick and George Martin's partially frightening string arrangement, create a psychedelic masterpiece that lacks the sunny disposition of its historical moment, hinting at the darkness to come. Not to be overlooked are Harrison's lead guitar parts and Ringo Starr's spastic drums. Though this song has Lennon's stamp all over it, it is also a stellar group performance, with each member contributing substantially to it. This song is hard to top. But if one were to try, "Penny Lane" is a good place to start. McCartney's baroque pop masterpiece contains his best lyrics in the psychedelic idiom ("Though she feels as if she's in a play / She is anyway," for example). Usually parochial tunes like this drive me crazy, but the group is locked in here, with McCartney contributing some of his best bass runs to date. Comparatively, the album ends with a few duds. "Baby You're a Rich Man" is one of The Beatles' lesser tunes from this era, and is saved by a thumping chorus. "All You Need is Love," which closes out the album (and was in-part recorded live as part of the first TV event to be globally televised via satellite), is, on paper, a bad idea, a glib hippy anthem. It totally is. But, thankfully, Lennon's throwaway verses and Martin's tongue-in-cheek arrangement (which prominently features Bach's "Marseillaise") give it a charm it should seriously lack.

In short, the songs on Magical Mystery Tour don't flow well into each other and lack any sense of conceptual cohesion. There is no rhyme or reason to the track sequencing. But, owing to the sheer strength of the songs, it works. With this many good songs, I guess it doesn't matter what order they are in.

Its not easy to write anything about The Beatles. What else could you write that wasnt written before?

So, instead of trying to do something new I will just give my impressions on Magical Mystery Tour (1967) and give you some data.

This album turned out to be my favorite Beatles album. Its really hard to actually chose a favorite album from the Fab 4, but it must be this one for me!
Magical Mystery Tour (1967) was released in November of that year and its supposed to be the soundtrack of the movie with the same name. I never really watched the movie, but I dont really think I need to.
Recorded in a year period (between November 66 and November 67) this album is considered by many people as a leftover from Sgt. Peppers Lonely Hearts Club Band (1967), released in June of that same year and recorded in the same sessions.
To me this album is side by side in terms of quality and innovation!

Is almost impossible to put ourselves back in 1967 and try to understand the impact of this music if you (like me) wasnt born back then.
The album as we know now wasnt the official package release. The Beatles, once again ahead of their time, released the album in the UK as a double EP with gatefold sleeve and a 28 pages booklet.
Capitol (their American label) didnt like this format and package everything together with the non-album singles Beatles released that year, against the band wishes.

Originally the double EP had: Magical Mystery Tour,Your Mother Should Know (Side 1), I Am The Walrus (Side 2), The Fool On The Hill, Flying (Side 3) and Blue Jay Way (Side 4).
But on the American LP (and later CD official release) we have Magical Mystery Tour, The Fool On The Hill, Flying, Blue Jay Way, Your Mother Should Know and I Am The Walrus on Side 1 and the singles on the Side 2: Hello Goodbye, Strawberry Fields Forever, Penny Lane, Baby Youre A Rich Man and All You Need Is Love.

And I think that the two worlds together is what makes the album so good, the new compositions and the great singles never released on album before.
The Beatles were in their peak when it comes to imagination, everything sounds so fresh, even today!
A high 5 that have to be praised in the next 10 centuries or so.
