No doubt about it - Iggy moved way out of his paradigm with the Idiot, an album that defiantly refused to be categorized as rock. So he moved right back to it here, and while I'd normally complain about him retrenching, it's only a small step down from The Idiot. That's mostly because The Idiot is incredible, but also because "Turn Blue" is far too long for its own good - it's sort of this album's "I Need Somebody," but "I Need Somebody" is a far better Doors imitation, and besides, this album features a far better Doors imitation itself.

I'm talking of course about "The Passenger." With the possible exception of "Dum Dum Boys," this has got to be my favorite of Iggy's solo songs. It's built around this badass chord sequence (starting with the minor's always a good call in my book) and features one of the all-time best vocal melodies Iggy ever wrote, as well as one of the all-time best vocal performances he ever put on. It's dark, mysterious, suggestive, and almost too cool for words. Along with "Lust For Life," with its killer drumbeat (later ripped off by Jet, a shitty piece of shit band who never had an idea to call their own) and Iggy proving himself the coolest guy ever. Yeah, it's in some commercial or other, but who really gives a shit? I'm too busy marveling over how cool of a line "I'm worth a million in prizes" is and letting Iggy's infinite charisma take over to give a shit about such things. Dude was the fuckin' coolest, we all know that, let's move on. Although I do wonder why he makes such a big deal about having been fucked in the ear. Apparently this happens to all modern guys?

So okay, "Tonight" and '"Neighborhood Threat" have been dragged through the mud a little by Bowie. I mean, I love the man dearly and I hate ever saying anything bad about it, but let's face it, Tonight was shit, the covers on Tonight were double-shit, and the Iggy covers on Tonight were triple-shit. But the ORIGINALS? Oh, now there's a different story entirely. "Neighborhood Threat" kicks so much ass that I'll forgive it for the "kiss your trash" line - you've gotta wonder why Iggy decided to throw it in, but this version is so cool I can forgive it. And the '50s-flavored "Tonight" is magnificent, with Iggy just belting it out and the synths reigning over basically everything. What's weird is that it sounds a hell of a lot like a Bowie song (think "Teenage Wildlife" in particular), which gets me wondering how it was that Bowie fucked it up so bad. Oh well - the point is, the original is great. I'll stop making fun of Bowie now, too, because he really is great.

Onto the rest! "Some Weird Sin" definitely brings the strangeness of The Idiot back. It's jagged, jumpy, and discombobulating, and it sounds even weirder surrounded by the more conventionally punk-oriented but still excellent material that takes up the rest of the album. As traditional rockers go, "Sixteen" is invigorating, and "Success" is all sorts of fun - I especially love the backing vocals. "Fall in Love with Me" works, too. It just about predicts the garage rock revival of the late '90s-early '00s! And look, I know the Strokes turned shit after Is This It, but we've gotta give him credit for helping bring Is This It into existence, don't we? That was a great album!

But really, it's all about the title track and "The Passenger." I mean, it's all good and some of it's great, but those two really elevate it to a minor classic in the rock 'n' roll canon, although not quite a major classic like Fun House, Raw Power, and The Idiot. People tell me it's his last grasp at greatness, and maybe they're right. But hey, what a fine last grasp it is.

Track picks: "Lust for Life," "The Passenger," "Tonight," "Success," "Neighborhood Threat"

I bought this album in Italy over the Easter break and nly listened to it yesterday, about three months later, not because I wasn't interested in it ( I wouldn't have bought it otherwise) but because I have this stupid way of picking out albums to listen to where I write the titles of the albums I have on small pieces of paper and roll them up. Then, I put them all in a hat nd pick one out. Lust for Life would just never come up, but that's the same way in which it took me about five months to listen to Abbey Road and Blonde on Blonde.

Anyways. Who cares.

The album, right.

I got this album because all the songs I hear in it I absolutely love. I knew Lust for Life, which wa and still remains my favourite song by Iggy Pop during his solo career, and I loved Some Weird Sin, The Passenger, Tonight and Success. They were all tracks that were in Iggy Pop's famous anthology Million in Prizes (a phrase from Lust for life). Well, the band news I came across upon listening to this album is that, apart from Turn Blue (whose lyrics, strangely enough, are the only ones not featured on the booklet, although they are brilliant) do not hold up with the songs I already knew. Still, the other weaker tracks aren't even that weak after all.

As in Transformer by Lou Reed, the mark of David Bowie is evident, although I haven't heard The Idiot, and I would beluieve that in this album there would be more Iggy in it than the previous one. All songs follow the same recipe. Some heavy guitar riffs and drumbeats, in the style or rock boogie, and Iggy screaming in a low toned voice, lines that seem visions from drug use in some songs, but also scenes that find their place in the underground of big cities, like Tonight, which tells the story of a man who sees his love "turn blue", basically o.d.ing in front of him, and he whispers in her ear "everything will be alright tonight" because she will be freed basically. It's a touching song, which contrasts with the madness of Lust for Life, where Iggy Pop puts together a lot of lines that seemingly wouldn't go too well together, but somehow are really tied up with the title of the song itself, and contribute to the song sounding like a musical version of an edrenaline rush. The poet that is in Iggy Pop, however, comes out with The Passenger, and this is the song in which he sounds the most like jim Morrisson. In this song he sings of something that sounds like a trailer park white trash Romeo that has nothing much material, but feels fulfilled thinking that he can still look at the stars and even the city's ripped backsides throught the windshield of his car.

This album further proves my theory, the theory I have of the 70's rock music TRINITY. I will explain it to you.

Lou Reed - DAVID BOWIE - Iggy Pop

Lou Reed was the man who sang about the junkies of the undergrounds in the city, the ordinary people of seventies social glam life. David Bowie was really the nobleman of the social life of New York, and represented the crowd that dressed up glamorously, although they may not even have had enough money for food, but had enough to get high at parties. Iggy represented the outskirts of that social life, the "idiot" as such, of court, ones that weren't so well accepted into society, but snook their way in anyways.

Because I know Bowie is a smart man, I know that he thought about this too, and this is why he associated himself with Reed and Pop, in ding so, he became their 'creators', and in the social life of the seventies, he was the king.

But back to the album, this is certainly worth listening to. It's got a certain heavy sound that isn't exactly as raw or as exciting as something by The Stooges, but finds its mix with the glam rock that Bowie was making at the time. It's very representative of a certain world that has, perhaps disappeared for a good reason, but kind of sucked the life out of music, because it's possible that the day drugs became less popular became the day that retards started taking them, and they in return did nothing for the world with them.

ALBUM COVER - A black and white picture of a junkie. Scary stuff, Iggy was never really the hottest guy in town. The cover is just awful, and half arsed.

I know Iggy Pop wanted David Bowit to have less of a grip on this album, but I feel if anything this just feels like if David Bowie had continued his glam-ish hard rock rather than experimenting with different musical mediums in the late 70s. That leads to a record that has got some great emotion and gripping parts of it, as well as that Stooges edge that can be enthralling if done right. It does lose me a little on some of the lyrical choices (especially on songs like Sixteen) and a good chunk of songs losing me when they were oh so close to bringing me in, but this is still a rocking tune that feels like the perfect blend between that proto-punk sound and that more intense grip music had going into the 80s, and I think it pulls it off really well.
