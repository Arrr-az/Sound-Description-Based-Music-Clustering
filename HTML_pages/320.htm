"Hey baby, why don't you make like Ian Curtis and... hang around? "

"Who's Ian Curtis?"

"The front man of this GREAT band"

"Oh. Why would it be 'making like Ian Curtis' if I were to hang around?"

"Because Ian Curtis hung himself in 1980, he was a manic depressive who suffered from epilepsy and his entire life was a constant struggle with this darkness that engulfed him. So, wanna go out some time?"

Yeah, Joy Division pick-up lines DO NOT work well.

When you talk about Joy Division, they have two albums that are so incredibly close in terms of quality it's hard to choose a favourite, but they're also so similar musically it's quite hard separate them. Closer was released in June 1980, and it has a certain mystique around it. Ian Curtis killed himself in May 1980, and Closer was to be released in the June so it was always going to have that mystique around it and go down in musical history in one way or another, for better or worse. Sometimes when bands release 2 albums that are so incredibly similar, the latter can feel a little too derivative and people can listen to it and think 'yeah, we've already heard this before' but Joy Division manage to make 2 incredibly similar albums and they both have their own intrigue and pull. Closer has everything Unknown Pleasures has - but more. It's more intense, its sense of isolation and desperation is amplified, it's more brooding, the lyrics feel even more depressing but it's also more inaccessible, and the one thing it doesn't have is the songwriting of Unknown Pleasures.

But what Closer has is a cohesiveness about it, the music and the vocals seem to immerse and it has this devastating atmosphere about it. I think it's the most bleak album you could ever listen to, even though I definitely think Unknown Pleasures is better artistically and musically I think Closer's intensity and sense of desperation is unrivalled, it's something I'd never heard before and it's something I've never heard since. A truly special album.

One of the greatest cliches anyone could ever come out with in talking about rock music is to call Joy Division a 'depressing' band. I remember people used to say it back in the 80s, and I'm surprised people aren't sick of that adjective yet. To me what is truly depressing is the junk food music which constitutes the vast majority of the Top 40 charts these days. There you will see record companies trying to flog terrible music with lots of boobs, muscles, tight-fitting outfits, sports-cars and anything else they can think of to make kiddies want to buy it. They sure aren't going to do it on the strength of the music alone. Popular music becomes a cynical exercise in marketing, rather than something to inspire you or make you think or feel something new.

Now I understand that the subject matter is dark here, but it is inaccurate to say that it is just maudlin self-pity. If you listen closely to Closer, you will soon realize that Curtis' lyrics aren't just about his own pain. The horror show of history is put on display on the album-opener Atrocity Exhibition and the angry, abrasive Colony. Furthermore, like Radiohead after them, Joy Division use technology to make music about the alienating effects of technology. Electronica, distorted guitars and machine beats create an eerie, alienating post-industrial soundscape.

Yet for all the innovation, Joy Division were a rock band and they possess virtues common to other great rock musicians. Twenty Four Hours has driving rhythms and brilliantly layered guitar effects, and Decades and The Eternal have hauntingly beautiful melodies. This is an iconoclastic record, a challenging record, but it is a masterpiece nonetheless.

"For entertainment they watch his body twist...this is the way, step inside."

Those lyrics are your effectual invitation to put this album on and lose yourself in it. Thing is, Closer can be too overwhelming and weighty to bear if you let it. It's as close as I'll ever get to going into an insane asylum and watching in horror all of the inmates bouncing against the walls and wailing about incoherent things. The dark abyss is easy to fall into. But strangely addicting, too.

"I'm doing the best that I can...I'm ashamed of the person I am. Isolation. Isolation."

Closer starts out sounding anxious and gradually becomes more comfortable with itself, like a nervous stray dog that takes a while to warm up to you and trust you. "Passover," for example, is a little less manic that the two songs before it. But no less rigid. Throughout all of the album, the drums are robotic and precise (I love the drumming on this album as much as any other album I own), and the guitars, razor sharp and abrasive. And Peter Hook's punchy bass lays the foundation for it all.

"Colony" and "A Means to an End" are both great songs, but I need to move on to the second half of the album.

"Heart and Soul. One will burn."

Beginning with "Heart and Soul," an even more ominous and dark mood emerges. It's a feeling of doom companioned with contentment - a feeling Ian achieved so well only because he seemed most comfortable in his own maniacal world. Ian's vocals are toned down as if he's finally got you alone and is talking to you like a trusted friend. When he sings the quoted line above, he's not telling you something you weren't already aware of. Rather, he's merely providing you a reminder, just in case you forgot.

Closer just feels so personal it's insane.

"Just for one moment, thought I'd found my way.
Destiny unfolded, I watched it slip away."

Next is my favorite Joy Division song, "Twenty Four Hours." It starts out like an average Joy Division song, but then bursts into a driving and determined verse with Ian's even more lowered and doom-filled voice. It's the most powerful song they ever wrote.

"The Eternal" slows the album to a funereal pace and a feeling of acceptance begins to replace doubt and fear.

"We knocked on the doors of hells darker chamber,
Pushed to the limit, we dragged ourselves in."

"Decades," another Joy Division favorite of mine, closes out the album. After Ian asks "Where have they been?" a couple of times, the instruments take the album home and lay it to rest, almost gently, at the end.

A darker, more disturbing, but extremely cathartic album I cannot think of. Closer shouldn't be the soundtrack for any person's life, but you'll never better associate with desperation and hopelessness than while listening to it.

Joy Division was as legendary, and in the spirit of the great bands that came out of the 60s psychedelic movement. The difference being that this band was dead serious about their music, life philosophy and the quality of material that they put out. They have become a mythical band for a couple of reasons ... One, they only put out two actual albums as a group, Unknown Pleasures and Closer, along with a mini disc that came with the paper backed book regarding the history and times of Joy Division. Two, they played so seldom live, and in such a confined area, that their performances have become the stuff of legend. And three, at the time there was no one like them, period. They seem to have arrived developed and fully formed as a new species of music to be recorded with and then disappeared with the death of Ian Curtis. I would venture that there is still Joy Division material out there that has not surfaced as of yet, only time will tell.

To say that their music wasnt happy would be an under statement. Their music was dark, moving, haunting, full of dispair, longing, self reflective and perhaps overindulgent (but thats not a negative here, we all need to overindulge sometimes). Even their choice of name, Joy Division, comes from a dark period in European history, where women were used as sex slaves for the pleasure of the Nazis in concentration camps. Ian was also epileptic, and the strobbing lights during live performances often causing seizers while on stage, adding to the demented spectacle unfolding in theaters. All of this created more sensation then one could hope for, and with their release and charting of "Love Will Tear Us Apart," the band was bound for glory. But glory ment coming to the United States and shaking our shoals, Ian killed himself right before that was to happen. Nevertheless, this band has deeply etched their place on the musical landscape of this planet, and in the lives of so many listeners across the world.

Their music is heavily laden with atmospheric conditioning by synthesizers, bass lines in time with heart beats, guitars that moved left, right and back to center, all with the low lovely voice of Ian holding his own in the mist of all these emotional spirals. His vocals were never mixed front and center, they were always a part of the production, his lyrics and vocals being used as any other instrument might be. One had to really listen to understand what was being said, to the point that most often the listener just drifted off into their own world as the music and lyrics entered our brains on a subconscious level.

This wasn't late in the night music only, this was solitary morning music, sunny afternoon music, keep you awake during the small hours of the night music. This music was so dark and warm, so perfect all of the time, one could not help but be drawn in. But it wasnt a place to stay, for if you stayed there too long there would be serious repercussions. Joy Division took you so deeply into regions of yourself, those places you wanted to see, places that you wanted to show to the world, but doubt, fear and the power of these places compels you to keep them hidden, even as Joy Division slides under fingernails and shows you the face in the mirror.

LOVE WILL certainly TEAR US APART ...

Mungbean's Happy Funtime Closer Quiz

1) Everybody knows that the opener is named after the Ballard novel, but what literary figure is Curtis thought to be referencing with the line "I still exist"?

spoiler: click to readIt was a favourite saying of Comte de Lautramont's - birth name Isodore Ducasse - Maldoror. A figure of absolute evil from an influential poetic novel whose imagery is both gothic and nihilistic. The parallels with the nightmarish "Atrocity Exhibition" are self-evident. Joy Division and Ducasse can claim to have played a part in the development of the gothic scene in music and literature respectively. The two-chord throb of "Heart And Soul", as well as Curtis' deep warble, mark it out as the most gothic song in the band's repertoire.

2) In the last few moments before pressing the album the final song was renamed. What was "Decades" previously called?

spoiler: click to read"Cross Of Iron". It's true of every song on any classic album ever, of course, but can you imagine Closer ending with "Cross Of Iron"? The song about young men with weight on their shoulders and hearts lost forever is called "Decades" and you wouldn't have it any other way. The one thing that makes it such a fitting title is that it contains one of the greatest fade-outs on record. A fade-out that, well, sounds like it could go on for decades.

3) Is it pronounced close-r or clozer?

spoiler: click to readPeter Hook clearly calls it clo-ser, so that would seem to be the best answer. This very debate occurred in my first year at RYM. It is that very ambiguity, however, which makes it one of my favourite album names. Clo-zer denotes the finality which the album has become synonymous, as well as the physical act of closing a tomb or coffin. Clo-ser is apt because this was a close as we were to get to Ian's warped world-view as, for him, the end drew ever closer. Better than Mellon Collie And The Infinite Sadness anyway.

4) The image of a tomb on the front cover was taken in which country?

spoiler: click to readGenoa, Italy. The album artwork was decided well in advance of Ian's suicide so while a dead frontman and the appearance of a tomb on the cover was problematic, Factory decided to respect Ian's wishes for the album. It really helps to complete the rich mythology of the album. An album which, it must be said, is hard to separate from that mythology and the tragedy of Ian's story when you're listening to it. Hard because it's leaking out of every bleak lyric, every angular riff and every mournful synth line.

5) How long after Ian died was this album released?

spoiler: click to readTwo months to the day. Ian hung himself on May 18th (1980) and the album was eventually released on July 18th. That makes it the posthumous release, of course. Those wishing to live fast, die by their own hand, and leave a pretty album would have to raise their game in future.

It takes a certain kind of person to appreciate Joy Division. People have told me before "I just don't get why Joy Division is so great" or "Ian Curtis is a terrible singer". And I have read reviews on here completely trashing this album, calling its fans "insipid" and "dull". But to me, Closer is the fullest realization of what Joy Division could have ever been. It is the closest musical reflection to the feeling of absolute hopelessness and despair that I have ever encountered. Its historical background (Curtis' suicide before its release) and the influence that it unquestionably generated just makes it all the more special to me. What turned me off from Closer initially was the ominous feeling that it so perfectly captured, and the mood of crushing depression that it created. This wasn't something I could appreciate at first. I think the main reason Closer remains so inaccessible and misunderstood is because in order to understand it, you have to empathize with it. Not many people are capable of this. Listening to it, especially the last 3 songs, is emotionally exhausting, and I don't think everyone can really handle or relate to the depth of feeling that Closer simulates so immaculately. It is a brooding, poetic masterpiece of morbid, hopeless beauty.

The first side of the album (the first 5 songs) are all some of the greatest post-punk songs ever written. That entire genre owes Closer for its raw sound and resentful power. "Atrocity Exhibition" is a chaotic, ominous narrative laced with industrial noise, accompanied by a tribal drumbeat. This shows how experimental Joy Division had started to become, abandoning the rock'n'roll sensibilities of Unknown Pleasures for a more organic, stripped-down sound. "Passover" is the greatest testament to Joy Division's ability to conjure up morbidity through sheer poetry, and the banging of thunderous, authoritative drums. Ian Curtis invokes all of the most deathly imagery he can, providing the biblical Passover as an analogy for autobiographical things that were going on in his life. "This is the crisis I knew had to come/ destroying the balance I'd kept/ turning around to the next set of lies/ wondering what will come next".

I feel like the second side of the album (the last 4 songs) is the most haunting succession of songs ever lined up on a single album. Nothing could be more uncompromisingly depressing than listening to it. "A cloud hangs over me, marks every move/ deep in the memory of what once was love". The song "Twenty-Four Hours" reeks of morbidity and suicide. It is one of the most ominous tracks on the album. "The Eternal" will forever be the most haunting and harrowing song that Joy Division ever made. Its depressive intensity is unmatched by anything else I've heard before. When I feel depressed, nothing resonates with me more than the lyrics "No words could explain, no actions determine/
just watching the trees and the leaves as they fall." This is the best way I've ever heard the feeling of depression described. Just when you think Closer could not get any more haunting or utterly hopeless, "Decades" starts off with the minimal percussion taps and elegant synthesizers, as Ian Curtis moans "We knocked on the doors of Hell's darker chamber/ pushed to the limit, we dragged ourselves in". It is so relentlessly funereal and full of sorrow. There is no relief from any of the ghostly brooding that the band generates in the last three songs, and yet, I think "Decades" is one of the most beautiful songs I've ever heard. It feels like I am exploring the depths of the darkest, eeriest catacombs, with nothing but a torch, encountering skulls, questioning the reality or feeling of death. "Decades" beautifully invokes the mystery of death in a way that nothing else has.

Closer was not something I really grew to appreciate so much until recently. Two years ago, I could not adjust or settle into its exhausting gloom. Now I realize just how relevant it is to the emotional spectrum of music. No other music is so uncompromising in its attempt to capture sheer hopelessness and despair.

You have come to the dark heart of rock n roll.

Well lets get into this. I read about this album in a New Music Express article in 1980, just after Ian had suicided. I still have vague memories of the article, which Im sure I still have filed somewhere, saying forget mainstream peddling of The Specials, The Pretenders and Madness as the new music.. Something important was happening up in Manchester.. On the passion of that article, I ordered Closer from Anthem Records, at that time only available in Australia on import..

Fast forward to 1981, and our skiing trip down the Snowies. There were six of us; four still into Supertramp, CSNY etc. Heck, my beloved unrequited love Tracey after (very respectfully) listening attentively to ten tracks from my truly awesome compilation tape of Boys Dont Cry (with the palm trees) and 17 Seconds said passionately, this band is awful! Humbly I allowed her words into my heart for contemplation. But.. well, I just played 17 Seconds again today.

Then there was my mate Dave, who was just coming out as gay. We drank a whole bottle of vodka together that night. Not a great deal to seasoned liverkillers, but at the time.. Tracey said disparagingly - to me - next morning of Dave, He was as close to alcohol poisoning as anyone Ive ever seen. They didnt like Joy Division, but Dave who famously once said: I always thought there was only a finite number of ways you could put notes together to make a song, until I heard King Crimson (thanks to us!!).. then I changed my mind! listened to Closer as we were sliding through the rolling gentle vistas of the Snowies and said he liked it.

As for me, I was on the point of madness from five years of unrequited love to Tracey. I drank my share of this Smirnoff with Dave, then headed out. 12.30pm at night, in nothing but shorts, a singlet and a light shirt barefoot just below the snowline. And walked and walked, praying to God a God who at the time I didnt even believe existed. Just praying. Because I had no one else to turn to, and nothing left to lose.

I walked and walked, slowly upward all the time, and came to a cliff. Below me stood the swirling ice of the Snowy River. I thought I would throw myself in. Then I thought .. there really is a serious possibility I really would drown..

Survival kicked in. I turned, back to the hut. Before me, as far as the eye could see, there was nothing. No light at all, but the stars.. the Southern Cross. I had walked over the horizon. The hut was gone.

I walked and walked, stumbling drunkenly, down and up two or three shallow valleys. Finally, there on the far left, way, way over from where I expected, right on the skyline, the loving calling lights of home, my friends.

In the morning, my first moment, Leef: Is McRae back yet? Yes, I rolecalled comatosely. I honestly'd no idea he cared.

Fast forward again to early 1984, summer in Sydney, a Hell Of a Summer, The Triffids.. Caved in to existential crisis upon seeing the Hindus and Muslims straight back to slaughtering each other after the visitation and supreme example of the Mahatma in Attenborough's film, RWR 666 at the helm and even that me caring not so much about as about another lost love: stoned out of my brain in complete darkness, listening to the second side of Closer, yea even the ghostly phase "The Eternal".. Suddenly, out of the darkness a voice, calling: "Sandy, Sandy.." Didn't just give a start, I full-on screamed.. screamed! And continued to scream. I was that terrified. It was the Angel of Death, coming for me.

But it was just my beloved Mark, just as troubled about Reagan - and unrequited love - as i was.. climbing in my window at 1am, in the hope of scoring a smoke, from my mighty psychedelic plants, which i'd been cultivating on my roof (another story!!)..

Poor Ian. On my Top 300 Songs of All Time, Ceremony clocks in at no. 1. He hung himself three weeks after he wrote it. Decades, from this album, slips in at 9. Same theme. Some rock stars are just posers. The greatest ones mean it. We knocked on the doors of Hells darker chambers/ Pushed to the limit, we dragged ourselves in.

Just listen to that twangy richenbacherish refrain against the drowning synth in the second verse of Decades.. as subtle as all get..

Poor Ian. I saw the film, at last, just about two years ago. Great eighties post punk black and white period piece.. steaming with hothouse anti-bloated-rich-rockstar indulgence, the way it was then .. yet the shrouding mystery remains..

Yes, marital issues. Yes, epilepsy. Yes, the pressures of touring and raising a young child, the expectations of the audience. But nothing figures the lyrics. See mass murder on a scale youve never seen/ All the deadwoods and jungles and cities on fire/ cant replace or relate/ cant release or repair/ Take my hand and Ill show you what was and will be. Could it be probably was poor Ian, actually saw. Too much. Too much darkness. But not enough corresponding light. Which does exist. Too much light to deny. Innocence Mission, Birds of My Neighbourhood.

In the film, I remember one line. Everybody hates me. No Ian. They all love you. They hate me, they hate me! That would do it.

Closer has it. If you want eighties synth-pop, but something that is also actually GREAT rock music.. look here. First.

Isolation rocks. As poppy as OMD, but great rock. Mother Ive cried for you believe me/ Im doing the best that I can/ Im ashamed of things Ive been put through/ Im ashamed of the person I am/ But if you could just see the beauty/ these things I could never describe.. And too right the entire band is sharp and syncopated as a razor blade that's absorbed its speed.

Colony is a flamin cooking tight rock song! And I'm thinking here's maybe one of the best clues on the album: "Family life just makes me feel uneasy." And A Means To an End. I put my trust in you.

Heart and Soul.. man, this whole band was so seriously darn cookin.. Journey that leads to the Son (?)/ soul that is bent on destruction/ struggle between right and wrong/ You take my place in the showdown (Calvary?)/ I'll observe with a pitiful eye/ and humbly ask for forgiveness/ a request well beyond you and I." Self-hatred, a fatal mistake and one of Satan's greatest duplicitous tools for sure.

Im here. Im 52 years old. Praise God, hallelujah, somewhat drunk in the sunshine today, considerably failed (too much damn rock music!!!!!), but at least alive.. writing this review.. which you most certainly would never be reading .... if .... So many happy days Ive had, years and years, with my friend Kathy, and other beautiful ladies, and my guy friends, and just alone, in the bush..

Were inside, now our hearts lost forever.

To this day, one of the ten best albums in rock.

Even if you suicide, even there, some of the NDEs inform us, all hope may not be extinguished. But the consensus is, no matter how bad life on this side seems, suicide is never a good idea. It tends to freeze you. You might even see your own funeral, the Western preacher's spouted platitudes: Hes at peace now, but you seeing into your mates minds, seeing them starting to believe it, screaming in anguish, "No no Im not at peace, Im not at peace!!" But they cant hear a word youre saying (Joyce Brown: Heavenly Answers to Earthly Questions, Signet, 2000, p 58-59).

Thanks youtube for refreshing me today on this album I havent heard for some fifteen years or so. But time passes very quickly, in some ways, later in life.

Now, whats up for my next rym review? Just at long last got me first ever John Denver compilation, and loving it absolutely to bits!

And Steven Morris is one of the great drummers of rock.

God please have mercy on Ian, even now..
