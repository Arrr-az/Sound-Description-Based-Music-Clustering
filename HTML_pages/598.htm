Tréboles de sangre
PUNK GAÉLICO. Este es el disco del comienzo de la leyenda. La primera piedra de una carrera fulgurante que colocaría a The Pogues como uno de los grupos más excitantes y adictivos de los 80. Una carrera basada en esa obsesión de Shane MacGowan por devolver el brillo y el prestigio a una música irlandesa a la que volvieron a poner en el mapa de una vez por todas. Aquí se nos presenta un grupo que se deja de experimentos o de grandilocuencias. Una banda que ha aprendido casi todo lo que sabe de los más viejos del lugar y que ha ingerido demasiados galones de punk de alta graduación como para que alguien tuviera los arrestos de decirles lo que tenían que hacer.

No creo que nadie se atreviera a guiar sus pasos, pero si lo hubo, no me cabe duda de que saldría escaldado. Simplemente no me viene a la mente ninguna banda que iguale a los Pogues en cuanto a conocimento del terreno que pisaban desde el segundo cero. Y por supuesto ninguna que se les acerque a la hora de tener tan clara una hoja de ruta que iba a quedar marcada a fuego por este inicio tan rotundo, tan enraizado y tan decidido. Punk gaélico se atrevieron a llamarlo. Sí, por mucho que no te quepa en la cabeza la relación entre este festival de violines, mandolinas y todo ese arsenal acústico a mayor gloria de los pastos de Irlanda, esto es punk por actitud y necesidad. Por la forma en la que se enfrentan a las canciones, yendo directos a la yugular, por darlo todo y todo de golpe, y por los gritos desvencijados de un Shane que no vocaliza ni por error, pero que desde el primer momento es el poeta laureado que te han contado.

Por todo eso, por sonar como si los Clash estuvieran tocando en una cantina en medio del desierto y por dejarte con tantas ganas de que esto no acabe nunca, The Pogues se licencian de forma prematura con una obra de arte que solo era el anuncio de lo que se nos venía encima. ¿Solo? Ni de broma. Démosles la bienvenida al grupo de elegidos que supieron debutar con una obra maestra incontestable.

Confessional...
The Pogues were a fantastic band, their first three albums were all all-time classics and would all appear very high in my own personal rankings, but when it comes to 'Red Roses For Me', I am sorry to say that I missed the boat, not once but twice. Here is my confession...


I saw The Pogues supporting Elvis Costello in Brighton, probably in 1983 or maybe in 1984. Entertaining as they were, I definitely wasn't ready to embrace this kind of music. It was much later that I got in to The Pogues, it was their third album that grabbed my attention 'If I Should Fall From Grace With God'. I loved that album and went back in time to pick up a copy of their second album 'Rum, Sodomy and the Lash'. BUT for some reason I never bothered with this debut album - a big mistake - it's fantastic. So there you go, I failed to pick up on this album twice (1. after the Elvis concert and 2. after hearing their third album).


Key Track: Poor Paddy

The Pogues were the first band to fuse the instrumentation, lyrical content, and melodies of traditional Irish music with the verve of punk rock. Though the entire band was comprised of musical adepts, it is frontman / primary singer-songwriter Shane MacGowan who deservedly receives the most praise and attention. Partially for his off-stage (hell, on-stage) antics, yes, but certainly also for his singular talent and vision. For MacGowan was one of the finest songwriters to emerge out of the punk generation, and undoubtedly stands among the titans of rock music more generally. He could write lively bouts of drunken revelry alongside poignant tales of the Irish underclass, finding universal themes of life, love, & loss in their particular circumstances (like many an Irish literary master before him), and often enough found time to wed the two; crafted infectious and recognizably Irish melodies liable to move you both physically and emotionally; handled tempo and dynamic shifts, both within and between tracks, with aplomb; and was nearly as comfortable with instrumentals as with songs. And further, he was so confident with covers that you'd be tempted to believe they were originals, which of course says more about execution than craftsmanship, but is nevertheless deeply impressive. One of the group's major virtues was to make originals sound traditional, and to make traditionals sound like they could be originals. And aside from his abilities as a songwriter/interpreter, MacGowan's voice occupies the unique position of being off-key enough to fit in with the punks while also being precious enough for the serious singer-songwriter crowd.

Now on Red Roses for Me, their debut album, MacGowan had not quite hit his stride (he would develop tremendously over the next two releases), but all of the above virtues are on clear display, albeit in a somewhat inchoate form. But Red Roses stands out for and leans into its drunken, punk'n energy and its youthful enthusiasm, the likes of which would never again be matched on a Pogues record. It's young and alive and boisterous and also sensitive, and it drinks too much because it doesn't fully know how else to process all of this, and it's a hell of a good time.

Rest in Peace Shane MacGowan.

The Pogues were an unusual band, Shane Mcgowan was steeped in Irish history and culture but he also understood the experiences of the Irish diaspora in London, the drinking and cash in hand work, the shebeens, the violence and the melancholy. The energy of punk was a huge influence too and is felt in the spirit with which they attack this stuff - a mix of McGowan songs, sea shanties and traditional Irish song.

Whilst not in the same bracket as 'rum sodomy and the lash' this one set out their stall from the start. It says something that even at this stage Shane's songs and ragged delivery were the best thing on the record - 'streams of whiskey' 'dark streets of London' 'down in the ground where the dead men go' and 'boys from the county hell' were all excellent and McGowans version of the Irish lament 'Kitty' would melt the hardest of hearts.

They Pogues set out to give pride back to the Irish communities in the UK and to reconnect them with their roots at the most difficult time - and boy did they succeed.

The Pogues es una banda de pop-punk-rock anglo-irlandesa con abundante música celta irlandesa. Inicialmente se llamaban Pogue Mahone (besa mi trasero en gaélico) y tras cambiar de nombre obligados por la discográfica debutaron en los años 80. Shane MacGowan (inglés de origen irlandés) era el líder y ejercía de cantante, guitarrista y principal compositor. Los otros músicos en sus inicios eran Spider Stacy (voz y flauta irlandesa, Jem Finer (banjo, saxofón, mandola y zanfona), James Fearnley (acordeón, mandolina, guitarra y piano), Cait O'Riordan (bajo) y Andrew Ranken (batería), Tenían serios problemas con el alcohol y se desfasaban en las fiestas e incluso en ocasiones hubieron broncas en los conciertos. "Red Roses for Me" supone una agradable revisión de la música tradicional irlandesa, con composiciones propias y alguna tradicional, con buenas canciones como los singles Dark Streets of London, Boys from Streets of London y la conocida Streams of Whiskey, además de The Auld Triangle. En otros momentos este trabajo me aburre un poco, aunque no quita que fue un debut prometedor.

- Temas destacables: Boys From the County Hell, Streams of Whiskey.

The Irish punk chanty is born! This is a magnificent, foot-stomping, Gunness-swilling blast, but note that there is intelligence, humor, and political consciousness at work as well. MacGowan manages to sound like a world-weary, philosophical, Anglo-Irish blues man as well as a carpe dieming party animal, and that is not easy to pull off. The band is tight and rollicking all the way thru. This is a great debut album.
