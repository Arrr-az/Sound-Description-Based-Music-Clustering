Warthur Jun 22 2012
+4 Vote downBookmarkVote up
The final Doors album isn't really the final Doors album - the band would soldier on without Morrison for a couple of years, and provide music to a spoken word album of his poetry in 1978 - but it is the last one the band is willing to stand by. The album is evidence as to why: what made the Doors really stand out from the crowd was the mysterious, wild persona of Jim Morrison himself, which shines through on this album without restraint or remorse to an extent it hadn't done on any previous album by the group.

Musically speaking, the previous album's evocation of their blues-rock roots has progressed to a full fusion of blues and rock, with two longer tracks (L.A. Woman and Riders On the Storm) being standout moments. The playing is decent enough, but the vital spark which really pushes this album to greater heights comes from Morrison himself, who grunts, yells, and roars his way through the material. Dropping the high priest act he'd previously adopted on some tracks, Morrison is less inclined to intone pretentious poetry in a stentorian manner and more inclined to just run wild, a mild change to his performance style which pays off marvellously. It might not be as psychedelic as their earlier work, but the Doors never hit the blues as hard as they did here, and it's an excellent final album from Morrison which deserves your attention.

LimedIBagels Apr 09 2012  
+8 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Before anything else gets said, it should be mentioned that this is a great road album. Driving, biking, walking, whatever; it's the kind of record that pushes you forward. And isn't that really one of the highest compliments you can give? 'I never been so broke that I couldn't leave town.' Wise words from a frequently unwise young man.

Okay, anyway. Calling this the band's best album might be arguable, but it's certainly their least dated one; the music itself has a friendly, communal feel that seems worlds removed from the in-your-face pretension of earlier work; Morrison's less showy in his lyricism, stating more than prophesying, and where even the great debut had some fairly goofy indulgences, the more conventional blues of L.A. Woman helps Morrison keep the album - at 48 minutes, their longest - running pretty smoothly the whole way through, and with a minimum of eye-rollers. Their formula is a practiced one by now, executed efficiently, propulsively, and often cheerfully (the last minute of "Hyacinth House" alone is sunny like nothing they'd done before), and anyone who's turned off by the decadence of "Light My Fire", "The End", or "When the Music's Over" might be surprised at how tight and punchy this material is. Even the particularly long songs, "L.A. Woman" and "Riders on the Storm", never get boring because they either swell naturally (in the former's case) or make use of gorgeous pings and glistens that intensify the imagery (in the latter's case).

This was the first Doors album to have a hired bassist playing the full way through, which means (among other things) that keyboardist Ray Manzarek - crucial as his bright psychedelic tones were to the group's sound - could now be more textured and spontaneous without sounding disjunct from Morrison's growly exclamations or guitarist Robby Krieger's stinging step-downs and slides. The boogie-woogie back-and-forth piano of the title track or the chorus hook of "Love Her Madly" (their last big hit) provide a more comfortable, mellower playing ground; voices and guitar licks rise in and out where you don't expect them, which lends a sense of constant shift even to the songs that rely on one central riff or bass line (witness the building menace of "L'America").

Sometimes the band overestimate their jamming abilities; "Been Down So Long" and especially "Crawling King Snake" could've been tightened-up without losing any fire, and none of these musicians were virtuosos. Morrison's delivery of "Hyacinth House" and even "Love Her Madly" is a bit lethargic and flaccid. (Probably not the only flaccid thing with that guy in the early '70s, amirite?) And there's still some laughable lyricism - 'his brain is squirming like a toad' is a real dud. But each track's method of letting you slip gradually into the groove like a warm bath - no morbid pun intended - makes even some of the more extravagant parts sound bad-ass. ('This is the land where the Pharaoh died.') The famous 'Mr. Mojo Risin' section in the title track (still pretty amazing that the anagram fit the man so perfectly) works because it's pitched like a unifying mantra. And where a few years earlier the band might've tried to milk the 'sonic boom' lines in "Cars Hiss By My Window", here Morrison just gives a matter-of-fact 'boom', and it hits harder as a result.

[Digression: If you're gonna get a version of this album, get the recent Rhino reissue. At first glance, it might seem completely unnecessary - there's already a remastered CD readily available, after all. But actually, Rhino's is the first L.A. Woman that leaves the original album untouched. When the Doors' catalogue was reissued five years ago, the band chose to remix and tinker with L.A. Woman, adding and cutting from a few tracks and including unused instrumental parts instead of leaving these versions as bonus tracks (as on all the others). I can't speak for you, but I generally hate it when they do this. It screws with a piece of cultural product in a way that feels like they're trying to whitewash mistakes or (more likely) trying to earn some extra hype $$$. (This is probably a good time to say that it's possible to care about music to an unhealthy extent.)

Rhino's version not only keeps the original mix intact, but also includes 51 minutes of bonus material that's actually a lot better than you might think. I have to admit I wasn't expecting the world from the additions; with the exceptions of the rote throwaway "She Smells So Nice" and the over-noodled "Rock Me" jam, they're all alternate takes of songs that made it onto the LP. And to the casual fan, most of the bonuses don't vary enough to be worth a full-on hunt. But for anyone who treasures this material a little more than average, this is surprisingly interesting stuff. The alternate versions of "The Changeling", "Riders on the Storm", "Car Hiss By My Window", and the inimitable title track intensify the close, 'sitting in the studio' feel without hurting any of the grander imagery. You can pretty much figure out why the band chose to leave these takes off the album: Morrison's voice is mixed a bit loud in the alternate "Changeling"; John Densmore's dusty drumming is sometimes a little sluggish in the alternate "Cars Hiss By My Window" (he goes for a slightly louder splash in the last 30 seconds and doesn't quite time it properly, even accepting his syncopated tendencies). But even those ones let out some great details that aren't available on the album. The tired, drunken giggles leading into "Cars Hiss By My Window" explain some of the song's dusty, intimate sound, and the mixing of Morrison's vocals allows his opening scream in the bonus "Changeling" to sound outright awesome. And the studio banter is entertaining as hell - Morrison prefaces "Changeling" with 'I hate to spook anybody, but this is my favorite number. Play your ass off, boy.']

You can almost see smiles on the non-singing faces here; the group seem to be content with themselves, getting a kick out of watching their crazy frontman burn through his excess and waiting to see where they'd go next. L.A. Woman is a great travel album because it hits the ground running - or at least strutting. It's as if Morrison woke up after a wild night and got on the road. He's gruffer now, and less 'literate.' Sinful? Maybe. But there's no remorse, or at least not nearly enough to hold him down. As David Fricke wrote for the liner notes, 'Morrison was ascending, proud and renewed, eager for the unknown ahead.' Of course, we know now that this 'ascent' didn't peak. But Morrison was so unabashedly 'live fast, die young' in his worldview that he anticipated the end before anyone. The best trips are the ones you barrel through without regret.

'L.A. Woman', the last Doors studio album (released in 1971), was their best since their 'Strange Days' release in 1967 (although I have sentimental attachments to both 'The Soft Parade' and 'Morrison Hotel'). Three months after the release of 'L.A. Woman', Jim Morrison's bloated corpse was found floating in a Paris bathtub. Maybe his death was a cover-up and he's living in Africa; either that, or he can be seen eating at a Burger King in Kalamazoo with fat old Elvis.

In any case, the album contains some of Morrison's best poetic imagery, particularly on the two rock epics "Riders on the Storm" (with its eerie lyrics punctuated by thunderstorms and jazz cadence) and "L.A. Woman" (with the greatest anagram in rock history Mr. Mojo Risin' = Jim Morrison). But blues is king on 'L.A. Woman'. I fondly recall my indescribable, jaw-dropping joy upon hearing "Been Down So Long" for the first time. Of course, I was stoned out of my mind at the time, but that's beside the point. The album is literally seething with blues, like the demented "Crawling King Snake", the devilishly playful "(You Need Meat) Don't Go No Further" sung by Ray Manzarek (finally available on the 40th anniversary remaster), and the ode to impotence "Cars Hiss By My Window". Add in the hit "Love Her Madly" and the ominous "The WASP (Texas Radio and the Big Beat)" and you've got one phenomenal swan song.

I had always loved The Doors' self titled debut album ever since I first heard it and I thought "could they get any better"? I said no for a long time, until I found this L.A. Woman cassette lying around. I wasn't expecting much, certainly not something as incredible as that debut, though I did love the title track and "Riders on the Storm". Then I popped the tape in...

I was AMAZED. This album managed to beat out their debut, and it wasn't even all that close. Everybody is in top form here, starting with Jimbo Morrison, who while near death still had a great voice, here it's deeper than on earlier recordings. Manzarek shines as usual on organ, and he creates some haunting effects with the keys/organ on some of the songs. Krieger also plays some of his best guitar, with great solos on most of the tracks, but especially on "Been Down So Long" and "Crawling King Snake". Densmore is solid as a rock on the drums as usual.

There are no bad songs on here, like on their debut, but there is something about this collection of songs that makes this an incredible listen, and better than their debut, but I don't know what it is. I knew from first listen that this was a different style of The Doors, this album being more blues based then anything they ever did before. Strange coming from someone who never liked or heard much of the blues to say this is one of my favorite albums. Now I may have to check out some blues stuff.

Now back to the album, which starts off brilliantly with "The Changeling", which is my favorite from the album, though I do love them all equally. Next up is one of the two top 20 hits from the album, "Love Her Madly", and is one of The Doors most famous tracks. The next two songs really show what style of music this album is based on; the blues. "Been Down So Long" and "Cars Hiss By My Window" are two of the bluesiest tracks on the album and are vastly underrated. Morrison gives an incredible performance on "Been Down So Long" in particular. "Cars..." sounds like a blues track that could have been recorded in the 30's/40's. The ending was a little strange when I first heard it, and it is a good wakeup call. I won't spoil it though.

Did I say "The Changeling" was my favorite? It is, but the BEST song is the title track, "L.A. Woman". Starting with the sound of a motor running loud, you know from the beginning its going to be a trip. All about the seedy underbelly of the City of Angels, it is sung with great power by Morrison. Best part of the song? 'Mr. Mojo Risin''... and that's just the first side!

Side 2 sounds to me like the more experimental side of the album, as there are many different styles present than just blues. If you want acid rock Doors, look no further than the side opener "L'America", which sounds more like the material that is on their self titled debut and the followup Strange Days (i.e., trippy). Next is the "happier"/"poppier" sounding "Hyacinth House", which is another of many underrated tracks from this album. Then its back to the blues with a cover of "Crawling King Snake" which I am guessing was covered because of Morrison's love for reptiles, hence his nickname "The Lizard King". "The WASP" is different; it is a spoken word song set to great playing by all three musicians while Morrison reads his poetry. It's intetesting and is yet another favorite from the album.

Does it have to end? Unfortunatly yes, but not before one of the best songs (many say the best) The Doors ever recorded is played in the haunting yet beautiful "Riders On the Storm". This is a jazzy sounding song which features another of Morrison's best performances (if I remember this is also the last song that was recorded for the album and hence, his final song with the group) and one of Manzarek's best solos (some beautiful playing). The rain pours as the album fades into silence... and is a fitting way to end the album.

Sorry if I rambled, but my love for this album is too great to be summarized in a measly paragraph. Look at all of the incredible albums released in 1971, which I think is the best year for music ever. You will see that this album is in the 30s, which is a travesty. This is easily up there with Zeppelin's untitled fourth album (which I love), Who's Next (which I once thought of as the greatest album of all time), Sticky Fingers, Master of Reality, Meddle... it goes on. This is just as good if not better than ALL albums that were released that year. Some think its a good album, some great, others may even think its overrated or bad, L.A. Woman is in my top 10 of the greatest albums of all time, and on any given day it can be the greatest album of all time...

Now to find it on vinyl...

(Updated May 4, huzzah! Finally found an original vinyl pressing with see-through window, and it sounds as good as ever, if not better!)

It's 1970 and The Doors are back in the studio looking to record their next album. Is it just me or does the fact the band's debut album was only released in 1967 and L.A. Woman would be the their sixth seem a little off. Six albums in such a short period of time! Is it any wonder they had such a dip in form in the middle of the run? Could it really be a surprise that they would turn up at the studio without any new material to go? Surely the relentless round of recording and touring was a contributing factor to Morrison's demise?

The session for L.A. Woman was set to follow well-defined lines: Morrison turned up with his book of poetry, Krieger had a melody or two, both hoping they would stumble across something that would fit together. This routine was shattered by long-time producer Paul Rothschild getting everyone together in the studio and announcing he was up and leaving because he just couldn't cope with the process any more. Into his shoes stepped faithful engineer-cum-producer Bruce Botnick and old customs went out the window. Instead, they decided to try and record live with as few overdubs as possible. They also decided that, rather than taking months, they would try and lay down the tracks in a week. And, finally, they decided that booze in the studio would be kept to a minimum. The result? My favourite Doors album. Sure there are far better tracks elsewhere but, as an album, L.A. Woman is much more consistent.

There is a really strong blues presence on this album. The version of John Lee Hooker's "Crawling King Snake" just crackles with sexual tension. Similarly, Morrison's performance of Willie Dixon's "(You Need Meat) Don't Go No Further" included in the bonus tracks is electric. But even better are two of the band's own compositions "Cars Hiss By My Window" and "Been Down So Long" which includes the line "I've been down so Goddamn long that it looks like up to me", a sentiment I've been able to relate to on several occasions. It's lines like these that draw me into an album and when I heard it for the first time I was hooked.

Equally impressive are "Hyacinth House" which might just be my favourite track on L.A. Woman "Love Her Madly" the introduction of which makes me think of "Love Machine" by The Miracles and "The WASP (Texas Radio And The Big Beat)". The album also includes the jazz-infused classic "Riders On The Storm" which I was delighted to discover came about by the band doodling around with the old standard "Ghost Riders In The Sky". I'm less taken with the title track which just hangs around too long and "L'America" which contains musical traces of Bernstein and Sondheim's "America" from West Side Story.

Even better is the fact that, for once, I don't have to rail against the bonus material. No pointless dialogue, no boring repetitious takes of the same track, no depressing signs that Morrison was falling apart at the seams. Consisting of only two tracks the aforementioned "(You Need Meat) Don't Go No Further" and "Orange County Suite" the compilers finally put something together that doesn't detract from the original material.

It's sad to think that Morrison was dead soon after laying down these tracks but, with his depressive and self-destructive nature, such an end seemed pre-ordained. At least he went out on a high and, even though I'm still not convinced The Doors deserve their position in rock's hierarchy, after listening to all six studio albums, I can begin to understand some of the veneration.
