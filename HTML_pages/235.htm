Now this is more like it.

My biggest bugbear with the Castle reissues of Fall albums is the sheer quantity of material crammed into each repackaged release. Multiple takes of a single song, inconsequential remixes and poorly chosen live performances drained the energy from the original album.

Grotesque (After The Gramme) should be held up as the blueprint for any further remastered Castle reissue. A cleansed version of the primary release, a couple of A and B sides of standalone singles and an interview with Mark E Smith talking about the album. The result is almost perfectly formed: the dynamics of the original ten tracks augmented rather than diluted by the bonus material.

But don't let the word "cleansed" fool you. The secret behind the prolonged success of The Fall lies in their disorganised, overpowering, grimy and relentless noise. And Grotesque is absolutely brimming with all those malodorous qualities and more.

The album opens slowly with a tongue-in-cheek rant about paying council tax – or rates as they were known back in the day. Even though the British punk revolution had only begun a handful of years previously "Pay Your Rates" already sounds very olde worlde. It's OK but shows very little progression. Equally staid is "English Scheme" embracing one of Smith's favourite subjects: the class system. Again, it may be amusing to hear Smith spit out lines like "The lower-class, want brass, bad chests, scrounge fags. The clever ones tend to emigrate like your psychotic big brother." but it hardly reflects a band evolving. But that all changes with the magnificent "New Face In Hell".

Those moments when a song segues perfectly with your internal rhythmic body clock are so rare they should be treasured. Risking being accused of preposterous levels of exaggeration, a song can sit so perfectly it feels like you're breathing it in. In the case of "New Face In Hell" it may not be the freshest breath you'll ever take but it's bloody invigorating. The guitar rattles along like an unstable handcart, the vocal cadence is so reminiscent of Phil Daniel's turn on "Parklife" Smith could have been Daniel's vocal coach, and, most amazingly of all, the kazoo is transformed into a practical instrument.

For that track alone Grotesque demands attention, but it's followed by the insanely titled "C 'n' C-S Mithering" in which Smith does nothing but mither like a petulant child, the track slides effortlessly into the Manc-abilly of "The Container Drivers" and then onto the oddly gothic entity that is "Impression Of J Temperance". This frankly bizarre concoction is what makes The Fall great. On paper nothing about this cacophony should work but the energy, drive and sheer nerve of the band pull it off effortlessly.

Inevitably Grotesque falls away with the straightforward "In The Park" and the pointless "WMC-Blob 59" but manages to hit a similar high in "The NWRA" (The North Will Rise Again), another north/south divide rant but, unlike the earlier "English Scheme", one that balances humour with cutting political insight. Bringing the album to an extremely satisfying conclusion are two of The Fall's finest singles – "How I Wrote “Elastic Man” and "Totally Wired" – and a very crazy and sometimes hilarious promotional slot with Smith interviewing himself.

Grotesque has very quickly become one of my favourite Fall albums and some of that can be attributed to the thought and care that's gone into creating this remastered reissue. But the fact is, when The Fall are on their warped game, they're a formidable force and deserving of all the accolades that come their way.

about 7 or eight years ago, i was in desperate need of new music. Dabbling in the more obvious 60's psych and standard classic punk bands(other firm favorites at the time were John Frusciante, Captain Beefheart and the mars volta and rhcp), I was ready to start finding the more arcane. Post punk, new wave, and experimental of late seventies and early 80's was where i was ready to go.

At a record shop, i stood with three cds in hand, and one had to go. Choices were Juju(Siouxsie and the banshees), Pere Ubu( Dub Housing), and the Fall(Grotesque...). I knew i wanted Juju, and that worked out great. Between the other two, The decision came down to the fact i heard Beefheart once played sax on stage with pere ubu( and it also had a cover pleasing to the eye, with the pink and black bouncing off each other). So Dub Housing it was.

I don't hate Dub Housing, but i think i did ruin for myself. I really liked alot of elements it has, but i really wanted it to be the masterpeice i was told it was, and masterpeice is what i craved. I listened to it over and over, hoping to find out it was actually magnificent. Truly though my heart wouldn't budge. It was just solid weird punk/post punk, but nothing that was spilling with ideas and depth like i had hoped.

It took years til i would find the Fall. Don't know how it took so long, but i'm pretty sure i would have been hooked had i bought Grotesque at that now non existant record store. Those years back then i spent much time in the bar or at home with a bottle of something. Those early Fall albums would have been the precise ingredient i was looking for those nights.

joeyramoney Dec 31 2006
+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
The more I think about it, this is my favorite fall LP. Whereas its little brother Hex Enduction Hour often unrightfully overshadows it, the energetic bile of Grotesque defines The Fall more than any other record. Mark E. Smith was never so literately paranoid, the call of popular success was never so far away, and the Fall were never so consistently brilliant.

First of all, the songs (in the sense of ‘passages of music’) on this album are some of the most inventive and inspired of The Falls’ long career. “Pay your Rates” is a great exercise in dynamics, whereas “C’n’c stop mithering” and the sublime “The NWRA” focus more on krautish drones. Like so damn many good records though (Psychocandy comes to mind), these 4 ana haff stars are earned by about half the LP. "New Face in Hell" is overly silly, the subject matter of "In the Park" seems out of place, "W.M.C. Blob-59" is unlistenable artiness, and i honestly don't think i've ever listened to "Gramme Friday" all the way through. Most of the time these tracks just get the skip button as 'filler'. In sunnier pastures, the two bonus tracks- the singles “How I wrote Elastic Man” and “Totally Wired” are both great. While the former focuses on rockabilly riffing (a theme that recurs on “Container Drivers”), “’Wired” is probably the crankiest song of a very cranky bands’ career. “English Scheme” (ultimately an indictment of British pretension) is just about the most joyous little piece of music I’ve ever heard, with it’s tinny, toyish little keyboard hook. What is striking about the Fall at this point in their career is their willingness to take on such quirky and disparate musical territory without ever sounding silly or anything other than totally sincere and intenseley garagebandian.

On Grotesque, Mark E. Smith emerges as a too-grating-to-be-“charismatic”-per-se-yet-commanding-as-hell frontman. He’s sorta like an asshole drill sergeant; you have no attraction to his harsh manner and perhaps loathe the man just a bit for it, but you gotta hear him out. That’s the beauty of the Fall- their music is generally unpleasant, but oddly compelling. MES’ lyrical perspective is fully formed at this point. We hear tales of almost absurdly particular matter, such as cloned veteranarians, fucking in the park, “debtors’ escape estates”, and sausage stuffed eggs. Yet this eye for the common forms a vaster and surprisingly well developed perspective of the grand. Here you find a cynical summation of British society seen from the ground up; take a few steps back and the picture gets even bigger, such that you can see a sweeping indictment of the state of western society as of the late 20th century. Of course, the more provincial elements of this stew often to the top. Take the final track, the 9+ minutes long “The N(orth) W(ill) R(ise) A(gain)”. Here, Smith sing/speaks lines like “the streets of Soho did reverberate/with drunken highland men/revenge for Culloden dead/the North had rose again”, vicariously blending earnest, unironic regional pride with his usual quirky vitriol in a manner that was never so well developed as on this LP. It’s hard to call this brand of aggravated sing/speaking a “rant’, as his elocution possesses a kind of odd grace with respect to the music and the words themselves. In this respect, Mark E. transcends the roles of lyricist or singer, since his words and delivery are so irreconcilably entwined with one another. Neigh, everything on Grotesque is irreconcilably intertwined with one another- the vocals, lyrics, guitars and drums etc. are all melded as a singular force lumbering through the countryside leaving a path of paranoid Thatcher-era clangor.

Ultimately, “Grotesque (after the Gramme)” defines The Fall for me. Few bands post-Ramones have so successfully exuded such brilliant wit within the context of the garage band and created such a damn good record out of it.

5-stars for the original _Grotesque_, minus bonus tracks. Although Smith has expressed hostility toward the notion of original album purity (among other things) and seems fine with outer singles breaking up the original recorded order, the bonus tracks interrupt the flow of the initially recorded Mancunian-tongues vulgar drivel.

The original _Grotesque_ is the height of this perpetually twitching entity called the Fall. Firing frantically on all bilious cylinders, Smith offers his most concise interpretation of the old damp north as it rusts. Behind him the band clatters on, balancing rockaboppers, punk snarlers, and looong painful noise-athons that only hold up to repeated listens if you've already let their distorted nonlinear worldview infiltrate your expectations. _Grotesque_ is simultaneously hysterical and morbidly stoic---at times too contemptuous to care, at other times jittery, fall-apart nervous and jangly. An album that wrings cheer out of darkness like a televised wake for a loathed public figure.

23 years later, and I still think this is the Fall's best album. Elvis might have taken five separate styles of music and synthesized them into a unified style that everyone liked, but trust the Fall to go one better: they've taken about twenty styles of music and synthesized them into a unified whole that almost no-one can listen to...

In any case, the brilliance of _Grotesque_ lies in its ability to balance conflicting styles and ideas: on one hand, it's almost a concept album, a detached and quasi-scientific examination of everything sordid and grotesque in English society (nostalgia, dumb truck drivers, pretentious socialists, conspiracy theories, drugs and sex, vets who get a little too friendly with their patients), but although the picture is unified, it's still made up of many smaller elements, and these elements differ considerably.

As the above would imply, _Grotesque_ sees the lyrics of Mark E. Smith getting even more wonderfully dramatic and cryptic; but at the same time, the band reviews its influences, and more powerfully than on their previous albums: punk ("Pay Your Rates"), rockabilly ("Container Drivers"), Krautrock (on the longer pieces "The NWRA" and "C'n'C/Stop Mithering"), and gratuitous unlistenable artiness (on stuff like "WMC - Blob 59").

But to me the most powerful songs on the album are the songs that merge rather than ape these styles and create an even more distinctive sound: the literal shaggy-dog story of "Impression of J. Temperance", the two-minute slice of frustration and bile that is "In The Park", and "New Face In Hell", which takes the detective stories of _Dragnet_ to a new level -- it starts off as a relatively subdued story about some guy who picks up govt. treachery on his ham radio, then the kazoo kicks in, and it escalates to a hysterical rave-up with both Mark E. Smith and his band on superb form. Along with "Wings", it's just about my favourite Fall song, and it's a song no other band could do. (Pavement, of course, were lucky not to hear from the Fall's lawyers when they did "Conduit For Sale!")

The modern CD includes the contemporaneous singles "How I Wrote Elastic Man" and "Totally Wired". They're both great, but don't let them take away from the unified work of genius that is _Grotesque_ .

After listening to The Fall for quite some time, Grotesque firstly and finally grabbed my attention with an unrelenting grasp, perhaps a fittingly titled and styled entry point for an outsider for its herky-jerky disjointed approach to post-punk along with an impressive array of varied material. I would later appreciate Perverted by Language for very different reasons, but this earlier release from the band hosts an immediately punchy and effective set of songs that excel for their wordplay and vocal delivery, of course, but are matched with a more fitting instrumental and melodic aesthetic than perhaps any other album they produced. Its first half is very close to perfectly written and delivered, and while not every song has the same drive as something like “New Face in Hell” or the wonderfully titled “C'n'C-S Mithering”, the consistency in sound and entertaining, humorous lyricism is simply phenomenal, only marginally losing steam during the last portion of the album. Mark E. Smith’s eccentric performances are as endearing as they are wonderfully manic, as are hilarious yet pleasantly odd choices like including the kazoo and practically out of tune and out of rhythm passages sprinkled throughout this set of shockingly infectious yet rambling melodies. There are genuine moments of surprise even when considering the expected madness, like the bass hook during the later half of “Impression of J. Temperance” that give chills and a sense of wonder even for such an offbeat group of musicians. The electric rockabilly rhythms intertwined with Smith’s yelping vocals, oozing with attitude, reminisce in true foundational punk rock, yet rise above the simplicity of their influences to create something entirely new and surprisingly surreal. While one could argue the band traversed more ambitious or even uncommon ground later in their career, Grotesque achieves a unique blend of sounds in a very tight and effective package, making it one of their strongest efforts and one of the most enduring albums of the post-punk movement. An essential piece of punk rock history for its singularity in achieved sound.
