Perfect in its imperfections, complete in its incompleteness, _Starless and Bible Black_ is the most disorienting and unbalanced album that King Crimson ever made. It took me a long time to change from seeing this as a lackluster misstep in between two monumental albums to unlocking its mysteries. The way I hear the album now it all seems correct; an unorthodox juggling act of power and delicacy, falling apart and rejoining in endlessly fascinating configurations.

More evidence of Fripp's shameless and utterly welcome eccentricity, Starless and Bible Black is one record that never really got a chance to shine. This is probably a timing thing; it's definitely the weird middle child of the Wetton-Bruford trilogy, and a lot of that's probably because a full four of these eight tracks are the sort of atonal improvisations Fripp so loves. I've grown to love these over the years, but they are tough sells, especially if you're coming to this group from a rock background as I did. But the improvs are fascinating if you go into them with an open mind, and the more conventional songs (by King Crimson standards, I mean, there's still plenty of weirdness to be heard) are just as good as we've come to expect.

More accessible songs first because they're easier to talk about. "The Great Deceiver" is something of an accepted Crimson classic, and it's quality material, riding a weird lurching guitar/violin riff (one of David Cross' best contributions to the group), a dissonant melody, and those signature King Crimson lyrics that probably only mean anything whatsoever to Robert Fripp. It's a high point, not just for the album but for the band's work as a whole, and so is "The Night Watch," a lovely ballad with some of Fripp's most sensitive guitar work. Speaking of sensitive, Fripp's guitar shimmers throughout "Lament," which breaks into some truly astonishing rock n' roll before settling back into ballad mode. God I love this band. Especially this incarnation of the band. "The Mincer" can't help but be the weakest of the bunch, and even then its two-sinister-guitars thing works in my book.

"Trio" is a nice way to transition from the songs into the improvs, since it's the most accessible of the four. It's a lovely bass-violin-mellotron flute improv notable for featuring no percussion at all; apparently Bruford, realizing that the session was going fine without him, decided to lay out. More proof that he's the champion of rock drumming. Peart, Bonham, Portnoy? They got bumped out in the first round. Bruford beat 'em all single-handedly. Hell, his decision not to drum was so crucial to the sound of the piece that he got a cowriting credit for it! The idea that all King Crimson improv is super-dissonant really shatters here, and anyway I think the group's sense of atmosphere is underrated by those who deride the improv pieces for not having melodies. Take for instance "We'll Let You Know." It's the weakest of the improvs in my book, but I still like it! Foreshadows the bass rumble of "Providence," that's for sure, and as usual Fripp's guitar invokes sheer terror.

But it's side two that sticks in some people's craws, and I'm here to tell you that side two is nothing to fear. I mean, it is in the sense that KC was all about the dread-and-anxiety thing during this period, but again, go in with an open mind and you'll realize these improvisations are more purposeful than, say, Cream's eight-year jams. Check out the continual crescendo of "Starless and Bible Black," not to be confused with "Starless," where Fripp goes crazy on the guitar-strangling, Cross soars on violin, Wetton rumbles along and Bruford drums like he has eight arms. They're all locked into the same thing, this continual sense of propulsion that locks into a pretty solid groove by the five-minute mark. You can't tell me they're just dicking around here, I won't have it. As I said in my review of Red, let us not admonish this band for standing so far outside of the rock n' roll narrative. Besides, have critics of live Crimson missed the fact that "Fracture" sets on definite riffs? And excellent riffs at that? Or that, once again, Bruford's percussion and the general atmosphere of the thing are both terrific? Atmosphere is good!

Ah, but enough evangelizing. To a degree, you'll either like the improvisations or you won't, but if you ask me the improvisations are well worth getting to know. These dudes were maybe the most compelling rock combo of the '70s, and this is yet more evidence of their excellence. Check it out, says I!

+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
...mmmmmmmmweh? This album is very, very different than the other 1973-'74 King Crimson albums. Those albums were extremely tight and complicated, while this one's heavily built on improvisation and spontaneity. It's still centered around the same convoluted, dissonant math-prog style though.

It opens with the furious speed-attack The Great Deceiver - an awesome, intense blast of unpredictable avant-garde rock and easily the best song on the album. Lament continues in the same direction with more weird angular hard rock.

The rest is very different, though. There are only two more actual "compositions" on the album. The Night Watch is closer in sound to King Crimson's first era than this one. It's a very pleasant and simple mellotron-based ballad about a painting. The album ends with an absolute epic: the intense, weird instrumental Fracture, featuring Fripp's most virtuose shred-picking yet - according to Fripp himself it's possibly the hardest song to play in the entire King Crimson catalogue.

The rest of the tracks are all improvisations recorded live with minimal editing. Trio is an absolutely gorgeous violin-based piece (named "Trio" because Bill Bruford doesn't play on it), the rest are weird, messy atonal piles of slop. They aren't bad though! We'll Let You Know and The Mincer (the only improv track to feature overdubs) have a mysterious, jazzy atmosphere and the title track has a couple unbelievably intense moments, but sadly drags on for way too long. The improvs definitely aren't for everyone, and they're a bit uneven.

So Starless and Bible Black isn't a classic like the other '73-'74 Crimson albums - it has too much improvisation and not enough truly inspiring moments. But it's a good album, and fans should definitely pick it up.

_Starless And Bible Black_ comes at the listener with all the subtlety of a bar room brawl only better choreographed and punctually orchestrated. The unfocused period of _Lizard_ and _Islands_ were well out of scope and the Fripp/Wetton/Bruford/Cross line up had exercised their strength with _Larks Tongues In Aspic_ and by this album were a well oiled machine working together in complete harmony and consistency. "The Great Deceiver" could be an auto biographical song concerning Robert Fripp, in title only though, as The Crimson King tore his King Crimson vision through another line up but one that would leave their most impending legacy and strongest progression to date. Even more so than the Adrian Belew era of the eighties that produced _Discipline_ and its two weaker clones, _Beat_ and _Three Of A Perfect Pair_. The energy and convincing aggression on _Starless And Bible Black_ is wide and consuming. The recording at the AIR Studios is massive and the bass tone especially sounds wide and vibrant while it gives plenty of scope especially to the two longer tracks on the B side of the album. The manic doom filled and aptly titled album ender "Fracture" and title track "Starless And Bible Black", which scorches away on progressive passages of improvisation juxtaposed with well arranged interplay between the musicians. In the eye of the storm lies a beautiful gentle flowing number. Tagging on to the back of "The Night Watch" "Trio" is excellently polarised with the rest of material on the album though inconspicuously fits into the concept giving an added texture but it soon crashes into "The Mincer", probably the weakest song on the album that comes across as an unfinished aimless jam that ends quite abruptly. _Starless And Bible Black_ like its bookended counterparts is a timeless album. The mark of any skilled group of like minded musicians is an ideal to produce music that may be peerless without thinking it to _be_ peerless. The method in which the musicians approached the music through their instruments is extremely intelligent. The trilogy of albums released by King Crimson in the mid seventies are well ahead of their time. _Starless And Bible Black_ sits in the middle chronologically and musically almost tearing between _Larks Tongues In Aspic_ and _ Red_ albums. A great release of tension.
