TWO TUMS UP
I was familiar with the Arctic Monkeys from their first album, completely missed their second coming with A.M. and had dismissed them as one of those has-been 00s bands that somehow still manage find their way onto the Pyramid Stage at Glastonbury.

Similar to others, I heard one song on the radio, registered that the Arctic Monkeys were trying something different and then forgot about it. Until, a few years later, I happened across someone enthusing about this album online.

And they were right.

It's hard to describe the sound of this album - you have to hear it. It's theatrical while somehow being very understated. I'm not sure exactly why it works so well. The lyrics are key - though a lot of the singing is verging on spoken word, this is somehow a very singalong album. There are so many fantastic lines that I just can't help myself. It's how he says them. The weird casino purgatory vibe ties it all together and makes it feel very cohesive.

There are some weak points but nothing too bad. Golden Trunks and She Looks Like Fun are weaker cuts. The outro of Four Out Of Five drags on for too long.

The best Arctic Monkeys album yet.
Alex Turner has redefined himself yet again, hard to accept the fact that he's the guy who made Whatever People Say I Am, That's What I'm Not years ago, it really shows how he has matured as a person and a songwriter. Instead of chasing the success AM had he let Alexandra Savior and Miles Kane influence him to make the most interesting project of the decade. Tranquility Base Hotel & Casino is an incredibly ambitious, concept sci-fi album, full of clever pop culture references, it's theme is a luxury hotel on the moon, yet the lyrical content feels so real and intimate.

The highlight of the album has to be the closer The Ultracheese, a slow ballad focused on the sense of isolation he feels after becoming a world renowned rockstar.

"Oh, the dawn won't stop weighing a tonne
I've done some things that I shouldn't have done
But I haven't stopped loving you once"

Difficult to not feel emotional after a line like that.

Es posiblemente el álbum mas polémico después de The Car (2)
Yo me adentre a Arctic Monkeys en el 2018, justo cuando salio este álbum, pero lo hice meses después. El disco salio en Mayo y yo empece a escuchar a la banda en Diciembre.
Vi la evolución de Arctic Monkeys en dos días.
Recuerdo que los fans mas acérrimos de la banda defendian este álbum diciendo "es lento, se supone que deba serlo, lleva por nombre tranquility", que es un argumento bastante tonto.
Es como defender un álbum porque lleve por nombre "bad" y decir que es malo porque el nombre lo dice.
La diferencia es que tranquilo no es sinónimo de malo, pero tranquility si es un álbum algo deficiente. Tranquilo no debe ser sinónimo de aburrido o cansado, y este álbum si se siente de esa forma.
Lo comencé a escuchar y me encantaba su nuevo sonido, pero después de un par de canciones ya me había aburrido. Es algo pesado de escuchar, incluso su ultima canción me gusta bastante, pero ya no quería seguir escuchando porque estaba demasiado cansado de este disco.
Yo lo recordaba mejor, haberlo escuchado de nuevo me hizo quitarle puntos.
Arctic Monkeys decide ser mas complejo y elegante con este álbum, lo cual esta bien porque anteriormente hacian un sonido mas juvenil, no le veo lo malo que quieran algo diferente. Toman ese sonido elegante del piano, el jazz y hacerlo conceptual, pero no les queda bien.
No saben hacerlo del todo bien.
Creo que le falla mucho el ritmo, porque tiene buenas canciones; American Sports, Four Out of Five, She Looks Like Fun y The Ultracheese, son canciones estupendas. Pero como dije, escucharlas en conjunto con el álbum no te hace disfrutarlas del todo porque ya estas cansado del mismo álbum.

Esta reseña no es de la primera escucha, ya he escuchado este álbum antes. Cuando escuche The Car, mi opinión se baso tomando en cuenta su carrera, y tomando en cuenta Tranquility, y también tomando en cuenta que Tranquility me había gustado mas. Pero ahora creo que debo escuchar de nuevo The Car y dar una reseña actualizada, porque comienzo a creer que The Car me gusta mucho mas que este disco.

Tranquil base of operations for Alex Turner's monumental lyrical flourishes
Striking and shocking it may have been upon release, Tranquility Base Hotel and Casino is due its second coming. Arctic Monkeys weathered the initial storm of people being shocked that they had surrendered to baroque sentimentalities and came out all the better for it. They moved away from a style and sound that had dominated their careers. Damned if they stuck with it, damned if they pushed it back. An uncomfortable period for any artist is that of reinvention. Some are capable of doing it time and time again like David Bowie. Struggling to push away that rage-ready Sheffield tact, Arctic Monkeys explode into an art rock exploration like no other.

Longue pop immediacy replicates itself throughout the album, a bold crashing through of Star Treatment sets new expectations for the band. One Point Perspective is another lengthy prose that blends pop aesthetics with confidence. Simple changes and technical qualities mark Tranquility Base Hotel and Casino as a piece that leans into the piano lounge stylings well but dispels the predictability with the starry, futuristic sounds of warped, historic instruments. A dolceola and harpsicord do not present the usual indie rock association for Arctic Monkeys, and it makes the album feel more and more like a desire to break free and go beyond. Not a higher brow, but a different one altogether. Intense and sophisticated gluttony pours out of the Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas aesthetics of Tranquility Baste Hotel and Casino’s title track.

Piano rock formations filter through and give way to crooning altogether. Album ender The Ultracheese is a key and lucid highlight of Arctic Monkeys’ entire discography, let alone this art rock effort. Four Out of Five has endpoints rattled through the whole track. The end is often the strongest portion of any work. It is the culmination of the thought that inspires a record, a writing or a film. To have bridging moments that reflect and continue on with such continuity and exceptional quality is a demanding treat that steers the track and its musical qualities of it so well. No excuses can be made for the wobbly Batphone that wraps up the album just before it launches into The Ultracheese. There is a pocket, from The World's First Ever Monster Truck Front Flip to Batphone that sees a change in pace, but not in tone.

Jazz lounge psychedelics offer a delightful range to Arctic Monkeys, one that moves them away from thrashy colloquialisms. Problems arise for those looking for traditionalism, rather than lyrical wit. Alex Turner is on the same, consistent form here as is expressed in earlier works and that of The Car. That latter album is closer to Tranquility Base Hotel and Casino than many are willing to admit because it would mean admitting they were wrong about the qualities and range found on Arctic Monkeys’ best and most sporadic album. A broad, deep and sincerely reflective period for the band sees them deal with the gluttonies of fame and sophistication in a way that removes them far away from the sound that launched them to mega-stardom. That much is necessary for reflection, and it doesn’t seem they’ll be headed back to that colloquial club anthemic period anytime soon.
