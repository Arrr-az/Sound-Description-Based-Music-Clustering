The Who Sell Out: Deluxe Edition (2009):

In 2001, Universal Music began to treat Who fans by issuing deluxe editions of the bands original discography. Since the bands fifth LP, Live at Leeds, was chosen as a starting point, predicting the next Who LP that will receive the deluxe overhaul has been a shot in the dark. As it turned out, the following year Universal Music made a strange but welcome choice and issued a deluxe edition of The Whos debut LP, My Generation. In 2003, both Who's Next and Tommy were given a deluxe makeover. And then.nothing!

I was really disappointed because, as previous reissues have proven, The Whos vault is only about dime short of a being treasure chest. Not only that, their catalogue has greatly benefited from its recent renovations. Six long years later and what email notice do I receive from Amazon? A deluxe edition of The Whos greatest LP, The Who Sell Out will be released next month. Pre-order today. Wow!!! I believe I will be doing just that very thang.

Like most deluxe editions, The Who Sell Out will thrill those that already know and love the album and The Who. Apart from the fact that there are tons of little goodies that are new to our ears, this is the first time the original mix of the album has been remastered and released on CD (dont worry, the unedited version of Rael is included as a bonus track). If you are game for upgrading your Who collection a practice Im always up for the light aint gettin no greener, son. The previously unreleased tracks Sodding About (aka Trying To Get Through) and alternate version of Summertime Blues or worth the price of admission alone. If you do happen to be new to The Who Sell Out, and money is no object, well get the lead out already. You will not be disappointed. Everyone else, enjoy your vinyl and/or 1995 remixed version.

Unfortunately, as with every deluxe edition, there are a few pitfalls to look out for. First off, if you happen to be one of those people that cannot tolerate CDs that are mastered to compete with the present day loudness war, you may want to take a step back. This isnt only the loudest version of The Who Sell Out Ive ever heard, it is also the least dynamic (the studio dialogue at the beginning of the demo version of Glittering Girl sounds as if they are talking through rolls of toilet paper). The mono mix - featured on disc two - suffers greatly due to the mass amount of compression applied during mastering. All the original recordings clicks and pops are pushed to the fore. The bass dominates the mix as the remainder of the instruments fight for space. Its kinda cool to here Entwistle jacked up to eleven but, overall, it does not compliment the recording. But who listens to psychedelic LPs in mono anyway?

One of the things that really irritate me about Universal Music's deluxe editions is the intentional withholding of previously unreleased material recorded during the sessions for the featured LP. In this case, a track titled Bob Sings Soul - noted in Andrew Neill and Matthew Kents excellent book Anyway, Anyhow, Anywhere: The Complete Chronicle of The Who 1958-1978 is not included. Allegedly, this is a novelty track that features The Who's road manager Bob Pridden taking the lead vocal. Its easy to reckon the reasoning behind the decision to pass this obvious filler over; however, Im sure its being tucked away for a rainy day. Hello, The Who Sell Out: The Complete Sessions!!!

Call it a pet peeve, nitpicking, or just plain anal, but what is with the grouping of two or more songs into one track? Do todays industry audio heavies not have access to standard editing software such as pro-tools? Is there a reason the unedited version of Rael, the backing track of Armenia City In The Sky, and the Great Shakes commercial are allocated to one track number? No? Just laziness? Hey, word to bird. We live in world where shuffle and playlists are standard features on modern audio equipment. Not everybody wants to hear all three songs in a row with a long silence between them. Break it up. This can be done using Nero! OK? OK!!!

Id like to think the deluxe edition of The Who Sell Out will be the final word on the album; however, so long as innovations in audio come forth, Bob Sings Soul is in the vault, and a couple thousand fools like myself have forty dollars to burn, I have my doubts.

My Generation
Tommy
Live at Leeds
Who's Next

Much like the Rolling Stones (and much unlike The Beatles) The Who arrive very suddenly, out of goddamn nowhere they get reeeeaaaally good. Call me nuts but their first two albums are confused and mediocre. The debut is largely cute but futile R&B Rock stuff. Mods? I don't get em'. Next was an album that was pushing and pulling all over the place, ending on a neat but corny epic song. In fact though I'd say The Who are and always have been a confused band, one that would have been so much better with a more screwed on head. I just don't really think the Rock Operas are all that, I find Townshend's obsession with his fairly run of the mill stories clouds the real point, the music, weighs it down a bit. Possibly their best album ends up being a FAILED rock opera? What's going on! Trying too hard, failing and succeeding, sheeeet. But there is one exception to this wonderful much of confusion, one actual focused laser beam that makes for a really pleasurable mix of concept and tunes. And it's the OTHER possibly best album they made, this one! The Who Sell Out is just...it's really just wonderful! For a bunch of unrelated reasons and that's rare to be able to say. For starters I love the basic sound most of these songs have, it's a bright shining almost reverbed sort of guitar pop, very 80's like in a way! I don't know if most would agree, but it sounds like Jangle to me. I think it's not impossible to hear echoes of this in XTC or The Smiths and British Janglers like that. They know how to use it too, this shimmering gorgeous sound. I'm currently trying to figure out how I didn't notice the magnificent tune on Tattoo the first time I went through this album. Was I fucking asleep or what? They also start sounding like...well The Who here. That sort of modestly epic simple rock music that would become both their trademark as well as their legacy. If you can't also hear part of where U2 came from here I don't know what to tell you. And modestly epic? Sounds kind of bad, but I don't mean it like that at all. It's never overblown and Arena rock like, not horribly corny like Journey (one of the worse legacies of The Who) it's always just measured enough, classy, satisfying. And it doesn't get old either, since they pull this trick on most of the songs except for the Entwistle ones. All of these are attractively alongside eachother without them getting samey, they don't have to fight for your attention. But the main cool thing about this album? It's an incredibly ahead of it's time parody concept album. And an incredibly beyond it's genre choice of doing so. Yeah yeah, with Zappa around parody based rock was nothing The Who could claim on. But the way they do it? Very different. Zappa parodies are obvious parodies, it's always completely obvious Zappa is joking around when he is. The Who are playing it straight, these are songs about silly thing mostly, like deodorant (Odorono). Yet only by really listening to the lyrics would you get that. And that makes it much funnier! Odorono can for real, be enjoyed as deadpan parody, or a beautiful little song. The truly innovative thing though? The little comedy skits. Most Rock fans won't make note of it, but I'm more than a rock fan, I'm also a Hip-Hop fan. The skits here? Framing the album like a radio playlist with advertisements and so forth? This is a very common sort of thing you see in Rap. Namely I think of the group who made it big over in that genre, De La Soul. Both their debut and second album make heavy use of skits that remind me A LOT of this. It's simply awesome to see it here both where and when. Too often people treat genres like Cold War enemies, the idea that Rock and Rap have contributed to eachother's evolutions over time is seen as SINFFUUULL by many. Bulllllshit. I almost expected Roger Daltrey to start hosting a game show and shit.

Rating: 5

Though "Armenia City in the Sky" and "I Can See for Miles" kick ass, the rest of The Who Sell Out remind me why I've never been a big fan of theirs: thin production, unconvincing vocal harmonies, too many unnecessary breaks to highlight Keith Moon's busy drum fills, and way too much of a British folk influence for most of their material to ever really rock. The idea to make the album seem like part of radio programming, complete with ads, is interesting, but it in no way makes up for very treacly songs like "Mary Anne with the Shaky Hand," "Sunrise," or the awful "Silas Stingy."

Le meilleur disque des Who premire priode (c'est dire avant "Tommy"), leurs annes "mod" musicalement oriente pop rock. Ce disque regorge de trouvailles au kilomtre, il respire bon l'insouciance et la joie de vivre, avec une distanciation second degr typiquement british. La dconne est au rendez-vous avec les pastiches de jingles pubs de radio pour "Heinz Baked Beans" (fayots faisant le rgal de toute la famille), un do nomm "Odorono" et qui transforme "la transpiration en inspiration", une crme anti acn intitule "Medac"...Ces morceaux sont un rgal d'coute, on sent que le groupe se fend la poire avec ces chansonnettes second degr, rien qu' entendre les imitations de John Entwistle et ces rythmiques fanfare-baloche. Autre titre trange mais bidonnant, la lgre et acoustique "Mary Anne With The Shaky Hand", fausse chanson pop nave qui raconte en fait l'histoire d'une jeune fille qui fait des merveilles avec sa main pour tous les garons venant de la ville et des alentours juste pour avoir le plaisir de rencontrer la menotte tremblottante de l'hrone...Sujet assez typique de Townshend pour l'poque puisqu'il voque la masturbation masculine sur le single de juin 1967 "Pictures Of Lily" ("...help me sleep at night.")

L'album est surtout connu pour le titre pchu "I Can See For Miles", tentative avoue par Pete de faire le morceau le plus violent de son poque. En fait, c'est un des morceaux les plus structurs et les effets de monte en puissance rendus merveille par les roulements batterie de Keith Moon et la guitare jouant un leitmotiv sur une note sont efficaces. Le vertige est davantage rendu par le climat de menace latente que par la sauvagerie manifeste toutes guitares dehors du morceau...Pete Townshend pensait obtenir un norme tube, ce qui ne fut pas le cas et lui donna envie de se consacrer exclusivement aux albums tout en dveloppant des concepts, une histoire. Paul McCartney, ayant eu vent des ambitions de Townshend pour ce titre tentera lui aussi la chanson sauvage tapageuse et composera "Helter Skelter", davantage oriente guitares, la structure et l'interprtation plus lche. Les grands esprits se rencontrent.

J'aime cette priode o les compositions des Who taient simples, efficaces, foisonnantes...avant de devenir le laboratoire exclusif de Pete Townshend, rexploitant le moindre riff, bricolant ses albums avec ses ides-concepts en tte et ne les lchant plus. Ce disque servira de tremplin pour "Tommy" puisque Pete se servira de "Ral" pour les thmes de "Sparks" et (l'interminable) "Underture". Personnellement, je prfre ce disque moins chichiteux, plus simple d'approche foisonnant vraiment d'ides excellentes et originales. La couverture en soi est dj un grand moment de rigolade, le groupe s'y montre capable d'une grande auto-drision, posant avec dcontraction parfois dans le plus simpole appareil et vantant les mrites de produits publicitaires qu'ils sont censs utiliser dans leur vie de tous les jours.

Today's sermon is titled Too Much of a Good Thing, Or, When Bonus Tracks Make a Great Album Not So Great. Now, in theory I have nothing against bonus tracks; the plethora of additions to A Quick One turn a mediocre release into a, well, more bountiful mediocre release. However, tacking 'em on to The Who Sell Out is problematic. Not only is it a concept album, but also a record where every track was a five-star winner, unified in sound and theme. Adding another half an hour of music ultimately detracts from the sheer perfection of the original release, like adding an attic extension to the Taj Mahal. Especially since the extras are so mediocre. They basically fall into three categories: interesting (in an academic kind of way) false starts, such as the alternative version of "Mary Anne with the Shaky Hand" (far inferior to its LP cousin) and extra choruses snipped from "Rael" and "Odorono"; whacked-out jams, such as "Jaguar", "Hall of the Mountain King" and the Coke commercials, which are fun but inessential; and twee pop, which must have sounded like they were destined for the garbage can even as they were being recorded (Keith Moon's "Girl's Eyes" may be the single worst Who song ever). Frankly of the 10 "official" bonus tracks, only Entwistle's "Someone's Coming" and Townshend's "Melancholia" would have fit snugly on the original Sell Out (and even then, Pete's version of "Melancholia" -- found on the first Scoop release -- has a brooding quality that Roger misses entirely on the band version). So, congratulations, Jon Astley -- you've managed to turn one of the few perfect records in my collection into a flawed one. On the plus side, though, Dave Marsh's essay in the CD booklet is one of the best things he's ever written on the band (and he's written a whole, whole lot).
