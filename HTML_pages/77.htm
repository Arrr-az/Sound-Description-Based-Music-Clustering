Despite being hated by the music press Black Sabbath's self titled debut album sold well and the record company were keen to follow up its success. It is difficult to comprehend these days but less than four months after the release of that debut album Sabbath were back in the studio working on the follow up.

Once again Rodger Bain was at the production desk and the walls of the Island and Regent studios were soon shaking to the heavy riffs of Tony Iommi as the band laid down what is generally regarded to be their greatest album.

The opening track and live show mainstay 'War Pigs' was initially going to be the title track, hence the cover, but this was changed by the record company fearful of a backlash in America where the first album had gone down well. In fact 'War Pigs' started life as early as 1968 as a track called 'Walpurgis'. It had a slightly different subject matter lyrically and was actually rejected by the reord company for being 'too Satanic' ! The track itself is of course these days hailed as a rock classic right from the demonic riffing, siren and doom laden bass of the introduction through to the jazzy melody of the outro which was actually given its own title (Spock's Wall) on the American release. Even the lyrical laziness of rhyming masses with masses can't detract from a track which voiced a generations opinion of the Vietnam war. With Iommi's flashes of guitar over the top of Osbourne's vocal, thunderous drums from Ward and Butler's bass holding the whole thing together it is undeniably one of their best compositions.

'Paranoid' follows and little needs to be said about it really. It is a typical peice of seventies hard rock single material. Curiously enough, like Deep Purple's 'Black Night' and Free's 'All Right Now' which also reached the higher echelons of the singles chart in the same year the band claim the song was simply a throwaway track they put together very quickly and because they were short of material.

'Planet Caravan' must have come as a shock on first listen when the album was initially released as it is more Simon and Garfunkel than Black Sabbath. Osbourne's dreamy sounding vocal was achieved by feeding his voice through a Leslie speaker. Iommi plays some great laid back almost jazz like guitar and Ward beats out a tribal rhythm on (presumably)congas. It is a lovely dreamy atmospheric song and as far removed as anything else on the album as you can possibly imagine.

Its back to the demonic riffing and supernatural science fiction lyrics more associated with Sabbath for the following 'Iron Man'. On first hearing the riff Osbourne stated that it sounded like a iron bloke marching about and 'Iron Bloke' was the initial working title. Butler based the lyrics around this title but changed it to 'Iron Man'. Starting off with a steady drum beat and some hard riffing it soon turns into a full blown stomping menacing strut and is rightly hailed as not only a Sabbath, but also a rock, classic. There is even a tastefully short bass and drum solo as a lead into Iommi's final guitar solo and Ward's drumming towards the end is as fast as any you will hear.

For me, the second half of the album is not as good as the first. 'Electric Funeral' gets things under way and is typically dark and menacing with a trademark Iommi Sabbath riff but the track as a whole just doesn't compare with the likes of 'War Pigs' and 'Iron Man'. It may even be heavier and in places faster than either of those but for me Osbourne's vocal detracts from the song and in places he is clearly struggling to hold it together. At some points the lyrics are indecipherable but thats not to say that it isn't a decent enough track. Just considerably inferior to the classics of side one.

'Hand of Doom' is another that struggles to rise above the ordinary but the pace and tempo changes and the rarity of Osbourne singing a melody give the track a certain something and as ever Iommi delivers top notch riffing and soloing.

The instrumental 'Rat Salad' is another surprisingly jazzy affair in parts and as well as being a vehicle for Ward's drum solo it features some nice playing from Iommi and some good steady bass work from Butler. Although I would have preferred to hear it as a section to a longer track rather than as a short stand alone instrumental.

The closing track 'Fairies Wear Boots' was split into two tracks on the American issue with the opening instrumental part being subtitled 'Jack The Stripper'. A loose mid paced jam with some typically frenetic Osbourne vocals over the top, for me, it is on a par with the classics of side one and ensures the album ends on a high note.

"Paranoid" was released in September 1970 only seven months after the debut album and like that album it was derided and panned by music critics throughout the world. Despite that the album sold quickly and was the first and only number one album that Sabbath ever had in the UK. The American release was delayed until January 1971 as the debut album was still in the chart there at the time of the UK release.

Nowadays "Paranoid" is generally regarded as one of the all time rock classics and for me it deserves that accolade. Personally though I slightly prefer the debut but there is no doubt that this is a genuine must own five star classic for any lover of heavy rock music.

This old warhorse of an album suffers from an all too common condition affecting most bands’ best, or perceived to best, or more popular work: over playing. See, most music journalists and radio programmers are notoriously lethargic, and rather than do the work (if it can really be called work) of truly understanding and assessing an artist’s music, all too often a band’s biggest seller becomes their “best” album simply by virtue of sales. And so we’ve all had certain albums hammered into our brains until we’re sick to death of them, no matter how good they happen to be. The net result is that classics like Dark Side of the Moon, Nevermind, Abbey Road, Kind of Blue, Are You Experienced, and many others (including the ENTIRE Led Zeppelin catalog… I mean get over it already) are rendered impotent due to over saturation.

In Sabbath’s case the damage was particularly acute. In my youth when classic radio stations played the band at all, it was invariably material from this album, and then only two of its well-worn cuts. (And I was listening in a major market: NYC!) Now Black Sabbath, to date and in their various incarnations, has issued 18 studio albums, 4 live albums, and I’m sure countless radio broadcasts of concert recordings languish in radio station vaults. And all we get is two measly cuts? This, my friends, is why mainstream music broadcasting (audio and visual) SUCKS SO BAD in our world.

But enough media bashing. Truth be told, while Paranoid is not my favorite Sabbath outing, it is a classic, and should have its place among metal’s legendary texts. The slightly tentative tone of their debut behind them, our heroes began to truly forge a sound all their own here. The RIFF is still king, as it was before, but these riffs are better by a good margin. Also better is the chemistry between the players, a fact that opener “War Pigs” bears out. The rhythms (part swing, part crush) and the interaction between Bill Ward’s drums and Geezer’s bass is truly impressive, as is the instrumental work during the song’s lengthy CODA, in which Tony Iommi really shows his inventive side as a guitarist. Ozzy for his part gives one of his best performances on record, and the song’s anti-war sentiment (as filtered through Sabbath’s old testament moralist stand and imagery) is potent and even timely some 35+ years hence.

The title cut follows and remains the band’s most famous tune and rightly so. It’s a quick, terse and catchy cut that boasts one of the most infectious riffs in history, as well as suitably depressive lyrics delivered in Ozzy’s now embedded style of following the chord changes with his ever unique voice. The acidic wash of “Planet Caravan” is pleasant enough to be Sabbath’s first visit to psychedelia, and perhaps reveals the band as still having their foot (or at least a toe) dipped into the Sixties rock styles they were in the process of escaping from.

And then there’s “Iron Man”, surely this band’s “Smoke on the Water” and one of the most recognized guitar riffs ever penned. The rub for me is that I really don’t dig the tune, partly due to over-exposure and part because there are at least 25-30 better songs in the band’s canon. For example, at least three of those cuts follow, so let’s move right into the droning, nearly psych dirge “Electric Funeral”. Iommi really gives his cry baby wah pedal a workout here, and Ozzy’s accompanying nasal whine compliments him perfectly. “Fairies Wear Boots” is one of the more dynamic early Sabbath tunes, boasting nice soft/loud contrasts, and bizarre lyrics retelling a tale of Ozzy’s less than pleasant encounter with a crew of skinheads. Finally “Hand of Doom” is odd lyrically, as it addresses and clearly questions the wisdom of Sabbath's own drug use, matched with some sweet, slow, syrupy riffs.

All told Paranoid is a metal landmark and certainly deserves the props it’s received over the decades since its release. But to arbiters of taste that tyrannically control the airwaves, please listen to at least one of this band’s other 20 or so recordings… we’ll settle for even light rotation if that’s all your lazy asses can handle.

With cover art like that, it’s gotta be good, right?

Black Sabbath is the quintessential Sigmund band. Like the OT (original therapist) Sigmund Freud, Black Sabbath were important without being very good. We owe a debt to both in that they forced us to think differently (Freud about the mind and Black Sabbath about rock n roll), but neither one of them actually did anything right. And just as today Freud’s thinking is respected while his methods are not, it’s hard to hear much of Black Sabbath’s second album Paranoid in any of the bands that list the band as an influence.
While The Who were maximum R & B , Black Sabbath were simply maximum blues. Their basic sound was an oppressively heavy, down tempo take on black American guitar music featuring little embellishment. Frankly, the music on Paranoid wouldn’t be so bad if it weren’t for the lengthy and ponderous instrumental passages and solos that pock the album.
In an interview once, Ozzy said that Black Sabbath was the last hippie band and the tracks on Paranoid prove just how accurate a description that was. Two tracks (‘War Pigs’ and ‘Electric Funeral’) are flat out anti-war protest songs while a third track, the stultifyingly dull ‘Planet Caravan’ is nothing more than Jefferson Airplane at half speed. Through in the cautionary heroin tale of ‘Hand of Doom’ and you’re looking at an album that can fit comfortably between Jimi Hendrix’s Are You Experienced and Iron Butterfly’s In-A-Gadda-Da-Vida.
As was mentioned earlier, while Black Sabbath was instrumental in the birth of the heavy metal genre, it was more important in that it proved that heavy music could be made, not how it should be made. Much of Paranoid is boring and ponderous and given over to pointless instrumental passages. Listen to ‘Iron Man’ (an insipid re-telling of the British children’s story with a new, darker ending) and notice how much of the song is just the same musical motifs over and over. The same goes for ‘Hand of Doom’, ‘War Pigs/Luke’s Wall’ all of ‘Rat Salad’ (the only completely instrumental song on the album) and the second half of ‘Planet Caravan’.
To make matters worse, Ozzy Osbourne’s lyrics are simply and juvenile, he doesn’t seem to be really trying to write something great. Plus, he doesn’t really sing so much as he chants like a school kid on the playground. Admittedly, he chants like a morbid, occultic school child, but still.
There are some bright spots on the album though. The title track is entertainingly speedy and blessedly short (many people say that ‘Paranoid’ is positively proto-punk, but their wrong, it’s the genetic seed that would become speed metal. Actually, the 40 second breakdown in the middle of ‘Electric Funeral’ is fairly punk, in a hardcore sort of way, what with its repetition of the title over a grinding guitar line, and it proves that the band could swing when it wanted to).The closing track, ‘Fairies Wear Boots’ is the most musically realized track, although the lyrics are best taken as a fashion tip… from guys who dress like 18th century cadavers.
It’s probably important that we give Black Sabbath their due, and acknowledge the role they played in creating one of the most popular rock n roll genre’s, but that doesn’t mean we have to be beholden to this 2 point album.

One of my higher 4.5's, and I might have to go all the way with it because drum solo "Rat Salad," while about as purposeful and enjoyable as "Moby-Dick" or "Toad" and easily the weakest song here, at least has the good sense to keep it short. But there is indeed a certain genius here. As is often the case with Black Sabbath, you kinda gotta look sideways to see it; you have to get past the bizarro semi-nonsense lyrics (there’s a lot missing in “Iron Man’s” narrative), the band’s technical limitations (Iommi still only knew that one guitar solo at this point, and he always double-tracks it, so the solo on “War Pigs” is the solo on “Paranoid” is the solo on “Iron Man”), and Ozzy’s complete ridiculousness as a frontman (he never sounds like he has any idea what the hell he’s doing, or even where he is, or even who he is, but I mean, do you want him to?).

The thing is, though, I like Black Sabbath’s limitations. They’re a lot of fun, and they contribute to the vibe that the band puts out, which is to say a buncha greasy high-school weirdos who dip out of class to smoke weed in the bathroom and slip zombie comix underneath their textbooks and get funny looks from their teachers, all of whom think they’ve got a bright future making license plates for the county. They also have a great sense of interplay and musical intuition, which more than makes up for their technical limitations. “War Pigs,” clearly the best song here, illustrates it beautifully - check out how it starts off as this slow grindy dirge, how it steadily picks up speed, how it just erupts into this series of brilliant riffs at the end. That huge riff (I assume it’s “Luke’s Wall”) that closes the sucker off? Jesus. Gimme that. See also every single riff on “Iron Man,” as well as its multiple instrumental passages, less guitar solos and more group rampages. Or check out that sick intro to the title track (which rips, and which benefits from a great Ozzy wail), or the pliable and almost funky rhythms on “Fairies Wear Boots,” which Iommi sometimes riffs over and sometimes sinks right into. It’s remarkably enjoyable stuff, especially the way Ozzy delivers that famous line from “Fairies” - “Smokin’ and trippin’ is all that you do… YEEEEEEEEEEEAHHHHHH!!!!”

I goof on Ozzy (wuagurghagh SHAAAAARON!) a lot because he’s inherently hilarious, but the thing is he’s a pretty good singer; to me, his finest performance here is on the lesser-known “Hand of Doom,” where he sounds like he’s about to die, alternatively too weak to get out of bed and manic in his terror; it plays with the lyrics perfectly. The song, of course, is enhanced by its great dynamic shifts (that bass-driven verse/explosive chorus contrast must have influenced Nirvana, right? And much credit to Geezer Butler here, as the sheer depth of his bass sound underscores Iommi’s low-tuned guitar quite tastily. ), and clever tempo change in the midsection. Clever is also a good word for “Electric Funeral,” which pairs a wicked wah with a double-time jazz section where Bill Ward proves he’s the most consistently surprising - in a good way - musician this group has to offer. It rocks. “Planet Caravan” actually doesn’t, but I like it too! It sounds like the band shrank themselves and their instruments just so they could record it inside of their favorite bong. It’s got a nice sense of atmosphere, and the band wisely lets it breathe.

Plus, I mean, those lyrics! Nuclear robo-zombies stagger across a blighted landscape! A dude travels through time to prevent a vague apocalypse and somehow or other becomes evil in the process! Generals gather in their masses just like witches at black masses! It’s a big weird proto-cybergoth party with plenty of lawnmower beer and pot brownies for all. Best Friday night you’ll ever spend.

I'm not so sure Black Sabbath would be remembered so powerfully, or have had such immense influence if it were just the debut that sat atop the 70's. Thankfully Paranoid was also made, and it's clear as day this is the one that echoes down through the decades, for oh so many reasons. It's really here that just...everything happens for lack of a better phrase. Where to start? Well I think it should start with Sabbath decisively pulling ahead of their contemporaries into a new thing. As hard and heavy as Deep Purple and Zeppelin would get? They wouldn't be coming close to the aesthetic being executed here. When I listen to those two I can hear some of the Metal influence sure, but at the same time the dominant sound I hear spinning off is what went on to build stuff like Aerosmith and Van Halen, as well as generic shit like Bad Company. Paranoid clearly establishes a new sound in a way even the Sabbath debut struggled to make claim to. Really only the title track there eked out the claim. Here? Almost every track has that vibe, and in several different flavors at that. Variety is the spice of life and that too is a strength to Paranoid. The first side really is a masterpiece of vinyl work frankly, having 3/4 classics helps surely. But the less talked about coolness of it is that variety being shown off. War Pigs has a slower sludgier tempo for the most part with breaks for quieter sung parts. The title track is a fast piece of incredibly catchy hard rock that seems to be the spark that eventually lead to the types of Metal bands that would dominate the early 80's. Planet Caravan is a spacey almost psychedelic song that is actually kind of pretty. And of course Iron Man is an orgy of powerful simple Metal riffs that probably more than any just establishes the hard heavy powerful aspect that is Metal. Today with so many Metal bands being hilariously pigeon holed in insanely specific whole sub-sub-sub-genres it's awesome to see the founder of the feast skipping through like four in one go. Imagine that shit nerds. But would that even matter I wonder if those songs were boring? Too bad they're well written as hell I guess. There's a reason that they're 3/4 classics you know, and it definitely isn't because people like variety alone. It's the songwriting stupid! Especially Iron Man and the title track, which cook a man alive with how easy and breezy these boys make their quality sound. The title track, man I could write all day about that one. I give lots of credit to Aerosmith (and EVENTUALLY Zeppelin) for liberating Hard Rock from the Blues. But Sabbath played their role too even if most of their career didn't quite bear it out, a song like that shows just how incredibly powerful and forward looking they could be. Speed up the tempo and lower the production and it could almost be a Punk song even! Or at least a Motorhead one! The second side of the record definitely shows a weaker hand, but by then it almost doesn't even matter. And hey the second side is good too. It showcases something that these guys have over early Zeppelin anyway, which is making interesting material. Songs like Fairies Wear Boots may meander a bunch, and lack the instant sharpness of side one? But the places they meander are worth hearing out, Sabbath goes some places with these tracks. Not just lifeless featureless wastelands that basic Hard Rock often seems to inhabit in my ears. Paranoid really is a monster record, and it lit a delayed fuse that would only really start burning up the music world way at the other end of this decade. If that's not being ahead of one's time I don't know what is.

Rating: 4.5
Highlights: War Pigs, Paranoid, Planet Caravan, Iron Man

Paranoid, ou la mort qui vient frapper à votre porte
Après la méga claque reçue par leur album éponyme en février de la même année, Black Sabbath revient 7 mois plus tard pour nous pondre un chef d'oeuvre devenu depuis LA référence ultime en terme de doom.

Je suis un fan inconditionnel du Sabbath et Paranoid, plus de 40 ans après, est unanimement reconnu comme étant la pierre angulaire, le sceau de ce qui peut se faire de mieux dans le domaine du doom/heavy/hard. C'est la base. La mesure-étalon de la discipline. La bitte d'amarrage sur laquelle on accroche son navire en cas de tempête tellement le cordage est épais, lourd et solide.

Le précédent album est bien différent. Plus dark, plus sombre. Avec Paranoid, on monte la température de plusieurs degrés pour atteindre celle d'un sauna. C'est chaud, c'est sec et ça picote dans les recoins. L'opus débute par War Pigs et Paranoid, deux chefs d'oeuvre du Sabbath mis bout à bout. C'est magique ! C'est unique ! C'est fou ! La face A est dingue de toute manière, et encore je pèse mes mots. Peu de mots pourraient correspondre à cette face. Planet Caravan est elle aussi absolument superbe. On sort du doom pour fourrer son nez dans un trip psyché de plus de 4 minutes. On sent les effluves de l'encens se dégager de la chanson. C'est tellement évident et palpable à son écoute. Idéalement placé avant Iron Man afin de reposer les esprits. Car ce qui vient d'être présenté relève de l'insolente perfection !

(aparté: pas facile d'écrire une review sur un groupe qu'on chérit en gardant un aspect critique sans se fondre dans le subjectif étouffant)

Donc je disais, Iron Man enchaîne dans une incandescence de l'enfer avec une guitare iommiesque de folie ! Iron Man les gens, Iron Man quoi ! Vous prenez feu avec ce morceau, littéralement. L'Homme de Fer vous broie la tronche comme c'est pas permis. Ça fait mal hein ? Eh ouais on est dans la cour des grands là, pas dans la courette de la variétoche à deux sous. Va falloir vous y faire si vous souhaitez vivre après ce brulot. Car la face B va vous achever si vous n'avez pas pensé à vous parer de votre armure.

La face B - Electric Funeral - commence fort avec la guitare wah-wah de Iommi. Allez hop c'est parti d'emblée. Ozzy prend le relai avec sa voix d'outre-tombe. C'est flippant ! Autant flippant que magnifique de maîtrise. Put*in quel génie n'empêche. On ne se rend pas compte du taf qu'y à derrière tout ça nom de dieu pour parvenir à un tel niveau ! Bref, Les Obsèques Électriques, fallait le trouver ce foutu nom quoi. En français ça en jette, surtout que la suite est Hand of Doom qui est reste dans l'esprit de la chanson précédente. On se balade dans la mort. J'trouve ça sympa comme promenade. Autant la faire de notre vivant afin de ne pas en avoir peur à l'orée de notre perte. (entendez par-là la fin, la mort) On y est préparé au moins. Ça se finira de toute manière donc autant en écouter des bribes pour déjà avoir un pied dedans.
Hand of Doom est composée de deux parties distinctes où la deuxième est beaucoup plus rythmée. Elle galope, chevauche dans la prairie, scannant alentour les ténèbres approchant. Puis elle se calme car elle est face à cette fameuse Main de la Mort. Oulala j'ai peur Sammy. Mais non t'inquiète mon pote, ça va bien se passer si tu la fixes droit dans les yeux sans détourner ton regard de mortel. Easy stuff, man.
Surtout qu'après y a une Salade de Rat, alors garde une place dans ton estomac pour la becquetance. Certes une salade de 2'30 minutes mais elle est consistante la bougresse. Elle te donne à bouffer pour la sortie en fanfare que sera Fairies Wear Boots. Le trou normand du menu.
Avachit après un repas bien copieux, vous voilà fin prêt pour déguster le dessert et le digestif. Pas belle la vie ? Car Fairies Wear Boots clôt à merveille Paranoid. Oui à merveille ! Elle fait partie de mon Top 10 des chansons de Black Sabbath. Sans problème. Pourquoi ? Ca me semble assez clair et limpide. T'as 6 minutes où t'as le droit à tout. Tout ! La chanson de doom par excellence. Tellement de variations dans un enchevêtrement instrumental de par sa rythmique lourde par essence, de la puissance jugulée. On pousse les décibels mais pas trop afin de garder cette teneur propre au Doom Metal qui se doit de rester dans un univers plombé en apportant des touches heavy (à distinguer en cela du Traditional Doom Metal, plus lourd et moins métallique aux entournures). C'est là toute la difficulté de ce 'sport'. Trouver l'équilibre parfait pour ne pas tomber de l'autre coté de la barrière (comme disait Alain). Je dis ça car perso, le doom doit rester le doom. Pas de mélange. On ne met pas d'alcool de térébenthine pour diluer la peinture dans le doom metal sinon on a droit à des choses horribles...et là je tairai les noms.

J'en reviens à mes brebis pour ne pas les égarer.
Fairies Wear Boots est un titre majeur dans le doom. Il appartient à cette catégorie de morceau qui transcende un être humain. (en tout cas moi ça me transcende)
Quand t'écoutes le Sabbath, tu en as pour ton argent, et tu fais pas le trajet pour que dalle. FWB clôt et parachève un chef d’œuvre. Ni plus ni moins. Cet album est une pièce unique dans la musique, dans son acception la plus large (au même titre que Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band ou encore Wish You Were Here). Il est ancré dans le patrimoine de l'humanité musicale à tout jamais.

Avoir cet album chez soi est le minimum qu'on puisse faire pour lui rendre hommage !
