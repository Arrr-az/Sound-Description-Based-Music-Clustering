ozzystylez Jun 26 2007

After the events of September the 11th 2001 the political climate of the planet took a dramatic turn and suddenly conspiracy theories, Bush bashing, self righteous indignation and student activism became popular. People felt that their basic human rights as citizens of whatever country they were living in were being challenged and abused. In the American Empire anger was directed from the political left towards the ruling classes and political views were polarised within the country.

Three years after the planes hit the towers and a full year or more after George Bush got dolled up in a flight suit and announced from on board a US Navy ship in safe waters that his war on terror was over and the mission had been accomplished, the Beastie Boys, apparently now a political force, sprang into action and produced a scathing critique upon the times that they found themselves living in. Fortunately for them the repercussions of the Iraq conflict still dominated the news and their bumper sticker sloganeering still had some relevance. In fact, bumper sticker and T-shirt slogans are still big business amongst lefties and righties, so To the Five Boroughs would still hold as much political clout had they released it today as it did when they chose to release it back in 2004, and that was, to use the political term, diddly squat. The main reason behind this is because they are musicians and not political activists. Admittedly there have been plenty of important and heartfelt statements made by musicians about the events that have constituted the early years of the 21st Century, but then those musicians werent renowned for their frat boy antics, dick jokes or public arrests for indecency. Look at Green Day for example.

Still, for whatever reason the Beastie Boys felt that they had to make a political record and make it they did. Having all of three years worth of material to comment upon, they centre mainly on obvious digs at George W and his foreign policy goals, they ask a lot of questions but offer no answers beyond impeaching the president. There is a mention of the Kyoto summit (again, another dig at Bushs policies) and not driving around in SUVs, and Adrock also admits that hes no better than us except for when hes rapping so thats a lie, but the material is like drunk arts school scholars vociferously debating with each other at the end of a heavy night of chemical intake, putting the world, as it is, to rights.

Neil Young made a similarly rushed album a couple of years later making the same points, but his record had the edge over the Beasties effort in that it had hook lines and melody and, for the most part, variation. To the Five Boroughs lacks those three essential ingredients which contribute to a good record (with a few exceptions, more on those later) and sounds as colourless as its cover. It has all the hallmarks of a rushed job, songs that have been made with no love for the sake of filling up chartable minutes and a despondency that leaves the listener wondering when the familiar three-way attack is going to kick in. The stripped down ethic becomes stagnant quickly and ideas are reused and rehashed, shamelessly and back-to-back. It seems that the Boys wanted to have people focus more on their lyrics, to hear the message that they thought that the record would carry, but when the message is peppered with the classic MC bragging about rhyming styles and macho bravado it is hard to really believe that the Beastie Boys have anything of merit to add to proceedings.

Even the lyric sheet has comments from the "editor" that express doubts as to, firstly, what the lads are harping on about and, secondly, the accuracy of what they are claiming, and one has to stop and wonder what exactly is going on here. Are the "editors" comments some kind of in-joke or is he really expressing concern as to the Beasties direction? No answers are provided to these questions either and the whole albums concept is directionless and confused.

So, that brings us to those saving graces. Opening track Ch-Check It Out (no unnecessary political statement making time was wasted on track titles either) delivers false hope that this is going to be a full on, in your face classic, and its successor Right Right Now Now (see what I mean) seems to carry the promise that the Beastie Boys sixth is going to be a balls out affair. But like a premature ejaculator who keeps on thrusting away with an increasingly limp dick, the good times ebb quickly away into sticky embarrassment. It is not until An Open Letter to NYC enters the fray like a needle shot of Viagra to the scrotum that things hot up again, and with its incendiary muted guitar line and unifying lyrical chant the promise of a good finale rises from the ashes. But this is a short lived reprise and it becomes apparent that, for the average, non-politicised B-Boy on the street, this is a sub-par release from Brooklyns finest.

Still, maybe some day in the not too distant future, George W Bush will pick this record up, give it a spin and hear what the Beastie Boys are spittin and then, and only then, might he see the error of his ways and make that call to Iraq, Afghanistan and the UN, apologise for his oversight on the battlefields of the world, and step down respectfully into his own bonfire. But that day is still a long away off, I fear, and the Beastie Boys have done little to draw it nearer.

Note from 2019: Well I completely forgot about this one. There was a lot of weird racial shit in the beginning of this review that I just decided to scrap because it would cause more problems than anything, and wasn't even that funny.

The Beastie Boys have a bit of a reputation for having similar song structure in every song. Now... this is completely true. But dammit, if Rakim can rap about how great he is on every song, the Beasties can have the same verse structure for 15 songs.

To the 5 Boroughs is no different. Every song consists of the three of them trading verses, with the other two members saying the last word of a line along with him. The thing about the Beastie Boys is that, quite frankly, they can do it because they've been doing it for 20-odd years. I also must make a bit of a confession: I have an unbelievably difficult time telling the difference between MCA and Ad-Rock. It's easy to distinguish Mike D, because his voice is deeper, but I have a harder time teeling the differences between the former.

As far as technical ability is concerned, depending on who you ask, the Beastie Boys are among the best to ever grab a mic, or the most annoying white people they've ever heard in their life. I think that they are leaning towards the former, because they're rhyming scheme is just as good in 2004 as it was in 1986.

As for the production, it may feel a little redundant over the course of an entire album, but the beats are generally stellar. Sure, some of them are pretty dull ("3 the Hard Way", "Rhyme the Rhyme Well", and "Shazam!" come to mind), but they fit the Beastie Boys perfectly. The tone and lyrics of the song never feel out of place, which is always a huge plus to me when listening to the album.

One of the most frequently asked questions regarding this album was whether the Beasties were still relevant at this stage of their career. Well... yes and no. Saying that the Beasties political statements are going to change people's minds, or that they are desperatley warranted from the American public, is like saying that Eminem's "Mosh", released 8 days before 2004 Election Day, played a significant role in changing people's mind about George W. Bush enough to make them vote for Kerry. Still, it is always nice to hear rappers that closely follow politics enough to make an eloquent point, and plus, I hate Bush, so I'm already a little bit biased.

In the end, I think that this album is both underrated and a bit overrated. I do think people should give this one a listen, because its a very good effort from one of Rap's most significant groups. On the other hand, Rolling Stone's perfect rating was a bit too much. There are many reasons to listen to To the 5 Boroughs, and all of them are good.

Highlights "Ch-Check It Out" (Despite being waaaayyy overplayed); "Right Right Now Now" (One of the best songs the Beastie Boys have ever done); "Time to Build" (Anyone who references the Kyoto Treaty in a rap song automatically gets bonus points); "Triple Trouble" (Can't go wrong with a "Rapper's Delight" sample); "Hey Fuck You"; "Oh Word?"; "That's It That's All"; "All Lifestyles"; "An Open Letter to NYC" (I hate New York, but it's still a great tribute); "The Brouhaha"; "We Got The" (One of rap's best album closers);

Lowlights "3 the Hard Way" (That's what SHE said!); "Rhyme the Rhyme Well" (Which, unfortunatley, they don't do in this song); "Shazam!" (If they had named the song "Kazaam!" instead, I probably would have like it more); "Crawlspace";

Sonically, this one is quite pulled back and reserved when compared with Hello Nasty. The psychedelic production and rich instrumental passages that highlighted their previous releases get dropped in favor of the most straightforward rap album in their discography. The beats are thin in some places and decent in others. Lyrically, it's a mix of serious political tracks and standard lackadaisical cuts, though I'll say the mixture of styles here is applied in a rather disjointed manner. Some cuts oscillate between playful and serious with very few sonic or lyrical bridges to make the transition fun and smooth.

"An Open Letter to NYC" is one of the best songs in their discography, and there are a couple other tracks here that evoke the witty and funky style they're known for, but songs like "All Lifestyles" suffer from a sort of difficult-to-place middle ground in that the subject matter is serious and worth consideration but forced lyrically in a fashion that feels uncharacteristically sloppy for the group.

This is not a bad album per se, but one that is a couple steps down from their best.

6.5/10

Mesmerizing, tantalizing, captivating, devastating
Ah, probably the most hated album of the Beastie Boys. While I don't think that this is the worst record by them ("Hot Sauce Committee Part Two" still exists), I agree with most people on its flaws.

First of all, the lyrics. They're kinda lame - most of the jokes / "funny lines" in the songs fall flat. Plus, some of the lyrics are highly unoriginal like MCA's usual "I'm from Brooklyn" line or Mike D's "I'm a Scorpio". They are extremely basic - we've heard all of this in the previous albums. Also, after 9/11 Beasties decides to write about politics - a huge mistake, as their lyrics sound...idk, just plain bad? You just listen to these amateurish lyrics, cringe and then move on. Beastie Boys can't write about politics - their previous non-album single "In a World Gone Mad" proved that. Secondly, the songs. Some tracks are forgettable, especially "It Takes Time to Build", "Rhyme the Rhyme Well", "All Lifestyles". These ones are really boring, and the rest is ok / kind of good but not the best. Actually, the only great tracks on that record are the singles (minus "Right Right Now Now" aka one of the worst Beastie Boys singles ever). "Ch-Check It Out" & "Triple Trouble" & "An Open Letter to NYC" are responsible for this album getting a score higher than 2.5. Thirdly, the overall production. I mean, this album is "fully hip-hop" without rock / funk/ etc influences. Bad choice. They were better with their distinctive alternative hip-hop sound and should've stayed with it. I've got a feeling that "ordinary rap" doesn't give them a chance to experiment with the sound and, as a result, we get...this.

I don't find this album bad. It's just painfully average. Average songs, average beats, average samples, average lyrics. Beastie Boys can (could) do a lot better. Such a disappointment after the groundbreaking "Hello Nasty".

Three best tracks (mentioned above but I'll repeat): "Ch-Check It Out", "Triple Trouble", "An Open Letter to NYC"
Three best tracks which are NOT singles? "Hey Fuck You", "Shazam!", "Crawlspace", probably. "The Brouhaha" is good, too.

Popular song has an age problem. Unless an act is specifically aiming itself at the adult-pop, adult-alternative scene, old-school soul demographics, they can end up looking very silly, very quickly. This is particularly the case when it comes to rock bands, and those that have negotiated the ageing process with their dignity intact are few and far between. Even rarer are those that manage to do it with their dignity enhanced. The only rock band I can think that has managed this has been Half Man Half Biscuit, and even then, there's a limit to the amount of dignity you can maintain with a name like that. Solo acts can sometimes manage to navigate their way to maturing audience with less hassle, but even then, those that manage to do it without trying a bit too hard to be cool are very much in the minority.

The Beastie Boys are an interesting example of the growing old with dignity within the realms of popular song. Given their earliest material was knowingly immature, they gave themselves a head-start with the whole perceived increase in maturity thing, and by the late 90s, they were not only seen as one of Hip Hop's legacy acts, but one of its most mature and enlightened too.

Thing is, by 2004's To the 5 Boroughs, while they were heralded as one of Hip Hop's most respected and mature acts, a whole slew of younger and more vital acts were dragging Hip Hop in new and exciting directions, while The Beastie Boys, were still The Beastie Boys. This was underlined on To the 5 Boroughs, which had a much more old-school Beastie Boys sound compared to their previous album, which was Hello Nasty, way back 1998. While lead single "Ch-Check It Out" performed respectably, To the 5 Boroughs just didn't seem like an event like most previous Beastie Boys had. There's certainly nothing bad about it, it's just that most of it doesn't make much of an impact. Where it works, it really works, and for me at least "An Open Letter to NYC" is the album's key track, and one of the best Beastie Boys numbers I have heard.

To the 5 Boroughs was the last Beastie Boys album that appeared on my radar, and while it's fine, it's not one of their best. At least they were growing old gracefully I suppose.

Bush-era politics and a post-9/11 New York.
To the 5 Boroughs is an interesting entry in the Beastie Boys catalogue. It's their fist album of the 2000's, their first in six years, a stylistic departure from Hello Nasty, and the album they chose to dedicate towards Bush-era politics and a post-9/11 New York. While the album can have its moments, as a whole it can feel flabby and lame. The instrumentals are all digital, completely foregoing the dense and interesting production of albums previous. Meanwhile the Boys's lyricism and flow are the worst they've been since Licensed to Ill. The whole thing feels like an attempt to regain the status the group had in previous decades. While I think there is sincerity within To the 5 Boroughs, it's not a project I would recommend unless you're already a Beastie aficionado.
