It was late one night back in 1980, not long after i discovered the wonderful world of music. It was over the summer, or maybe it was a weekend. Either way, it wasn't a school night (i don't think, anyway). And i was up late listening to music on my cheap little jambox radio. Turned down low, of course, so Mom and Dad wouldn't know i was still up listening. Back in those days, there was still such a thing as music on the AM dial, so that's where i was exploring. And all the way down in North Carolina, i picked up an AM station from Canada. I have no idea what station it was now, or where in Canada it was. I just remember it was in Canada. So here i was checking the station out, and they played XTC's "Generals and Majors". First i'd ever heard of XTC, and i loved it.

And, believe it or not, i was actually able to find the single in town. One of those big, black 7" singles. Remember those? Back in those days... Anyway, so i grabbed it. On the b-side was "Living Through Another Cuba". EGADS! It wasn't long before i picked up the parent album, Black Sea. GADZOOKS!

And i still love the album. From start to finish. Not a dud on the whole package. Excellent, extremely well-written, catchy - and sometimes a little dark - pop-rock tunes with doses of psychedelia, quirkiness, and experimentation. "Generals and Majors", "Living Through Another Cuba", "Love at First Sight", "No Language in Our Lungs", "Towers of London", "Paper and Iron", "Sgt. Rock (Is Going to Help Me)... man, i've gotten lots of enjoyment out of those tunes over the years.

I've bought more XTC albums over the years, as well, and this is far from being their only good offering - but it is still, to me anyway, the best. And the CD is even better, adding 3 bonus tracks - "Smokeless Zone", "Don't Lose Your Temper", and the excellent "The Somnambulist"... but read this review for such logic.

I love this album.

- Vos mains sur le buzzer... Top ! Je suis un album de pop anglais sorti en 1980. Je vaux à moi seul au moins cinq albums des Beatles. Ma production n'a pas vieilli d'un poil trente ans plus tard. Je suis... Oui ?
- C'est... La mer noire ?
- Oui ! Gagné ! Un - à - la - suite !

A l'ère d'Internet, on ne peut plus raconter n'importe quoi. Tout circule très vite sur toute la planéte, et on peut très vite se retrouver menacé de mort pour avoir par exemple pris ses aises avec un plat à tagine (un certain Grolandais peut en témoigner). D'où ma question: est-ce que je risque quelque chose à affirmer que Black Sea tient la dragée haute à n'importe quel album des Beatles ? Cela dépend aussi de ce quon attend d'un album de pop. Car sur Black Sea, il n'y a pas l'ombre d'une ballade ("No Language in Our Lungs", à la rigueur). Je n'ai même jamais entendu un album de pop aussi dynamique, grâce en partie à la performance du batteur Terry Chambers (un poste pour lequel les Beatles ne sont pas gâtés, c'est vrai). Tout l'album donne envie de remuer et de se défouler, en particulier le single ultime "Generals and Majors". Il y a les chansons qu'on regrette d'avoir en tête, celle-là c'est quand je ne l'ai pas en tête que je me plains tant elle est parfaite. Mais tout Black Sea est fabuleux: le ska accéléré de "Living Through Another Cuba", les singles "Respectable Street" et "Generals and Majors", et en clôture "Travels in Nihilon", le titre le plus en adéquation avec la pochette. Une sorte de traversée des éléments déchaînés, avec la basse qui reproduit le flux et le reflux des vagues, la guitare qui déclenche des coups de tonnerre et la batterie dans le rôle du déluge. On ne peut qu'applaudir la production fabuleuse, claire et riche, à l'opposé de celle de Drums and Wires, avec beaucoup d'effets savoureux (les coups de marteaux qui rythment "Towers of London"), ainsi que la recherche de sonorités différentes pour certains instruments. Ainsi, sur "Love at First Sight", la caisse claire a un son caoutchouteux qui accompagne à merveille les riffs typés Gang of Four, alors que sur "Living Through Another Cuba" elle est plus métallique, et lorsque le batteur s'emballe le morceau vire au drum 'n bass ! Pas mal pour un disque sorti en 1980... Et puis il y a les paroles. Ah, là, c'est autre chose que les âneries du style "Monsieur Chou-Fleur va cueillir des fleurs". Les voisins acariâtres, les hauts gradés militaire qui s'ennuyent, les jeunes amoureux qui veulent tout sur un plateau, la guerre froide, et le top du top sur "Sgt. Rock (Is Going to Help Me)", Andy Partridge qui demande l'aide d'un super-héros pour vaincre sa timidité et pouvoir enfin aborder une femelle. Dernier points à souligner: les faces B de XTC sont toujours soignées, celles qui figurent ici en bonus le prouvent. "Don't Lose Your Temper" est correcte, "Smokeless Zone" prolonge un peu la frénésie de la première face à coups d'harmonica en folie, et "The Somnambulist" est plus éthérée mais néanmoins très sympathique. L'idéal est de se procurer le remaster, car sur les premiers pressages CD, Virgin case les bonus en plein milieu du disque, entre les deux faces, pour une raison qui m'échappe. Sinon, pour résumer: album parfait, sans rien à jeter, et qui annonce de grandes choses. Car XTC a encore beaucoup de choses à dire...

XTC are the band the word quirky was born to be used on. There are weirder bands than them for sure but they strike a splendid balance between being fun and just wayward and out there enough to make them really stand out. Just listen to the vocal parts of "Burning with Optimism's Flame", why the hell does Andy alternatively speak, yodel or mumble before finally singing a bit? Doesn't matter one iota but it sure is entertaining.

"Black Sea" is an astoundingly potent pop record. These were still the young and brash XTC who pumped out high energy anthems like "This Is Pop" and since Andy's meltdown was a long way into the future it meant they were still a band who played concerts regularly, which helped lend their music a more wired edge.

The jury might eternally be out as to why I've slept on Andy Partridge-helmed projects for so long. Perhaps it's because I'd been indoctrinated into the Talking Heads fold early on in my life (props to those two hip-ass second cousins with three-plus decades of age between them and me) and never gave much thought to actually trying XTC out despite the fact that these bands toured together and, for all intents and purposes, have similar styles of herky-jerky new wave. Surprising precisely no one, I'm a huge Heads fan, but after hearing Black Sea and an excerpt from Skylarking, XTC is a formidable comparison and deserves heaps of praise.

Whereas the songs that Byrne penned for the Heads have appeal by virtue of their awkward yet funky propulsive energy, Partridge's ear for the pop-informed melodies by XTC is exceptional, so I think that there is a greater degree of recall for me with this body of work. He has a playfully sarcastic bent to his well-written lyrics, and Colin Moulding is no slouch, either.

These songs are so packed with energy and idiosyncrasy that it almost feels like Black Sea is a backdoor server to my brain, bouncing restlessly from number to number with amusing and articulate lyrical musings. It's a tremendous effort, but I lap pop/new wave/post-punk with quirky-to-unhinged-pipeline sensibilities up faster than anyone else I can think of, so I was bound to enjoy it at an absolute minimum, but I'm basically blown away. These guys were fantastic.

It only took me thirty two years to 'discover' this album, having quite recently started my XTC adventure. For some reason I didn't entertain XTC as a music obsessive late-teenager. The only reason I can muster at this late stage, is perhaps they were a little too complicated, but that's not a valid reason, much of the music I liked at the time had some complexity and eccentricity about it.

I have possibly enjoyed 'Black Sea' more than the other XTC albums I have listened to so far. It's a very consistent collection, and it offers the complete repertoire of what this band has to offer. This is basically great indie-pop, and so intelligently arranged with amazingly creative lyrics. Yes this is the real deal, and I truly understand why their music is so highly rated. I am only very sorry that I was not there at the time... silly me!

 Car_Crash_Interior Jul 25 2022
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
The beginning of the post Black Sea period
XTC, every single time they [resent a new album it's so full of bliss, psychedelia, and stimulation you can't help but give these post-punkers multiple listens to catch all the subtle nuances they slip in lyrically and musically. Black Sea is no exception. After the massive haul of songs on Drums And Wires eleven songs feels like a seriously conservative and well cropped list. Production wise Black Sea is tight and sharp. While it's got kinks here and there they are easily glanced over by the sheer volumes of entertainment that is presented everywhere else.

Opening with Respectable Street and Generals And Majors XTC come out of the gate strong and defiantly stronger than their last album, especially with their comedic critique of the military and the hypocrisy of combat and those who engage in it. Living Through Another Cuba continues more modern political critique in both a funny and serious way. Of course the comedic aspect squeezes in in the way that the song is like a Post-Punk latin conga. The steel drums tumble all over that song in the most fashionable and tasteful ways. While the following track Love At First Sight looses its potency just because its hook isn't as strong as the others. Which says something when practically every single song has amazing hook after hook. I Will call out Rocket From A Bottle and No Language In Our Lungs that both perfectly capture the goofiness and spry sporadic energy that the band does so well. Towers Of London slows the pace down to a somewhat average level. Markably noticeable amongst all the other much thorough music.

Paper And Iron (Notes And Coins) stays the course, the blunt banging drums and the gnashing guitars are so angular amongst Andy's stressed vocals. It's a sight to behold, but then we come to the follow up Burning With Optimism's Flames. This is a perfect case (along with Living Through Another Cuba) of the whole band working so scarily well together (Shout out to Terry Chambers who is killing it on the drums)! Andy's lyrics each verse where he spews words and words and words and drones his voice to the beat is just so clever! Sgt. Rock (Is Going To Help Me) levels up even more. By far the most ear catchy chorus on the whole LP. A little 60's sway and click of the glockenspiel that cap off the already magnificent.

Closing off the album the album is the freakishly chaotic Travels In Nihilon. It's chaotic almost like a Public Image Ltd. song. the tumultuous drums explode over the rolling bass and guitar that follows Andy's surprisingly concentrated vocals. When you look back at this album it's even more focused on the percussion than almost anything else that XTC had done up to this point. The band is tight and their skill transcends the roots of all they had done previously and Black Sea is the mark of the second iteration of XTC. There is everything before Black Sea and everything after. Because you can hear where this album changes the way the band behave and interact on English Settlement and Skylarking.
