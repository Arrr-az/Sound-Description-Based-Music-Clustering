The fourth of five albums released in 2017, and the band shows clear signs of losing steam creatively. Only two tracks are worth listening to, and even those two are much too long. There is a greater emphasis on space rock this time around, which doesn’t shine through on many tracks, largely due to being devoid of spirit or energy.

“Crumbling Castle” is the de-facto 11 minute suite. A prog-rock suite that doesn’t sound particularly engaging. With annoying guitar licks and a thick layer of noise, the track is a confusing mess. At times, it breaks off into more symphonic, flute-driven prog rock ala Jade Warrior or Colosseum, but of course, the entire track is ruined by amateurish vocals. The rest of this record fails to innovate or excite. At best, the closing track “The Fourth Color” is a spaced out re-interpretation of the melodies and counterpoint of Yes; even if it is coloured by amateurish melodies and the use of spaced-out ambience to fill time.

How this band continues to falter musically is beyond me. This is certainly not the worst of the five albums released, but that’s not worthy praise to a band that has proven themselves far more capable as musicians and songwriters. The rut they dig themselves into creatively by this point is nearly inescapable at this point. Fortunately, the next and final record they produced in 2017 features a radical sonic departure. One can only hope it’s at least passable

I'm a fairly new Gizzard fan, and currently going through their entire discography in order of release. Before this journey, I had only heard a few of their most popular and highly rated albums. I am finding their 2017 era to be pretty interesting. Most of the Gizzard I've heard thus far has been fairly immediate and easy to enjoy. But in 2017, pretty much all of their albums from that year were more subtle, and each one of them growers. During this time, the band were obviously changing things up and exploring/expanding their sound, with a lot more jazz influences and focus on textures and scales.

Out of all of their 2017 albums, this one took the longest for me to "get." It went from being what I thought was their most overrated release, to being one of their most fascinating. This is definitely their most "detailed" and textured album so far. You can listen to this over and over again, and discover new things each time. They focus on soundscapes and textures and fill in every inch of sonic space with things to listen for. Being from Gizzard, this is a true concept album, with songs that reference each other lyrically and musically. True to the album's title, many riffs here sound angular and seem to be based on polygonal patterns. But it's the lyrics, and the unusual ways the band mixes the vocals with the music, that made me finally appreciate the album and keep me returning to it. These might be the best-written songs of their career so far (by that, I mean up to late 2017), the songwriting managing to strengthen the concept. And it's also impressive how they often use the sound of the vocals as part of the texture and sometimes have the voices hypnotically wash in with the instruments and effects.

I think that by the release of this album and the even more subtle Gumboot Soup, by the time 2017 was finished, these guys had grown a lot as a band after flexing themselves sonically over the course of five interesting albums. Looking forward to where they went from here.

Giving out an album for free is a daring move given the challenges bands face these days, but then again if you've put out five albums in a year making one of them a freebie perhaps is a good idea - after all, if you've flooded your discography with new material, new listeners might not know where to start. A free release helps break them through the decision paralysis.

Working on the basis of "the first taste is free" is also more than thematically appropriate for King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard, who play a very psychedelic style of progressive space rock; there's plenty of gentler and more refined instrumental performances deep in here, but they're sharing the space with intense psychedelic workouts, pulsating motorik rhythms, and intricate vocal harmonies. The band draw from 1970s prog to modern electronic music when they put together their sonic toolkit, and every tool gets ample use here.

I'm not sure I want dozens and dozens of King Gizzard albums, but this has certainly left me more inclined to dip into their ample back catalogue.

Searching... for The Fourth Colour
Polygondwanaland stands as not only one of the greatest contributors to King Gizzard & The Lizard Wizard lore in the form of the gizzverse, but it also stands as a progressive rock epic among the greats of King Crimson, Rush, and Yes. The lack of standard 4/4 time signatures give it a quality unlike those of King Gizzard's contemporaries, and when it finally goes into 4/4 for Searching... it gives it a menacing quality as though the album is marching menacingly towards the final track. It serves as my favorite album of all time, from the sleek opening of Crumbling Castle, to the killer bridge of Loyalty, to the chaotic ending of The Fourth Colour it doesn't lose its energy or soul one bit.
