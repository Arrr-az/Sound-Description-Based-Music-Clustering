I’m gonna stand by my original review. I listened to Mr. Jagger, Sir Jagger, and half of the Glimmer Twins talking about recording this record. He went on about the drugs, and the orgies, and the album cover, and the film [of which he personally wanted more concert footage and less drug taking, while his counterpart Kef Richards thought there should have been more film of the drug taking ... or was that just more drugs]. Either way, he went on to say that during these years this was how an album was made ... I’m not sure that’s true, though it does speak volumes for an album that I’ve always felt was rather weak, and signaled the decline of the Rolling Stones. Granted the album does have it’s moments, but those moments would have made up a great single album, rather then this weighted double platter.

Of course he went on to point out all those involved, and the staggering amount of time it took to create, but listen, these guys were millionaires, they could afford to spend forever recording, and spicing things up with whoever they could fly in to give them advise or inspiration ... and I have a theory, and it sort of goes like this, ”If you put four monkeys in a room with the best engineers and session musicians in the world, add to that advise from sensational talent, you’re gonna come out on the other side with something good.” Oh, and that applies to U2 as well ... really, those guys go into the studio with the idea of writing a new album, and they spend a year there, with the best talent, like Brian Eno, and they produce a very listenable album, but I’ve gotten sidetracked. Now Exile is even BIGGER! Mick tells about reels of tape being lost, and rediscovered, and then he and Kef time-slip back into 1969, and add lyrics that fit the tunes. It doesn’t sound like 1969, they sound like today singing karaoke to songs I’ve never heard before. I’m not going to dismiss these songs, I’m sure there are many who will find them exciting and delightful, I’m just not one of them ... man, I even hated the cover, and I’m not sure if I could have tripped out to Beggar’s had it been released with the toilet jacket either, but that’s just me.

The real treat is listening to Mick and having that “Ah Ha” moment. He’s asked about the current nature of downloading, and he says that he’s rather comfortable with it. He goes on to say that there was a window from 1969 to about 1995 when artists where paid rather handsomely for their records, an aspect that didn’t exist before, and doesn’t exist now, with all things sort of balancing out. This is where the “Ah Ha” moment comes in ... the Rolling Stones have re-issued, or is that re-reissued one of their most controversial albums with sort of new/old material, attempting to cash in once or twice again. See, it’s not enough to have just the music from Exile, one MUST own the package, and to own the package, is to make The Stones even richard, I mean richer.

I’m not gonna talk anyone out of spending their money here, I don’t even want to, I’ll just keep my memories and not get sucked up into the game. For those who didn’t read my initial review, here it is for your pleasure, re-issued with some tasty words, thoughts, and typing abilities that weren’t available a couple of years ago.

I was there when this joke of an album was released onto the public. I was there in 1972, money in hand, waiting to get my new Stones album, my apartment was arranged and equipped [nod nod, wink wink], waiting for me to get home and spin these records. I’d even gotten myself a new Stanton cartridge for my turntable in honor of this moment, it came in a really cool little silver coloured metal box, all black velvet lined, with it’s own special machined screw driver ... which, unlike the album, I have to this very day.

So I’m walking home and checking out the cover. Man, this had to be one of the worst album covers ever. I mean, there was nothing pleasing about it at all. But hey, turns out I’ve got myself a double album here, the weight of it alone should mean there’s lots of great stuff between the sleeves of this jacket.

Flash forward ... I remember speaking with John Mellencamp for a bit, discussing musical inspirations, best loved albums, and he told me that ‘Exile On Main Street’ was his favorite Stones album. I said, ‘John, that was a terrible album, they’ve never done anything like it since.’ He went on to say that it wasn’t like ‘Sticky Fingers’ and I gave him that, and then asked him to go on and hum any one of the great [?] tunes form the release. He never did.

Now, to the Stones credit, there are some fine songs on the records, but this is not a great album. And even the fine songs are, shall I say, lacking at having any of the edges polished. I hear you, ‘What’s wrong with a rough album?’ I’d have to answer, ‘Nothing, if that’s your intention, but this, these pages were just torn out of past rejects thrown together, packaged and shipped out.

Let’s put some rumors to bed, and shine some light on some truths. Not all of this album was recorded in the basement of Keith’s home, Nellcote, near Nice, in France ... where the studio, was so damp that the instruments could not remain in tune for even one song. A place were thousands and thousands of dollars of heroin and other drugs were freely flowing into the house, up the noses and veins of most of the musicians [primarily Richard's, Miller and Keys]. Founding members of the stones who didn’t use, found the place preposterous and stayed away [Jagger, Wyman and Watts]. This is why you will find these key members playing on less then half the tracks. Oh, and that great recording studio in Keith’s basement, it was so bad that they had to bring in the mobile recording studio to even finish this piece of dribble.

In England, at Mick Jagger’s country home, is where many of the basic tracks were laid down. Most likely in France, is where the tracks, ‘Casino Boogie, Rip This Joint, Turd On The Run, Ventilator Blues, Happy, Shake Your Hips’ and several others were recorded. At any rate, ALL of this material was taken to Sunset Sound Recorders, in Los Angeles for complete overdubs of ALL piano, ALL backing vocals, ALL bass riffs, ALL lead guitar, ALL lead vocals, and ALL keyboards. Mick brought in a virtual who’s who of session men, like Billy Preston and Dr. John, to as best they could, straighten this thing out.

Ya, they did a great job on this one from the get go ... NOT! But when you have legendary drug users the likes of Keith Richard's, Gram Parsons [who despite history, never played on the records], William Burroughs and Terry Southern, floating in and out, it must have been tough to concentrate on even getting high.

There were many personal problems both between wives and band members during this time. There was also a conflict in the musical direction the band was headed. Mick, openly stated that he was tired of Rock & Roll, and perhaps this album shows directions they would like to head in the future.

There is nothing tight on this record, the music is sloppy, scattered, off handed, has difficulty finding it’s next chord, yet alone its next step. Vocals by Keith are so terrible, they take me to the point of laughing. This record should have never been made, it should have remained as Dylan and The Band’s ‘Basement Tapes,’ something for us to discover years later and connect the dots.

So, where does this leave us? In the years since its release, ‘Exile On Main Street’ has charted at number seven on Rolling Stone’s ‘Top 500 Albums Of All Time.’ But I truly believe that people say they like it just because they’re afraid they’ll appear stupid or not cool if they don’t say they dig it. As if they were to leave this record on their shelf long enough, it’s going to soak in and one day they’ll wake up saying, ‘I get it!’

Last weekend I’m in my local used CD store, and sure enough, there’s a copy of ‘Exile On Main Street.’ So what do I think? I think, ya’ know, maybe I was wrong about this album, perhaps I should give it a second chance. So I buy album, I drop the discs on the tray, put my feet up, open the tiny booklet that I can’t even read and kick back...THIS STILL LETS ME DOWN, THERE'S NO CONTINUITY OR EVEN THE FEELING THAT THIS RELESE IS ANYTHING MORE THEN OUT TAKES, YOU GUYS OWE ME ANOTHER $14.00!!!

OK ... thanks for all the messages, regarding how much many of you like this record. Personally, from the album I have kept these songs, ‘Tumbling Dice, Loving Cup, Let It Loose, and Stop Breaking Down.’ I could live without any of them but I choose not to.

The album artwork: When the buying public took their first look at the design and imagery of the sprawling record cover, most people admitted that they didn’t “get it”. Having just soaked in Warhol’s ultimately iconic cover with a zipper for Sticky Fingers, fans should have been ready for anything, but John Van Hamersveld’s designs seemed to confound them, asking them to digest a rough, anti establishment, punk-before there was punk collage of images that may have, initially, combined with the unfamiliar musical stylings to impact sales. And so when Van Hamersveld, who’d established his industry cred via his poster and package designs for Hendrix, The Beatles (Magical Mystery Tour), Jefferson Airplane (Crown of Creation) and others, was approached by the Rolling Stones, who were in a studio in LA putting the finishing touches on this new album, to work on the graphics and packaging for a songbook project the band wanted to release, he along with Robert Frank and Norman Seefjoined set about creating a disturbing jacket that attempted to feature the outlaw, circus freak nature of the Rolling Stones themselves.

Although it’s widely assumed to be a collage, the main shot was actually a photo of the wall of a Route 66 tattoo parlour, taken by art director Robert Frank as he passed through in 1950. To underline the key point, that the disreputable, drug-addled and tax-exiled Stones were as much outsiders as the circus freaks, the reverse of the sleeve had shots of the band members assembled in a similar collage effect.

This is the hardest of the classic Stones albums to get into but the most rewarding once you do. Once you learn to love Exile on Main Street, it's easy to think that Beggar's Banquet and Let it Bleed were artistic compromises. The Stones might have loved dirty blues from the beginning, but until Exile it was always mixed up with pop touches and sweet bits in the melodies. Exile on the other hand is gritty, raw, real and uncompromising. It's a still a hybrid- it's rock, blues, gospel and country all mixed up together, but this is not a commercial record by any measure. It didn't sell well on its release and even the critics didn't get it at first (though eventually it would be acclaimed as a masterpiece.) It is here for me that the band seem to have the greatest mastery over the numerous influences. They do soulful numbers well, they do raunchy hard-edged rock well, the ballads are beautiful and heartfelt.

If you don't love this album at first, persist with it. Don't expect something as immediately enjoyable as Sticky Fingers. But once you 'get it', you will realize you've struck pay dirt- this is the best album in their whole, vast discography. The idea that there are 200 or more albums in pop music history that are better than this is ridiculous. This is rock and roll royalty.

A toss up between 'Exile on Main Street' and 'Let It Bleed' as the Stone's best album. I think I actually favor the more cohesive sound and feeling of 'Exile', and in particular I enjoy the stripped-down and raw nature of most of the blues tracks. Keith Richards and Co. are in fine drunken swagger on such tunes as the roadhouse rave-up "Rip This Joint", Slim Harpo's sinewy "Shake Your Hips", the country blues "Sweet Virginia", and Robert Johnson's "Stop Breaking Down". Although it sounds amazingly focused, the recording sessions in France were anything but cohesive and well-rehearsed; in fact, it's amazing the album was completed at all, considering Keith Richards' astounding heroin addiction (it was said that weekly thousands of dollars in heroin passed through the mansion The Stones were recording in), and the other members, Jagger, Wyman, Watts and Mick Taylor only made occasional visits to lay down tracks. It wasn't until the sessions moved to Los Angeles and Mick Jagger took charge that the album was engineered in its present form.

And oh what a form it is! Richard's song "Happy" was purportedly recorded in one drug-induced sitting in the basement of the French mansion (with overdubs added later at a safe distance), the songs "Shine a Light", "Tumbling Dice" and "Loving Cup" found there inspiration in a evangelical church Jagger attended in L.A. (although it seems almost blasphemy for Mick to step inside a church), and the list of hall-of-fame worthy pianists who played on the album is mind-boggling: Dr. John, Billy Preston, Ian Stewart and Nicky Hopkins. There are so many great songs on this double album that any list I offer would be extensive. So I'll be brief. In additions to several I've already mentioned, other favorites include "Sweet Black Angel", "All Down the Line" and "Ventilator Blues". 'Exile on Main Street' represents the last masterpiece The Stones made before mediocrity set in. Mick Jagger started hangin' with Bianca at Studio 54 and turned to disco. After that, they became a parody of themselves, or, in Keith Richards case, a parody of a pirate. Too bad. Great album.

This, is how a double album is done. Double albums are kind of a lost art, made largely meaningless in the CD era, and are even more threatened by the Digital era. Some ill advised CD size double albums were made sure (many awful Hip-Hop ones that still count as the worst musical experienced I've had), and I don't really know of many since MP3's came around. But I don't think it's an irrelevant subject, if anything the responsibility toward making longer albums work has been neglected since CD's came along. Many album from the late 80's on would be so much better with the lessons of great double albums taken into account. More is not necessarily more, in fact it can be less. The longer you go the more dangerously you risk smashing your ship on the rocks. Treat it like the powerful substance it is. And if you want to know how to go about it here's one of the best examples around, Exile On Main St. Of course I'm not suggesting everyone could make this...it takes talents and skills not every bunch of musicians will have. Let me start with what really makes Exile so impressive, it's sameness. That's right, normally a problem, but here no problem. Now it's not a REASON the album is good, instead it's that in spite of this dangerous ingredient that it doesn't slow it down any. It should, but it doesn't at all. And this is coming from someone who isn't in love with Blues Rock at all. If a Post-Punk double album casually blew me away it wouldn't mean much for me to say it worked with being samey. I'm saying this about a BLUES ROCK ALBUM, that's extremely high praise understand. Let It Bleed had a fair eclecticism within the Blues Rock sound, this doesn't. And yet no problem. It's almost hard to explain, but it has to come down to them being just so good at it by this point. Albums are generally little marathons for the listener, for our mind and ear. They are in their way exhausting to do, least ways the double albums are. Plotting out an album? I've yet to see another double album as impressive as this one, some maybe I think are better, but none have pulled off the magic trick this one did.

The Rolling Stones are my favorite band of all time. No other band has captured my spirit, and just made me feel real good as often as they have. The number of great songs they've done is countless, and they made several of what could be considered among the greatest albums of all time, including "Beggars Banquet", "Sticky Fingers", "Let it Bleed", "Between the Buttons", and on a good day "Aftermath" and "Some Girls" could be thrown in there too. However, as phenomenal as those albums are, and as much as I love The Stones as a band, nothing else they ever did quite captured gritty free flowing brilliance and the otherworldly magic they captured on this album. One of the things I like about the Stones is how, as great as they were, and as popular as they were, you never got the sense that they were TRYING to appeal to anyone in particular. In reality, they probably were, but compared to most popular bands, where albums and songs feel carefully planned and crafted, The Stones early records often feel like just a bunch of friends jamming. They happen to be incredibly talented friends, playing really great songs, and the songs are polished enough for the rawness not to be distracting, yet the vibe is always warm, inviting and real (and gritty and badass, and of course, fun at the same time). On this record that attitude and that feel is taken to the extreme. In fact, during my own early listens of this album I found it to be underproduced and unmemorable (same goes for other people I've talked to and read opinions of). Subsequent listens however revealed its charms more and more until eventually it became one of the absolute greatest albums I've ever heard in my life. Not only is this album not underproduced, it's the most well produced album of all time. That's not hyperbole either. This, "Exile on Main Street", is the greatest sounding record I've ever heard. My speakers illuminate when I play this perfectly mixed, perfectly mastered album, and in a better world, most rock albums would aspire to sound more like this. Of course a lot of people don't like the production. Even people who like the album complain about "muddy sound", and "vocals too low in the mix". This is understandable, because as I mentioned, I didn't like the sound at first either. However, much like most things that truly are brilliant, the sound of this record takes time to soak in before you realize its greatness. Barely any of these songs got radio play or became well know (to non fans) classics, and the album is better for that. There isn't a whiff of any kind of commercialism, or pandering anywhere. It's pure art through and through. Yet, the album isn't ARTY at all. It's just a bunch of really really great, fun, bluesy, rockin', and honkey-tonkin' songs that flow together perfectly, and as a whole create something naturally, intensely beautiful. I'm listening to the album now for probably the thousandth time in my life, and even being in the depressed state I'm in, I can't help but keep a perpetual smile on my face as I listen to every one of these perfect, gritty gems. This isn't merely The Stones greatest album, it's exists on another plane from their other albums. Even the other great Stones albums are merely brilliant seminal masterpieces. This album is in it's own world. It IS it's own world. A world that's pure as water, yet harsh and hedonistic as whiskey. Like a lot of truly great music, it manages to feel comfortably familiar, and completely unique at the same time. It feels more honest and human than most other albums, and yet it feels like a beautiful alien land compared to most other albums as well (perhaps partially because most albums DON'T feel honest and human). I'd almost rather not even think about the individual elements that make this album so great but one thing I have to mention is the use of instruments. The brass sections, and rhythm sections, especially when married with the production is just sublime. The great thing is that this album superficially feels minimalistic, but when you listen closely to the songs they're actually densely layered. All the sounds just flow so naturally, and the production is so perfectly modest, that the songs don't seem dense to the basic senses. Like I said, I'd almost rather not mention individual songs, because I love them all, but I'll mention briefly that "Sweet Virginia" is my favorite song, and "Turd on the Run" is the most underrated. Also "I Just Wanna See His Face" isn't a throwaway, it's great, and the album wouldn't be the same without it. I tend to get carried away when I discuss my favorite albums, so this review might seem sickeningly gushing, but I guess this album just impresses me, thrills me, and warms my heart so much I can't help that. This album is a one of a kind masterpiece and it'll probably be one of my favorites for a long long time.
