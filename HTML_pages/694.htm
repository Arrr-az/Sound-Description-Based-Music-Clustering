Notable mantras:

~'Big fucking shit! Right now, man....'

~'Let the products sell themselves! Fuck advertising, commercial psychology! Psychological methods to sell should be destroyed!'

~'The idea of my life seems like a symbol.'

~'The people will survive...in their environment.'

~'They are children playing with guns/They are children playing with countries.'

~'Our band could be your life.'

~'I'm fucking overwhelmed!'

Notable other things to listen for:

(Assembled in random order because almost every song on the album sounds distinct and separate from those around it even if it might not feel that way at first.)

~The way "Shit from an Old Notebook" begins like someone turned on a tape recorder mid-jam, quickly followed by Boon just tearing through the curtains with those opening lines. "Notebook" is the kind of straightforward call to action that we simply don't get enough of anymore, partly 'cause we don't have many musicians as in-touch with each other's strengths and weaknesses as these three dudes were listen to how precisely (and quickly) they all synchronize, with Hurley's amazing rolls (first one under 'commercial psychology'), Boon's extended closing solo, and Watt's nursery rhyme rhythm underneath it all meshing into a one-and-a-half minute burst of awesome. Also, I don't know if that split-second chord Boon plays at 0:41 was an accident or not, but either way I'm really happy it's here. Let yourself be heard!

~The sheer jazziness of Boon's reaches that bring out "Theatre Is the Life for You", as lyrical as the saxes he imitates.

~The way "The Glory of Man" seems to be accelerating even though it isn't. And that trebly four-chord riff that kicks all our asses.

~The culminating line of "There Ain't Shit On T.V. Tonight", a song that you might think would be a straight-up indictment of media saturation - and it is - but comes to the conclusion that 'We are responsible.'

~The splash cymbal skimming in "Nature Without Man".

~The driving guitar climb of "West Germany", which I've tried for years to play with timing as on-the-mark as Boon's and still can't get perfect. And dig how it feels like Hurley has slightly syncopated the beat in the last third.

~Boon's bluesy intro to the (live, audience-recorded) cover of CCR's "Don't Look Now".

~"Themselves", which is perhaps the strongest and most succinct musical evisceration of Reaganesque corporate greed and thus, a song still relevant to this day. Listen to how Boon keeps one track of guitar strumming in the background, leaving the focus on Hurley's unifying tom rolls and the snarl of the louder chords. Protest music.

~The way "Do You Want New Wave or Do You Want the Truth?" surprises you by breaking the more aggressive flow of the previous songs to let you meditate for a minute or two the band pose philosophical quandaries over gentle guitar that swirls like you're slowly away on a magic carpet. Except it's Friday night and you're just lying on a friend's floor listening to the stereo.

~The bang-your-head-against-a-wall frustration of "Nothing Indeed".

~The 'Hey! Hey! Hey!'s of the "Ain't Talkin' 'Bout Love" cover, which actually sound more sincerely energized than the ones in the original.

~The surprises. I already mentioned how "Do You Want New Wave" seems unexpected, and so do a bunch of other goodies: the rippling Latin lullaby arpeggios of "Cohesion"; the anxious backing vocals (is that echo?) in "God Bows to Math"; the surprisingly sad, futile guitar riffing (played around Watt reading a landlord's note) in "Take 5, D.", which ends with Boon jamming out noisy blurs on his guitar like he needs to take the frustration out. Even the album's slighter passing weirdnesses, like the absurdist vocal jazz beatnik parody of "You Need the Glory" (is that a zither?), pass quickly enough (and arrive at the right unexpected places) to be worth a listen.

~The killer riff of "#1 Hit Song", and the way Boon phrases his guitar under his vocal parts: he scratches slightly louder at the beginning of each bar, and then plays the subsequent arpeggios softly. It's impressive both (a) because that kind of instant-contact dynamic control is hard to master, and (b) because it creates more of that endearing frustration-through-boredom as Boon mocks what he sees as typical soulless love song lyrics.

~'Maybe partying will help' offering both the excuse and the cure to existential loneliness or so the last half makes you believe, anyway. (That shit is a party.)

~"The Big Foist" the lazy skateboard ride to end all lazy skateboard rides.

~"Corona" the bumper car ride to end all bumper car rides.

~The way you keep expecting "No Exchange", with its fairly solid and final sort of cadence, to build up to a big roaring climax it just keeps building one-chord loudness...and then ends. They'll help you stand, but they won't walk for you.

~The way the slack series of guitar licks in "June 16th" somehow manage to sound exactly like the song title. Don't ask me how.

~The fractured, angular funk of "Please Don't Be Gentle With Me".

~How they turn Steely Dan's "Dr. Wu" into a song that actually sounds like it demands response. ('Have you done all that you can do?' seems like a very different question in this context.)

~The intro of "Viet Nam", which despite the 'hardcore punk' label still slapped on the band from their earlier days sounds painstakingly rehearsed. Listen to the pinpoint timing of Watt's bass slide a split-second before the guitar riff by the time you think back on what you've just heard, they're already into the funky punk. Also, note that this is a band smart enough to know that the Vietnam War actually fucked up their lives too these aren't the kind of guys who give pussy excuses like 'I wasn't even born yet.'

~Watt's bass in the last 25 seconds of "History Lesson - Part II" he seems to be inverting Boon's guitar figures before they both come to that glorious high C chord (which you expect to be lower).

~The way "Storm In My House" starts out as a repeat of "No Exchange", then quickly gets joined by a completely different chord progression that eases the adrenaline into you rather than throwing it.

~The way all three members join in to sing the chorus of "This Ain't No Picnic", making it seem simultaneously (a) like they've all got these kind of problems separately, and (b) like they're all in this together.

~The snarls of barre chords cutting into the vaguely Mid-Eastern tinged soloing in "Little Man With a Gun In His Hand".

~The way Watt keeps stuttering down that triad in "It's Expected I'm Gone". Hurley works a quick accelerating hi-hat rush 43 seconds in, and the song - again, unexpectedly - goes into a lyrical, downright mellow jazz solo from Boon. Then they all stop on the dot, pause for a bar, and then do the first part again. 'I make certain that my head is connected to my body.' Damn right.

~"Jesus and Tequila", which is probably my favorite song on the album. Between the kick-ass riff, the funny and self-deprecating lyrics, the way Hurley's drums once again seem to be building toward something, that bridge of soft guitar arpeggios that are probably the most plainly beautiful things on the album (that aren't from "History Lesson"), and that doubly kick-ass riff after said bridge, this song gets more done in two minutes and fifty-three seconds than most artists do in their entire careers.

~"Political Song for Michael Jackson to Sing" opening side two like you've just been thrown onto a moving train. Hurley's drums recall the communal thump build-ups of The Clash's Topper Headon. And, of course:

'If we heard mortar shells, we'd cuss more in our songs! And cut down on guitar solos!'

*pause*

*guitar solo*

---------

I don't think we should make Double Nickels sound like some sort of immersive conceptual experience it isn't. This is not an album like Zen Arcade where it could be argued that 'the whole is greater than the sum of its parts.' No, this album is the parts a practiced style executed to its full, inevitably grand (if exaggerated) potential. Sure, the musical aesthetic is sparse(ly upfront) enough to make you constantly aware of the fact that you're listening to music coming out of speakers, and the politics generally err on the side of ambiguity. (For every 'fuck you!' or 'fuck them!', there's a 'fuck me!' or 'fuck us!' hot on its heels.) But when you're in the mood for this album, nothing else will do, and out of 45 tracks only a handful - the formless quiet-jumping-to-louder "One Reporter's Opinion"; the stop-start nothin' of "Retreat"; the "Old Notebook" repeat "The Roar of the Masses Could Be Farts", the throwaway guitar wheedling of "Mr. Robot's Holy Orders" (at just over three minutes, the longest song here), the good-lyrics-with-blustering-music of "Spillage", the great-drumming-with-no-song-around-it of "Martin's Story" - don't at least give me a buzz. Musically, the band are basically muscling-up the cynical jitterings of Gang of Four and grounding the emerging funk-rock movement with a tight sense of musicianship all three players knock it out of the park even more gloriously than usual. Essentially, this album is a collection of jazz poetry, except this coffeehouse is poseur-free, and despite the broad targets the politics don't seem like figurative posturing.

Actually, this stuff doesn't even feel like a coffeehouse it's more like the back of a car, like the one on the cover. (A cover which looks kinda like a painting, in a bizarre, antiseptic way.) That's what Double Nickels sounds like, really: like you're cruising around with a bunch of smart friends on your day off from a job you hate. It's music made by people who feel like they're shit outta luck in terms of culture and discourse, but who also find solace in being able to hurl their ideas at like-minded people people who might understand and might even hurl ideas back. Needless to say, this is a benchmark in independent rock, given to us by a band who always make it clear that they want you - and us - to succeed. Double Nickels on the Dime is music for making decisions, Mr. Narrator.

A.

"Double Nickels On The Dime", ovvero la storia del rock americano in miniatura. Quarantatr perle che raramente arrivano a durare due minuti e mai raggiungono i tre, nelle quali l'hardcore che aveva caratterizzato i primi dischi dei Minutemen sembra aver lasciato il posto a uno sgangherato funky che fa da collante a questa lunga serie di brani cos eterogenei nel riprendere gli stili pi diversi che hanno contraddistinto il rock d'oltreoceano. Blues, country, folk, psichedelia, hardcore e chi pi ne ha pi ne metta: a una attenta analisi si riesce a trovare un po' di tutto in questo folle calderone.
Il luogo del delitto la Los Angeles del 1984 ( rectius , il porto di San Pedro, come gli stessi autori tengono a precisare sul retro della copertina del disco) e i responsabili del fatto sono i componenti di quello che forse il trio pi esplosivo della scena rock statunitense: D. Boon (pace all'anima sua), voce e chitarra, Mike Watt, basso e George Hurley, batteria.
D. Boon fa ruggire il motore della sua auto e si parte: il basso subito aggressivo di Mike Watt ci introduce in "Anxiuos mo-fo", prima travolgente miniatura del disco, alla quale seguono, senza respiro, "Theatre Is The Life Of You" e l'incendiaria (anche per il testo) "Viet Nam". Funky, hard-rock e punk si sposano a meraviglia in questi primi tre brani, e allora via con "Cohesion", pezzo per sola chitarra acustica dal sapore Spanish che sembra quasi ricordare un John Fahey un po' pi nervoso del solito.
Dopo questo breve (!) momento di relax si riparte a raffica, si picchia duro a suon di funk e il lavoro della base ritmica davvero strepitoso: calore, tecnica e gusto e per arrangiamenti mai barocchi si fondono a meraviglia, creando una tale completezza da permettere alla chitarra di inserirsi con assoluta leggerezza e svincolata dal peso di dover riempire un vuoto, come spesso accade nelle band formate da tre soli elementi. La voce di D. Boon, per contro, resta forse la cosa pi punk dei Minutemen, l'anello di congiunzione con le origini del gruppo, rimanendo insieme al funky l'unico comun denominatore di tutto il lavoro nel suo insieme.
Con "Do You Want New Wave Or Do You Want The Truth?" e la successiva "Don't Look Now" l'atmosfera si fa pi rilassata, nel primo brano - parlato - a tratti quasi malinconica, per poi riprendere la sua vis con "Shit From An Old Notebook" che parte con un micro-assolo di chitarra di hendrixiana memoria.
Di qui in avanti le rivisitazioni e le citazioni degli stili musicali pi disparati si fanno sempre pi frequenti ed esplicite, dalle venature quasi jazzy dell'intermezzo di "Toadies" o dell'intera "Martin's Story", al country-punk di "Corona", al folk un po' avanguardistico di "Take 5, D." (cantata da Mike Watt), al divertissement di "My Heart And The Real World" che, a tratti, sembra anticipare di un buon decennio l'hardcore melodico di NOFX & c., all'avanguardia e alla psichedelia di "You Need The Glory", solo per mettere in luce le pi evidenti.
Poi, come non citare le "ballate" del disco, forse gli episodi pi apertamente pop: "History Lesson, Pt. 2" leggera e nostalgica al contempo e "There Ain't Shit On T.V. Tonight", dal sapore quasi bossanova (la pi grigia che si sia mai sentita) nei movimenti ritmici della chitarra.
L'elenco dei brani e delle loro contaminazioni potrebbe continuare a lungo (molto a lungo, considerato che la tracklist arriva al numero 43): come non nominare neanche pezzi come "West Germany", "Jesus And Tequila", "Dr. Wu" et cetera ci si potrebbe chiedere, ma, in realt, la cosa stupefacente di questo album che, nonostante la brevit degli episodi, in molti di essi possibile, a ogni nuovo ascolto, scoprire dei veri e propri microcosmi musicali che vanno arricchendosi di volta in volta e spesso risultano talmente ben amalgamati e concentrati da risultare difficilmente scomponibili in elementi riconducibili a un singolo genere musicale e ci tramite delle composizioni che sono tutto fuorch compilative o fini a se stesse, ma riescono addirittura a suonare innovative, in un'opera che, fatti i doverosi distinguo, pu essere accostata a livello concettuale a quella realizzata qualche anno prima nel vecchio continente dai Clash con "London Calling".
Ovvero, riassumere il passato per reinventare il presente (e gettare basi per il futuro), e non un caso che un simile atteggiamento sia scaturito da due gruppi nati dall'influenza della rivoluzione (o controrivoluzione, a seconda dei punti di vista) generata dal punk; ognuno ne tragga le conclusioni che pi gli sono congeniali.
"Double Nickels On The Dime" potrebbe essere visto come una fotografia con l'America musicale (non mainstream, ovviamente) del 1984 in primissimo piano e quella degli anni precedenti a fare da sfondo, ora pi visibile, ora pi sfocata e a tratti difficilmente distinguibile dal soggetto principale.
Oppure come un monumento. Un monumento in miniatura (si perdoni il bisticcio) del rock underground statunitense.
In ogni caso, per chi scrive, uno dei migliori dischi degli anni 80 (nonostante suoni cos poco "anni 80"), ma, bando alle ciance, nel frattempo siamo giunti al fatidico numero 43, "Three Car Jam": questa volta sono i motori delle tre auto dei Nostri a rombare, ad annunciarci la partenza della band, verso dove non si sa. La "history lesson" finita.

Longer albums can be sprawling, overflowing with ideas, imaginative, inventive and unique - or they can be tedious and overwrought, Double Nickels on the Dime is definitely the former. It's about as good as Punk gets. Even though just like my other favourite Punk album (London Calling) it isn't a straightforward Punk album, it has a lot of of different influences. Namely Jazz, Funk, Country with an experimental edge. Even though it's one of my favourite albums of all time, I'll admit that it isn't the easiest to digest and get into. I remember it taking a while for me to be able to fully appreciate it, I could never listen to it all the way through without turning it off, despite it having good moments I just got bored which absolutely astounds me. How could this album bore anyone? Honestly, it's one of the most entertaining and creative albums ever. But it just felt like I was never going to thoroughly get it and love it.

Its conception is pretty infamous, after they band heard Husker Du's Zen Arcade they decided to go back into the studio and write a whole load of new material and change its aesthetic completely, as it originally wasn't going to be a double album. Knowing that only makes me appreciate it more, it would've been so easy to make an absolute mess of it. Writing a great album and then doubling it in length, but still having the songwriting ability and talent to pull it off perfectly... That's a wonderful thing.

I really can't think of a weak song on the album, though there's no real stand out songs except for 'Corona', which I knew instantly because of MTV's use of it. Even though overexposure can be a bad thing, I had no idea where that song was from, yet I knew it like the back of my hand and it made me connect to the album a whole lot more, just because it had a snippet of something I knew. The guitar riffs are incredible, and there's a great diversity to the playing. The lyrics are brilliant as well, social commentary containing themes about the Vietnam war, racism in america and the working classes but they also have a very poetic turn of phrase.

It's an incredible influential, especially on alternative rock. It's an album I'm so thankful I found. It's so loose, free, fun. It's what Donuts is to Hip Hop, what Bee Thousand is to Indie and what Pink Flag is to Punk. Albums overflowing with creativity and ideas, even though it's much longer than those mentioned I love every single second of it.
