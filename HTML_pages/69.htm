The responses for this album were so poor that I didn't even bother to listen to it for a while, I put it on a low priority level always trying the other new releases first until a few days ago. I should have known not to trust the others on this one, since I loved both The Information and Guero and both of them received rather mixed reviews. All I have to say is damn this shit is dope, Beck hasn't lost anything over the years and he's still putting out good music. Instead of trying to figure out why others are dissapointed with this, I'll just keep enjoying this really good record. Dangermouse is on board for the production this time around and he laces Beck with some rather mellow obscure tunes but that have a quite modern style. There is no comparision to make with Gnarls Barkley here, Dangermouse just adapted to a sound that would suit Beck well and he suceeded quite well and not making something too poppy or too hip-hopish. It's rather short at 33 minutes, but every song is good and stays in the same sound making this one a consistant affair. If we start to compare to his other individual albums, perhaps it's not the strongest but that hardly matters to me since I pretty much enjoy Beck's entire catalogue. This is a good lesson to me once again, to really judge the albums by myself rather than relying on the general opinion. So why are you reading this anyway?

The more I listen to Beck's Modern Guilt the more I'm convinced its contents are something he knocked together when in the shower or waiting for the kettle to boil. The songs don't sound rushed or unfinished just jerry-built, and, although the finest materials were available to pull the project together, Beck chose to use whatever bits and pieces he could find lying around. It's akin to having the finest wood and marble at your disposal when designing a kitchen but choosing to construct it from plywood instead. There are instances when I thought I was listening to samplers for some future project rather than the finished article.

The sparse framework of the songs is accentuated by processed electronic beats which – sticking with the kitchen analogy – occasionally sound so tinny and atonal it's tempting to believe Beck is beating out the rhythm using spatulas and whisks. The overall atmosphere is one of nervous tension with the gaps between the pulsating beats hanging as ominously as the notes themselves. Bleak echo, sinister scratches and dark intense beats carrying an underlying sense of menace are all put to good use and Beck's decision to invite Danger Mouse to step up behind the production desk proves to be an inspired one.

But, whereas the atmosphere created is one of noxious anxiety and effectively compliments the barren music, Modern Guilt remains an album which repels rather than embraces. "Gamma Ray", "Replica" and "Profanity Prayers" are the pick of an uneasy bunch, while "Chemtrails" is just wishy-washy ambience.

As a stopgap to the next thing Modern Guilt just about suffices but, if this album was supposed to be the next thing, Beck's judgement should be called into question.

I now own a handful of Beck studio albums yet, despite that, I still can't say I wholeheartedly like the bloke. As much as for the music, part of the rationale for purchasing his work is to check out which musical road he'll be heading down next – and I'm no longer convinced that's a good enough reason for me to continue.

So back in July or thereabouts, I heard Beck had a new record out, and honestly, I didn't rush out to listen to it. Because, really, the guy _always_ has a new record out, and sometimes they're good, and sometimes they're just okay, but I'm not the kind of guy who can spend his entire life listening to Beck albums, you know? Now Portishead, THERE was an album to get excited about! This one, well, it was probably fine, even if it _did_ have Cat Power on it, but the best you can really ask for someone in this phase of his career, with so many great records behind him, is that the record be solid. So a couple months later when I finally got around to checking it out (slow week or something), I was pleasantly surprised, mostly because it was short- like I said, I avoided listening to it primarily because of lack of time, so that he managed to say what he had to say in 33 minute and 40 seconds, without filler, was a plus. People are always, you know, telling me I should watch "The Wire" because it's a great TV show, and I'm sure it is, but a sixty hour commitment up front is a lot to make, especially when I could use that time and listen to "Pink Flag" 100+ times.

So because it was good, and because it was short, I kept coming back to it, and sometimes I listened to his other stuff too, and it just came to me this week that, honest to God, this is his best record. Really, who the hell makes their best record when they're sunken deep into middle age and the expectation of endless rehashes? They're great songs. I could listen to them over and over again, and in fact I do. This is why first impressions are often incorrect.

Edit 2010/8/26: I keep hearing this record. I was on hold with the bank today and they were playing "Soul of a Man". A couple weeks ago I was in Applebee's (don't ask me why I was in Applebee's, I don't know) and they put on "Orphans". Maybe this record is actually popular. I don't know, I have no frame of reference. Maybe somebody just decided that the combo of Beck and Danger Mouse is the new Erik Satie for white people. I'm afraid one day I'll be walking through a Super Target and "Replica" will come on, and I'll be the only one to notice. It happens to me all the time now. I was in Meijer the other day and they were playing "Telstar", and I was the only one who could hear it. I'd think I was going crazy if it wasn't the only thing that was keeping me sane.

So does everybody on this site really love Magma? Y'all are starting to worry me.

Modern Guilt was actually the first Beck album I had ever truly listened to. Sure, I had heard his singles many times before, but I had never actually listened to any of his albums before. The album's production was helmed by Danger Mouse, so one can not be sure what to expect. Beck's music had been steeped in hip-hop influences before, so it's not like one had to fear that Danger Mouse would bring too much of a change to Beck's music (Beck has done that on his own many times in the past). While it's hard to really say for sure, I think this remains as my favorite Beck album next to Sea Change. While Beck had released good albums in the past (Odelay and Mutations), even they had some fairly bad tracks on them. Even after many years in the music business, tracks like "Chemtrails" and "Walls" manage to sound unlike anything else he has done before. Whereas tracks such as "Gamma Ray" and "Modern Guilt" are standard Beck and are also highlights here. The only real downside to this album is that the tracks after "Walls" tend to go down in enjoyability with the exception of the great album closer "Volcano". Overall, this is a fantastic album and honestly one of 2008's best.

Beck est un type fini. il a 38 ans, deux enfants, et ça fait 12 ans qu'il tente de faire mieux que son album culte Odelay, en échouant à chaque fois avec plus ou moins de dépit. Il a pourtant essayé de faire autre chose ; il a pompé Gainsbourg et Nick Drake sur Sea Changes, il a essayé d'être cool dans Midnite Vulture. Manqué. Il a pourtant essayé aussi de faire la même chose qu'avant dans ses deux derniers albums. Rien de mémorable. Personne ne lui a dit qu'il ratait son coup à chaque fois, d'ailleurs ces albums n'étaient pas tous mauvais, mais tout le monde se rendait bien compte que chaque nouvel essai était juste un de plus dans une discographie qui s'éloignait inexorablement du génie des premiers albums. Et puis soudain, le temps passe, et nous voilà déjà en 2008 et voilà que sort le huitième album de Beck, Modern Guilt, un album que personne n'attend, qui ne suscite aucune impatience, un album dont on se fout un peu en fait, produit par Danger Mouse qui produit n'importe qui, et avec Chan Marshall, fraîchement convertie en country girl qui fait bailler tout le monde, venue faire des choeurs qu'on entend même pas sur deux morceaux.

Je l'ai écouté attentivement cet album, en partant même d'un bon à-priori car je trouvais la pochette façon 60's assez classe. Mais après une première écoute bien sérieuse, j'étais incapable de dire si c'était moi qui avait écouté l'album ou si ce n'était pas plutôt l'album qui m'avait écouté tant celui-ci était timide, pas bruyant, gentiment mou, avec autant de goût que de l'eau plate. On sortait de ces très courtes 34 minutes en se demandant "oui et alors?", on ne voyait pas vraiment où Beck voulait en venir, on avait là une collection de 10 chansons peu mémorables, avec l'absence de tout ce qui pourrait ressembler à un tube de près ou de loin, on avait une énorme sensation de vide. Les chansons de Beck n'ont jamais tenues sur grand chose, mais on ne s'en rendait jamais vraiment compte. Ici, avec une réverbération peu avantageuse, voir même des samples mal gérés car trop évidents sur Gamma Ray, on entend le vide des chansons et on a l'impression d'une sobriété presque gênante venue d'un type qui faisait des albums foutraques et débordants d'idées. Comme s'il avait décidé de ne plus rien prouver, de ne plus rien essayer, Beck semble effectivement sonner pour l'une des premières fois de sa carrière plus comme d'autres personnes que comme lui même, il n'invente plus rien, il se laisse aller, il fait de la musique néo-shuffle-60's (le morceau titre), il fait des machins pseudo-expérimentaux façon Four Tet/Radiohead qui tombent à l'eau (Replica, pire morceau de l'album), il fait le cowboy stoner un peu caricatural (Soul Of A Man) ou bien il se prend pour Elliott Smith dans le Volcano final qui pâti d'arrangements peu inspirés.

Alors quoi? Assiste-on à une grosse fatigue déprimé de la part de l'ex-petit génie pop? Ou bien n'a on pas tout simplement là un album de transition? Il est intéressant de voir que Modern Guilt est le retour de Beck sur un label indépendant (XL Recordings, en dehors des USA), un fait qu'on aime bien -surtout nous autres qui écrivons des chroniques de disque- symboliser en un changement d'ère, le début d'un nouveau chapitre blah blah blah. Et, oui, vu comme ça, cet album n'est finalement pas le naufrage qu'il semble être au premier coup d'œil, se révélant être, après plusieurs écoutes, un album certes franchement bancal, mais finalement assez prometteur. Ayant besoin de refaire ses preuves après des années de paresse, le nouveau Beck qui revient, peut être plus mature et sérieux, peut être moins branleur, est en tout cas un musicien qui a changé, qui est devenu étrangement sombre, qui a évolué (un petit peu), et si cet album reste franchement inégal, il contient des morceaux qu'on pourrait même qualifier de brillants comme Chemtrails, étrange morceau lent au refrain hanté très prenant et au final complètement génial. D'une manière tout à fait inattendue, il parvient même à convaincre quand il offre des versions de quarantenaire (comprendre molles) de ses anciens morceaux (Orphans, ouverture manquant de pêche mais qui reste en tête malgré tout), restant le type cool qu'il a toujours été au fond, pour peu qu'on ne veuille pas à tout prix y chercher un nouveau Devil's Haircut. Derrière lui, la production de Danger Mouse est sobre, et se fond bien dans un coté néo-retro-moderne de type qui a la gueule de bois pas désagréable, donnant aux morceaux une couleur agréable et un coté familier.

On se retrouve avec un album bizarre au final, ne souffrant pas du coté trop long qui achevait ses précédents albums, mais n'ayant pas vraiment le temps de décoller. A mi-chemin entre une platitude du songwriting qui inquiète un peu, et une habilité à toujours réussir à faire d'excellents morceaux malgré tout, ce nouvel album est donc à voir alternativement comme un coup de pelle en plus du bonhomme pour s'enterrer et une large brasse pour essayer de remonter à la surface. Un album qui ne décide donc en rien du sort de Beck, mais qui est toujours meilleur que l'affligeant The Information que nous avions dû subir il y a deux ans. Envahi subitement d'une vague de bonté et de positivisme naïf à l'écoute de cette gentille tentative, comme un prof qui voit un élève en difficulté faire des efforts, le message qu'on aurait envie de transmettre au héros déchu des 90's est scolaire et simple : "Des résultats encore trop justes mais encourageants. Continuez comme ça".

An oft-forgotten album, and a bit of a weird one in his discograpy. Modern Guilt sees Beck mainly leaning into relatively generic, Radiohead-style alt-rock of the era. Unlike pretty much everything he's done up to this point, a lot of these songs are typical enough to the point they could've been written or performed by other artists without anyone noticing.

That being said, I still think it's done really well. Despite what I said, a lot of the songs still are interesting, since I'm only talking in relative terms here and it'd take a bit longer for Beck to get genuinely boring.

While not all that memorable in his discography and sounds a little too much like Radiohead (Profanity Prayers would not exist without Bodysnatchers), Modern Guilt is nice and definitely has its strengths. 7/10
