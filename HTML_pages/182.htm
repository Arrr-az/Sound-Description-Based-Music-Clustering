'One Hundred Years' captures perfectly a sense of morbid unease and then every song after it pretty much sustains that feeling right up until the bitter end as Smith's tortured wail of "...I must fight this sickness!" fades away amongst the distorted washes of synth and disturbingly indecipherable excerpts of dialogue. You honestly come away from it thinking Joy Division is quite cheerful by comparison because at least with JD there was a sense of carthasis whereas this just leaves you languishing in utter despair long after the record has finished.
Part of me wants to dismiss it's wanton gloominess as alot of self consciously stylised gothic nonsense but at the end of the day I can't help liking it.
Musically it's dark swathes of FX laden guitar give it a thoroughly evil sound that brings the listener to the very brink of the abyss with an intensity that is something akin to a really great Horror Movie soundtrack. So as gothic nonsense goes this is superior in every way and certainly deserves to be heard by more than just your usual self harming, middle class, pale and sensitive outsiders.

TheCunningStunt Feb 23 2024
+3 Vote downBookmarkVote up
The Cure really are a great band, though there's another band floating around that are rumored to be better, they're called The Prevention. But there's neither here nor there. The Cure are a band that definitely won't cure you of your depression, they'd only make it worse because they're a band that do sad and depressed better than pretty much anyone.

It's undiluted sadness. With other great bands who are notorious for being sad and depressing, they at least had something else. With Nirvana, they had sarcasm. With The Smiths, they had a sardonic sense of humour, irony and a juxtaposition between the misery and the jangly guitars. Joy Division sometimes were overly energetic. With The Cure, they had their pop sensibilities and were catchy as fuck - but not here, early in their career it was just unrelenting, brooding, melancholic, ethereal sadness. That said, they obviously aren't the only band to have that overriding sense of sadness and hopelessness, but I do think there's something incredibly powerful when you come across a band's music that's completely void of hope and completely bleak.

Disintergration for me, is their best album, because they manage to capture what The Cure are about perfectly. It captures the sadness, it captures the pretty melodies, it captures the pop sensibilities, it's both bleak and sad yet atmospheric and beautiful but when it comes to the darker side of The Cure, there's nothing better than Pornography.

Pornography is one of their all time best albums. It's the third album in a dark, gothic trilogy and you can see why it's the last one because I don't think they could have gotten darker, gloomier or more depressed than this. It truly is one of the bleakest, depressive and nihilistic albums I've ever listened to with a harrowing subject matter and a real intensity to it, as well as it being icy and impenetrable. Even though it's really bleak, I always felt it had an underlying catharsis to it. With other releases, they did have something to counterbalance the misery, a pop sensibility, but with Pornography everything about it is just incredibly bleak. This is definitely one of the best in their discography.

Ruiner123 Sep 27 2012
+5 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Popular consensus would lead you to believe that this is the definitive Cure album. Judging from the strength and potency of the entire record, it would be rather hard to argue against this. Pornography sees the band reach a level of starkness and bleakness not present in the preceding two records. Right off the bat, the album professes absolute musical maturity = giving songs added depth in terms of arrangement and sonic facets. It also boasts an intricacy in studio techniques such as reverb, creating atmosphere and instrument technique that culminate in defining the album's pure dark sound.

"One Hundred Years," ominous in its notes and words, starts off the album in dramatic fashion: "It doesn't matter if we all die." It's as if Robert Smith encountered something within the timeframe of the completion of Faith and the beginning of the recording sessions for this one, as if he had accepted some bleak fate of which became the ultimate source for the gloominess found in the record. Wailing guitars, dreary bass tracks and violent drum sounds make this song one of the scariest I have ever heard, right at home on the soundtrack to a horror film. "A Short Term Effect" crashes beautifully into our aural proximity at track 2. The entire structure of the song is just too magnificent to try to put into words. "The Hanging Garden" is quite possibly one of the best songs in the entire Cure discography as it doesn't sound as morose as their most well-known tracks; within its undying energy (accentuated by a magnificent drum track) the song contrasts heavily with the opener, retaining the dreaded drama we've come to crave from the Cure while also retaining the band's exclusive sonic endeavors. "Siamese Twins" brings Side A to a close and in climactic fashion. Here we see Smith experiment with his guitar, adding gorgeous layers of guitar dubs, with the lead hook being so ungodly irresistible and memorable, the song alone makes the album worth the listen. Smith's bawling in his vocal delivery adds yet more depth to the song. Although relatively harmonious throughout, the song's coda welcomes a tinge of dissonance, which adds to the overall demeanor the album tries to convey. Yes, you are dying, but it is a lovely death, nevertheless.

Side B starts with the epic "The Figurehead," once again led by an amazing drum track, which takes center stage for a while until Smith's ghastly vocals come into prominence. The songs offers one of the best guitar grooves in Post-Punk history, making this arguably the best track on the album, and clearly one of the best in the band's catalogue. It also offers an interesting dynamic as the groove shifts from beautiful to horrific seamlessly, only adding to the established dramatic essence of the album. With its bleak lyrical content, the song truly solidifies the sound The Cure would become known for. "A Strange Day" sees the band bring the drama found in the preceding track significantly lower; the song succeeds in yet another impressive guitar track, with a great groove fed to us sporadically, as such, it makes the song succeed as we crave it more and more.

"Cold" begins with an ominous synth bar which sounds like the lowest notes of a century old organ being played. It actually manages to convey the song's name, leaving a feeling of frigidity within the listener. It's this same gothic sonic arrangement that would go on to influence Danny Elfman's dark compositions. The band once again ends the record with the eponymous track. "Pornography" begins with an eerie sampling of radio or television sounds, and acts as a testament against media; the fact that the band chose to name their album "Pornography" can attest to this notion. It is a very successful closer as it basks in a gorgeous ghostly atmosphere. The sonic explosion coupled with the fantastic wailing by Smith serves as the perfect bookend for an imperfect record.

I will readily admit that Pornography is not the perfect Cure album, relying way too much on its imperfections to drive the idea of nothingness home. However, it's this very same factor that makes it a fan favorite and is considerably name dropped in Post-Punk and Cure talks. And rightfully so as The Cure would severely depart from this album in their attempt to attract a much larger audience, that being the American market. Pornography is a slice of the atmospheric death prevalent in England, of the nothingness that overtakes the social slaves of the nation, giving a name to their pain, falling victim to the carnal desires within one, being set aside with the rest of the day's trash.

This is hardcore Cure: the most bleak, murky, inaccessible and least poppy album The Cure ever made. With Robert Smith wailing in the midst of a swirling tornado of wailing guitar, Lol Tolhurst's adept drumming at points seems like the only thing anchoring the album to any sort of solid ground. Yes, it does mark the point where The Cure started to turn into the almost cartoonish big-haired chaps we know and love today, but there's nothing cartoonish about the bitterness and anger in these lyrics. Whilst the punks were protesting against the state and injustice, Robert Smith here was setting forth a protest against life and the inevitability of death itself.
