His vocal style was raspy since Small Change but absolutely nothing could prepare the world for Tom Waits next big thing. Tom dropped the nice pseudo Sinatra style alltogether and embraced the music of Captain Beefheart.

The stories are also less coffee house poet, he's like the weird uncle who tells you outrageous stories about sailors, criminals, hookers, and drunkards that cannot possibly be true... or are they?

The record begins with Underground, a percussive piece with a trombone battling a contrapuntal guitar with Waits shouting his lungs off, sounding like a weird Disney villain song. The instrumentation is decidedly old-times on the next few songs, like Dave the Butcher's organ. The instrumental sounds a lot like something straight outta Trout Mask Replica.

Johnsburg, Illinois then is a beautiful ballad, only to be followed by 16 Shells From a Thirty-Ought-Six. Another highlight is the New Orleans jazz of In the Neighborhood.

The record stays all over the place. Frank's Wild Years is a darkly humorous story with jazz backing. Gin Soaked Boy is electric blues. By the end, the instrumenal jazz ballad Rainbird, it's certain that Tom Waits was forever changed and to this day he thrives on the persona invented on this record.

8/10

To transform the common phrase that many have said to describe this part in his career, Tom was in an old '55 at the corner of Heartattack and Vine, and managed to take a turn on a completely different street. Unlike with other artists with evolving catalogs (Radiohead, The Beatles, you know), the crank of the wheel between Swordfishtrombones and its predecessor was sharp, jarring, completely unanticipated, and awesome -- every nook of the word's literal meaning.

In the stead of the smoky, seedy characters Waits narrates from on his earlier works -- the ones you imagine sucking at a cigarette under a fedora outside a greasy spoon -- you're creepily introduced to freaks with weird little lives that you have no idea what to feel about. The stomps, the discordant organs, the deep brass, it all makes this sound that at the time must have driven people insane, but we'd come to recognize it as quirky, earthy, indescribable genre of Waits.

Thought to be Part One of an unofficial trilogy -- followed by Rain Dogs and Franks Wild Years -- this album has a theme of debauched towns, missing home, and falling into obscurity and myth, in one way or another. The laments of "Shore Leave" and "Johnsburg, Illinois" show a homesick sailor with a picture of his girl in his wallet, facing the strange facets of another world and wishing he was home at her side. The barking, antagonistic "16 Shells From a Thirty-Ought-Six" takes a portrait of a maniacal man fixated on a crow he couldn't kill, so he captures it and tortures it for amusement. "Town With No Cheer" and "In the Neighborhood" are brochures for sad, empty little towns, and the rest seem to be about a wild character who up and vanished, leaving behind rumors and myths about who he was, and where he is now.

Under the stories are innovative arrangements of clunky, obsolete instruments, accompanied by brass and organs. "16 Shells" smashes around with anvils and boards for percussion, and "Town With No Cheer" has a bagpipe in it. All of it is strangely simple, but lingering with the theory exuded in his earlier releases.

The only songs that reminisce about the old ways are "Johnsburg, Illinois," which is a sweet little piano ballad, accompanied by a stand-up bass, and "Franks Wild Years," a jazzy little organ-bass ditty, over which he tells the hilarious spoken story of Frank, who has a perfect life, and destroys it on a whim.

I started with Swordfishtrombones, so it hold special memories for me. Despite that, I think it's some of his best work, as his experimentation with songwriting had only begun. You can hear his excitement as he grins and says to himself, "No one else is doing this right now," and "Man, people are gonna hate this." It's delightfully unsettling, moody, filled with great poetry and Waitsisms, and sung from all of his voices: the Rod-Stewart, the growl, and the smoky soothsayer.

That own little world he lives in? This is him unpacking.

It's funny, but when I first heard this I assumed that Waits' music had always sounded like it does here. What a weird dude, I thought. I didn't realize he had already released several albums of drunken piano ballads. So, yeah, this was quite a shift in sound for him, and it's a wonderfully odd sound, demented organs, guitars, brass, off rhythms, and that voice sounds perfect over it. Lyrics are dark, knowing, funny, and touching at times. There are a few people that don't like him because they think he's faking this whole bizarre beatnik schtick, but he sure sounds immersed in it here, and it all comes off like a great novel, moving and surprising at every turn.

Don't come to this expecting a "Time", "Downtown Train", or "Hang Down Your Head", because there's nothing as enduring or as touching here, or anything as flat-out cool and dark as "Clap Hands" (though in "Solider's Things" and "Swordfishtrombone" respectively, there is one song in either category that starts to approach that kind of quality). My mother has never been very receptive to some of my musical tastes, nor those of my brother or my dad. In fact, I often felt sorry for her having to share a house with three music nuts whose enthusiasm for the topic she described as not dissimilar to trainspotters' enthusiasm for steam driven locomotives of yesteryear. But I thought that it was a little harsh when, having been asked by my young cousin (who is just forming her own musical opinions), what the music my Dad was playing in the car one day was, she replied, "Oh, it's just Uncle Paul's silly music." In doing so she has probably hindered the poor girl's musical development by turning her away from Tom Wait's Swordfishtrombones. And that is a great shame because she will perhaps never know the rainy day heart ache of "Town With No Cheer".

Another person who suffers my musical obsession is my girlfriend. She dislikes Tom Waits too, but for the peculiar reason that his voice makes her feel sick, because she imagines the friction in his throat and it makes her heave. This is also a shame, if only because she will never know the raw funk of "16 Shells From A Thirty Ought Six".

I, however, feel very safe and at home, nestled in the familiar bosom of Waits' sound that seems to ignore any moves in popular music, borrows from few, and influences so many. It is a bizaare carnival where all sorts of characters from every downtrodden walk of life mingle and play on the hoop-la games, raincoats pulled over their heads to hide from the drizzle and the eyes of the law or their wives.

I am safe here. Creatures of the night appear ten-a-penny when you have Tom's fascinating world playing out in your ear cavity, and many a late night bus ride has been made all the more bleak by the gravelly voiced one. And I like bleak.

Swordfishtrombones is perhaps the best of a great bunch, shifting naturally from queasy silent movie drunk music to heartfelt ballad to caveman funk and back again without so much as an apology. And why would he need to apologise when he is perhaps the only artist untouched by vanity, pride or ambitions of grandeur operating today. It is his vision and his alone that he has chased down dark alleys on bleak nights for many, many years, and those that have followed are very rarely brave enough to go all the way with him, but they all wish they could.

Except my mum.

This is the album that gave us the new Tom Waits. The gravelly voiced barfly sitting at the piano has completely morphed into this character who prefers to dress like a senior citizen and hang out in junkyards. From here on in, it'll be pipes and overturned barrels as percussion, out of tune pawn shop guitars for rythm and a bottle of plastic bag of rusty nails as a vocal coach.

The songs on Swordfishtrombones are classic Waits. Every tune is worthy of mention. Lively and funny on "16 Shells From A Thirty-Ought-Six" and "Frank's Wild Years"; reflective and poignant in "A Town With No Cheer"; and achingly heartfelt with the greatest 90-second love song ever on "Johnsburg, Illinois." Plus, there is a personal favourite, the proudly blue-collar anthem "In The Neighborhood." Keeping with the melancholy, is there a more heart-breaking song about war than "A Soldier's Things?" (Without even mentioning the battlefield.) Waits can make you feel the impossibility of trying to move on when you're loved one isn't coming home, an example of reserve and subtlety for you future writers.

The album closes with "Rainbirds," a piano and bass instrumental that feels like that old Waits persona playing in that bar, after it's closed, nobody there, save for somebody putting up the bar stools and getting ready to go home. It's the end of the night and Waits has expressed and made you feel every honest emotion into your day.

It is nothing short of beautiful.
