Gosto um bom tanto da letra de "I'll Be Your Mirror", mas detesto os vocais da Nico. Frequentemente (na verdade, sempre) a maneira como uma msica cantada faz a letra brilhar muito muito mais ou muito muito menos, e esse o caso de muito muito menos. Fico imaginando alguns outros vocalistas que gosto cantando essa msica e fico com a certeza que, caso fossem eles aqui, gostaria muuuuito mais dela.

A singer who'd previously been jobbing as a songwriter for a record label so renowned for its small-budget copycat recordings that Les McQueen's Creme Brulee were signed to them in The League of Gentlemen. A drummer that makes Meg White looks like John Bonham. An actress and model with a voice so offputting that over 40 years later, even with our knowledge of Perry Farrell and Fred Durst and Neil Young and Alec Ounsworth and whoever the hell else, some still find her voice offputting. A viola player who had studied with such populist, rockist figures as LaMonte Young and John Cage. A debut recording constructed almost entirely of feedback, with a locked groove, that people now consider a precedent to industrial music. A manager who was mostly famous for painting a can of soup. How on earth did they become the most influential rock band of all time?

Lord knows, but that's the facts. Every time a band wants to make some noise, The Velvets are there. Every time somebody specifically sets out to have simple drumming, The Velvets are there. Every time a pervert grabs a microphone, The Velvets are there. Every time somebody confronts their audience with any remotely unusual ideas about gender roles, The Velvets are there. Every time somebody sings an unambiguous song about drugs, The Velvets are there. Every time a rock band imagines themselves as an idiosyncratic cult act rather than as cookie-cutter megastars, The Velvets are there. They're an essential part of the DNA of punk, of glam, of No-Wave, of new wave, of alternative rock, of indie rock, of noise rock, of shoegaze, whatever you like. Clear lines can be drawn back to this album from modern points of interest as disparate as Kayo Dot, Radiohead, Franz Ferdinand, Have A Nice Life, and Neurosis. Most of music's most notable innovators since - David Bowie, Public Enemy, Patti Smith, Slint, The Stooges, Suicide, Talking Heads, King Crimson, Pixies, R.E.M., Sonic Youth, Joy Division, Can, John Zorn, Brian Eno, even bloody Burzum - owe them something. It really is astonishing what this album started.

The simplest explanation is that the Velvets sang about dirty drugs and dirtier sex, and that's ultimately what everybody wants from rock music. The truest, however, is that it's just an incredible album. To this day it holds the power to shock and enthrall. Has anybody ever truly written a song like "Venus in Furs" since? And have they put it on an album that also has a love song as sweet, sincere, and unpretentious as "I'll Be Your Mirror"? How about following up a ballad as woozy and lazy as "Sunday Morning" with a rock'n'roll anthem about hooking up with your dealer? Then there's the necessary ballast provided by the solid, driving rock'n'roll of "Run Run Run" and "There She Goes Again"; songs that are perennially under-rated in the way that they make this an album, and render its visions complete - without these obviously 'rock' structures, a generation of listeners might not have considered that the subject matter of songs like "Heroin" could be placed into them, and without this kind of subtle innovation to go alongside the brutal leaps forward of "The Black Angel's Death Song" and "European Son", the album might simply have disappeared. Yet it didn't, and as we approach 2010, this album still slays millions and attracts a new fan base each year.

Indefatigably awesome. What a shame that they never even came close to matching it in the years that followed, either as a band or as solo artists.

There is no question that The Velvet Underground & Nico belongs in the canon of most influential rock works ever made. Seemingly everything that was adopted by English punk and stoned American indie kids can be found in embryonic stages here. Preference for artistic vision over musicianship - you got that. Using dissonance within the framework of rock music - you got that. Without Lou Reed's use of lyrics to build vast thematic worlds instead of deliver sentimental triteness, you wouldn't be able to get lost in Mark E. Smith's or Jonathan Richman's lyrical worlds.

But the key word here is influential. If you listen to this record without the context of music history, it's a vastly different ride. Then it's just reduced to a set of deconstructive, oblique, Eastern-tinged rock songs that have potential to do far more. It's reduced to just another jam session. Sometimes that works (See "European Son" - whose deconstruction of blues sounds decades ahead of its time). Sometimes that doesn't. That's why it gets only three stars. The reason we are made to listen to this record is because without it, (echoing what you've been told for eons) those few people who were inspired by it in the '60s and '70s would not start bands. And we wouldn't have The Modern Lovers, Wire, The Fall, and countless others; we wouldn't have entire genres: shoegaze, twee, punk, post-punk.

Of course, if we didn't have the Velvet Underground, certainly some other artist would have decided to use a guitar to make drony noise. Perhaps it would be more interesting to see who that artist may have been.

I decided to go back and check this band out again, being the current cult status that has crept up around them.
I have to say that I see why some people find this music appealing.
I also understand why it was never very popular.
Creativity alone is not enough to make for good music, it takes good musicianship
and good production/arrangements. These guys had the creativity, they just didn't
have the music skills to execute their lofty ideas well . Even considering this music was done at a time when a lot of bands with rudementary skills were making hits it is easy to see why
VU didn't make it in the music industry. It is far more than just the fact they rejected the status quo, most musicians were doing that at the time.

Some examples of where it falls short:

Fem Fatalle - guitars out of tune.
Venus in furs--kind of interesting if it had been recorded on better instruments
by guys more capable of playing them. Hypnotic appeal. Guitar horribly out of tune.

Run Run Run sounds like what I imagine Manfred Mann would have sounded like
if someone had recorded them practicing in a basement while they were still
learning how to play. Terrible guitar playing that is not in time with the music.

The timing on Heroin is bad.

Lou Reed is a terrible singer and guitar player on these songs.
Come to think of it, if Lou Reed had simply been the closet songwriter for this music
and someone with far better musical abilities had sang and played guitar on this music, it may have well ended up being quite listenable.


It is often hard to tell if the dissonance in some of the songs is planned or if it is just the result of poor musical instrument technique, or guitar
intonation, or instruments not in tune.

It is obvious the only real musician in the band was John Cale. His instruments are in tune and
well played.

On the positive side:

The actual music itself is very interesting, a wide variety of approaches.
It has an overall very experimental sound. If Pink Floyd had recorded these songs using the same
producer and engineer in the same studios it was recorded in instead of Syd Barrets drivel they would probably have gotten very famous long before Dark Side of the Moon.
If "There she goes again" had been released as a single perhaps VU would not be almost entirely unheard of, even though the backround vocals are off. I can easily see it as a mid sixties hit.

It is all mixed with nice stereo separation, making it far more appealing than it might have been. .
The miking techniques and the production is really superior considering mixing in stereo was still reasonably new. The percussion is well recorded and the the frequency response of the recordings is very wide with good dynamics.

It is a shame some of the songs have such poor musicianship, the recording techniques are far better than much of the music from this time period. The guitars sound very big and the vocals are very in your face. Whoever mixed this music knew exactly what he was doing.

It was interesting to go back and listen to this again, but this is not something I would listen to again. It might appeal to someone who is very stoned, simply because it has an interesting hypnotic overall feel to it. But if you play a musical instrument and are a freak about being in key and in tune, listening to this could make you stir crazy.

= alert: the first paragraph is just for me, skip it if you're not interested in the history of my personal relation with the album =

I have a difficult relationship with this album. It's one of the first ones I discovered through the net, when I was about 17. At first I quite enjoied it, but I was still very subdued to the musical critics and I didn't really have my own taste. Thanks to Scaruffi's confrontations to the Beatles (which were and are still my favourite band) I've soon begun hating it as a reaction. The Beatles would be pathetic compared to the Velvets? No, they are not, it's the Velvets who are pathetic. I know it's a childish reasoning, but that's what I unconsciously thought for years. Scaruffi didn't understand a bloody thing of rock and music, and The Velvet Underground had to be immolated to exorcise his sins and his arrogance.
The Velvets celebrated heroin, they were decadent, rough, almost punks and in those very years of my life there were just a few things I couldn't stand as much as drugs, decadence, roughness and punk.
At some point of my musical exploration I had to admit the Velvet Underground was truly one of the most important bands for the history or rock, though. It was impossible to deny. Yet it didn't mean I had to like the album, of course. From time to time I listened to it again, just to make sure nothing had changed, but my prejudices were still to strong to let me appreciate it.
In the meantime, my musical exploration (which had started from Radiohead, some trip-hop, some psychedelia and hard rock and tons and tons of classic progressive rock) began taking paths I'd never have predicted some years before. The Cure, Tuxedomoon, Wire, Gaznevada, Minutemen, Sonic Youth, The Jesus & Mary Chain, The Stranglers made me understand there was some punk-influenced music which I could actually like, and some time later Henry Cow, This Heat & co. convinced me that the boundaries between progressive and punk were much thinner than I used to think. In a couple of years I found myself listening to The Birthday Party, Bauhaus, Refused, The Mass, Hot Cross, Lightning Bolt, Neurosis, PainKiller, Hella, Zu, Alter-Natives and loads of stuff deeply rooted in punk, no-wave or hardcore punk.
There was no excuse left to keep on hating The Velvet Underground. I liked a lot of music coming directly from their sound, their ideas and their esthetics. So I tried to listen to them again, from time to time as usual, but with a different approach. I didn't want to force me to like them, I just wanted to remove my biases and understand if I liked them or not.

= the true review starts here =

So here I am after about one year of attemps to listen to them in the most honest and open-minded way I could. I still can't find their debut album (nor their sophomore effort) a masterpiece. It's surely one of those albums everyone should listen to, a milestone of rock music, historically speaking it's incredibly innovative, radical and important. But I don't think it's that achieved as a whole.
The tracks sung by Nico, in particular, don't have anything of the "avantgarde" sound of the best tracks. And please notice: the problem is not I don't like Nico or I can't accept them to be just "pop" songs. The point is that I find them dull, unisteresting and boring. "All Tomorrow's Parties" could have been a great song if it had been sung differently, with less melody but more soul. "Femme Fatale" and "I'll Be Your Mirror" are just mediocre songs, and there's nothing which could have improved them.
There are then a couple of other tracks which don't capture me at all: "Run Run Run" and "There She Goes Again". They're not bad, but I can't find them special, anyway. I've always had problems appreciating avant-gardish rock stuff which doesn't refuse its blues roots, but a track like "Run Run Run" seems to me quite of a tin-pot blues in any case. With the exception of Morrison's free-noise guitar soloing, of course. "I'm Waiting for the Man" is quite good, but it's still too bluesy for me. The focus on repetition and the ossessous rhythm pattern is definitely amazing, but the blues harmonies make me feel like the VUs are trying to fool me. You want to play the intellectual, you want to reshape rock music? Strip away the blues or you're just working on the surface.

I've ruled out six songs out of eleven. There are only five left, and they're some of the most impressive songs I know. Let's analyse them one by one.
Sunday Morning: Just a catchy pop song, with a soft melancholic aura springing from the bright carillon notes of Cale's Celesta. It's not one of those songs aimed to change the world, but it's so delicate and tender!
Venus in Furs: I've always been a huge fan of John Cale's, even when I was blinded by hate for the Velvets. Here we really have John Cale shining through, with a dazing viola ostinato which frightens me every time I listen to it. Maureen Tucker's mournful bass-drum/cymbal work and Sterling Morrison's harsh, impure sound even make Lou Reed's self-indulgent rant pass in the background enough to be easily ignored.
Heroin: The lyrics are good and they fit perfectly with the music, and so does Lou Reed's singing. That's quite unusual on its own, but it's evident that the theme is very heart-felt by him. The climax from the slow, open-chord notes to the frantic, desperate apogee is one of the most enthralling episodes of rock music. John Cale's drone, Maureen Tucker's shamanic beats and Sterling Morrison's piercing grind each combine creating the emotional peak of the album.
The Black Angel's Death Song: Yet another great number by John Cale, whose raped viola dissolves tonality into a modal magma leaving Lou Reed free to declaim his dirges.
European Son: It starts as a blues but rapidly diverges to a free, rough and atonal jam which constitutes the most radical section of the whole album. Sterling Morrison's guitar sound is the most abrasive and metallic clangour music had ever seen until that very moment. The self-intersecting, dead-end guitar lines make melody rise from unstructured chaos, bringing a whole new universe to explore for any future rock musician.

In conclusion: if the album featured only these five tracks and Sister Ray (my favourite VU track) it would be one of my absolute favourites. Unluckily, it doesn't, and both this album and "White Light/White Heat" seem to me some of the greatest lost chances of rock music.

It's hard to review this album. Hell, any 5-star review is hard to write just because of the effort it takes to not devolve into a totally cliched bit of writing while at the same time trying to pinpoint exactly why this album is at the top of your review scale. Well, why not look at some of these cliches and see if they work here. I'll even throw in a few of the negative ones to keep things interesting.

The Velvet Underground and Nico changed my life! -- Umm, not really. Sure the initial listen through was a very memorable experience, but that applies more to Peel Slowly and See as a whole than any individual part. But something about it did make it stand out against the rest of their output. It's arguably their best balance of accessible and alienating, with the sheer beauty of "Sunday Morning" and the percussive gallop of "I'm Waiting for the Man" balancing out the pure dissonance of "The Black Angel's Death Song" and the epic noise-fest of "European Sun". Between those two duos there's a huge selection from either group. For every strung out epic like "Heroin" there's an epic like "All Tomorrow's Parties", for every "Run Run Run" there's an "I'll Be Your Mirror", and the sexual dysfunction of "Venus in Furs" is nicely counterweighted with the ice-cold love song of "Femme Fatale". It's memorable, highly enjoyable and in it's own way beautiful, but not life-changing.

It's not like anything you've ever heard before! -- Maybe in 1967 when it was first released it was, but the sound's been co-opted with increasingly diminishing returns ever since then so it's hardly a revelation, other than the fact that it's actually good. The sound itself is actually pretty standard if you think about it, the only new addition was John Cale's lurching viola squeals and the frank discussions of scoring drugs, doing drugs, S&M and whatever those last two songs are about. It sounds like a criticism, but keep in mind music doesn't need to be original to really hit you.

Lou Reed can't sing! -- And? I'll take his jaded monotone over any classically trained vocalist any day, especially given the overall tone of the lyrics. Imagine, if you will, someone like Greg Lake caterwauling through "Heroin". Sucks, right? Reed's voice may be thin and his range isn't anything to brag about, but it works perfectly here.

Nico ruins the album! -- Now those are fighting words. Sure the fact that she was all but forced into the band by Andy Warhol might leave a sour taste in your throat, but look at her three vocal contributions here: only "I'll Be Your Mirror" could be called anything less than stellar. "All Tomorrow's Parties" is much improved over the assorted takes on the band's demo reel (included on Peel Slowly and See) with her icy tones, and Reed wrote "Femme Fatale" specifically for her, and it highlights her best vocal traits, not that her less great ones are ever shown. I will give you that she is an acquired taste, but she's far from an album-ruining last minute addition.

"Heroin" is far and away the best song the band have ever done! -- I kind of agree, it's the album centerpiece for a good reason (though I'd argue that "All Tomorrow's Parties" is just as good a candidate for that position) but it's not a one horse race for sure. "Sunday Morning" is close behind, probably the most different track from "Heroin" that you can find on the album. Using it as the first track on the album was a great last-minute choice (according to the infamous '66 acetate that was initially going to be a radically different take on "European Son"), and is probably the best Reed vocal in the band's history. Realy the only tracks that have never been my favorite at one point or another are "I'll Be Your Mirror" and "The Black Angel's Death Song", and I wouldn't classify either as a bad song, just a bit below the awesome quality of the other 9 tracks.

There is absolutely nothing that could improve this album! -- Hang on there, skippy. Sorry to burst your bubble but 5 stars != perfection. I love this album to death, hence the lengthy expose here, but I can't say there's nothing that could improve it. "Venus in Furs" works better with Cale on vocals (the only bad change from the material on the Peel Slowly and See demo reel). The mix could be a bit clearer (although there is a certain charm to its proto-lo-fi sound). There could be more Nico (imagine her taking on "Sunday Morning") and even one Cale vocal. There's always these little nitpicks that you wouldn't even bother noting if you didn't spend a good 3 months with tis consistently in your stereo.

Shut Up BRG! -- OK, can't argue with that one...
