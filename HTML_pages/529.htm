The main problem with Kraftwerk's classics is that they appeared, well, a really long time ago. As a result, newer listeners may be inclined to interpret some of their songs as outdated, cheesy, simplistic, etc. But consider what it was like for those of us who first heard them decades ago. And then consider the countless artists who were directly influenced by Kraftwerk.

Electronics-dominated music was initially seen as something intentionally avant-garde, alienating, cold, distant, metallic, fascist, too anti-fascist, potentially faggy, potentially guitar-killing, etc.

But some artists recognized that synths and sequencers and re-rigged Rolands were merely the latest tools to help convey the universal message of all music - hear and feel me! Kraftwerk started the Seventies exploring the same lengthy, droning musical textures as any other self-respecting, mind-blowing Teutonic college kids with access to pot, acid, and fiery tomes written by angry French people. But by Trans Europe Express, Kraftwerk had gradually worked a warm pop consciousness into some of their material. Ya gotta be a businessman to some extent. But they knew the world was still rapidly changing, often in technologically, seemingly spiritually-numbing ways so they still made attempts to relay their reactions to those changes to us via the same old repetition-based formulas.

The Man Machine continued this evolution to the point that some of the songs off of that disc nearly would have fit nicely on MTV if it had debuted in 1978 instead of 1981. "The Model" is the most obvious example, but "Neon Lights" managed to live on in the form of American college radio. That song still "shimmers" with an almost perverse nostalgia. The rest of the tracks do, admittedly, pigeonhole their motives, and don't do a whole lot to dispel negative stereotypes of early new wavers. I too would have liked to hear more songs about robots where the vocals didn't try to mimic the sound of a robot (yeah, John Foxx, I'm looking in your direction. Love ya man, but wtf? You want a continuing stream of royalties to pay the sound board repair guy at Garden Studios, don't you?), but again, this was shockingly new at the time. Just like Star Wars. Before that, there was 2001: A Space Odyssey, and a few obscure early Seventies films like Silent Running and something called THX-1131 or something like that directed by Spielberg or Lucas or somebody. Oops, getting off-topic.

Anyhoo, judge this anyway you please. Just remember, some of your heroes never would have made it big (and therefore to your cd or mp3 player) without this.

  • I'm always somewhere between surprised and not by Kraftwerk's status with many people. In many ways they straddle the line between simple pop music and something more. They use some of the simplest melodies and deliveries in many of their songs, most of which, if edited down to three minutes, could have been radio classics. But I feel some of the point is not to be merely three minute radio classics. Kraftwerk had something more in mind than just pop gone electronic. Even in their brightest, most radio-friendly moments, there's an air of haunted emptiness.

  • Of what I think of as the big two Kraftwerk albums, this one leans more towards cold-hearted pop and Trans-Europe Express more towards approaching warmth, but at the same time Trans-Europe Express feels the more robotic of the two.

    And then you get here and you get what you probably expected from the start, at long last Kraftwerk the fathers of electronic as we know it, the fathers of 80's synth pop as we knew it, comes out of the historical shadows and presents itself. At what precise moment Kraftwerk decided to all out adjust their sound to this generally more accessible format I don't know. All along to some extent at least in Germany they had made modest success of a kind. There was a niche for them as far back as Ruckzuck, but Man Machine seems to be the place they finally saw fully the possible audience for what they'd been fostering all along. The formula is simple enough. Take the sound shown on Trans-Europe Express and saw it down a bit, get rid of the concept, and let it inform strong individual songs. The result of course is great! It only makes sense. But that doesn't make this that easy to sum up or understand of course, it's not actually THAT simple. For starters it's almost impossible to imagine how this may have sounded in 1978. It was still not a time where this sort of music would sound commercial, even if a mere three years later it might. Hell in 79' Gary Numan would be making the first major hits that sounded like these! Yet I can't help but wonder still how incredibly fresh this could have sounded back then. Man. Because truth be told, this is a great example of a revolutionary album that doesn't sound like a big deal anymore. It can't. It's own revolution has robbed our modern ears of how neat it must have been. The freshness factor gone forever. Trans-Europe Express still sounds pretty strange, but Man Machine not so much. But that of course isn't to suggest the only thing that ever made it good was newness, that is self-evidently untrue with any listen you give. As much similar music as you might have heard since? This stands strong among them, with a handfull of timeless electronic classics on board. And characterizing it as commercial sounding isn't the whole story either, because they haven't gone and turned into the Human League or anything. On the contrary their old sensibilities remain, even if largely shrunk down. The running length of the songs here is still a tad long on at least three of them, two are just over six minutes, and Neon Lights is nearly nine minutes. And the way they play out still plays to their old sound as well. For instance few of them really have commercial song form, no verse chorus verse thing. Only The Model flies close to that. Otherwise their old style of having mesmerizing and repetitive wandering tracks remains. So that STILL makes it a little tough for anyone just assuming it will be like sitting down with Soft Cell or something. No, the guy who really transitioned this sound to easy song form is still probably Gary Numan. Kraftwerk themselves are still looking to create sounds before songs in some sense, even if by this point the sounds they craft have easy and catchy melodies. That aspect of the records I guess makes it feel a little less special, the really awesome and still fascinating aspects seen on their best pre-78 work, like on their debut, Kling Klang on the sophomore, Audobahn, and Express...poof. This last period of the classic era for them, this pairing up with Computer World is kinda sorta just a different animal. Not so much special anymore, and more a plain out wonderful simple electronic group.

    Rating: 4.5

    Without Kraftwerk, groups like Daft Punk would probably not exist today and this album really shows where the likes of Daft Punk got their inspiration. Kraftwerk’s music takes you into a cold futuristic world using electronic sounds and simple vocals. This trip into Kraftwerk’s mind is a really interesting experience. Whether you like electronic music or not, this is a really entertaining ride into a sci-fi musical landscape.

    The first song drags you into this robotic universe slowly preparing you for what will come later. Slowly, you will discover that this album is not as joyful as the first track made you believe. You will then stumble upon a post-modern world that seems rather dark having the sole sound of synth and the cold voice of the singer acting as a guide. You might be a bit reluctant to fully enter this new universe that this music opens to you, but believe me it is worth the ride. Every single song is unique and quite complex so on every listen you might discover something new or experience this great album in a new way.

    If you’re a fan of new electronic acts, you’ll be able to see where they got their inspiration. If you’re just somebody who’s interested in a discovering a brilliant 70s music act that left there mark on electronic music, this is in my opinion the best way to discover them, in one of their finest moment.

    Through Thick & Thin...
    Man, you've gotta admire Kraftwerk! In 2009 their back catalogue recieved the extensive deluxe reissuing from EMI, and it's just crazy to think of the number of popular artists who have sampled (or ripped on) Kraftwerk.

    But I'm sure it wasn't an easy task to get the recognition they have today... I mean they began their music career acting like smart dressed robots, sitting in front of synthesisers and chanting in monotone German over what sounded like the Pong soundtrack, all in a world that at the time was dominated by spandex, falsetto, screeching guitars and purple haze! Man they must have had to endure a lot. People throwing eggs at them as they tried to support other more conventional bands, people yelling abuse at them, "The robot act sucks, this will NEVER take off!!!" They must have had some serious epiphanies after years of trying and getting nowhere, battling the voice in their heads that said "What the flaming hell are we ACTUALLY DOING?!"

    But it all payed off, they stuck to their guns and never sacrificed their artistic vision. And now we have reissues of classic albums such as Man Machine. Testaments that if you have a dream you should walk through fire to make them come true. After all the blood, sweat and tears, Kraftwerk eventually reached the point where they became a landmark in music history...
