Oh yeah, long day at the office, feeling tired all the time, but I'm home now...... run myself a little bath, lots of bubbles, get it hot, not so it burns but so I am sweating, clean my pores, make my skin go all wrinkly. What shall I listen to? Bit of Green Day? Why not?

Holy flux capacitor Batman! Holy time traveling substance Batman! What are these words that are floating from my gullet? I haven't sung in the bath for ages but right from the beginning of the album and the two familiar snare drum triplets I am singing in perfect tune with that nasally voiced chap I had almost forgotten existed.

Holy drum solos! There, on "Burnout", is the first drum solo I ever learnt, one that caused me so much consternation, and the one that made my dad proud enough to start listening to Green Day himself. Whether or not he began to enjoy them is another matter. In fact, I find it highly unlikely that he did as, back in the mid nineties, this was a kid's band. I was a kid then, or at least a teen, and this was on regular rotation on my stereo, alongside the likes of Offspring, Pearl Jam and Nirvana.

Green Day were certainly the most enjoyable of the bunch, and Dookie, for me anyway, will always be what Green Day are all about; ridiculously catchy songs which were fast and no fuss (with the exception of "Longview" and "Welcome To Paradise", both of which have some breakdowns in them which still make my heart race faster today) executed with power and precision that made you want to jump around and pretend that you knew how to 'be punk'. We were kids, bless us, with no memories of 1977, but we loved the idea of it all the same.

But how come I am still so in love with this album, even as a man? Christ, I am a man aren't I, 25? A quarter century, boo hoo, where did the years go? I don't know but I am happy that they are filled with such fond memories as these.

As a music geek who takes the role very seriously, it is probably bad practice to rate this so highly, but when something gives me so much pleasure, especially whilst splashing around in a nice hot soapy bath, then it would be a crime not to praise it for all that it's worth.

Forgiveness Jun 01 2013  
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Unfortunately, this album has proven to have been terribly prophetic for me. Never would I have guessed back in '99 that the very lyrics I found particularly disturbing would eventually be the ones I'd relate to more specifically than those of any other band. Anyways, I think for this reason, I can account for my undying taste for Dookie all through the years. What does the music herein sound like? Well... I'm probably about to go into real dipshit mode, or some other such term with which a true Dookie-fan would recognize a pretentious and long-winded reviewer of Dookie... but... just burstingly melodic punk music. Pure aural activation on the deepest wavelength of the soul, catchy as hell, mad as hell, and not going to take it for the time being. And: the words. Lyrically, this is definite talent; lyrics like "Coming Clean" or "Burnout" seem effortless, they're so short and unrefined, but are so striking no one could doubt they come from the real life and times of Billie Joe. "I'll live inside this mental cave, throw my emotions in the grave, Hell, who needs them anyways?" Perfect. "Close your eyes and kiss yourself goodbye, and think about the times you've shared and what they meant. To me it's nothing."I find these lyrics empowering in a way that few others are, because the inhibitions of a lyricist who has dignity or is functional are nowhere to be found. In their place, lyrics that slash at their subjects with a wild, mauling transendence of good form, to release the frustration that a person I can identify with feels; maybe his heavy sex-life has taken tolls on the ligaments in his back and wrist, maybe he's relaxed his standards of hygiene because he seldom leaves the house, maybe he's lately so starved for company and engagement his self-pity is making him delusional and sociopathic, and yet, here, in a rare supernova catharsis, he overlooks this tragicomic opera of bullshit his life has become and does. not. care! With the merely three-man combination of guitar, bass, and drums perfectly coalescing each song, these lyrics make for an almost spiritually potent compliment to any amphetamine or methamphetamine roll, such that life is sure to temporarily seem worth living. But my Lord, why haven't I mentioned the singing? Billy's singing is what truly resonates with the love in my soul, I think. Angelic, it is, from a sort of handsome, blunt-smoking, anti-role-model big-brother kind of angel who gives you the straight dope you never get from teachers and adults; voice like a cascading waterfall which makes you feel alive and free...sort of a more pop-punk heir to MacGowan, I've always argued, with definitely the same tough spirit of MacGowan and similarly Irish, as the worldview borne out will attest. If this style of punk were likened to science, Billy's singing would make him the Stephen Hawking, ie, the best of the best. Then again, that Hawking comparison really goes for the entire package, but the singing... ringing out with the strength of a church-bell but unstrained, a loud punk sing-song as selflessly indulgent as Rotten's singing ever was but much fuller-bodied, gusting wide and narrow from Billy's lungs like a damned gale. Enunciation stretched and flattened in a state of rage so long-dormant it has mutated into some gleeful dreamlike state. If this weren't enough, all of this is punctuated by those kind of classic getting-the wind knocked out-of you "oohs" and "uhhs" also reminiscent of MacGowan that no other kind of artistic expression approximates. All this vomit describes the mania-inciting formula Billy was given when he sniffed so much crystal he went to another dimension. I need to shut up now, have done for over an hour, so to summarize: Dookie is a perfectly realized work of art that immortalizes that type of nineties upper-middle-class loser you may have been lucky enough to know, who talks trash on everything, selling zips of weed for his job, saying "I'm hitting the blunt right now" on the phone to his mom, and having over a decade's experience with ritalin and adderall, but still just the attention span of a goldfish. Everything about Dookie is deceptively blunt, so that the aforementioned discoveries of such biologically catchy "pop-punk" melodies and the intricate, frenetic drumming behind them eventually begin to suggest a buried strand of rather alarmingly sharp intelligence, no doubt misapplied only to burning out (smoking pot, breaking condoms during intercourse, styling their hair in uncouth fashions, using the "f" word, and sharing needles by which they've just injected their veins with the methamphetamine so prevalent at the time among their SoCal digs) within this group of young California burnouts. Those slightly older kids who come off kind of sketchy and untrusted on the high-school social radar and have extremely advanced cynicism for their age. The grown up version of your childhood friend's half-sociopathic older brothers who sorta scared the shit out of you whenever they were around... always up to something; swearing, smoking, roughousing way too rough, and with that Jack Nicholson jaw-clench forming through their terrifying smiles when their free-will begun losing pace with the overdeveloped savagery centers of their brains. In a way, it's almost a blessing to relate to certain of these lyrics as much as I do, because it makes me realize in some ways I'm cooler than the many of my cooler and more successful peers who aren't smoking their inspiration and giving themselves the creeps; could those bastards claim the same cred me and Billy Joe can? I think not. To this day I remember the alarm that hit me as I opened these liner-notes on Christmas 1999 to read the lyrics to "Having a Blast," which seemed to explicitly mock my hopes that the boys wouldn't come off as too bad of influences right off the bat, protective as my loving mother was over my then-unsoiled ten-year-old mind. But "Having A Blast" was necessary then, and still is; I mean, when the second song on your major-label breakthrough album is an imaginative exploration of your occasional fantasy of just throwing in the towel with a mass-murder-suicide, if your second track finds you giving so few fucks as to explain with an illegal degree of honesty how such a mentality can develop when one is so jaded and depressed outside of the kicks they get as to find oneself contemplating the option of strapping oneself with explosives in the middle of a crowded business district... you must be doing something right. And hey, what about "Pulling Teeth?" No band will ever write a better song to describe the pathetic yet cathartic realization of just how low you've sunk these days, as you attempt to satisfy your stimulant-fueled masturbation binge for ten hours straight whilst just barely managing to zip up in time for the courteously well-prefaced comings and goings of your mother. Yes, it looks like the 20th century divorce epidemic, un-dis-likeable guitars that sound "crunchy," and singing in a pop-punk early MacGowan singing-style that seems "glorious" all combined to form the pop-punk masterpiece that had always been predicted. Truly, anyone who ever considered Dookie inferior to "real" punk rock was just jealous of how handsome and badass Billy was at the time. This is punk music, PUNK MUSIC; one of the greatest music genres to mention in any context, punk, ya fucks; you don't think so? Well, I do. Even the Pistols wanted to make a million dollars. Forget about your cred, you, it's time you buy this album, so you can finally learn what it means to be a real american.

Hmm. It’s weird thinking that just over two years ago, I lived and breathed this band. I was a 12-year-old (for some of that time, 13) with no TRUE friends; everyone around me was out to exploit me. Dismiss that as paranoia as you’d like, but try living in the shoes of the butt of every joke, the person who everybody knows they can disrespect and step on. As a result, I turned to Green Day. They were the cushion to those days, the salve that healed the wounds of loneliness, and I was obsessed with them. Then I grew up and stopped being obsessed with them, and soon after, I stopped listening to their music, moved on to Grunge and then Glam Metal and heavier Punk Rock.

And just this year, I’ve swallowed my pride and listened to Green Day for the first time since I was 13. First I tried giving American Idiot a bad review and couldn’t muster it; then I didn’t even consider giving Insomniac a bad review; and now, Dookie, it’s YOUR turn, pal.

I like you. You aren’t versatile at all, but that’s not a bad thing. You are consistent and steady and a pleasant listen.

To get more technical, Green Day was just starting out. It’s surprising that their quality improved so drastically from Kerplunk, not just the recording quality, but lyrical and instrumental quality. In this album, the guys don’t just sound like a garage band; they sound like they’re actually out to get record sales—and that, they did, over 16 million worldwide (that’s for you history buffs).

Here’s a warning, though: this album isn’t very Punk Rock at all. Of course, that’s not a problem if you’re not like I once was. There was a moment in time when I wouldn’t touch Pop Punk bands because they seemed too wimpy. Don’t be like me. Swallow your pride and listen to the music for THE MUSIC ITSELF, not the genre. Green Day pretty much made Pop Punk that sometimes bordered on Power Pop, but it was of high quality, so you can’t really complain there.

So overall, this is a solid album. I recommend it to anybody who wants a light listen with quite a bit of profanity and racy references to prostitutes and bisexuality. If you were an adolescent like I was when this album was my respiratory system, then you’ll eat this right up because you will be able to relate to everything written, front and back. Even if you’re an adult, you might enjoy it for the sound and just for the “hell of it” attitude that Green Day radiates. That’s how they felt, and that’s more than certainly how they intend for you to feel when you listen.

HIGHLIGHTS: Burnout, Having a Blast, Welcome to Paradise, Pulling Teeth, Basket Case, Coming Clean

I remember buying this album on a school trip in the 6th grade on the basis of seeing the video for "Longview." I was pretty thrilled at first with the entire set here. "Longview" is one of the best slacker songs of the 90's and "Basket Case" is unbelievably catchy. It took a short time for this album to really catch on to a wider audience. At the time this was the greatest album ever to me, but for some strange reason this has not held up as well as my other favorites from "back in the day." Anytime I get in a mood to put it in I become increasingly annoyed by Billy Joe's singing. Don't get me wrong I do like this album (check the rating) I just don't love it as much as I used too, but this is by far their best and a 90's classic.
