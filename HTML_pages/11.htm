Grampus Sep 09 2023
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
It was back in the eighties I moved into the house I'm currently living in. It was a fixer-upper, didn't even have a functioning kitchen, but it was cheap and had plenty of potential. The garden was so overgrown I contemplated calling in a wildlife expert to assess the possibility of there being species long-thought extinct lurking within its depths. It took weeks of backbreaking work and five skips full of debris to clear away the undergrowth before there was any thought of landscaping. When it came time for development the first things planted were a couple of eucalyptus trees. Just saplings really, no taller the knee-high. Fast forward thirty years and those saplings had undergone a growth-spurt of epic proportions. During that time they'd grown taller than a two-storey house and become such a hazard in high winds had eventually to be cut down.

Reflektor is Arcade Fire's eucalyptus tree.

In my reviews for both Neon Bible and The Suburbs I reflected on how maddening it must be for the band to have every new release compared to their debut album Funeral, and how determined they were to try and musically distance themselves from it. Fourth album Reflektor puts several miles of daylight between the two.

It could be argued Reflektor is as much a James Murphy album as it is Arcade Fire. Certainly the influence of Murphy's work with LCD Soundsystem is reflected in the production and some of the arrangements. Best described as a dance rock album taking inspiration from Haitian rara music together with other Caribbean rhythms, Reflektor is a courageous, if flawed, departure. And those flaws can all be traced back to the album's length.

Many believe Reflektor would be a better album by cutting out certain tracks completely. I don't buy into that. Rather than totally excising songs a little judicious pruning would improve matters. Removing the tedious ending of "Supersymmetry" would be a good starting point. Smashing together the two parts of "Here Comes The Night Time" would also help. Elsewhere, a little nip and tuck would result in a better, slimline version of Reflektor.

Highlights include "Reflektor", with assistance from David Bowie, "We Exist", whose bass-line recalls both Michael Jackson and Bon Jovi, the first part of "Here Comes The Night Time", the glam rock beat of "Joan Of Arc", "It's Never Over (Hey Orpheus)", the track most reminiscent of LCD Soundsystem), and "Afterlife".

I really thought I'd dislike Reflektor a great deal more than I actually do. Credit to the sheer boldness and bravado of Arcade Fire for winning me over. Not completely but enough to admire the band's willingness to explore new territory, to develop and experiment. Up to this point Reflektor is the least impressive of Arcade Fire's albums but, given the quality of what's gone before, that doesn't make it a bad album. It does, however, make Reflektor akin to an out of control eucalyptus tree.

Continua la bulimia compositiva, al punto da presentarsi con un doppio disco per 85 minuti di musica.
L'apertura è affidata ad una cinematica strumentale "[Hidden Track]" (dieci minuti), che si dilunga in micro sintetica elettronica, quasi space;"Reflektor" (sette minuti) occhieggia a certa dance alternativa, filo francese, filo Air e french-touch. Certi brani si attanagliano meglio allo splendore della loro carriera come "Normal Person", che non sfugurerebbe all'esordio, o "Here Comes The Night Time".
Il disco è stato registrato in Giamaica e le influenze paiono derivare da un viaggio ad Haiti. Pertanto tante percussoni, funky, world-music, ma assai ben condita con l'elettronica a delineare un synth-pop arioso.

I had this whole spiel written up about why some of my favourite albums are double LPs, but I deleted it because I realised it was far too long and far too rambly. Much like Reflektor, really. I don't feel that way on Reflektor, where half of the album sounds monotonous and kinda boring.

It's just a bit sad, really.

I've always thought my soft spot for this were due the fact I was 14 when it came out, but in the end, this is probably their best album after mid-00s?

the idea of a 'concept' in Arcade Fire's albums usually works as a sort of a constrain due to how narrow these usually are: The Suburbs has that truckload of tracks that basically circle the same area of duh, suburbian life! and nostalgia, while we all know by now how those concepts worked for Everything Now and WE. the first disc here, on the other hand, it is as simple as 'we are another band! doing other things!' and the band actually allowing themselves to be uncompromised, free to try to connect with the music outside a clear path dictated beforehand. the fact that this involved a lot of collaborators helps a lot, putting the band in an unclear path which they have to go through – what honestly must be the answer for a band that since its conception is based on being emotionally persuasive and too sure of their success on doing so: throwing them among the mess of the 'reflektors' meets additional musicians meets james murphy and having them figure it out.

I remember when it came out people mentioned how the band was copying Talking Heads' later years, which is kinda right: the Stop Making Sense/Speaking in Tongues phase of the Byrne band, where they welcomed a big nucleus of musicians outside the band while ditching self-serious Eno production, brought new light into it, outside the also self-serious David Byrne. the concert and the album both feels special due to how close to each other they seemed there, a tremendous feat considering how they all hate each other nowadays and the stories about their relationship while the band were together. in a way, that extravaganza could well be just a way to make the relationship between them work well enough so the music could do work as well - and it did, in ways that they couldn't replicate later, like in True Stories, a way more Byrne-centered endeavor.

anyways, here all of that works as a self-inflicted challenge/production obstacle, one where the band had to go through themselves in the mist of everything else to actually find what is worth keeping. specially because the music in both cases is never about how that challenge took place (somehow I think of Graceland, as a good example of an album being about its challenges, Father of the Bride as a not so good), rather having the challenge sound like a secondary element, a footnote on a collection of nice, fast, more subtle than usual, nicely produced and crispy pop tunes. the whole group of percussionists that worked here are never the center piece either, but their influence is just all over the songs – as it is the pass the band gave themselves for making plain ‘rock music’ while pretending they are another band. on the first disc, they are not the prophetic band that they believed themselves to be by the time The Suburbs (and Everything Now, and WE, and Funeral) came out, but a direct indie band who actually knew what they were doing produced by a guy who really knew what he was doing.

for a band used to building their songs over the surface emotion they so proudly shove to your face, or doing the same practice with their ‘concepts’, the first set of 7 songs feels detached, loose, free to actually roam to any territory it wants. while moments at Here Comes the Night Time, You Already Know and Joan of Arc hints at it, it is at its very first song, Reflektor, that the band is already complete: the electronic production, the new ‘rhythmic section’ and the old emotion and presumption are already one.

by the time we hit the way more serious disc 2, the sound they aim for feels earned. before the bombastic and emotional duo of Eurydice/Orpheus songs, they have the patience of Here Comes the Night Time II. before going deep into the now actually self-serious, conceptual, and emotionally manipulative songs of the album, they take their time actually developing a sound, an actual idea of how to present all of that, to find a good and patient pop song in the middle of all the noise created by their own ideas of French singing, Greek mythology and coming of age drama. to live beyond Win and Regina’s persona: if WE feels like the couple singing to each other, one big ego to another, Reflektor seems to be something besides the couple, some good pop music that can out live its creators.

Some merit here - but disappointingly meandering...
Arcade Fire are one of the big bands of this time, one of the most commercially successful indie / rock bands of their generation. Their place is well deserved in my opinion, they have brought us music which is quite original in style and presentation.

Conversely in 'Reflektor' Arcade Fire have brought us something which is quite unoriginal and transparent in its inspiration. That is eighties synthetic pop, which has its merits, but does not really win me over from an entertainment point of view.

This album (well in old-speak double album) is lengthy, and a little like 'The Suburbs' is sprawling and actually quite dull in places. This is all a little self-indulgent, a more concise album of say ten good tracks would have gone down better with me. And as I will always write, busy music listeners simply cannot waste time with long closing tracks such as 'Supersymmetry'… and other meandering tracks of little merit (see disc two for best examples of time-wasting).

Arcade Fire returned with something far more positive with 'Everything Now'.
