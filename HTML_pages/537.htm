Even when I first heard this, it made me think about RYM, about what it's for, and how it's used. Somehow I doubt that this is what the record's makers were wanting me to think about, but there you go. You create something, you release it into the world, it has its own life; you watch it fight for a foothold, struggle to get by, you wince as it's bullied by insecure critics, you see people falling in love with it, and then you can begin to hope that you won't have to bury it, that it will outlive you.

It would be easy for most of us, I think, to dismiss Clube da Esquina with a world-weary wave of the hand. Here we are on RYM, this record has hundreds of ratings and an astronomically high average. Meh, no thank you, I want something rated by two people, reviewed by none (apart from that personal memo one of them has left wondering if he left it with his ex-girlfriend in Harrogate).

So as a corrective to that let's say: this is an obscure record. Most people in the world have no notion that it exists. I didn't know it existed until I saw it on RYM; there's a reasonable chance that you didn't either. And that we have found it here is both a good and a bad thing.

Good because, having found it, we can listen to it, and we will hear a nicely varied, well-made lump of Brazilian music; not outstanding, perhaps, but very nice. I like it despite feeling temperamentally at odds with so much music from the country (I feel at odds with this, too, but I can convince myself in this case that I'm the one at fault). Bad because this isn't really how we should have discovered it. Looking at the 1972 chart and seeing something unfamiliar near the top, downloading it and giving it a listen... where's the romance, where's the thrill of discovery? We should have found it in the racks of a damp or dusty record shop, battered, unloved and unwanted. We should have bought it on a whim, not sure what we were getting, and then enjoyed a slow smile as we realised that this was more than just a curio; that this was something really quite good. Or at the very least, we should have had a friend make the discovery in this way and then play it to us, his hand shaking slightly as he dropped the needle onto the platter.

I don't know where I'm heading here, really. I want to wheel out the old cliche about RYM being a wonderful slave and a terrible master, but I can't see how I'm going to work it in. And I want to make a distinction between discovering something because it features highly in a faceless chart and discovering it because of an individual, even one hiding behind a username. Pretend I've done that now, and that you found it convincing and witty. I went on to speak of the record as a physical object. You enjoyed that bit especially, particularly the part where I drew a parallel between the essays of Walter Benjamin and never-popular children's TV series Mopatop's Shop.

What I really want to do is write something about what music, not just RYM, is for. I have a theory. It's a bad one, no doubt. It's for another review, one where I can try on my E.M. Forster impression. May somebody's god save me. In the meantime, I'm not going to listen to Clube da Esquina, because my mp3s are not battered enough for me to care. I think I'll saw my copy of Glazunov's first symphony in half, turn one half upside-down, and glue them back together. Yeah.
