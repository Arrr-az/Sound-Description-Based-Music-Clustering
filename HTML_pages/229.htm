E is a bit like the friend we all have that you always worry a bit about, the one prone to dramatic pronouncements who the world is always weighing too heavily on to bear. The one who takes every social problem as a personal affront and failure, who excludes him or herself from gatherings under the assumption that their presence will only bring everyone down. He tinkers away with his toy instruments and dares not dream of anything better.

Beautiful Freak is unsurprisingly an album of outcast music, the land of misfit songs. "Novocaine for the Soul" is about as close to positive it gets, with a twinkling music box melody and a wish for numbness to get through. Then, it's a trudge through suburban dementia to "Susan's House" because being alone could be deadly, and into an endless cycle of entropy. At least we can find beauty in our decay and maybe one day find that American dream ('but it won't mean a fuckin' thing') and we can take comfort that everyone else is wrong. This album's raw moment of despairing catharsis is "Not Ready Yet" with E saying he won't abide being told to lighten up (and besides, in "Flower" he says 'everyone is trying to bum me out'), which seems fair. Some art is not meant to admit light.

There are a hell of a lot of people out there who have no time for the way I write reviews. I'm too long-winded, too wordy, too clever for my own good. I admit I'm all of those things but what most people don't know is it took a huge amount of arm twisting to begin uploading these things for public consumption. I always wanted to write but never had the confidence so many years ago I thought of combining my love of words with my love of music and began writing down thoughts and feelings alongside a prcis of an album and the circumstances behind its purchase. In effect my "reviews" became the equivalent of a diary, hence my initial reluctance to share.

From the very first Eels have projected a personal relevance which is both difficult to discuss and equally difficult to put into words but I can never listen to any Eels album without that personal angle intruding. Without that level of intimacy Eels would just be another band and E just another songwriter, with it they will always remain close to my heart.

Before I met my current wife some would say that I'd discovered a means of ingesting "Novocaine For The Soul" as I seem to have spent a lifetime suppressing my emotions. I'm sure a whole troop of psychoanalysts would point their fingers at some repressed childhood incident but I never really wanted to know the answer. It's a personality trait which has affected those around me as much as myself and, in the past, I did think about doing something about it, but if one of my internal controls was always set to conceal how on earth was that obstacle overcome in order to seek help? Thankfully I now have someone to whom I feel close enough to discuss how I'm really feeling.

Why bring this up now? Because albums like Beautiful Freak would act as the musical equivalent of the psychiatrist's couch.

Beautiful Freak is a truly wonderful album, but whether I believe that because of my introverted nature is a matter of conjecture. After all lyrics like "and no one will know my name until it's on a stone" are hardly to everyone's taste but they were bang on the money when it came to how I felt about my place in the world. Later in the same song ("Your Lucky Day In Hell") is the line "am I just a walking bag of chewed up dust and bones" which, again, perfectly expressed those same feelings of worthlessness. Throughout are lines and circumstances which resonate with all the power of intimate recognition from the "it's like I got a wire crossed upstairs.I turn the channel but nothing is changing, the only truth is that everything is a lie" lines of "Mental", to the unerring portrayal of the debilitating impact of a life-changing event in "Not Ready Yet". Was I suffering from depression? Who knows but, perversely, it's albums like Beautiful Freak which helped me understand myself a little more and that, even though the songs are sad and personal, there is an uplifting quality about them.

The album highlights are "Susan's House", "Your Lucky Day In Hell", "Flower", "Novocaine For The Soul", "Not Ready Yet", "Rags To Rags" and "Spunky" but this is an album to sink into rather than cherry-pick from.

If my reviews are diary entries, then Beautiful Freak is one of the more personal but I would be doing all concerned a disservice if I excluded that angle. My writing may never have been intended to be read by others but sometimes I feel the mere act of releasing it into the wild helps relieve some of the burden of the past.

Part of my love for Beautiful Freak might come from the fact that I first heard it at the exactly right moment of my life. To say that I was a depressed teenager is probably a bit of an overstatement, but suffice to say that the lyrics really resonated with me, and as I was just becoming more interested in music when I heard it, it had a huge impact on my taste.

The somewhat surprising opening single Novocaine for the Soul is the first of many songs to strike a perfect balance between angsty, dramatic lyrics and a heavy alternative rock sound. Susans House is a feverish tale about an urban odyssey where E observes a 15 year old boy with a bullet in his forehead, and a young mother who cant be more than seventeen. The track flows effortlessly into the quirky anthem Rags to Rags, after which the tender yet offbeat title track follows.

The hard rocker Not Ready Yet is among the best songs of the album, and also the one where Es mixture of soft and edgy vocals comes to its perfect right. My Beloved Monster is a weird but cute song, allegedly written and recorded in 30 minutes as an experiment with Jon Brion. Flower is another gorgeous song with its wordless background chorus. Guest List does not only have a terrific, dreamy atmosphere, but also some lovely lyrics about the need for belonging, whereas Mental has a nice, aggressive beat. Spunky and Your Lucky Day in Hell might not quite hit the target as well as the other tracks, but they still fit into the album just fine, and contribute to its excellent flow. Finally, the album ends on a high (albeit depressing) note, with the heartbreaking Manchild.

There are certainly complaints that could be raised against Beautiful Freak, most notably that the lyrics might be a tad adolescent, but as a guy who first heard the album as a teenager, I just find it charming in that yearning debut sort of way. It would not be fair to call it a freak, but it is definitely a beautiful, even wonderful, creation.

There are few albums that mean as much to me as Beautiful Freak. Through most of my teenage struggles, this record has been there and spent warmth and comfort, despite its melancholic nature.

Beautiful Freak is the beginning of the story of a misfit and his struggles in life, and with last year's The Deconstruction the story has been added yet another chapter (actually it begun with A Man Called E, but this album is where it really got great).

"Susan's House" is a spoken-word track that in which E observes the environment on the way to his girlfriend's house, and every syllable is right where it belongs. "Not Ready Yet", "Rags to Rags" and "Mental" are brilliant angry indie rock songs.

There are moments of incredible beauty, like "Flower", "Spunky" or the outstanding closer "Manchild" and even a great trip hop track with "Your Lucky Day in Hell".

For me this album is perfect and I've been in love since I first heard "Novocaine for the Soul", Eels have been the most important band for me for most of my life, and I don't think I'd be able to judge this record objectively, but fortunately I don't have to.

Highlights: Susan's House, Novocaine for the Soul, Not Ready Yet

So Eels are responsible for one of my favorite albums ever and one of the first albums to get me into music, that being Electro-Shock Blues so when I saw their debut come up in a used CD store, I knew I had to grab it.

Beautiful Freak is certainly less sad than Electro-Shock Blues however it makes up for that in sheer angst and self pity. Despite the catchy hooks of the album, don't think this is a happy album, this is an album of isolation and a lifetime of turmoil and mistakes.

'Novocaine For The Soul' is the most well known song here (and maybe of the Eels career) and even then, that has the line "You better give me something, before I die" and it doesn't get much more cheery than that whether that be the hellish journey of 'Susan's House', the hopelessness of 'Rags To Rags' or the beauty of 'Manchild.'

Despite that, there's always a sense of hope in E's music weather that be the title track or the classic 'My Beloved Monster' that hints that, even when stuff is at its worse, stuff can get better or at least hurt less.

The music here is also pretty great weather that be the atmospheric/cutesy ballads to the almost post-grunge bangers sprinkled throughout. The arrangements all around are catchy, creative and somehow all incredibly intimate.

I don't think Beautiful Freak is perfect due to tracks like 'Your Lucky Day In Hell' or 'Spunky' which are just kinda mid compared to the rest of the album however this album hits way more than it misses and it hits hard. Eel's really established themselves well on this debut and the fact they only kept improving is nothing short of amazing. (if unbearably sad)

Definitely recommend
