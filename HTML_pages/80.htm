What are we listening to, early Genesis?

No, but after 4 albums of a great but diminishing returns-type earthy metal minimalism approach, and result, the Sabs (I know that doesn't sound good at all) pulled a Lou Reed somewhat and started hanging out with people with much cleaner fingernails and nostrils, and a higher music sheet reading pedigree. And a cleaner smelling and more expensive studio and/or producer. An attempt to display artistic growth, in other words.

They all played the prog game rather comfortably and effectively, with Geezer and Tony in particular managing to rise to the occasion without any irony. There really aren't any bad tracks on this - even though "Fluff" will put visions in your head of nuns running a bake sale in a field with rabbits and unicorns running about. Or something like that.

I still can't make that final commitment and conclude that this is my favorite Sabbath album, but I swear by the two foot wooden cross around my neck that some of their best-ever songs are on here. My favorites, anyway. Like "Sabbra Cadabra, " "Killing Yourself to Live" (misunderstood lyrics?) and "Spiral Architect."

Anything remotely involving Ozzy isn't supposed to be capable of being this arty. But as the Damned also proved a decade later, running around in castles and graveyards long enough while wasted can be really inspiring.

This one probably made even Peter Gabriel nervous.

The culmination of all-things Sabbath, 'Sabbath Bloody Sabbath' offers a far more complex and meaningful foray into the outer edges of oblivion and the inner reaches of insanity than on any previous album. No other band, except perhaps The Who, has ever mirrored the feelings of we lowly commoners better than Sabbath -- despair, inner-turmoil, the savagery of drug addiction, isolation -- if you were a troubled teenager in the 70's, you indentified with what Sabbath was screaming. And on no other album does Ozzy Osbourne's vocals reach such a fevered pitch. His voice is piercing and overwhelming in spots, such as on the masterpiece title track, 'Sabbath Bloody Sabbath'. This song is the crowning achievement of Sabbath, with a bludgeoning, downright satanic beat and the harrowing vocals of Ozzy shrieking above the fray. Malevolent, inherently evil -- it overshadows previous attempts at such imagery and is truly frightening in its conjuration of the demons from within.

The album is notable for a Sabbathian (Sabbathish? Sabbathonic?) innovation, the addition of the organ wizardry of Yes keyboardist Rick Wakeman, which definitely strays from the Sabbath canon (or cannon, if you prefer), but in hindsight proves to be a wise move. This is not just another plodding piece of Paranoid 101, it is advanced Sabbath, and the effort shows. Sabbath tweaked their heavy metal sound with keyboards and strings, but they did not stray from what makes them tick, and the effort is not only stunning, but it doesn't become just another of the same-old, same-old. 'Looking for Today' and 'Killing Yourself to Live' (written by bassist Geezer Butler after a stay in the hospital due to drinking problems) again show that this is the ultimate album for the famed helium-lunged arias of Ozzy, and the musicianship and complex compositions on 'Sabbra Cadabra', 'Spiral Architect' and 'National Acrobat' are first-rate. I suppose having 'Paranoid' in one's record collection is necessary, if only as a Sabbath primer and generic hit dispenser, but 'Sabbath Bloody Sabbath' is the genuine article. There is no better Sabbath album.

Black Sabbath seem to lose track of...well, Black Sabbath. It's not that this album lacks the key components: it's got Iommi's riffage, Ozzy's demented vocals and plenty of 70s heaviness. The problem is it's trying too much to imitate others of the time, Zeppelin in particular with the prog and somewhat mellow psych influences. The majority of Sabbath Bloody Sabbath really does sound like LZ offcuts, and even if parts are perfectly competent it's just not what they do best.

Some later albums would show Sabbath perfectly capable of toning down the darkness and still making it work (particularly most of the Dio-fronted offerings), but here it feels too much like it's in the shadow of other contemporaries rather than them doing it their own way. Despite how highly regarded this one is by many, for me it's one of the band's weakest works. Killing Yourself to Live is the only track that really measures up. The title track also has some great riffing at it's core, and Fluff is an interesting softer number. For the rest, I can't help but keep feeling Sabbath are better as dark druids than progressive hippies.

Besides the enviable ability to be concise, Black Sabbath in the 1970s had one thing that tilted fortune in their favor: the vocals of the still-young Ozzy Osbourne. I'm sure those vocals were multi-tracked and pitch-corrected, but Ozzy had an almost childlike way of tapping directly into the angst embedded in the brain of the typical 15-18 year-old WASP male. Hence the band became ridiculously successful and Ozzy became a legend, before becoming a caricature of himself.

In that era, Sabbath were able to forge accessible melodies and deliriously effective heavy riffs, yet do so with an attention to detail and craft that was rivaled by only a few other bands. The use of alternative instrumentation in the form of synthesizers, piano, strings and judiciously deployed acoustic guitars (check out "Looking For Today") gives Sabbath Bloody Sabbath a "progressive" connotation, and perhaps Tony Iommi also deserves some major credit for this album's well-honed creative nuances.

Sabbath's most diverse and carefully conceived album, surprisingly optimistic. And probably the one Black Sabbath album without very much to laugh about. Takes you on a believable journey from hopelessness and despair to radiant joy. (really!)



Track listing

1.Sabbath Bloody Sabbath
2.A National Acrobat
3.Fluff
4.Sabbra Cadabra
----------------------------
5.Killing Yourself to Live
6.Who are You?
7.Looking for Today
8.Spiral Architect

Released in 1973 on Warner Brothers

The Players
Ozzy, Tony, Geezer, and Bill (vocals, guitar, bass, drums)

Guest Star
Rick Wakeman of Yes on keys (what???)

Production

Well produced, but the lowest volume of any Sabbath album (and most of them are pretty quiet). This seriously needs to be remastered! Tracks five and seven are especially soft: I always have to turn up my stereo when they come on. Nonetheless, has a bright (!) sound throughout. Geezer's bass comes out a little muddy (Yep. Iommi produced it), but both the drums and Ozzy's voice are nice and crisp. Guitar not as wildly distorted and powerful as on earlier Sabbath albums, more hemmed in by "processing", but still makes you say "cool, man!"

Other Sounds

Lots of synths on tracks four and six, string section on final track, guitar solos only on half of the tracks ( 1, 2 and 5 have echo, reverb, harmony lines, and various other effects. 7 is the only "classic" Sabbath solo, straight ahead wailing), Iommi uses acoustic guitar on half of them (tracks 1, 3, 7, and 8) and varies the tone in other ways elsewhere, we also get harpsichord, flute, and hand claps, and last but not least, Geezer got an album credit for playing his nose. Whew! The result: the most sonic variety ever on a Sabbath album.

Helium boy

Ozzy sings almost exclusively in one of the highest pitched voices you will ever hear and is in tune almost the whole way through. Studio magic perhaps? Very annoying to some people, and occasionally to me as well (there's one part where he sings "Looking for Today" six times in a row, each time ending on a squeaking balloon high pitch. Ouch!)

Styles

Not much of the blues and jazz influences remain from the first four albums. The only song we see them in is the up-tempo "Sabbra Cadabra", where it is more a return to their roots than blues/jazz integrated with "the Sabbath sound". Track 1 is the most "Heavy Metal" sounding of them all (with a few surprises), "Fluff" is a quasi early Romantic period instrumental, "Spiral Architect" is symphonic rock, "Who Are You?" is synth rock along the lines of Emerson, Lake, and Palmer, and "Killing Yourself to Live" has been cited as an early grunge song (although I'm not really sure why...but it definitely has its own thing). Result: the most stylistically diverse Sabbath album.

--------------------AS SUBJECTIVE AS IT GETS----------------

We all know what THEY say (whoever they are): "Black Sabbath were nothing more than a bunch of ignoramuses who could barely play their instruments. They became popular only because they performed loud, stupid music that appealed to the lowest common denominator." This is the one Sabbath album, more than any other, that THEY can not possibly walk away from truly believing the same thing [1].

I have to admit, Sabbath did play their share of loud, stupid music that I, as a lowest common denominator white trash male [2], found and still find appealing: especially as a child, it gave me a feeling of strength and control in a world where I did not have much.

The album SABBATH BLOODY SABBATH starts out with one of those loud stupid riffs (just like my life began), presents short term solutions to escape the pain("free love" in "Sabbra Cadabra", drugs in "Killing Yourself To Live")[3], questions those solutions and gets to the heart of the matter (the song "Who are you?"), figures it all out (too concerned with pleasing others while really just "Looking for Today!") and ends with a shining, "ultimate answer" to it all: "Of all the things I value most of all, I look inside myself and see my world and know that it is good."

May seem like a cheesy, hallmark greeting card on its own, but makes a lot of sense in the context. Just like the cover of this album. It looks kind of goofy and evil, and was condemned by religious groups [4] but there is a peaceful, heavenly scene on the back cover. Too lazy to turn the damn thing over I guess.

Another thing to consider: the corny message came true for the Sabs. They stuck to their guns, playing the music they liked even while being lambasted by critics, and went on to world-wide success. The album clearly points this out to you: the triumphant "Spiral Architect" ends with fake crowd noise. They are saying "See? Four undereducated, industrial kids from Birmingham could do it. Anything is possible if you believe in yourself!" Sadly, they did not take their own advice and we now have the album TECHNICAL ECSTASY (sigh)

-------------------------GET REAL!--------------------------

Ok, so that's just an interpretation. The album invites it though. All of the tracks move perfectly from one to another[5], and all except the last end inconclusively (either fading out or stopping abruptly). It is as if the entire album is a song. Some examples:

The frantic ending to track 1 gives way to the sorrowful riff of track two, whose nightmarish, climbing final guitar line leads into the innocent, musical box music of "Fluff" (as if you have just awoken from a bad dream to find somebody pressing your forehead with a cold cloth). The classical guitar intro to "Spiral Architect" rises out of the ashes of "Looking for Today", moving from minor to major as a symbol of the whole journey. etc, etc.

There are some "cracks in the wall"

The instrumental "Fluff" has one more repetition than necessary (just before the final one I always feel like "Now!" and am always disappointed)

Most of the songs, in fact, have too much repetition (all except Sabbra and Killing). The over repetition in combination with the low volume makes this album a poor choice for greatest hits collections or mix tapes.

Some of the bass notes Geezer chooses to play cause unwanted dissonance in the title track and on "Fluff".

The slow, mysterious vibe of "Who are You?" is very campy.

But in the end

Piddly complaints. Some of the repetition is worth it in context (title track: mirrors the "no way out" lyrics. The stuttering in "Spiral Architect" lets you hold on to that feeling) If you listen to the album from start to finish, it really takes you to an exhilarating place. And you probably do not have to be a white trash male to feel the exhilaration. Everybody has had a struggle of some kind.
