This is The Jam's first brilliant record. All Mod Cons cover their range of styles from A to Z with slower tracks & ballads, a great Kinks cover ("David Watts"), & punky mod stuff all with outstanding lyrics. I enjoyed it the first time I heard it, but it's taken many listens over the course of a few years for everything to truly fall into place & make me realize that I couldn't live without any track on the record.

"English Rose" is stunning, as is "Fly" with its opening lyrics:

The way that sunlight flits across your skirt,
makes me feel Im from another world,
To touch your face in the morning light.
I hope youre always gonna be around.

Listening to Paul Weller sing that basically destroys me every time.

When I was wandering through Soho, London earlier this year & I came upon Wardour Street my mind immediately reverted to this album & "'A' Bomb in Wardour Street." It's one of those instances that continues to make me think to myself, man, I couldn't live without The Jam.

Mission statement, or harmless, unfunny pun? There's a lot that can be read into All Mod Cons from the title alone. It might be their most 'mod' album, in that it harks back to the original mods more directly than anything that came before it (most obviously in "David Watts", a cover of The Kinks), but by the same token, it could well just be that the title was a flippant response to any attempts to pidgeonhole them. They were, after all, a punk band first and foremost, and punk was surely born from the rockers much more than the mods - was Weller kicking against the punks, or aligning himself with them?

That's the story of All Mod Cons - a simple question about Weller's allegiances that never gets truly answered. Even the album's two best songs sit on either side of the divide - "English Rose" is a delicate, pretty acoustic ballad that could have found a home on an album like Odessey & Oracle or Pet Sounds, but "Down in the Tube Station at Midnight" is a different beast altogether - a tale of far right politics and gang violence, the Sex Pistols could conceivably have told the same story and slotted it on a double A-side with "Bodies". Just look at the artists who seem to have these songs to heart most - about 75% of Billy Bragg's career can be traced back to "English Rose", while you imagine Rancid's bass player busts out the bassline from "Down in the Tube Station at Midnight" during every soundcheck (presumably while the rest of the band ask him which Clash song it's from).

Even the title track, which opens the record, holds no real answer. In a general sense, it's about people who blindly follow trends - but which trend is it attacking? Punk itself? Or are they indulging themselves in the belief that punk broke down boundaries like that and liberated youngsters to like what they wanted?

In retrospect, there are a lot of things about The Jam that don't really make sense - mostly, how is it that the band who recorded more household name punk and punk-adjacent tracks than anybody else (save for, arguably, The Clash) have never really been included in any consideration of the genre's best bands? The canon holds that the Pistols, the Ramones, and the Clash are the top three, and even bands who came later (Siouxie & The Banshees), were less successful (The Damned), or had a less overtly punk sound (Joy Division) tend to find themselves mentioned before The Jam, who often go unremembered. All Mod Cons is the album you'll need to turn to if you want to make sense of it. The Jam were a solid punk band, but after a certain point, they never seemed truly sure of the idea of themselves as punks and found themselves straining at the leash. Sound Affects is where they really make the leap away from punk entirely, allowing Paul Weller to then move on to The Style Council and his solo career, but All Mod Cons has the first stirrings of the band's identity crisis and acts as the most obvious bridge between Weller's roots and what he'd become.

This is the one mate.

All the Mod Cons was the third album by The Jam. They were gaining momentum, recording fast, in the middle of the punk maelstrom - it was 1978 - and the Jam rocked - but they weren't punks -they were bleeding mods an all. But Weller was also writing about the city, and society, and duplicity, and violence. And he was 21 years old.
_All the Mod Cons_ is a sharp, snappy, powerpop album with a rock sensibility, some smashin feedback, and a nack for the hook and the 3 minute song.

If you like the Buzzcocks, and the Kinks, then you have fine taste and this is what you deserve. If you like Van Halen and Supertramp then your a daft twit and oughta smarten up.

Side One is an astonishingly great side of six songs. The title track is 1.18 of honey. A cover of the Kinks David Watts is masterstroke - a ballad, "English Rose" is so lovely you'll be soon requesting a tea and a biscuit accompaniment. Side Two has some solid rock songs, and culminates in the unforgettable -"Down In the Tube Station at Midnight" -

Whispers in the shadows
Gruff blazing voices, hating, waiting

"I've a little money and a takeaway curry
I'm on the way home to my wife
She'll be lining up the cutlery, you know she's expecting me
polishing the glasses and pulling out the cork"
and I'm down in the tube station at midnight

Life before 'counterurbanisation' ( or just a good teenage album)
Owned on remastered European CD

This is one of those albums that you've got to listen to more neutrally to appreciate as 'good' music. I first got the album when aged 13 and I think it was more or less like, say, Nirvana or Eminem or My Chemical Romance etc in being identity music, songs for 'me' as a young adolescent.
The attitude was probably more important than the quality( though this album is made up of much higher quality song writing than the majority of the UK new wave stuff we used to listen to.)
I think the ones that stand out for me now are the ones I wasn't so into when 14 or 15. I mean, songs like 'Fly', In the Crowd', 'The Place I Love' and 'To Be Someone' ( perhaps the best song on the album)the more low key reflective songs rather than the overtly anthemic ones.
At the time, I think this was very representative of the youth culture of that generation in England and perhaps the rest of the UK and what impacted it emotionally through music. Not everyone liked The Jam but they were the biggest group among my peers in London.
It's very much a record of youthful emotions and teenage
music memories, but if you didn't listen to it that way years ago, it's still a good album of quality song writing in the English tradition, with social commentary and a native obsession with social class in particular( 'To Be Someone' is quite prophetic about what happened to the British working class after the 70s, for good or for bad, depending on your point of view)
I hear quite a lot of Rubber Soul era Beatles as much as the usual Who or Kinks, especially on those more introverted songs.

All modern conveniences implies the title or perhaps its the other meaning of the word cons as The Jam are depicted in a completely bare flat. Its not hard to find this a metaphor for England itself, allegedly among the most prestigious and sophisticated countries in the world but awash with anger turning into violence as disillusionment, class warfare and economic inequality created a toxic brew that would set the scene for the turbulent Thatcher years.

Rape and murder throughout the land
And they tell you that you're still a free man

Its testament then to 20 year old Paul Wellers songwriting ability that grumpy as AMC is, it doesnt do the obvious thing and double down on punk. In fact the punk aesthetic is largely gone, The Jam opting for a more seething, slow-burning rage on Mr Clean, a takedown of an upper class twit that both echoes The Kinks and foreshadows Blur. Mr Cleans sound is fascinating, sounding more 80s then 70s with its clean clear bass, crisp drums, precise guitars and echoing vocals. But then theres Mr Cleans counterpart, Billy Hunt in which a working class Walter Mittys delusions of superiority are mercilessly mocked over brisk, catchy rock. (Its also rather daring of them to name the song Billy Hunt - if you know, you know.) Both these tracks owe something to Ray Davies so its only fitting that The Jam confess with a nice, energetic cover of David Watts.
The band unexpectedly dips into jangle pop with In The Crowds reflection on the rubbishness of modern life, the music possessing an unhurried quality thanks to its lazily chiming guitars as Wellers narrator moves from defiance to acceptance and gives up on revolution. An even bigger surprise is English Rose where Weller goes all soft with a soppy acoustic love ballad but its an important reminder of love and sincerity amidst all the rancor. Theres a stretch of tracks from Its Too Bad to The Place That I Love which I dont care about that prevents the album from being a true classic, but it certainly ends strong with a memorable one-two combination. First is A Bomb In Wardour Street, a rousing depiction of a punk club being stormed by police who are causing the violence theyre allegedly defusing, the track brought to life by Rick Bucklers big propulsive beat. Last comes Down In The Tube Station At Midnight, a surprisingly clean piece of pop-rock with relaxing bass, smooth guitars and pleasing backing vocals telling the story of a family man being beaten to death on his way home on public transport, this juxtaposition summarizing AMCs preoccupation with Englands civilized facade hiding a dysfunctional reality. As the narrative concludes and the track fades out to ominous train noises, the music cheerfully resumes again, representing how people just carry on after these acts of brutality. Its a chilling way to close an album that shows that even if The Jam were becoming a more mainstream rock act sonically, they hadnt lost their fire entirely.
Theres some humdrum moments on here but the highs are high enough that they make the record worth picking up. Recommended.

There is so much to like about All Mod Cons. "Mr. Clean" is a teeth-gritting burial of suburban capitalist scum. "English Rose" is unlisted on the back-cover of the record but is a splendid love song for the end of a long day. It flows nicely into "In the Crowd", another rock banger. Ultimately, the record leaves you with the excruciating and tragic "Down in the Tube Station at Midnight". You may never want to return to the uncomfortable subject matter of the song, but it is so well composed and performed that you likely will return to it anyway. This is a good record.
