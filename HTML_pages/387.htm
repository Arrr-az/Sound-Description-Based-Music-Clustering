The mere mention of the name Johnny Marr amongst certain sections of the RYM fraternity is enough to make gussets moisten amongst females and liberated males and men who have never strayed more than a millimetre away from manliness start to veer slightly towards their innate feminine sides as people talk about his jangle and wibble wistfully about the intro to the Smiths tracks such as William It Was Really Nothing or How Soon Is Now as they contemplate yet more ways to cite their devotion to him.

But did you know who Marr's hero was? No? Well let me impart upon you some of my inimitable brilliance and tell you it was none other than the bloke that wields the axe so effectively on this album...step forward Mr John McGeogh. Such was Marr's admiration for this chap he was once quoted, with typical northern exuberance as saying 'that John McGeogh, he was good'.

And good he was too and never more so in this album which is effectively his masterpiece...or at least side A is, as it shows his 'nice end jangle' perfectly in basically all the tracks which in turn are perfectly accentuated via just enough danceable sounds to get the more pop orientated amongst us jigging about whilst at the same time giving enough Gothic impetus to satiate the appetite of those insistent on the quirk of doom....effectively side A of this album is amongst the best Post Punk / Gothic stuff around and was the perfect antidote to the brilliant monotony and miserablism of bands such as Joy Division, A Certain Ratio etc etc, whose depression we revel in but cannot dance to.

Side B is a completely different story...almost as if the band have slapped some sense into themselves for being too damn nice, they serve up a big dosage of infinitely more weird and gloomily epic pre-Kaleidoscope stuff just to remind us that we should not dance to the pain and disturbed thoughts of others. In what I consider a relatively poor second half to the album, tracks such as Night Shift and Voodoo Dolly, whilst no doubt starting off with the honourable intentions of being all awesome, creepy and epic just drag into bouts of long noise.

Overall the A side is just so good that this album remains a must for all Post Punk / Goth fans to get hold.

After a dismal start with their first two albums, Siouxsie and The Banshees turned a corner with their third effort, Kaleidoscope, an album that saw them start to drift away from their early punk roots to become a more experimental gothish outfit. Their fourth album, Juju, was even better, and is widely considered to be one of the band's best.

There's really nothing not to like about Juju from my perspective (aside from the fact it's shamefully absent from my collection at the moment). In the span of just two albums the band had evolved from near-crap to one of the premier pop-goth bands. There's really not a bad song on the whole offering from the excellent "Spellbound" and "Arabian Knights" singles to the even better "Into the Light" and "Sin in My Heart" to other solid tracks such as "Night Shift" and "Head Cut". The whole album has a solid, cohesive flow with a nice, dark witchcraft feel to it.

I do need to do something about not owning this, but the recently-remastered & expanded CD should end up here before too long.

Siouxsie and The Banshees found the middle ground between litany and epileptic fit in the feverish ceremonies of Juju, with tracks like "Spellbound", "Into the Light" and "Arabian Nights" flaunting breezy psychedelic vibes, while tracks like "Monitor", "Sin In My Heart" and "Head Cut" combined the subliminal threat with the spasms of the exorcism, showcasing excellent guitar and percussion work by John McGeoch and Budgie respectively. In the meantime, the escalating horror of "Voodoo Dolly", their masterpiece, put Siouxsie's vocal acrobatics in the limelight, and slowly expanded until it collapsed in a dizzying pandemonium.
