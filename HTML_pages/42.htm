A lot of iconic artists have absolutely essential debut albums. But to me...this LP is really stuck in its time. The Beasties were still being pimped out as these ridiculous frat boys by Rick Rubin, and their rhyme scheme is really corny. Yeah, it has some greats on it. Stuff like "Rhymin' and Stealin", "No Sleep Till Brooklyn" and the massive radio hits are all awesome, but then there's stupid stuff like "Girls" that really drag the entire product down. Granted, nothing is awful, it's just that knowing what became of the Boys, I think this is a non-essential listen. Pick out the classics, but a lot of this album is genuinely stuck in 1986.

This is the first album I ever owned and listened to on my own, when I was about 10 years old. I don't think I've heard it in the past 20 years, but these songs are quite famous and somehow amazingly familiar... [/review]

Just kidding, I'm not into that concise haiku bullshit. I'm gonna ramble on about this. I rarely remember that the first time I ever actually listened to music on my own was when I was 10 and I spent a few months extremely excited about 80s Hip Hop. I think that was around 1986 when this came out. Something like that. I didn't grow up with much music in my household, but my parents did meet at a polka dance in New Jersey and my dad had a big stack of polka albums I probably heard sometimes. So I guess I was like 10 and I had some ultra-cheap tiny boombox I would walk around with, playing the Beastie Boys and Run DMC and that kind of thing. That music seemed so massive and mind-melting at that time. That phase lasted about 3 months and a few months later I discovered Van Halen, AC/DC, Metallica, classic rock radio, etc and I quickly became the rock and roll beast I am today. I totally forgot about hip hop and didn't pay any attention it to again until my early 30s when I got interested in "abstract/experimental hip hop" or whatever codename people use for "good hip hop". Around that time I also had two girlfriends who really loved hip hop but didn't really listen to it very much. They were both really into avant-garde stuff, hipster stuff, etc, but once in a while they would tell me that they really craved that big-ass hip hop beat. They were both from a younger generation than me, the hip hop generation, so it was just normal background cultural identity, not really a personal signifier. In the space of a decade or so, the "mainstream youth rebellion" music changed from metal to hip hop. I never disliked hip hop, but I basically ignored it for most of my life and even nowadays I'm too busy exploring other genres to really give it proper attention.

So I always say that I started with "classic rock" and metal, but it's actually not true, because I started with 80s hip hop. I guess this is the kind of thing I'm supposed to be disclosing on RYM for the sake of "truth in ratings". Those 3 months of hip hop obsession are really lost to memory though.

But there must be something really special about this album by the Beastie Boys that sits apart from hip hop as a genre. A few years ago I was catching up with my dear friend Andrew Choate, who is one of the most brilliant writers and deep listeners and creative bad-asses I've ever met, a real powerhouse. I first met him when I was a freshman at Northwestern and joined the campus radio station, where he was already one of the high priests. He was one year ahead of me in school and he seemed way ahead of me in terms of musical knowledge, but maybe the difference wasn't so big in retrospect. I remember one day I was in his dorm room and he opened a dresser drawer full of CDs. I looked through and saw Silver Apples, Henry Cow, National Health, Soft Machine, etc, all kinds of shit that I was also really into. We were really like soul brothers. At that time in the mid 90s it was pretty unusual for 18 year olds to be into the whole Henry Cow thing. I think we were the only hardcore RIO fans at the campus radio station, which had a big contingent of free improv and free jazz connoisseurs. Me and Andrew were also really seriously into that stuff and soaked up that whole scene in a big way. That was the heyday of Ken Vandermark, Jim O'Rourke, John Corbett curating all kinds of heavy stuff and bringing Mats Gustafsson to Chicago. We got all the new releases from FMP, Intakt, Leo, Emanem, Hat Art, Random Acoustics, etc. That music was really the hot thing going down at the time. That old-school Cutler/Frith/RIO/Cuneiform world was considered a bit out of step by most people there. We were quaint "avant-proggers". But me and Andrew were really hardcore about Soft Machine, Dagmar, etc and we still are because of course that's the real deal. I also saw King Crimson play live a few times during the Thrak tour around that time and I was still hot and heavy over all things Fripp. Over the years, me and Andrew have retained our precious bond and still have some parallel lines of flight, even though we've generally lived a few thousand kilometers apart, me on the east coast and him in chicago or the west coast. When we exchange words these days, it's often about the obscure hardcore avant-garde side of music, but somehow a few years ago we talked about the Beastie Boys. He was going through some tough times dealing with a tragic accident involving his father, so there was some reminiscing about his youth in his thoughts. So here's the thing. Some ten years after first meeting Andrew and after all kinds of musical bonding, he told me that Licensed to Ill by the Beastie Boys was the first album he ever had. And then I remembered that it was actually the first album I ever had. That blew my mind into outer space.

So how does it hold up in 2012? That's a really dumb question, because this album is... Licensed to Ill. Seriously, this is like Zeppelin IV we're talking about. I'd like to say that anybody who doesn't find it mind-blowing is a hipster poser, but I guess that isn't actually true, but it's probably true 90% of the time. Or maybe way less. Of course it's all about what you hear when you're a certain impressionable age. Anyway, it's clear that the review was actually done after the first two sentences, but I'm just sayin' about that other stuff because holy wtf mind life vortex. These tunes are a real godpop mindfuck.

I'm not sure there's any album more important to me than this one. See, when I was a kid, my conception of music didn't expand much further than my mom's omnipresent Bat Out of Hell and Rumours albums (plus Boney M. at Christmas), and the limited repertoire of AM 900 Oldies, plus a couple of outliers in "Patio Lanterns" and "Copperhead Road" for whatever reason. My own music collection (on cassette) mostly consisted of The Muppets and some random children's entertainers forgotten to history by now (or at least to me). So in 1989, I have no idea how or why Licensed to Ill came into my possession but it did and it changed my world.

The Beastie Boys were a revelation to me in a lot of ways. They were often crude and bratty, and their subject matter well beyond my years, but it was like entering a new science fiction universe. I was a West Coast Canadian kid, and their dense mythology of White Castle and Brass Monkey put them on par with comic book superheroes. Hell, they even had their own mythic origin story in "Paul Revere" as gunslingers riding through the desert. More rhymes than Phyllis Diller and Abe Vigoda? I had to take them at their word, but man, right from that first thunderous beat (lifted from "When the Levee Breaks") and that winding-up scream of 'BECAUSE...' they had me for life.

And this was it, this was my Rosetta stone of music and pop culture. Literally for years I would be tracing the bits and pieces they sampled and referenced back to their sources. When I heard them I wasn't even into rock and roll music yet, they provided the gateway back to it instead of the canny rock & roll-derived path into hip-hop the album had rode to platinum status. When I was seriously getting into music in the mid-90s and avidly watching MuchMusic, it was when they were brilliantly expanding their sound and putting out some of the most groundbreaking music videos around. I grew up with their career, and without their music I would be a different person today.

While this album has long been overshadowed by their subsequent huge achievements, it still impresses. A lot of it comes off dumb, but tempered enough by their innate cleverness and their later willingness to let their geek flags fly that it's clearly a perfectly constructed character stance with a sense of fun bigger than all outdoors. Their flow is distinctive as hell, with each of the boys having a distinct personality within the group right from the beginning, even if sometimes they're just yelling shit more than they could really be classified as rapping, and for an album I have listened to for over twenty years, it's incredible that there are still lines that will make me laugh out loud (i.e. 'I oughta whip out my GIRLS!'). 'The beats are clear, the rhymes are bold,' no lie. Turn it up loud.

R.I.P. MCA, you were a unique voice in music, you were always my favourite, and you will be sorely missed.

When Run D.M.C. succeeded with Raising Hell, Def Jam had a trio of punk-rockers that had switched style into rapping, named the Beastie Boys. They debuted the same year (1986) with Licensed to Ill and this right here is the original hip-hop album by any white rappers. Rick Rubin is the mastermind behind the album's production and he gives most beats a hardcore, Def Jam sounding style with naked drum machines and little else. There are some instances of punk guitars like "No Sleep Till Brooklyn" and "Fight For Your Right to Party" but they are less and most highlights are the hip-hop songs anyways. Lyrically, the Beastie Boys - which consists of MCA, Mike D and Ad-Rock - aren't really about anything more than girls and bragging and they deliver their rapping old school style by rapping the verses together, line for line and sometimes word for word, changing voices which can get confusing and often make it hard for the listener to identify the rappers. After a while it's easy to get it though, since MCA has a very rough voice while Mike D and Ad-Rock both have somehow nasal voices with Ad-Rock sounding more childish. They often yell their lyrics and it's an overall energetic experience where the listener is hyped up throughout the 40 something minutes. In the midst of this purely 80s album, classic party songs like "No Sleep Till Brooklyn" and "Brass Monkey" are playable and likable at any time, plus there's also the classic "Paul Revere" which foreshadows gangsta rap in many ways as it's a story of the three Beasties meeting in the middle of the desert with a bunch of gun-references thrown in and even a moment in a bar where Mike D shoots two people dead. Even if many beats haven't aged well, this is a classic album that should be enjoyed by most hip-hop fans who don't necessarily need the rapper to deliver their style the same way on every song.

Recommended tracks: Paul Revere, Brass Monkey, No Sleep Till Brooklyn

Bravado at its finest. If by some miracle you're unfamiliar with this one, the Beastie Boys' debut, _Licensed To Ill,_ is simply a massive achievement of testosterone. For every bar of drum machines turned up to 11, there's another lyric of gleeful misogyny, senseless violence, macho fantasies and the kind of old school boasting you can only get from late 1980s hip hop.

At the time, of course, the concept of a white rapper was pretty outlandish (stereotypical, but it's true), but the three MCs sure weren't helping their cause by being tools to everyone in sight (this reviewer distinctly remembers an interview before the release where MCA claimed "The only reason we haven't put out a video yet is because as soon as we do, they'll have to change the name from MTV to BeastieTV!"). Their reputation from this era is well documented - hey, Bob Saget made a joke about them on Full House! - but it gives no indication that the album would be as much fun as it is.

While this may seriously mess with the 'credibility' of the album, sorry, elitists - this used to be the drug of choice for only the biggest douchebags in high school. And hey, so was Limp Bizkit. So what do the Beastie Boys have that they don't? Oh, let's see. Great production, awesome lyricism considering the subject matter, some well-executed diversity, vibrancy, some genuinely funny stories (when they aren't drinking a different liquor in every song, they're being pirates or bangin' some chick with hot new wave hair) and an overall sense of freedom in all its debauchery. Make no mistake, they're being assholes for the sake of being assholes, but instead of dragging down the proceedings, they had so much fun putting all this together that it wound up being the ultimate party record. It doesn't take itself particularly seriously and puts a lot of different sounds together just because it sounds _awesome._ If this doesn't make you wanna party, you're officially either a pussy or a dick, and either way you're probably not gonna get laid.

Okay, maybe the Beasties meant this all as satire. They're certainly clever enough as they proved in their later work, but this satire worked so well it actually makes you feel like - or want to be - a part of the same crowd. And if that's not indicative of a rousing success, nothing is. Long story short, this is a rowdy, womanizing, sometimes downright idiotic record, but it's a brilliantly put-together idiotic record.

The first half is where all the super-frat, heard-it-a-million-and-one-times tracks are (that were original at the time). The second side is where they get really busy, showcasing all the stuff they'd totally do right on the next album.

Still, time to give nuff respect. "The New Style" is worth this LP's weight on its own, but that's before loops like "Paul Revere" and "Brass Monkey" come into play. The Beasties nailed a formula here, and sometimes it can be too easy to dismiss it because nobody did it as well. You can make a case for Run-DMC, but I feel like the Boys really took that torch and ran with it. That white Jews were making beaucoup money off of 'black art' only made things... political, you could say.

Over time, I think this album's been exposed for the influential piece of work it really is. The aesthetic personally gets old for me - it's definitely more of a gimmick than Rubin or the band would like to admit - but I'm willing to give this credit for being what it is in the history of the art of rhyme.

Historians specializing in the development of rap/rock as an established genre of music are often bickering on internet forums or in coffee houses about which record gave birth to their chosen field of expertise. One school of thought argues that the true crossover of the two genres didnt happen until as late as 1992 when Rage Against the Machine released their scorching eponymous debut but created along with that a legion of poor imitators. It wasnt until this time that rap/rock became a prevalent force in the music industry; before that it was a viewed and treated as little more than a novelty that occurred from time to time.

Another school of thought believes that the movement began in 1986 when Run-D.M.C. teamed up with their big-haired, big-lipped neighbours, Aerosmith, and remixed the Smiths Walk This Way. However, critics of this theory argue that if this is the case then the genre rap/rock* had its genesis in 1975 when Aerosmith first released the song as there are few differences in lyrical style between the two versions. This would mean that Steven Tyler technically invented rap as the original Walk This Way predates the Sugarhill Gangs Rapper's Reprise (Jam-Jam) / Sugar Hill Groove, commonly attributed as the first rap record, by four years.

However, the It All Started in1986 theory is lent considerable weight by the appearance, in that very year, of the Beastie Boys obnoxious debut, Licensed to Ill. From the outset it is quite apparent that the group are rock kids who discovered and fell in love with rap as the album begins with a sampled loop of John Bonhams big drum sound from When the Levee Breaks. This is the first of many tips of the hat to the trios rock music heritage. As we progress through the record we are treated to shredding solos from Slayers Kerry King, a fleeting sample of The Clashs I Fought the Law and a prevailing rock and roll attitude which is impossible to ignore. The enduring image that one might take from this record is that of three drunk punks arriving at (or more likely crashing) a frat party with a beer keg over one shoulder, a young girl over the other and a heavy metal salute held aloft.

It is difficult to say whether the arrival of Licensed to Ill heralded in the beginning of an era which saw rap/rock considered as art, or even rap as art. It certainly doesnt seem artistic in the traditional sense of the word. Its goofy, sexist, tasteless, bragging and suffers from inconsistency; incendiary numbers like Fight for Your Right and No Sleep til Brooklyn often give way to songs like Posse in Effect and Paul Revere which sound like they were dreamt up the morning after a banging party or whilst rendered immobile through cannabis intake.

But it was never an album that was supposed to be considered as art. It was (and continues to be) an explosion of testosterone, three bratty kids rebelling against anything that was worth rebelling against, joking and pushing each other in a rap tug-of-war. The effect is wildly entertaining for the most part; funky, heavy, slammin, bangin, goofy (see Girls for the best example of this) and anything else you need a party album to be. Be warned though, the following morning it is likely that you will be left with a sore head, three strange women in your bed, a post fornication couple slumbering on your front lawn and literally gallons of vomit in every unreachable corner of your house.

*the more you have to type rap/rock the more you wish there was a better, more condensed word for the genre as a whole. Crock? Ckrap? Hip Rock? Rock Hop?

Just after the huge success of RUN-D.M.C.'s Raising Hell came a trio of white punk-rockers turned rappers that would break all the sales records. They where the first white rappers and they really proved that rap could sell a whole damn lot of records. They where backed up by the hottest producer at that time Rick Rubin. Just like in the Raising Hell & Radio albums his beats are very raw and rock influenced. The single "Fight for Your Right" is among the most popular pop songs of the 80's, the best songs on the album however are the hip hop singles "The New Style", "Paul Revere" & "Hold it Now, Hit It". The Beastie Boys where similar to RUN-D.M.C. in style but they where alot more juvenile and less serious. Technically they are not great rappers, but together they make a great combination. They spit (or yell) some of the funniest shit and it's all so fresh with the Rick Rubin production. Who whould have thought that these 3 guys would have a career that last more than 20 years and be critically acclaimed? Licensed to Ill is where they where young and they just didn't know better. Turned out they put out some unique timeless music.
