It's amazing to think about, really. The step up from Pablo Honey to The Bends is just incredible. In just two years, Radiohead went from being just a run of the mill, generic, throwaway alternative rock band to being one of the best bands on the planet.

A few years ago, this was one of my most listened to albums but when I want to listen to Radiohead these days, I'll always turn to Ok Computer, Kid A and strangely enough In Rainbows but there's no denying its brilliance. The recording of the album is quite an infamous story, the band had been close to breaking up because of the pressure of their instant success due to 'Creep' - the song that they still loathe. They had been touring for two years, and with each passing show, they hated the song more and more. They were a one hit hit wonder, and that's exactly what they didn't want. They were faced with the dilemma of crumbling because of the success and fame or trying to do something brilliant to move on from it. I think we all know what they ended up doing. The band concentrated on making bigger and better music, that's exactly what they went and did and they recorded The Bends.

I always thought of this as being quite a run of the mill alternative rock album in the mold of their debut, but after getting it off the shelf for the first time in a long time to give it a listen, it's anything but run of the mill. I think it's easy to look at this and compare it to something like Kid A which is full of experimentation, innovative sounds, atmosphere, soundscapes and when you look at them side by side, The Bends may look more basic and primitive. Not that there's anything wrong with basic, but I love listening to Radiohead because of the boundaries they pushed. If Radiohead had stopped making music at The Bends, it'd get a whole lot more love and respect than it does. It really did bridge the gap between a very dull by the book sound to a sound that'd end up revitalising and redefining the genre as well as the decade, there's early traces of them beginning to push themselves as well as the boundaries and you can see their experimental side on here. The shift in aesthetic is really quite apparent. It's a gigantic leap forward artistically, it's multi-layered and the ideas are just bigger and better. Thematically, it's on a whole other level. As is the music. Everything about it is just great, the sounds the guitar made in particular but also the songwriting.

This is where they started to hit their stride. This was the start of an unbelievable run of amazing albums, they never made a bad album after Pablo Honey and The Bends stands out as one of their best. Their third best, behind Ok Computer and Kid A. An album that should be on everyone's shelves.

Radiohead-The Bends
U2. Jeff Buckley. R.E.M. Pink Floyd. The Smiths. Even Nirvana and Pixies. Radiohead proudly lets their influences seep through on their sophomore album. The Bends is not a revolutionary departure from Pablo Honey in any way, shape, or form. Yorke's lyrics are still tormented, but now they're more ambitious. Metaphors, multiple verses, anthemic choruses, etc. The three guitars are still there, but this time they weave together so fantastically that you might throw on a few songs that Johnny Marr had a hand in making just to compare. Yorke still can't make a smooth shift between singing, falsetto, and screaming, but he's improved with all three and his vocals are generally strong throughout the album. Bass lines now exist, and even drive a few songs (namely "Bones"). The songs, while still poppy, are now anthemic and complex. "The Bends" utilizes a break consisting just of drums and vocals, then reuses it, but the guitar comes in stronger, teases a solo, and plays a select few notes so impeccably timed you might need to put your hands in front of your ears so you know where your ear drums are when they come flying out. "Black Star" almost abuses dynamics in the intro and soars, and a bluesy guitar lick is played going into the chorus. The Bends is still largely made up of pop songs, but they have hooks, dynamics, and intricate songwriting that reveals more with each subsequent listen.

The album closes with the unconventional, heartbreaking "Street Spirit (Fade Out)", which is a sign of things to come on OK Computer. Yorke delivers a terribly haunting vocal performance in front of a simple guitar line and the occasional bass or orchestral note in the chorus. "My Iron Lung" delves so seamlessly into a Nirvana-style screaming chorus that you don't even realize how much ground a pop band just covered. The U2-style music, Jeff Buckley style vocals of Fake Plastic Trees change with such subtlety from one verse to the next that it's easy to dismiss it as the "Creep" of the album (a title not fitting for any song on the album) and not notice the grandiose nature of the simplistic songwriting. "Just" goes from an angry electric part to barely-backed lyrics with guitar lines between them into a bluesy guitar lick played with Yorke's vocals until going back into that angry chorus. Every song has the accessibility of a pop song but maintains all the layers a band like "Radiohead" should have.

There is no weak track on The Bends. Every track is strong on its own and especially in context of the album, and similarities are scarce. Each song has a unique take on a couple recurring lyrical themes that confronts love, loss, and heartbreak from a different angle. Yorke is far from the cryptic lyricist he is on several subsequent albums. Greenwood and O'Brien's guitar parts speak to one another effortlessly and can safely drop out at any point because of a strong rhythm section. Simply put, there's nothing about The Bends not to like. Acoustic, electric, poppy, spacey, loud, soft, simple, complex. Whatever it is you want in sound, The Bends has got it.

Rating: 5/5
BSFTA: The Bends, High And Dry, Fake Plastic Trees, Just, Black Star, Street Spirit (Fade Out)

Je ne pensais pas que ça m'arriverait un jour. Pas avec cet album là. Avec d'autres, ok. Je veux dire, si je réécoute du Marilyn Manson lors d'une de ces ballades sur le boulevard du temps qui passe qui seront de plus en plus récurrente au fil des ans, je peux me dire que même l'aveuglement affectif ne me permet pas de ne pas voir que ces morceaux que j'ai beaucoup aimés étant plus jeune ne sont finalement pas si géniaux que ça. Voir même pas du tout. C'est normal, c'est Marilyn Manson. Mais avec The Bends de Radiohead? Vraiment? Cet album que j'adorais quand j'étais ado a un tel point que j'avais dis un jour que je le préférais à OK Computer parce qu'il était plus fun à écouter, ou quelque chose dans le genre? Cet album dont j'ai appris parfois les parties de guitares par coeur pour en réenregistrer certaines sur mon petit ordinateur dans des versions que le terme "embarrassant" ne décrirait pas avec assez d'acuité?

Quand j'ai réécouté cet album aujourd'hui, il y a des choses qui m'ont choquées. Que je ne peux plus laisser passer. Que je ne peux pas considérer sans froncer les sourcils. Sommes-nous tous absolument aveugles face à cet album ou suis-je devenu aigri? C'est vous qui voyez. Reste que, merde, cet album n'est pas terrible et pêche par une naïveté terrible. Il est un peu à l'image de son artwork : un peu cheap, un peu ridicule, très 90's dans le sens pas très positif du terme, et avec des choses de mauvais gout qui n'avaient l'air cool qu'en 1995. Sérieusement, vous avez vu la tracklist sur la jaquette arrière? Ecrite dans une police laide, super large, qui prend toute la place, et à côté, l'image copiée-collée de la terre (mais en... ovale?). C'est plus qu'une tracklist, c'est un vrai résumé de ce qu'il y a à l'intérieur. Déjà à l'époque, je trouvais ça un peu laid. Maintenant, je suis obligé de cacher le boitier quand j'écoute l'album, pour ne pas le voir. Et je parle pas des photos toutes pixellisées et tordues à l'intérieur ou des dessins copiés-collés sur Paint.

Mais oh, pardon, je m'égare, il y a la musique. Il y a tant à dire sur la musique. Pourquoi j'aimais cet album à l'époque? Tachons de nous souvenir. J'ai retrouvé une vieille discussion sur l'internet où je disais, en 2005, "The Bends a des morceaux vraiment merveilleux, un son tout a fait classe, un chanteur au sommet et tout et tout". Vraiment? Niveau composition, The Bends est effectivement meilleur que l'atterrant Pablo Honey qui était tellement mauvais qu'on se demande comment le groupe a pu ensuite faire des choses mille fois plus réussies, preuve s'il en faut qu'il ne faut jamais désespérer et qu'un très mauvais groupe peut devenir très bon, peut-être, on sait jamais, ça donne de l'espoir à Vampire Weekend j'imagine. Mais, sérieusement, sur 12 morceaux, combien tiennent vraiment la route sans sonner insipides? 5 si on est très gentil. Et le reste, c'est un festival de tubes doucement FM et irrésistiblement mélancoliques qui transpirent la puberté. Je veux dire, un morceau comme Sulk? Ou Bones? et Black Star? Ah, on évite toujours de parler de ceux là, de tout ces compositions banales et clichées, on fait l'impasse sur leurs refrains plats, forcément. On préfère les oublier. On se concentre sur les tubes. Oh! Par exemple! Fake Plastic Trees! Vous aimez Fake Plastic Trees? Cette guitare qui grattouille des accords pleurnichards? Cette rythmique lente avec de la reverb et cet orgue derrière qui a l'oeil qui se mouille? Comment diable peut-on être touché par autant de mélasse? C'est de la soupe messieurs-dame, c'est pré-maché pour vous, c'est creux, ça ne fonctionne pas, c'est juste chiant comme un album de Travis, et pourtant ils la joue encore en live, pendant que des gens allument systématiquement leur briquet et font des mouvements d'épaule de droite à gauche les yeux fermés, comme bercés par tant de jolie mélancolie, oh que c'est mimi. Mon dieu, quelle horreur. Je n'aimais déjà pas le morceau à l'époque. Au moins quelque chose qui n'a pas changé. Et quand un Thom Yorke qui se servait encore un peu mal de sa voix se lance dans des gros "aaaaaahhhhh" devant de grosses guitares dans des trucs comme Planet Telex (pourtant un des rares morceaux à sauver de l'album), vous m'en voulez si je pense à U2? Parce que j'y pense.

The Bends est un album complètement à la ramasse en fait. Avec des influences pas très ragoûtantes et un gros son calibré commercial. Comme si on avait poussé ces pauvres jeunes homme à essayer de sonner stade. Oh, il y a cette production. Ces guitares electro-accoustique très laides. Ce côté studio tout propre, sans défauts, sans vie. Ces effets sur les guitares électriques qui me donne des boutons. Plein de tics 90's qui aujourd'hui ont pris un coup de vieux impossible et rendent ces morceaux un peu caduques, peut être tout aussi dérangeant à écouter que des vieux tubes synth-pop des années 80. Sauf que c'est moins drôle un son de basse 90's qu'un vieux clavier dégueulasse de 1983. Du rock alternatif The Bends? Non. Juste, du bon vieux pop-rock tout gentil, et n'essayez pas de dire le contraire, les jolies ambiances de "(nice dream)" ou l'impossible ennui qu'est "bullet proof" en sont les preuves les plus flagrantes. Ecoutez le refrain du morceau-titre par exemple. Soyez honnêtes. Vous tolèreriez ces guitares pompières avec un autre groupe?

Et pourtant, zut, je ne peux pas cracher sur tout. Il y a toujours Just qui reste un chef d'oeuvre. Mais les différences par rapport au reste expliquent pourquoi : moins de grosses guitares braillardes, moins de refrains tout pleurnichards, non, plutôt des suites d'accords un peu tordues, plus de dissonances, et puis ce solo de Johnny Greenwood, ah, Johnny, ta mèche, toi, Johnny, qui sauve presque l'album avec ton jeu de guitare qui chamboule un peu l'ordre établit par des chansons qui sont pourtant terriblement quelconques. Bien sûr, oui, The Bends avait en lieu les germes de ce qui allait venir ensuite.

Mais pour l'heure, en 1995, bien souvent, oh, échec, échec, échec, et je ne peux pas écouter cet album sans me dire qu'il n'est pas vraiment réussi, et je ne comprend pas l'engouement sur ce qui ne devrait être qu'un énième album de pop-rock-radio des années 90, mais qui a le droit à une réputation incroyable, grâce à la présence de morceaux très réussis comme My Iron Lung ou Street Spirit qui cachent derrière eux d'autres machins immatures, ennuyeux, laids et mal foutus. Ça et le fait que, voilà, bon, c'est Radiohead. Non, The Bends n'est pas un chef d'oeuvre, c'est du rock puissamment quelconque au milieu duquel émergent des morceaux plus réussis parce que moins dégoulinants. Sauf que, tous aussi aveuglés par une fan-attitude plus ou moins en déclin, nous n'osons pas vraiment le dire. Dire que cet album a vieilli. Beaucoup. Trop.

Soudain, on peut mieux comprendre pourquoi OK Computer a été un vrai choc pour les gens lucides en 1997 : parce que, jusque là, en fait, Radiohead était un groupe médiocre.

Et ça, il serait temps qu'on se l'avoue enfin.

Et j'arrête ici cette review. Et je me relis pas. Et je retourne écouter de la musique récente. Que je descendrais peut être dans 7 ans. Ou peut être pas.

4/20

(c'était 10 avant, mais vous continuez à surnoter cette merde, désolé).

When someone first loaned me Pablo Honey, back when men were men and sheep were furry, I wasn't at all impressed. I still can't say I'm impressed - actually, I can't say whether I would be impressed or not as I haven't listened to it since then. It just didn't impell me; in fact "Creep" struck me as lacking much of a hook at all, regardless of the claims of the fans who were exclaiming impending godhead for the band (at this moment, for the life of me, I couldn't give you the slightest hint of what it sounds like). Nowdays the next generation appears to be embracing the limp Coldplay. When I first heard Parachutes I thought, "where the hell are the hooks every critic is amazed by?!" (I thought this, I didn't say it, as I was in the truck of my Coldplay-loving friend on the way to the local bar) "Yellow" seemed particularly lumpen and Chris Martin's 'exquisite tenor' was well below average as a singing voice and quite pedestrian. A Rush of Blood to the Head was an even lesser output, thus countering the sophomore un-jinx Radiohead achieved with The Bends. I'm not going to even bother with X&Y.

Now that I've pissed off a third of Our National Treasure, I can get back to the business at hand. Some of my disdain for Pablo Honey may have been from some misplaced musical nationalism I was feeling at the time. So a couple years later when I was a full-blown Britpop worshiper, gobbling up every Select, Melody Maker, and NME I could get my hands on, I was primed for another try. I had read good things about The Bends and was willing to do away with previous doubts and engage my interest, so I went out hunting on the cheap. I found a $3.99 used cassette at a local shop and brought it home for a perusal. Cripes! Are those hooks? In every damn riff? Well, this judgment was for the first side as the tape was gummed up pretty bad and I had to pull it before further damage could occur. Very soon I had gone back out and upgraded to a used CD (God bless those with poor musical taste!) and fully experienced this solid piece of work. Yeah, there were buggering good tunes all over the landscape here.

Best of all I didn't have to disengage my intellect to enjoy the music. Although to a lesser degree than their later works there was still the overgloss of experimentalism, but in service to the licks. I can sit back and absorb the wondrous creativity in the instrumental interplay and the muted passion in Thom Yorke's voice, but still do shots of bourbon and pump my fist and screech "'Planet Telex' and 'Fake Plastic Trees' are great 'choons, Mac!" Now I'm not going to pull a modified Teen Beat on all this; their catalog, while full of great stuff, does not make your orgasms better or more numerous, or make bad beer taste better (unless you're already drunk). I can say I'm proud to have most of their albums and don't find it at all trendy to listen to them and compliment the group on quality. I won't wear the t-shirts and proclaim the music changed my life, but I will play it loudly and without fear of hipsterism. And The Bends sent me on my way.

And remember kids: bangin' a movie star doesn't mean you're an artist, it just means you're rich and famous. There's a difference you know.

Radiohead are traditionally seen as a band who are not bothered by the expectations of others and thus they fearlessly do exactly whatever it is they want at any given time. This is for the most part completely true (and it's why most of their albums sound completely unexpected), with the exception of The Bends. At this stage the band were dealing with critics who had already labelled them a one hit wonder after "Creep", audiences who only wanted to hear "Creep" and a record label who really, really wanted them to record more "Creeps", and so there was an incredible amount of pressure on the band for their sophomore. What made things worse is Radiohead had never been too keen on their sudden smash hit to begin with (the signature guitar disruptions were an attempt to sabotage the song, which backfired spectacularly) and thanks to its runaway fame, they had already began to despise the song they felt had became an anchor around their necks - the first informal taster for The Bends was "My Iron Lung" which was a very unsubtle dismissal of "Creep". Rather than cave in to the pressure of the follow-up, Radiohead's motivation for their second album became the need to demonstrate to the world that there was more to them than just one song and that whatever they released needed to shut up any doubters who were dismissing the band as already-has-beens.

So The Bends is all about Radiohead proving their worth and it does it with flying colours. While it’s a clear continuation from the grunge-era guitar walls Pablo Honey rather than a radical shake-up, Radiohead are not just showing a whole lot more ambition and range here but they're also not so subtly showing it off and flexing their capabilities. There’s a wider range of tones and moods than Pablo Honey ever had and running through the album is constant and obvious experimentation with production choices, arrangements and atypical song structures; even Yorke has stepped up with his lyrics, starting to develop his own characteristic voice. "Planet Telex" is a very intentional starting point for the album because it demonstrates from the lift-off that this isn't the same Radiohead who made their humble appearance a few years ago, its spacey atmospherics, loop-like drums and electric piano distancing the band as far as they can from the previous album's limited soundworld. Though "Planet Telex" is the furthest out there that The Bends goes, much of the album sounds like Radiohead challenging themselves to do something new with every song like each track began as a songwriting exercise to write something in X sound or style. It's never predictable where the album goes and just when you think you've figured The Bends out, it throws another curveball or sidestep to a different style and sound with the next song.

That's much of the album and not all, and thanks to that it becomes ridiculously obvious what the lowlights are. The Bends is a classic by its reputation and due to its importance in the wider Radiohead timeline, but it's not a classic wholly on its songs - otherwise we wouldn't have "Sulk", "Black Star" or "Bones" cluttering the tracklist. They're so close to Pablo Honey that their inclusion here was either the band throwing an uncharacteristic bone to the past audiences or they simply didn't have as much new material as they thought they did and thus some b-roll material was thrown in to fill the album. It's the only way to explain the gap between their middle-of-the-road rock antics and the rest of the album: if The Bends is about looking forward, then these three songs inexplicably revert back to the past. On Pablo Honey they may have stood out more positively but in the new company they're in they're sticks in a mud and almost haplessly straightforward compared to everything else going on.

That "everything else" meanwhile really is one of music's great and unexpected level-ups. Half the album was released as singles in one way or another and they're largely all incredible and miles ahead of anything on the last album. "Planet Telex" soars with its space-age textures, "High and Dry" is a rare glimpse of Radiohead in an unabashed pop mode in a way they'd these days rather forget about even though songs like this demonstrate how brilliant they were at it, "Fake Plastic Trees" launched a thousand other British rock ballads draped in strings and none of them ever bettered the earnest emotional power of the original, "My Iron Lung" is a noisy and unpredictable kiss-off that gets more delightfully sassy as it goes while bridging this and the last album together in a very natural manner, and "Street Spirit" as the closing chapter points the way forward to the next, previewing the anxiety and depression of OK Computer as it submerges itself into a gorgeous, haunting darkness that's scarily blissful towards the vocal runs of the end (a sentiment like "immerse your soul in love" has never sounded more foreboding). The direct guitar anthem "Just" is the only one that's never particularly grabbed me and it's for no real reason why that I could point out (great video though!), but I do find that the borderline joyously bouncy "The Bends" actually does everything it does but better, and in my imaginary timeline these would have swapped their single and deep cut statuses; "The Bends" also very powerfully brings the album back to earth after the shock cold open of "Planet Telex" which further adds to its impact. "Nice Dream" and "Bullet Proof" in-between are both slow and atmospheric, too gentle to jump out but beautifully serene enough to sink into, showcasing the band's growing desire to build and sustain moods rather than simply go out loud at all times; plus, the sudden wake-up call of "Nice Dream"'s ending is the most memorable structural whiplash thrown at the listener on the record.

The thing is, The Bends is clearly a transitional album: not only for Radiohead themselves whose story would start in earnest with OK Computer (as most people would attest to), but also for British rock as a whole as you can easily point to specific songs here that acted as launchpads for the entire careers of other groups. That's a ridiculous amount of accolade and cultural importance which can easily obstruct that at the end of the day this is still an album by a band caught in the middle of a more significant development phase. Therefore, all the usual hallmarks of transitional albums apply here too despite The Bends' significance in the Radiohead biography: it's one foot in the future and the other still stuck in the past, moments of brilliance and exciting peeks in future directions interspersed with old ways still lingering around and hints of change that have yet to be fully realised. A good half of this album is inarguably brilliant and still stands strong with the rest of the band's discography even if they undoubtedly became a much more exciting band later down the line, and though the other half of the tracklist is more hit and miss there is a rush of excitement in hearing the band figuring out who they are with such vivid ambition. Often that energy and ambition also translates to genuinely excellent pieces of 90s guitar crunch. Over the years I've gone back and forth and back and forth with The Bends (in RYM it's probably one of the albums I've most changed the rating to), from early indifference to various honeymoon phases when it cracked through my defenses and then wild pendulum motions depending on which aspect of the band resonates with me more at any given moment. It probably tells as much about me as a listener as it does about The Bends' nature as an album in-between phases and while I've now landed somewhere between "good" and "really good" with it, don't let the comparatively "low" (pft) personal rating fool you. This is still an essential 1990s rock album for its influence alone.
