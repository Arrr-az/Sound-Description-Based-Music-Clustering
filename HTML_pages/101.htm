Bowie's second instalment in the Berlin trilogy is a criminally underrated masterpiece, brimming with invention and vigour.

'Low' was most certainly an amazing achievement and an absolute treat to listen to, but unlike most, I feel that "Heroes" tops it in every regard.

The first side (like 'Low', containing the more conventional songs) has more urgency than the tracks on 'Low'. There is more energy and more depth to the song writing. Where 'Low' was fragments/ideas with magnificent textural framing, Heroes' first side shows really intricate song writing being beautifully combined with dense and innovative arrangements that take the clunky synth and electronic sounds of 'Low' and fuse them into a more sophisticated whole.

The lyrics are also much more intriguing. 'Low' was lyrically and vocally quite sparse, but on "Heroes", Bowie delivers some of his most compelling lyrics. Just a couple of the fantastic snippets that pop up include:

'Your lips cut a smile on your face'

'There's slaughter in the air
Protest on the wind
Someone else inside me
Someone could get skinned'

'Sons of the silent age... Don't walk, they just glide in and out of life
They never die, they just go to sleep one day'

And of course we all know the gorgeous lyrics that the title track possesses. Not to mention Bowie's ultimate vocal performance on this track. In fact, the vocals on the "Heroes" album soar like on no other release of his. The songs climax and Bowie belts out some amazingly expressive and emotive performances. Overall, the first half of the record takes the best parts of 'Low's' first half and 'Station to Station' and turns it into the most successful and engaging group of tracks that he has ever released.

The second half of the album sees Bowie reprising the ambient themes explored on 'Low' but once again this album outdoes its predecessor. The instrumental tracks on "Heroes" are even more evocative than those on 'Low'. Bowie and Eno once again strip away the clunky synth and electronic sounds and create more focused and more atmospheric pieces second time around.

Bowie feels like more of a presence on this album overall. Less detached, and not hiding away from himself as much. This is further displayed by the re-introduction of his sax playing on a number of tracks. He adds personality and flair to 'Sons of the Silent Age', provides one of many gorgeous melodic layers on 'V-2 Schneider' (which trumps both 'The Speed of Life' and 'A New Career In A New Town'), and his free-jazz styled, heavily treated playing on 'Neukoln' is oddly appealing. Bowie also plays the Japanese Koto on 'Moss Garden' giving a beautifully subtle and sensitive performance on what is a perfect ambient piece.

If you own 'Low', love it, and are wondering which album to get next... well I can't recommend "Heroes" highly enough. If you own 'Station to Station' and love it... ditto. If you have some of Bowie's other albums from some of his other periods and are curious about which of the more experimental albums to start with, this is the one. It's one of those albums that is experimental without compromising quality song writing. It takes the ideas first generated on 'Low' and expands and improves upon them, while introducing a highly unique and infinitely appealing aesthetic all of its own. It is also more timeless than 'Low'. It's sounds are still fresh and haven't dated a bit in the 30 years since its release.

It is not only the best of the Bowie and Eno collaborations (The Berlin Trilogy), it is also, in my opinion, the greatest achievement by this most amazing artist.

Hi! How are ya? Since you're here, lets talk about the David Bowie album, "Heroes". In case you don't know, the album was released on October 14th, 1977 and is the second part of the Berlin Trilogy that Bowie recorded with producer Brian Eno. It saw Bowie continue to delve into a more ambient and texture-rich sound that helped revitalize his career, and was recorded at Honsa Tonstudio in what was then West Berlin. The main reason I want to revisit it is because it shares nearly the same album artwork as Bowie's forthcoming album, The Next Day.

The recording of this album was in a time where Bowie was very inspired to push himself and create a sound that was challenging and rewarding at the same time. The album starts off with more straightforward songs, but saying more straightforward on this album just means that they're the least "out there". The opener "Beauty and the Beast" is an absolute assault on the senses, with jumpy vocals, fun-house synth sounds and a collage of other instruments all merging into one freakin' fun song. The lead single "Heroes" follows a similar path, though with mellow and atmospheric backdrops that lets the song sail around while Bowie sings in a more refrained manner. "We can be heroes! Just for one day."

Lyrically, Bowie is on top form on this album, giving detailed and ridiculous imagery to match the equally unique musicianship that accompanies it. This was during a time where Bowie himself was coming out of a more depressed state of recording, so his songwriting here was beginning to contain more glimmers of hope and a more upbeat attitude. Going back to the opener "Beauty and the Beast" is a perfect example of the renewed sense of energy and fun, with Bowie playfully singing, "My-my! Smile at least! You can't say no to the Beauty and the Beast!" His sense of humor and fun is infectious in these kinds of songs, and you can't help but smile (well, at least I couldn't).

The album is a fun and upbeat listen for the first half of the album, with detailed and fleshed-out instrumentation to make everything that much more interesting. However, it's when we get to the second half of the album when we really start to get into experimental territory. From tracks 7 to 9 we hear very dark, moody and atmospheric ambient music that is entirely instrumental. While it may seem like such a dramatic shift in song structures would be jarring, Bowie pulls it off really well. It is in these tracks where we hear Brian Eno's influence the most.

The track "Sense of Doubt" is perhaps the best ambient track on here, with ominous and descending piano chords tumbling downwards while walls of wind and rain blow around and eery keyboards dance in the background. This is continued in the next track, "Moss Garden", which begins with the sound of an airplane flying overhead before everything disappears and we hear mostly clear and pristine string plucking as distorted and reverbed sounds envelope your ear drums. It's definitely the most mellow and (dare I say!) relaxing track on here that has an almost hypnotic quality to it.

Even though these ambient tracks are contained in the second half of the album, Bowie does not allow the album to end on such a melancholy note. The last track, "The Secrets of Arabia", is a return to the sounds heard in the beginning of the album, with a funky rhythm section and zany instrumentation and hand-claps that are all anchored down by quirky vocals and lyrics. Hearing Bowie sing "The SEEEEEEEECRET LIFE... of Arabia" never fails to be entertaining.

It may have come out 35 years ago, but this album hasn't aged a day. All of the instrumentation and experimentation contained here still feels fresh and unique, even though many others are utilizing similar sounds today. It is perfectly sequenced (which is saying something when you have a mixture of instrumental and vocal tracks) and never once loses its sense of ambition or depth. If there was ever a perfect place to start if you wanted to hear everything David Bowie has to offer, then this is it. "Heroes" simply has it all.

This stunning album is the second in Bowie's so-called Berlin Trilogy, arriving not long after the slightly overrated (yet groundbreaking) Low (1977) and before the underrated Lodger (1979). Of these three, it was in fact the only album that Bowie recorded entirely in West Berlin. "Heroes" continued Low's format of mostly short, fragmented rock songs on side one and spacey ambient music on side two. What is immediately noticeable is that the songs on side one of "Heroes" are considerably fuller-sounding than those that appeared on Low. They are practically bursting at the seams with details, virtuosic guitar solos from Robert Fripp, and hooks that often seem very strangely placed -- such as the awkward "yeahs" in Joe the Lion -- but somehow it all manages to work.

The lyrics are much stranger than those heard in Low. To try to figure out what they all mean can seem like a waste of time, but occasionally the lyrics create vivid, eerie images -- for example, Bowie tells us that the Sons of the Silent Age "never die, they just go to sleep one day." Bowie's awkwardness throughout side one of "Heroes" may be difficult or confusing for first-time listeners, but repeated listens reveal that it effectively contributes to the album's sense of urgency, excitement, and paranoia.

After the excellent Blackout, which features Bowie in perhaps his most gloriously deranged performance on a studio album, side two of "Heroes" starts off with the carefully structured instrumental V-2 Schneider. The three sprawling tracks that follow cover a variety of moods -- from the somber yet ultimately hopeful Sense of Doubt to the peaceful, contemplative Moss Garden to the nightmarish Neukoln. These ambient tracks do not sound dated like some of those that appeared on Low.

The Secret Life of Arabia then suddenly kicks in with a dramatic tonal shift. Some critics complain that "Heroes" did not end with Neukoln, but with its disco riff and odd yet catchy backing vocals, this surprise track is easily one of the best on the album. It emerges from the clearing the ambient tracks created and the listener feels transported to a distant world in the space of the album. Bowie recalls that he "was running at the speed of light," and at this point in the album such a thing does not seem impossible anymore.

"Heroes" is a landmark album that could very well be David Bowie's greatest achievement. Although Low ultimately proved more influential as it successfully explored the space between rock and ambient music, "Heroes" refined Low's innovations, resulting in a true masterpiece.

David Bowie-Heroes (1977)
I'm not well versed in Bowie. I know The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust, and I know his singles, but as far as his other albums go, I'm not the guy to go to. Heroes, however, makes me want to change that. I've never been a big fan of Robert Fripp, but Bowie was able to change that. His avant-garde, jazzy play works great for the experimental, synth pop-rock, genre bending experience that is Heroes. There's four instrumentals in a row, and while as individual songs they might be the low points of the album, as one piece (especially the last three) it may be the most emotional and moody part of the album. Parts of it sound like it could be in 2001: A Space Odyssey or an old silent film while other parts are much more ethereal. Unfortunately, they do have their dull moments in them and prevent this album from being quite on the same level as Ziggy. There's concept there for sure, and Heroes, Sons of the Silent Age, and Blackout, easily the three strongest songs on the album, really show it.

There's horns, synth, and jazz guitar, but all of it works. David Bowie can do so many different things and do them all so well. It doesn't take a genius to see the genius of this album, that's for sure. Bowie's vocals can be operatic while remain so painfully human, or they can be distorted to give a wall-of-sound characteristic behind Fripp's edgy but rooted riffs, hooks, and surreal solos. Still, the album is not without its dull moments, and the closing track doesn't seem too appropriate for me. I see the point of having one vocal side and one instrumental side, but since it isn't really one completely instrumental side, it seems more like poor pacing. The first side feels urgent, save for the emotional centerpiece and title track, while the second seems to try to recapture that emotion of Heroes over 2-3 times the length without an operatic, human vocal performance.

Rating: 3.5/5

BSFTA: Heroes, Sons of the Silent Age, Blackout.
