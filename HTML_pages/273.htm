Anne_Marie Dec 14 2010
+15 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Whenever I think of Gorillaz, the first thing I think of is this album cover. It's like a comic book line up of the characters you are about to watch/hear. It's also quite fun to imagine those same characters playing instruments, dancing on stage, singing... Which is, apparently, fairly close to what you probably saw if you actually had the pleasure of attending a Gorillaz concert. I can't honestly think of a more unique live band experience than what I was told this was like...

So, on to the music...

The "Intro" sort of sets the mood for the album, kind of weird and creepy and full of hip-hop style synths. Track 2, "Last Living Souls", has quite a unique mix of styles, combining heavy electronic beats and blips and acoustic guitar and keyboard solos with eclectic vocal changes that range from talk-singing to surprisingly beautiful deep vocal melody. And the amazing thing is that the flow between the styles is seamless.

"Kids With Guns" does pretty much the same, but has a sound that I usually associate with sampled music. For some reason I've never thought of that in relation to this band before now. I guess it's possible (and maybe likely?) that they sample their own music? This review is probably showing everyone just how little I know about these things... whatever. It's not for lack of interest... (PS - Hot Chip did a fun remix of this song, though it's about 7 mins long... for some reason I wasn't that bothered by that fact when listening to it however)

"O Green World" has that garage sounding vocal filter that singers love to use. And it just doesn't get old. Cool sound. This song might be just a tad too long though. Then "Dirty Harry" has that really recognizable chorus of kids singing the melody. It also has more rapping than the album has had up until this point. And even though it's a tad on the boring side as it progresses, it serves as a nice prequel to "Feel Good Inc.", which just totally owns this album. You can't walk away from that song without shouting "feel good!!" in a high pitched voice (either inside or outside your own head) for a good... 48 hours.

"El Manana" has a slight jazz thing going on and kind of slows down the beat, but it's still classy and extremely smooth. I've got Moby and Air on the mind when listening to this track. "Every Planet We Reach Is Dead" continues the smooth feel but adds some funk to it. But it's definitely a little on the long and slow side for me in this particular case. And "November Has Come" still has that sort of retro feel but it more minimal. It's got a blues-y melody. I'm not usually too much of a fan of that style, and it's not really a completely different story here...

Next comes "All Alone" which picks up the pace a bit, but still remains more minimal than the first part of the album was. This is also pretty much a straight up rap song with vocal samples in the background. This is the first track that also features girl vocals in a pretty little middle-of-the-song breakdown. Then there's "White Light" which starts off reminding me a good bit of Ten Benson. And in usual Gorillaz fashion, it has a classical musical interlude between the first and second halves. I'm a pretty big fan of it, and coming in at just over 2 mins, it's certainly no chore to listen to.

Now we've got "Dare." I'm pretty sure that I forget all the time that Gorillaz did this song. It's one of those songs that has managed to infiltrate life in general, like a lot of radio singles tend to, or at least used to. I'd know these random songs really well but have no idea how or where I would have heard them enough times to practically sing along considering I never listen to the radio. Well, this is like that... but the awesome version of that. Because this song is awesome. That girl vocal melody, man. It sticks with ya.

"Fire Coming Out of the Monkey's Head" is an incredible title for a song. You can't not get a mental image of exactly what the song describes when you read the title. Want to hear a guy telling a strange story over beats and humming followed by a middle aged folk guitar player singing a lonely tune on his front porch at sunset? Then you've come to the right place. And "Don't Get Lost in Heaven" is like a choir's response to the story told in the previous song.

Next, the title track "Demon Days" starts with a psychedelic slow build. It reminds me of the movie The Beach in one part for some reason. I think it may just be the Moby-ness of it. Add a church choir, a final Aesop Rock or Blackalicious style rap track with a reverbed melodic vocal chorus ("November Has Come") and you've just polished off this album! Congratulations!

Honestly, I think one of the best aspects of this band has to be the "bass" lines. The mix of vocals in here is also unusually diverse. Those two things, combined with the hip-hop beats make this album a great dance album, but also just a really fun pop-in. Hurray for Gorillaz!

Best tracks: "Last Living Souls", "Kids With Guns", "Feel Good Inc.", "White Light", "Dare"

Sounds like: Beck, Moby, Gnarls Barkley

#98 on NME's Top 100 Greatest Albums of the Decade

I love how people are reviewing this album as if it's a fine wine. Sure it is expiremental at heart, but come on! Stop trying to justify the big posters all over new york with the "FEATURED ON THE NEW iPOD COMMERCIAL!" Billboard top ten or not, corporate cum hasn't tasted this good since "One More Time" was playing every time I went to Wings n' More.

Damon Alburn is a bit of a mystery to me. Mogwai hate him, the music video for "The Universal" made me hate him (how could it possibly not!?), but I love Think Tank to death and thought the Gorillaz debut was very interesting concept and overall pretty decent. The idea of having a hit band and nobody having a clue who is in it ("Whoa, you mean they're aren't 4 people?", "DUDE I KNOW THERE IS A JAPANESE GIRL IN THE BAND!!!...I...I SAW HER!"), and even once you know who is in it (Damon and Danger Mouse) you still don't really know who is responsible of what. But really who cares (except snobs, nanny nanny boo boo) when you got such great cartoon characters to pose in magazines instead.

So yeah cool concept or not, it's pretty 2002, ya? The music certainly isn't. I don't think it's the whole "dark" theme that makes this album feel more complete, but that there is a theme at all which the debut lacked(you know the whole, "Is this a punk record or regge record or a Day of the Dead soundtrack sampler?") From the warm simplicity of "Last Living Souls" to the ethereal "Demon Days", it's hell of a ride and never feels afraid to walk out of the "party record" label from time to time.

Even after playing "Dirty Harry" 101 times as a bad vinyl bootleg months before release, I still can't get enough of it's throbbing bass and children choir. The thing that makes Demon Days such an exceptional record is that even though there are choirs, string sections, and sudden rap takeovers, it always fits into the context of a nice, tight pop song.

As you can see from other reviews posted, everyone has a different favorite and worst song. Many times I found my favorites ("White Light, "Dirty Harry") to be people's least favorites, and my least favorites ("El Manana", "Every Planet We Reach is Dead") to be other's gems. Overflowing with personality and variety, there is something for everyone on this disc. When you think about it, will there ever be a Roots Manuva, MF Doom, or De La Soul song played at a sorority party again?

I think I still have this album, but I am not really sure about that. I am assuming that it is somewhere in my house and I just can't find it at this moment. Anyways, Gorillaz is a band that is quite different from your typical four man group because they are cartoons or animated if you must. I really didn't know what to expect with this band sand to be perfectly honest, I would expect them to be sort of an electronic type of band and I was kind of right. The cover of Demon Days is a nice comparison to an album that most of you should know.

What can really be said about Gorillaz that has to really be put out there? The magic behind Gorillaz is that of Damon Albarn of Blur and by some comic book artist by the name of Jamie Hewlett. So when you put it into a much clearer light, there are about three members in the band. Of course the members of the band that everyone seems to know is that of 2D, Murdoc, Noodle and Russel. How they perform live is kind of confusing to be honest and I am still baffled by how they do it. Pretty sure they use some sort of projection technique.

Going back to where I started, the music is something that would very easily catch your attention. It is somewhere along the whole tip hop genre with a little bit of everything else. The vocals are mellow and the guitar riffs are rather catchy to say the least, with most of them getting stuck in your head. Of course having a member of Blur makes this band sort of have an alternative rock edge and there are even a couple of hip hop influence in this album. I didn't really fall entirely in love with this album because of the fact that it is a little too long for my taste, fifteen tracks, and the fact that I didn't really feel like experimenting with a whole lot of different genres at the time.

This album caught me by surprise and if you are into the trip hop genre or just want something refreshing and different, I would not be too hesitant for you to pick up Demon Days.

Mediocre and Lethargic
Well, here we are with another Fantano "Classics Week" album. I must say of the records that The Needle Drop rated for 2024's "Classics Week", this is probably my least favorite and most confused listen. Gorillaz's Demon Days (2005) combines the aesthetics of Alternative Rock, Hip-Hop, and Art Pop in a generally disjointed way with little to offer in terms of interest or emotional agency. This record doesn't do anything for me.

At this record's absolute highest points ("Feel Good Inc.", "Dirty Harry", and "November Has Come") there are a few memorable instrumental passages, motifs, and in "November's" case, a good feature from MF DOOM.

Outside of those few moments, there isn't much to talk about.

Demon Days and Gorillaz as a whole would be better off remaining in their fictional world.
More
Standout Lyrics- Nothing in this record stood out to me

Favorite Tracks- Feel Good Inc., November Has Come, Dirty Harry

Rating- 4/10 Bad Album
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Room for Improvement-

This record's main issue is how uninteresting and lethargic it is. Damon Albarn's vocals are so boring and he sounds completely unengaged with the music he is making from start to finish. Paired with the uninspired and similarly sleepy instrumentals, this album has no emotional potency or agency whatsoever.

Most of the features feel completely out of place leading to this record's major identity crisis. Is this a Hip-Hop album? Is this an Alternative Rock Album? Is this an Electronic album? I don't know. Combining genres is a good move when you can make them fit together but on Demon Days it does not work at all.
