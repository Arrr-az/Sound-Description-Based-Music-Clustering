Perfection...
Perfection. This album yielded two brilliant 'hit' singles, the classic 'Babylon's Burning' and the lesser known 'Something That I Said'. The white-reggae classic 'Jah War' was also later released as a single.

This album is perfectly recorded and blends brilliant musicianship with fantastic lyrics and lead vocals. Some of the tracks are long (by standards of the time) and other tracks are perfect 2 minute 'punk' classics. This album is an absolute must for any serious music enthusiast.

The Ruts groupe de punk anglais formé en 1977 et qui s'est arrêter tragiquement avec la mort par Overdose du chanteur Malcolm Owen. Avec The Clash, c'est un des groupes qui amenés les influences Jamaican Music dans le punk. Plus que de jouer du Ska ou Reggae façon Punk Rock, ils ont intégré ces rythmiques au Punk Rock. J'ai l'impression que si aujourd'hui c'est un groupe un peu oublié à cause de sa courte existence, mais qui a eu une influence assez importante. Henry Rollins site d'ailleurs cet album comme un référence et a même été le chanteur du groupe en en juillet 2007 pour un concert de soutient à Paul Fox le guitariste du groupe atteint d'un cancer qui malheureusement décadra quelques mois plus tard . Après le décès de Malcolm Owen. le groupe continuera sous le nom Ruts DC un peu dans les années 80 et se reformera 2007 et continue de tourner. J'ai vu Ruts DC en 2016 lors d'un fest Mass Productions et c'était vraiment la grande classe.

Politically activated mash up of rock, punk and reggae and dub driven on by the adept guitar work of Paul Fox (surely he should have been a bona fide rock star)
Has its moments of terrificness but falls away into the routine near the end with Out of Order and Human Punk.
Bonuses, " Give Youth a Chance," another nice reggae tune. I do like when they go down this route, it makes a nice blend with the punk and never sounds contrived, not like when some acts go reggae and the only response is "Stop Stop Stop, Pleeeees Stop!"
"I ain't Sofisticated," shouty punk.
The Crack, is really the band playing about with a little reggae, rock n' roll, a little nice guitar, Tony Bennett and fifties Doo Wop back to reggae and the odd sheep noise. The goons running noises for anyone who can remember. Just background throwaway fun.

bedroomAOR Jan 09 2015
Vote downBookmarkVote up
VR956: Gotta Get Out of It Out of It Out of It...
I'm supposed to like this album because if I don't, Henry Rollins will hit me. That's really the gist of my desire to love eternally the sounds of The Ruts. The Ruts were one of about sixteen thousand punk rock bands that hit their local scene, achieved minor success in a single and eventual LP, then dissolved due to a personal tragedy. In their case, lead (and only) vocalist Malcolm Owens was found dead of a heroin overdose in the year following this album's release, so it is fairly understandable that anything subsequent merchandise of The Ruts has been limited to archival footage, live recordings, and really lengthy interviews about the legacy they've miraculously built for themselves from the combined efforts of "In a Rut", and The Crack. I'm obviously here to discuss the latter, but I also really don't want to, lest I be brutally advised on how I should be taking their music. I shit you not, I'd expect that fist to come catapulting towards my nose and eyes if he should ever read what I have to say about them OR The Ramones OR Dead Kennedys OR... oh god, he hasn't seen my rating for Let Love In yet, has he?

As is painstakingly obvious at this point, I am not a fan of The Ruts. It's not that they're bad songwriters, that they are sonically-displeasing or disgraceful, nor is it anything to do with them being hacks that stole their best punk riffs from the godfathers of the age. It has nothing to do with anything of the sort, so... why do I dislike The Crack? Why can't I wrap my mind around an album that's so straightforwardly punk rock? It probably has to do with the fact that I've never been able to easily attach myself to classic punk, the very few CDs I've come to adore being wholehearted efforts. I like Wire. I like Sex Pistols. I have a sort of liking with The Pogues. When push comes to shove, I can put punk rock on and have an okay time, but... it just wasn't my upbringing to have music like The Ruts as an aid to any feeling I may endure. It definitely wasn't that I evaded anger in my youth, because boy, there was plenty of that. I guess it's just that I don't get the release from punk rock that nearly all of its veterans do when it is played. Catharsis from what Wikipedia calls 'hard-edged music, typically with short songs, stripped-down instrumentation, and often political, anti-establishment lyrics'? I was only doing two things when I was sixteen: breaking shit to Strapping Young Lad, or mopping ceaselessly to The Smiths. Neither really got me anywhere, so maybe I could have learned a thing or two from The Ruts. Alas...

It's not a hopeless spin, on those nights when I do want to try and avoid the inevitable future assault that this entry will grant me. "Something That I Said" is a great single, the kind of song that energizes me and makes me want to kick life in the head as so eloquently recommended. And like... I totally feel how I could delve into The Crack if I was armed with nothing but it on my player, a shot of strong scotch, and my thoughts. Don't get me wrong: I can have a sober affair with The Ruts that won't go badly at all, but I'm not really too compelled to do so. The Ruts just aren't my bag, nor do they handle the stitching in the manner I'd want it touched. The Crack is a perfectly fine record for people who love ordinary punk rock, and I'm sure this album has been the soundtrack to so many who have kicked their share of asses. I just hope that one of those asses doesn't end up being mine, really.
