Suicide is a band that can never be as exciting to listen to as they are to talk about. Alan Vega on stage swinging his motor chain, while Martin Rev pelts out his steam-punk, organ crunch--its exciting shit. I mean, I feel cool as fuck when this shit comes on while I'm waiting for Sonic Youth to set up for a live gig. As alienating and scary as Suicide is, this is music best heard in a communal setting. You just want to force yourself into the guy in front of you and duck for safety when Vega tosses a bottle into the audience. As an album, not an experience, it is solely lacking with its limited spectrum and imagination. Its more like a horror film than anything, you want to talk about the gruesome death scenes ("Frankie Teardrop"), the awesome opening ("Ghost Rider"), but its all just cheap thrills that are never as good as you remember them being. File this under: "So I've been getting into some new stuff"

Before I've compared In The Court of the Crimson King to Black Christmas and Pinkerton to Ferris Bueller's Day Off and now I get to do another one in my series of comparing albums to films. This is to music what Man Bites Dog is to film, and the best part is that I heard this for the first time the day after I saw that film.

See, Man Bites Dog is a bit of a weird movie. IMDB classifies it as a comedy-crime-drama but I've also seen it classified as a horror film. It's about a documentary crew following a serial killer around and how they get dragged into assisting him, but that's less what it's ABOUT and more just an occasional issue which comes up. Anyway, the main point is that it's a film which both seems to be doing nothing in particular (most of the time it's a series of vignettes rather than an actual plot with a start to finish) and also one which seems almost anachronistic for the time it came out, but which got overtaken rather quickly. It came out in 1992, and it's very brutally violent, and some moments of it still have the shock value they must have had, but it was clearly released right on the point where ultraviolence in films could be the norm rather than a cult classic - this was the same year than Tarantino blew off the doors with Reservoir Dogs, after all. Its only really uncomfortable and shocking scene, without wanting to spoil anything, is only THAT shocking if you haven't seen Irreversible already. And the gore loses a lot for being filmed on a budget in black and white. So essentially my review of it is that it's a revolutionary film that was way ahead of its time, and it certainly has its moments now, but for the most part it's better as a history lesson than taken on its own terms.

And I think that every one of those points can be taken over to this album. I'm slightly in shock that this came out in 1977, but at the same time it does sound like an album which desperately wants to be shocking but just doesn't have the framework for it yet. Occasionally it completely nails it (the opener and Girl are the best on offer here) but the rest of it ranges from fairly inoffensive (which feels like the biggest insult possible to this sort of band - their album art features razor blade wounds for crying out loud) to really annoying - Frankie Teardrop has some fairly innovative riffs but nothing to justify being that insanely long or that many obnoxious screams.

Jeez, after Youth of America I thought experimental punk music was totally my thing, but it turns out the experimental punk has to also be good. At least it's only 32 minutes long so it doesn't stick around long enough to be a complete disaster. Hey, something else it shares with Man Bites Dog!

Suicide were the most original of the New York punk bands, as they replaced the guitars and drums with completely artificial sounds. Their songs appear like rock music from a different dimension where the guitar was not invented. The pseudo-rockabilly opener tells the story of the comic book hero Ghost Rider.

Alan Vega is a sensual and neurotic singer, always on the verge of intense yelling and creepy whispers. The keys by Martin Rev supplement this, with repetitive, quasi industrial instrumentals that sound more like Throbbing Gristle than Kraftwerk. The track Johnny sounds like an Elvis parody.

The crazy centerpiece of course is Frankie Teartrop, that takes up almost a third of the LP. It tells the downfall of a factory worker who, in a fit of madness, murders his wife and kid, finally himself. The song then tracks his decent into hell. The music is very minimal and Alan Vega's screams are horrifying.

One of the most interesting and amazing albums from the punk genre.

7.5/10

I can't praise this album enough. Truly one of the most original and clever musical works of all time. Most people don't get it on first listen, but once you get it, it reveals itself to be odd but beautiful. On the surface, the lyrics seem to be senseless, but in reality they are unthinkably profound abstract poems that comment on the condition of the human race and the Earth in these times, and combined with the frenetic and powerful music make up an awesome whole that assaults the senses. The torturous screaming makes it even more terrifying. If you don't like this album, then you don't understand what music is all about.

+17 Vote downBookmarkVote up

I'm often asked why it is I enjoy the music I do. I often describe it with adjectives along the lines of "abrasive," "haunting," "unnerving," and "strange," and people (not around here, but in the meatspace, which I only visit when I absolutely have to) ask why I don't just listen to normal things. You should've seen that look my mom gave me the other day, when I told her that I was a Tom Waits fan and there was nothing she could do about it, which may not have been my exact words. It was as though I told her I was going to destroy the sun by nuking it.

Suicide would be a great example of this, I'm sure. Granted, they aren't the kind of band that comes up in conversation all that often (unless you're dealing with huge synth punk aficionados, of course), but if I said "I like Suicide," the first thing people'd assume is that I was making some sort of grotesque joke - even for my rather dark sense of humor, that would be a little too far. Then they'd ask me what Suicide sounded like, probably assuming that they were depressing and therefore bad, a correlation which I hate, by the way. And when I tell them that I find them more disturbing than a fully loaded clown car, they'd ask me why I listen to them, and why I'd rank this pretty high on any given list of my favorite albums. After all, who listens to music to be disturbed, right?

Well, I'll tell ya who. I do. If i interests me, I will listen to it despite whatever emotions it evokes, and oh lord does this interest me. It freakin' FASCINATES, in fact. I mean, it worries and unnerves me, but it's great! If you're the sort who refuses to listen to all music that isn't nonstop fun-filled good times just on principle... well, you're just as closed-minded as people who refuse to listen to all music that is nonstop fun-filled good times just on principle, and both sides are really missing out on a lot, but the point is that you will despise this album. But me, I've had some pretty traumatic experiences with nonstop fun-filled good times sort of albums (and, before you jump on me, overindulgent self-righteous "masterpieces" - I do have Leftoverture at half a star, don't forget), and so I don't at all mind that this album is cold, dark, hostile, and paranoid. In fact, it's a rare song that conveys these qualities better than "Ghost Rider," unless of course it's "Rocket USA" or "Johnny." I mean, those are the freakin' triumvirate of cold, dark, hostile, and paranoid. They're like Marc Antony, Caesar Augustus, and M. Lepidus, except for "Rocket USA" (my least favorite of the three) is certainly NOT useless. Unlike M. Lepidus. I mean, the fuck did that guy even do? "Ghost Rider" could use some more Nicolas Cage, but that's about all the complaints I have about it.

So I haven't actually told you how this band sounds, have I? Besides throwing out some adjectives? Well, what do you want me to do, write GOOD reviews? I. Think. Not. At this point, the camera should be absurdly close to my face, only for it to zoom out again because nobody wants to get that up close and personal with my nostrils. So here's what it sounds like. One guy, a fine gentleman by the name of Martin Rev, plays keyboards. He's pretty badass, because he always picks the most perfect, most creepy as shit keyboard tones you can imagine and just rolls with them. Another guy, one Alan Vega, sings. Alan Vega might not quite be as cool as Vince or Vic Vega, but that's okay, because few of us are. Besides, he's a phenomenal vocalist. He's the one who REALLY makes things creepy, not so much singing as yelping, mumbling, screeching, pleading, moaning sexually, and screaming in agony. "Girl" and "Frankie Teardrop" basically amount to a fifteen minute (give or take, all right?) vocal tour de force of fun and profit for all who find the deeply unnerving sexual neuroses of "Girl" and the violent depiction of insanity and homicide painted on "Frankie Teardrop" to be fun and profitable. I understand this sort of limits my options here, but work with me, all right? The fact that he goes from this to the most twee bondage freak in the world with "Cheree." And don't you give me that "only twee bondage freak" bullshit. I'll have you know that Stuart Murdoch wrote a song about someone who was into S&M and bible studies. So there.

So "Che" isn't all that great, admittedly. Enjoyable enough, but a small step down, especially after "Frankie Teardrop" gets done bein' all "Frankie Teardrop" and doin' all "Frankie Teardrop"-type stuff to your head and all. But it works. Just not as well as the rest. You might not like this one at first - I was bored stiff by it - but keep at it. Just think of Suicide as Young Marble Giants' evil twin, and you'll be fine.

Track picks: "Ghost Rider," "Cheree," "Johnny," "Girl," "Frankie Teardrop."

 TheCunningStunt Nov 14 2011  
+7 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Ahead of its time, Suicide is one of the most unique albums ever made. There's no other album quite like it. The most influential pioneers of electronic music will always be Kraftwerk, nothing comes close to them in terms of pushing boundaries, innovativeness and influence. But I'd argue that Suicide come closer than most. To come close to Kraftwerk's influence, you have to be incredible - and Suicide were just that. Influence aside, one thing I can say with certainty is that they were incredibly unique. They capture an atmosphere no other album has managed to capture. It's one of a kind.

The memorable keyboard lines, the thumping drum machine with the minimalistic synth is such a simplistic formula, but it works wonderfully. When your sound is so minimalistic, if the songwriting isn't up to scratch it'd be incredibly obvious. Drum machine, synthesizers, keyboards and vocals. That's all there is to it. It just goes to show you what two guys with a whole lot of passion and love for what they do can achieve.

Even though the whole tone of the album and all of the songs have a harsher punkier and industrial feel to them, at its core a lot of the songs are Synth Pop. 'Cheree', 'Johnny' and 'Girl' are all so brilliantly catchy, yet the dark edge to them just gives it an extra dimension. Those three songs are probably my favourite thing about the album, but the most memorable and speaking objective, the best moment is most definitely the infamous 'Frankie Teardrop'. It's probably the scariest, most intense and unsettling song I've ever listened to. I remember the first time I heard it, I wasn't aware of who Suicide was and I wasn't THAT interested in music when I first listened to it. When I first heard it, I didn't know whether I liked it or not. I don't know whether it's because I heard it as a stand alone song, or whether listening to it a few months later in the context of an album helped - but the first time, it unnerved me. Yet I was still incredibly interested in it. I remember listening to it again a while later, and I was talking to this girl online, she was on webcam so I sent her the song to listen to. It was at night, and I just saw her scream a few times and she looked even more unnerved than I did. Even though it's incredibly dark, the story is so interesting and compelling. The storytelling techniques are fantastic, it tells the story of Frankie who has a wife and kids, works in a factory, but he loses his mind and kills them both and then himself. One of the most 'holy fuck' songs I've ever listened to. Disturbing in ways I can't fully articulate, it makes my worst nightmares audible.

Suicide paints the picture of crowded streets, yet with someone so oppressed and isolated in the middle of it. It's one of the most dark, intense, creepy, bleak and unsettling album I've ever listened to.
