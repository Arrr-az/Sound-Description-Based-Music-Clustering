"If there's been a fool around, then it's got to be me"

One of the weirdest gigs I ever attended was seeing Fleetwood Mac play Sheffield Arena back in 2009. While it was an entertaining enough show, there was a general undercurrent that resulted in an odd vibe. Christine McVie had departed the band for a solo career some years prior, Stevie Nicks, all slow hand-movements and ethereal drama, was evidently road-weary and had little notion of what country she was in, never mind which city. To Nicks (far) left stood Lindsey Buckingham, who despite being the bands primary focus instrumentally, gave the impression that he was phoning it in, and it was evident that he had lost all interest in being a member of the band some time ago, with the only reason he was there being that his solo career just wasnt paying the bills. Behind them stood the inscrutable John McVie, a man who even at the height of Fleetwood Macs commercial zenith, always looked like hed much rather be a member of Chas & Daves backing band. The only member of the band visibly enjoying themselves was Mick Fleetwood, whose infectious enthusiasm bounced from the back of the stage to the far side of the packed-out cavernous arena. Despite the odd vibe, you couldnt deny that the band played well and the majority of the audience were utterly enraptured by their performance of material which leaned heavily on albums like Tango in the Night, their eponymous 1975 album and, of course, Rumours. By the end of the night, the four primary members of the band had played a good gig, reminding everyone what they could achieve when they put aside their instincts to pull in different direction and worked collaboratively in the name of mutual benefit.

Years later, the thing that stood out for me about that gig, was its parallels with the scenario that Rumours was created. Its well known that the individual self-interest of the five band members was temporarily left at the door of the recording booth, regardless of their desire to tear each other limb from limb. The trials and tribulations that the band members experienced immediately prior to, and during, the creation of the album are well recorded, and the details of who said, and / or did, what to who have been the subject of multiple documentaries of varying quality. What doesnt vary in quality are the eleven songs that make up Rumours. Sure, to some, Fleetwood Macs Rumours will never be anything more than the last word in bland, corporate, white-powder-coated corporate rock. For a few years this was certainly the accepted wisdom, particularly among my generation, however in the last twenty years, it has been reassessed due to the re-activation of Fleetwood Mac as a going concern. Forty years after its release, youre hard pressed to argue that Rumours isnt one of the most potent albums to be released during the American AOR boom of the late 70s, with its evergreen songs being a testament to the bands three primary songwriters and their namesake rhythm section.

Rumours is one of those albums where every one of its individual tracks could potentially have been a single. Nicks and Buckingham were at the top of their games, but as ever, it was Christine McVie who brought the very best material to the table. McVies consistency as a songwriter is one of the more overlooked aspects of Fleetwood Macs career, whereas a standout Buckingham or Nicks song is regularly hailed as a masterpiece. The downside of McVies consistency was that she was so good so frequently during Fleetwood Macs commercial peak, that very few of her songs stand out ahead of her others, leading her to be relatively undervalued compared to her colleagues.

Kicking off with the relentlessly upbeat Second Hand News, you cant argue with Rumours being a masterclass in songwriting. McVies Songbird and You Make Loving Fun are typically top-draw material, while Nicks Dreams and Gold Dust Woman allows her to do her scarf-wafting ethereal hippy-chick thing to solid effect, and Buckinghams Go Your Own Way gives him the opportunity to take a few immature potshots at Nicks following the disintegration of their relationship. Its always struck me that while Rumours was an exercise in Fleetwood Mac airing their dirty laundry, it was only Buckingham who took things a little too far when it came to settling emotional scores, and as a result, he comes across as the least classy member of the band by some significant distance. The flipside of this though is McVies Dont Stop, where she contrasts Buckinghams petty accusation throwing with a message of optimism and hope for the future. This results in Dont Stop being a genuinely great song with an irresistible chorus and an anthemic bounce, and as such is one of the high-points of Fleetwood Macs lengthy career.

The fulcrum of Rumours is The Chain, the only song on Rumours for which every member of the band receives a writers credit, and a tune instantly recognisable to fans of Radcliffe and Maconies 6 Music show and Formula One. A hymn to sticking together through thick and thin, its co-credit is fitting, and its changes in pace give it the feeling of a show-stopping epic. Of course, its use as sporting theme music and a radio jingle has caused it to be so familiar that its impact is lessened slightly, put you cant deny that its one of the most thrilling moments on an album not short on outstanding moments.

Perhaps one of the reasons that Rumours spent so long being undervalued is its familiarity. Its one of those albums that most music fans have heard at least once in their life, and the fact that it has become musical shorthand for some of the worst cultural excesses of the late 70s has meant that it perhaps doesnt always receive the detailed listen that it deserves. Approaching Rumours without prejudice reveals it to be a fine rock album, with powder-dry, crystal clear production, packed full of great drama-filled tunes by three writers at the top of their games, and a band whose very existence depended on the quality of the material youre listening to.

Yes, Rumours has been picked apart by too many folks more interested in the fact that the individual band members were having personally turbulent times, rather than the music itself. Yes, it was too commercially successful for its own good and at least half the songs on here have been overplayed down the decades. Yes, half the time you wonder why Buckingham and Nicks werent just banished from the studio by the other three members of the band until they had matured into sensible and emotionally responsible grown-ups. Thing is, despite, or maybe because of, all this, Rumours is an undeniably brilliant album and taken just as a collection of songs, it remains little short of breath taking and unarguably Fleetwood Macs definitive album.

"You don't know what it means to win"

Actually, cherryeater, the guy on the cover is Mick Fleetwood, not Lindsey Buckingham.

Man. I love this album. Thing is, though, I love the erratic wonder of Tusk more. But still, this album cannot be denied.
Rumors is as ever-present to the late '70s as bell bottoms jeans, honky 'fros (like the one Lindsey Buckingham sports on the back cover), pukka shells, dark sunglasses and cocaine nuggets stuffed in your nostril. This album is the sound of bleary, post-breakup glare of the morning after and the day before you pack up your little puke green Gremlin and ride off into the Los Angeles sunset, recriminations and illicit substances swirling in your head.

Stevie Nicks, goddess of rock, keeper of dreams, stirs the cauldron. She's more matter-of-factly than most people give her credit for. She knows you have to fly even if she wants you to stay in Sly Stone's bed all day with her, but she doesn't wanna get 'tween you and love, and who is she to keep you down anyway? Her crystalline wisdom is part of what makes her so attractive (being supernaturally hot is also helpful). Her advise is the kind you only get somewhere down the road, not immediately, but just when you need to know: Thunder only happens when it's raining, which is bullshit, but somehow true anyway, because she deems it so. Rulers make bad lovers, you'd better put your kingdom up for sale. Take a little time. Listen carefully to the sound of your loneliness. Stevie Nicks understands, but she wants to make sure you know the consequences of your desires, of your lifestyle. Her songs are loaded with warnings to heed her wisdom, to listen, to keep vigilant. And it's not ego when she says in 'Dreams' and the once-lost 'Silver Springs' that she promises that you'll go crazy without her, that you'll never get away from her no matter how long or how far. No, it's not ego; it's the knowing. She knows you up n' down, in and out. She knows what you love. And she knows the street value of what you have is nothing on what you lost. When the rain washes you clean, you'll get it it. And you'll "get" her. But never "have" her again.

Lindsey Buckingham uncovers the old trails of Brian Wilson, strings it up, and finds no peace in the freedom he's granted by Ms. Rock Goddess. 'Course, we all know what happens if you mess with the divine: purgatory, wander around this place forever, the sound of her voice haunting him, even when another woman takes him in ('Never Going Back Again'), even when he finds a moment's rest in tall grass to "do his stuff" ('Second Hand News'). In anger, he contradicts himself by giving her an emotional middle finger ("you can go your own way, you can call it another lonely day") while at the same time showing devotion ("if I could, baby I'd give you my world"), asking her to open up, all the while he's throwing accusations at her in his desperate, wounded
fury ("packing up, shacking up's all you wanna do"). Get it together, L.B.! You know, Stevie once accused Lindsey of "sleeping with his guitar", his involvement in his music and crafting himself into a true Guitar God, that he neglected her. 'Go Your Own Way''s guitar solo has him getting intimate with his instrument once again, his heart stinging, his eyes teary, his guitar and carnal need all that's left to him. If Stevie travels, unmoved, by thought and emotion, then Lindsey travels by foot, guitar in hand, under turbulent skies, looking for what he can't even name. Looking for a place that he can't hear her voice. Good luck, pal.

Christine imbues her songs with an elegant punch on the arm, telling you to cheer up and enjoy life. Her songwriting peers, Guitar God and Rock Goddess, snarl, bite, sigh, moan and just generally wrestle with what they feel. Not Christine. She's not a Goddess of air or fire, she's just our earth mother. In the pantheon of Rock Chicks, Stevie's always been the one you want to dance on fire with, the one to create magic with. The Rock Girlfriend of all time. Chris, meanwhile, comes off as your best female friend, the jokes you have with her are bawdy, the drinks are less bubbly, more gut punching. She has the time and patience to take care of you, which Stevie, bless her, could never do. Her night flights are all hers. So you hang with the down-to-earth songbird, long for the intangible gypsy always. Christine tells you, don't stop, tomorrow's almost here, she wishes you all the love in the world. While Stevie and Lindsey dissect promises or kick them to the curb, Ms. McVie protects them, even if she knows most of 'em come up nil in the long run. She dumps John McVie, just like Stevie kicked Lindsey out of her heaven, but only Christine would console and give hope to John where there is little or none. You think Stevie or Lindsey would do the same to each other? Shit no.

And so the soap opera goes. We all hate TV soap operas with their melodramatic overly complex relationships and characters far too self-destructive for their own good. But man, we've been buying up the same from Fleetwood Mac for over 30 years now in MASSIVE quantities. We need it, we can't look away from their strife. It's just too beautiful. Play it the way you feel it.

Pop it in and realize I've never heard the oddly joyous "Second Hand News", and I'd better savor the feeling because it'll become familiar after a handful of plays. I willed myself through the rest of the album - doesn't matter if you've heard "The Chain" in every Ross Dress For Less I've ever visited, you gotta put on the headphones.

Impeccably constructed: that's the first thing I thought. Whoever recorded the guitars did yeoman's work: always clean and ringing - we're ages from blues-rock. Mick Fleetwood holds everything down ably, quietly putting emphasis on various segments through architectural drumming. You probably know the chunes on here, and I don't have much to add about specific songs, but did you know that if you listen carefully, you can hear Lindsay whisper "fuck" before "The Chain"? In the silences you can hear someone making chka-chk noises... it's a neat example of the obsessive concentration that makes Rumours for me. No matter how bleak the subject matter, FM cuts the bloat and grimly gets to the base of things.

To come up with a score, I toted up all the great tracks on here: "Second Hand News," "Dreams," "Don't Stop," "Go Your Own Way," "The Chain". Half the album is great, half is listenable. I'll stick with 3.5; perhaps after a lifetime of these songs presenting themselves to me at the whim of jockeys and muzak programmers, a little active deployment is what these songs need to shine.
