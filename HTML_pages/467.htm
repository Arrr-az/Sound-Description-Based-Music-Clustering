If anything, this album has taught me never to make assumptions about music, or for that matter, how I listen to it. You see, there were two big assumptions I had about this album that prevented me from enjoying this as much as I do now, and though I could very well harbor similar assumptions in the future, this album functions as a case study of why I shouldn't ever have preconceived notions about music.

Assumption 1: Pointing the finger of blame at lazy journalists and poserish "connoisseurs" of post-punk, I automatically assumed that Wire would sound exactly like Gang Of Four, as the two bands are frequently mentioned in the same breath, if not the same sentence. Now, I love Gang Of Four. Entertainment! is a brilliant album, perfectly blending scathingly political lyrics and irresistibly danceable funky music. And usually when bands are tied together like that, they do sound similar to one another. So when I picked up Pink Flag, I naturally expected more of that. But what I got were frenetic little minute-long guitar bursts with indiscernible lyrics, interspersed with slower songs, some sinister and some jangly. Needless to say, my reaction was rather like, "What the hell?" The brevity of the songs was jarring, even though I love bands like Guided By Voices and Minutemen who have similarly short songs. So it all seemed like kind of a blur to me at first, and though I didn't initially dislike it, I just thought it was going to be one of those records that I respected more than I loved.

Assumption 2: I thought this album would never really grow on me. I thought I'd take up the polemic that Pink Flag is infinitely inferior to Entertainment! since I have a friend on here who has the exact opposite opinion, and sometimes I just like stirring things up. But mostly, I thought that after three years of serious music listening, my tastes had become clearly defined enough that if an album didn't really click with me in the first few listens, it never would, and the grower albums I used to have all the time would be a thing of the past. Ah! It didn't take me long to realize the extreme navet I had just exhibited. Some of the songs on the album had begun to creep into my head and stay up there for hours, particularly "Ex Lion Tamer" and its "Stay glued to your TV seeeeeet!" chorus hook. Since I'm admittedly a sucker for anything catchy, I began to listen to it more and more, making sure to drink it in carefully so I didn't miss anything. And even though Wire were post-punk in the sense that they were innovative and diverse in their music, one thing people overlook about them is that they're damn fun, and even a little bit (dare I say it?) Ramones-y. That is to say, you could easily pogo to "12 X U" or "Mr. Suit" with their shouty, bratty lyrics and speedy guitar lines, and even the album's sole instrumental, "The Commercial," reminds me uncannily of "Now I Wanna Sniff Some Glue" in its breakdown, so sometimes I expect that familiar old shout of "1-2-3-4-5-6-7-8!" to come in, ha ha. (Although Wire's idea of fun is a little more brainyI read in the brilliant book Rip It Up And Start Again by Simon Reynolds that "106 Beats That" came from an effort to write a hundred-syllable song, but it wound up being 106...and here I thought that title was some obscure gambling reference!) But then they completely divert the mood with midtempo, slightly sinister tracks like the title track or "Reuters," or melancholy-tinged janglers like "Fragile" and "Mannequin," the latter of which has always been my favorite on this album with its reverberated guitar and "oooh-whooo" backing vocals. In general, I really like it when albums throw me for a loop like that, since I hate homogeneity in sound.

I think there's still a lot for me to explore in this albumsuch as the lyrics, which I still have a tough time understanding with the speed and the thick accentsbut overall, this has taught me that nobody, not even I, can judge how exactly I'll react to a certain album. And that's fine with me.

Update: I've finally come to realize something that was staring me in the face a few months ago and which I should've noticed long before: this is a bona fide 5 star. Awesome, awesome stuff.

Pink Flag conserva hasta la fecha una actualidad sorprendente tomando en cuenta el ao de su aparicin. Es ms, a mi humilde entender practicamente solo son algunas brevsimas y enrgicas descargas ramoneanas las que ligan a los bisoos Wire con su poca y su entorno punk-rock, ya que mucho del material en este disco podra pasar por cosas que aparecieron despus, ya sea en los 80 y 90 e incluso hoy. Sin duda esto ltimo habla a las claras de la influencia que el sonido del grupo ha tenido en artistas de lo ms diversos, pero ni eso ni alguna otra cuestin ligada a la trascendencia son lo ms relevante de Pink Flag: lo mejor de todo es su msica, simple, directa y vital, pero a la vez bien pensada y que resiste lecturas diversas. Si tengo que elegir un solo disco del '77 (ao seminal e histrico en el rock), que sea este y nada ms.

Pink Flag is not necessarily the first Post-Punk album, not a lot on here really sounds quite different enough from normal Punk to be so. But, at the same time? It's the first album to put a foot down that road, an abrupt left turn from the ordinary punk avenue. And you know what I mean people, don't get it twisted. We're talking pure strain British Post-Punk here, that chilly metallic other world shit that would upend worlds of rock on the British isles and long term change and produce far more than the MUCH more famous vanilla Punk. So no, take your Pere Ubu's, Televisions, and Talking Heads and file them some place else, because they weren't the first at this. And take your first English Post-Punk claims too and humble them, your Banshees, PIL's and Magazines. Because Wire beat them all to the punch. Pink Flag really is one of those albums where the bend of musical history is apparent in the work itself and not just if you clip it next to another album is the evolutionary sequence. For all the praise I have for Wire of course I can't deny that there was something inevitable about this record no matter how you cut it, the combining of artiness with Punk. It's why you gotta come to us historians to get the scoop folks, we see all the big patterns and shit. We know the cycles. For rock I've never tired at pointing out that the evolution of Punk into Post-Punk is a process that already happened once before, and it shouldn't really be a secret since it concerns probably the most popular and lauded period of rock of all. The 1967 centered High Sixties, where British Invasion charm turned into psychedelia and hippies. Without a fail a bunch of mop topped nice boys started growing out their hair and wearing insane colors. And the music pretty much did the same. It was experiment across the board. My point is, simplicity doesn't usually last long before artists get antsy and want to expand on it. And no Punk on earth isn't an artist (good or bad artist being a different discussion), rare is the creative person who won't want to just keep on trying new things. The road from Ramones to The Pop Group was something that was always going to take place. Even so though Wire does stand out a tad, because these guys weren't really....ever Punks. They were art school boys from day one, and their take on Punk here is art minded in the same way. You could even call it deconstructionist punk or some shit. A cursory listen reveals...a weird punk album but a punk album nonetheless, challenging even. The easygoing thrashing of The Clash and Pistols and all of them sounds like this...yet doesn't. For starters there's the incredibly almost parody lengthed songs, the best of which is actually the shortest, 28 second long Field Day For Sundays. These are taking Punk to it's logical extreme (foreshadowing Hardcore over in America), but taking it seriously at the same time. It's like the members of Wire were all fascinated by this Punk thing, taken in by it, and wanted to dissect it and stretch it and see how many ways they could still make it work. It's Punk science experimentation, and that too is audible. The first gen Brit Punks and their albums are mostly red hot affairs of guitar fire, but Pink Flag meanwhile has a cold and clinical backdrop to it's Punk energy, which actually makes it really COOL. I know "cold and clinical" is almost always a negative description but not here. Taking Punk into colder environments is a major part of English Post-Punk and even American Post-Hardcore down the line (talking to you Fugazi and Math Rock). And yet, while stranger melodies, super strange song lengthes, cold construction and so forth make this a wild listen when you focus, it also largely works as a helluva fun Punk listen. The guitar sound is damn near heavenly, the buzzsaw of the Ramones taken to workmanlike perfection and exercised over what are often pretty catchy and anthemic shout outs. While sure stuff like Reuters points down Post-Punk lane, stuff like Mannequin can sit alongside the best vanilla punk anthems of 77' comfortably. And that's what makes Wire so great, ever the scientists, and ever the guys trying to give you a good Punk time. Fundamentally they respect what they are co-opting, which makes all the difference.

Rating: 4.5
Highlights: Reuters, Lowdown, Pink Flag, Mannequin
