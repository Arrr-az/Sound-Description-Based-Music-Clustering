The Ramones are such an important rock band because they sum up better than most just how blazingly UNIMPORTANT rock music is. So much writing about rock music attempts to pump up the stuff with all manner of overblown vitality, but it's just junk food. It's cookies and candy. Most of the best of it is made by young people who have nothing unique to say about the human condition yet. A lot of it (even the good stuff) might even make you dumber. Embrace that, I say. I do.

I put on a Ramones record and I want to smash windows to the frivolity. I bop my head around to the primal simplicity. I'm ripping up my jeans and fantasizing about starting my own band. I think and act like a fool.

But I recognize that. So it's okay.

Recognize that in yourself, too, and you'll be okay. At least by me. (Admittedly, that's not worth much.)

So, this album is nothing but great punk rock songs, little explosions of melody and buzzsaw guitar. I can sing along with it all. It's four New York City creeps playing blunt and minimalist rock tunes about being New York City creeps. From start to finish, it never stops slashing at you. It leaves you with your head in your own lap. It's fourteen songs that the band would rewrite over and over again throughout their career. But that's okay. It's just rock music.

Favorite song: the pounding "53rd and 3rd", Dee Dee Ramone's sordid little sketch drawn from his own days as a male prostitute.

Number of tracks: 14. Running time: 28:52. Recording time: 17 days. Total cost: $6,400. Endless hours of mindless enjoyment and life-affirming redemption: priceless.

Released at a time when most kids in my senior class were getting their college, military or auto plant applications in order, the first Ramones album – never mind their very existence – left many of them reared on the grand notion of The Epic Twiddly Guitar Solo, Motown, dumbed-down, lowest-common-denominator blues rock, and mini prog epics based on Tolkien, the legend of King Arthur, or Mussorgsky, slack jawed and very confused. Familiarity with proto-punk touchstones like The Stooges, MC5, New York Dolls, Dictators, and a legion of 60’s garage rodents immortalized on countless 7-inch singles on countless fly-by-night indie labels may not have been mandatory for “getting” The Ramones, but it sure as hell didn’t hurt.

And while it’s become de rigueur to lament rock and roll of the mid-70’s as overblown, detached, and elitist, even by those who weren’t even born when it all went down, it really wasn’t as dire as some may lead you to believe. Unless you were too lazy to overturn a stone or two (alright, maybe a few more), there was plenty of great music to be found. Just don’t ask me to name any of it. OK, howzabout Mott the Hoople, Sparks, Kiss, Roxy Music, Sweet, Aerosmith, Slade, Blue Oyster Cult, Be Bop Deluxe, The Kinks, The Rolling Stones, Bruce Springsteen, The Sensational Alex Harvey Band, The Faces, David Bowie, and Skyhooks fer starters?

Although “Ramones’” lyric sheet often reads like something out of a stroke survivors’ language recovery therapy group, it’s nice not to have to wade through tried-and-true chaff like wizards, the cosmos, lovin’ your baby, and the lonely life of a touring musician just to get to the meaty bits. Opening salvo “Blitzkrieg Bop” just may be the greatest first song on ANY album, the band absolutely nailing their attempt to write a chant song ala the Bay City Rollers’ “Saturday Night.” “Hey ho, let’s go” – marching orders for an army of America’s disaffected youth.

Whether the band was making a conscious effort to veer away from lyrical content trampled underfoot by those who came before them or simply writing about what they knew hardly matters. The songs, all of them credited to the gang er, band collectively, are masterful manipulations of monotony, melody, and momentum, mantra-like for the most part, making their mark through a lean, buzzing fusion of bubblegum and well, I’m not sure. I’d say punk, if not for the fact that punk didn’t yet exist, at least not in name. The wall of noise here is in part thanks to Craig Leon and drummer Tommy’s production decision to funnel guitar and drums into one channel and bass and vocals into another.

Despite their unsure footing as musos in both the studio and on stage, The Ramones certainly seemed to know what they DID want (to be your boyfriend, sniff some glue, your love, the world) and DIDN’T (to go down to the basement, to walk around with you) and how to articulate it in two-minute bursts of pent-up, downstroked fury. There was no hedging or beating around the bush with these guys. They got in, got out, and changed some lives along the way, offering up child care tips (“Beat on the Brat”), lamenting a lost love (“Chain Saw”), and talking shop (“Havana Affair” and “53rd & 3rd”). The closest they come to a ballad is “I Wanna Be Your Boyfriend” which really isn’t much different from the rest of track list here, just played at a slightly slower tilt. And they start a grand Ramones tradition of great covers with Chris Montez’s “Let’s Dance” from 1962.

There are eight bonus tracks wedged into this expanded edition from Rhino, including three demos ("I Don't Care," "Now I Wanna Sniff Some Glue," and "You Should Never Have Opened That Door") previously unavailable anywhere, two only available on "All The Stuff (And More), Volume One," two previously available on a Norton vinyl single, and the single version of "Blitzkrieg Bop." The overall sound of the original album is cleaned up a little, but is still raw enough to satisfy purists and should be required listening in junior high schools nation-wide.

One - Two - Three - Four!
Ramones project part 1: Review of album 1.
In the past week I've listened again to the first four Ramones albums in sequential order; all classics, all as good as they've ever sounded (and in the case of Road to Ruin even better than I remembered). But this one's the one for me. Best songs, best flow, best humor, best riffs. Not to take away from the others, each of which is a terrific experience and each of which has its own evidence of incremental development, but here I sing along out loud almost every time to almost every tune, an experience that I'm sure is amusing as fuck to the people I'm riding past (unsafely) with my Walkman on shouting out "I'm a Nazi schatzy/gonna fight for the Fatherland" or "53rd & 3rd/trying to turn a trick" or "Now I wanna sniff some glue" or whatever. I'm amused myself and I can't even hear me, except sometimes on the pretty ballad, "I Wanna Be Your Boyfriend." The A-side is flawless; B-side dips only slightly for "Havana Affair," but the overall flow is so good that I forgive it completely and sometimes even enjoy it (and sing along) too. A nearly perfect listening experience. Bonus tracks on all CD issues of this spoil the effect. Do not play them. Put them on a mix tape and listen to that separately.

You know, the older I get, the more terrifying and dystopic I find the Ramones' vision. And I know some might balk at hearing the Ramones referred to in those terms, but these guys did indeed have a vision, one that did them in because, I mean, they got too attached to it and it frankly isn't a very interesting or sustainable one. According to the Ramones' Gospel, music peaked in the pre-psychedelic '60s, and any effort to build anything after that constitutes a betrayal of what music is "about." Because see, that's the right way to do it. Any point you can't make in two minutes, 35 seconds or under wasn't worth making in the first place, any clever lyric or arrangement just gets in the way of the rock. And I don't know. I just don't know.

Yes, there was something to be said for this at the time. If you want to argue that prog and stadium rock had their excesses, I would agree with you wholeheartedly. A lot of fascinating, forward-thinking music sprang out of the punk scene, and I'm sure plenty of that was conceived as a reaction against Led Zeppelin or the Eagles or Kansas or whomever. I don't even mean that as a slight against any of those bands, but a lot of good art has came into this world because someone wanted to shake things up. Where the Ramones lose me, where punk in general loses me, is when this withdrawal into the past is taken as a fight for rock music's soul, which is treated as a fight for all of music's soul. Look how many artists the Ramones have claimed kinship with - the Beatles and Beach Boys are the two that spring to mind first - got so much fucking better when they decided to transcend the confines of "two minutes, simple arrangement, straightforward lyrics" and really go in for a more expressionistic style. In a certain respect, the Ramones are a product of the '70s wave of Eisenhower/Kennedy-era nostalgia, just as surely as Grease or American Graffiti. Remember that guy in the latter movie who said rock had gone downhill since Buddy Holly died? That's kind of how I see the Ramones, and I object to that sort of totemized nostalgia on principle.

Still, if there's one thin I learned from the Ramones, it's that you can't get too crusader-y when it comes to music. It's true that I don't take this band's limited palette as a sign of their elevated musical righteousness, but they knew how to make good music with it. Granted, it's a lot to take in one sitting - twenty-eight minutes can feel rather long when a band has so few tricks - but the band's goofy sense of humor and knack for a good hook really brings this album together. I can't say there's much I don't like here, except maybe the cover of "Let's Dance." They set b-movie charm to a kickass riff on "I Don't Wanna Go Down to the Basement," work some dynamic shifts into "Blitzkrieg Bop," give the '50s ballad format a fun new sheen on "I Wanna Be Your Boyfriend," and even make fun of their own simplicity on "Judy is a Punk." It's fun stuff, it really is.

Still, it's nothing to build your life's philosophy around or anything like that. You can see this band's limitations on the second side of this album, where they take the "one line repeated over and over" approach that worked so well with "I Don't Wanna Go Down to the Basement" and recast it as the less memorable "I Don't Wanna Walk Around with You" (note that it's still got a cool "whoa-oa-oa." Only the tough "53rd and 3rd" and maybe the Cold War-themed "Havana Affair" (reading Graham Greene? Our Man in Havana + The End of the Affair?) really gets to the level of side one's highlights. Johnny's guitar tone is perfectly suited for the material, Joey sounds just ridiculous enough to pull it off, the rhythm section is driving and doesn't get in the way. Pretty good to dust off a few times a year, but nothing to build your manifesto on.

The Ramones only have one song, but it's the best song you'll ever hear. All 14 tracks are stylistically the same. Quick, catchy, fast paced, power chord orientated, similar in length, similar in structure. Stripped down Punk at its very best, and Ramones effectively kickstarted Punk. Every single one of their albums sounds the same, as mentioned, but this contains their best collection of songs.

It came along at a time where something needed to happen, Progressive Rock definitely peaked in the mid 70s. There was still plenty of things that were considered great coming out of Progressive Rock, don't get me wrong, but I think its best days were gone and something more stripped down, hard edged, powerful and relatable needed to come along. I think, ultimately, music needed something completely different to what was available at that time. I'm not the biggest Progressive Rock fan, even a lot of the 'big' albums I don't really like so I can't even begin to imagine what it would've been like when Emerson Lake & Palmer were putting out their bland, uninteresting fetid garbage. Something had to give, and Punk Rock was that something and Ramones was one of the very first Punk Rock albums.

Lyrically and musically, it's about as simple as you're going to get. They can't really play, they know that, you know that, but they really don't care. They embody the punk and attitude. When I think of Punk Rock, I think of The Clash and The Ramones. But I imagine it was a breath of fresh air, having something different from Prog symphonies about wizards and dragons. Something a new generation of people could relate to and love. I think they just wrote about what they knew and could relate to, one of the main things I love about the Ramones is their lyrics. They're the least set of overwrought lyrics you'll ever listen to, it feels like they didn't take long at all to come up with them. I could say that on an album I've given 0.5 to, and mean it as an insult. Like "urgh, did they write these lyrics in the studio as they were recording or something?", but the fact of the matter is - they were good, despite them feeling like they took a matter of minutes to come up with.

I think the Ramones are the finest example of how technical skill really doesn't mean a whole lot. I've mentioned it in other views how technical skill means very little to me, and I feel like a broken record saying it, but I guess it's something you have to talk about. If being a gifted musician is what appeals to you, then I can completely understand why. Being able to play instruments brilliantly definitely is definitely a wonderful thing to listen to, as long as it's void of self-indulgence and I do like listening to stuff where the musicianship is brilliant, as long as it's done right, but when it comes down to it... Being able to play/technical ability vs being able to write a brilliant song, I know which one comes out on top.

The explosions of distorted guitar, the melodies and brilliant hooks are timeless despite how minimalistic they are. So catchy, energetic, free, raw, fun and infectious. It pretty much represents and embodies everything rock music should be.
