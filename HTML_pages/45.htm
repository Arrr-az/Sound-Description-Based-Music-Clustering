Ill Communication is like the little brother of Check Your Head, the group didn't really change much this time around and they just released a pretty similar album to the last one. The album is also held together with funky instrumentals and even some hardcore punk songs (going back to their roots) but once again everything just flows well in a very hip hop way. The production is very 94'ish (hey it's 1994 afterall!) but it's hard to really to compare it to any other group cause both of the early 90's albums just have their own unique sound, they really add their special touch into these beats. While most mid 90's producer where switching for simple effective loops, the Beasties takes the 1994 sound but keep their progressive approach that they were known for in the Paul's Boutique days. For example take Root Down, there's just so many things happening in that song as the samples keep switching back and forth. They had the guts to "rap" over guitars for their first single, but they are just a group that could take this sort of risk because of their mostly white "alternative" established fanbase. The Beasties never lack in originality and even if "Ill Communication" has the same formula as "Check Your Head", there just ain't no other album out there that sounds close to this. The only problem with this one is that it starts to drag a little near the end, but still it's another essential album to have just like all their previous albums.

Overall: 76 - 78 3.85/5 76 - 78%: Solid; few major reservations; TRY IT

While their previous album was more revolutionary, this album is probably my favorite to listen to. In fact, considering how anti-Check Your Head I am it's kind of funny how much more I like Ill Communication. Maybe it's just as simple as the group opening this album with one of their best tracks ever, not to mention one of the coolest loops I've ever heard. But the back and forth nature of the album, jumping forward to Clash-like genre experiments and backward to the stuff that the Beasties made a name for themselves with, just feels way more natural here. No longer do the funk jams act as a sleeping aid (in fact, "Sabrosa" is my favorite Beastie Boys song) and no longer do the tracks feel ramshackley thrown together. There's a distinct flow to Ill Communication that is maybe lent by the slightly extended runtime; in other words, no idea is allowed to simply fizzle out or get by on mere appearances. Well, "Tough Guy" I suppose is the exception that proves the rule. A whole album of that sort of stuff and we might have had a problem. It's still a better punk song than we got on Check Your Head, though.

Honestly, I suppose I'm envious of those that can hold the Beasties two black and white releases in the same regard, but for me this one is clearly better than the other. I mean, it's a risky proposition in the first place and in my opinion not a surprise it took the group two tries to get it right. But with this one they just got it so much more right. The funkiness of "Root Down" is worth the price of admission alone, let alone "Sure Shot", "Flute Loop", "Sabrosa", "Get It Together" or "Sabotage". Ill Communication ups the stakes on everything from Check Your Head and replaces most of their punk roots with the funky stylings of Mario Caldato, Jr., which is greatly welcome to these ears. It just sounds much more comfortable. Where some fans are rightfully disappointed the Boys finally treaded water for a release cycle, I'm just happy as hell they went back to the drawing board determined to get such a cool sound right.

The Beasties are very dear to me and their positivity (not displayed on Licensed to Ill haha) really helped me through tough times probably from the time I first heard them knowingly (I'm sure I heard them before but I was an oblivious kid with my radio in 98) on their single 'Alive'. I actually don't remember which Beastie Boys album I bought first because that was the days before RYM and the memory gets fuzzy when it comes to orders but I'm pretty sure it was Ill Communication off the strength of my love for Sabotage and Sure Shot. I'll admit I didn't get everything right away but the songs I liked left a mark and increasingly as I scooped up Hello Nasty and the Sounds of Science collection and shortly (but not shortly enough) after Paul's Boutique and Check You Head (of which I didn't like that much right away.. egad), the songs that were at first 'weird' to me came to my consciousness as bangers. Like B Boys Makin With the Freak Freak. I remember turning down the whole 'dick in the mashed potato' vocal sample because I seemed to think my parents were prudish as my friends' parents were. They didn't let me buy Crazy Town's record because it had a parental advisory sticker on it.... so I got the clean version. I always turned down the volume of records when they said 'fuck'. I had to surpass the end of a great Rage Against the Machine as you can guess. I would also make edited versions on my mixtapes where I'd press stop and then play very quickly to make a gap, which would annoy me to no end these days. Anyway, my parents wouldn't have cared either way haha. What a tool I was. Back to the Beasties. Some of the genre hopping they did on their 90s records is amazing to me. But I will admit it took me a little while as a kidnager (thanks, Mike!) and teenager to get into tracks like Eugene's Lament and Transitions off this one and the dub on Hello Nasty, but it all came together.

This is why I took it kind of personally when a friend of mine said in a bar in Vancouver about Sabotage; 'nothing like a li'l Beastie Boys right guys? right?' in a very dry, sarcastic way. Though as much as I liked the Beaties I can see how people wouldn't like them, but it's questionable to deny their prowess. On the mic two of the three of them have whiny voices to the ears of those who didn't grow up with them or did and didn't get into them. But then on the other hand they had the skills to pay the bills as producers and actual musicians making organic jams and punk songs and that through a lot of their career. Ill Communication is usually looked upon as the lesser effort when put side by side with Check Your Head, at least critically. But I've seen a lot of fans who think this is the better of the two and I'd agree. Maybe because I bought it first, but also I like the song selection and sequencing more. Hello Nasty is just as good of a musical stew and they did add even more flavours to that bouillabaisse but this one has the most "ka-nowledge" thanks to MCA's two solo tracks. The spirituality he had came in spades, though he did have a joint that pre-cursed these years by five on Paul's Boutique in the form of A Year and A Day in the B Boy Bouillabaisse, the epic slab of hip hop and hilarity on the tail end of that record. Would've been so cool to be a teenager in 89 listening to that for the first time with the rest of the world.

Sure Shot is one of the most perfect hip hop tracks ever and the production couldn't be more perfect on a single in my eyes. The well versed structure that Beasties would play with is used at it's best right here and the lyrics are funny and thoughtful. The most effective for me was Yauch's "I wanna say something that's long overdue / the disrespect of women has got to be through / to all the mothers and sisters and wives and friends / i want to offer my love and respect to the end" is a good apology for the misogyny and a new sage Beasties without losing any of the edge. And if you needed further proof you could cop the Time for Livin' / early beastie Boys channeling Heart Attack Man and Tough Guy, both Mike D fronted fast punk workouts. Root Down channels the old school with funky results and one of my favourites remains Futterman's Rule, where Yauch just SHREDS on bass, maybe my favourite instrumentals of the B Boys. And they have LOTS of great ones, for real. Ill Communication, with the rest of the 89-99 Beasties material still prevails as great, diverse music and still stands the test of time in my collection through middle school, high and all these years beyond (of which there are too many for someone who hasn't gone to school yet!) with a few other select artists. Really, if I knew then what I knew now I would have made different music purchases but these Beasties, Chilis, 311 and Matt Good records still live on with all my new favourites and new-found wisdom in music.

Such a great period. Drenched in raw energy - punk fused with jazz-funk and hip hop and experimental rap on top of that - the Beasties create game-changing big beats for our at the club as well as stoner lounge I will call it.

Ill Communication - Beastie Boys
The Beastie Boys entrance into the 90s was a weird one. Check Your Head was an odd release that saw the trio go across the music spectrum. From Jazz to Neo-Psych to East Coast rap to even Post-Hardcore and Alternative rock. It was a wild and eclectic experience. While I did enjoy the album overall, it was a significant step down in comparison to the legendary first releases of Paul's Boutique and Licensed to Ill. But with Ill Communication the boys step up their game. Fleshing out and combining the many sounds, styles, and influences into a more digestible and more focused release.

Ill Communication is pretty damn great, much more up to snuff compared to Check Your Head. Featuring fun and bouncy hip-hop, agro and energetic punk, psychedelic jazz/funk/rock fusions, and sometimes combinations of the 3. The trio indulges in all of these styles and creates some genuinely awesome songs in the process. Sabotage is almost universally considered to be one of the Beastie Boys best songs, and for good reason. Fantastic rapping, killer riffs, and just so fucking fun, its one of the best rap rock songs ever. Then right after it you got Get It Together, which is an excellent east coast boom-bap song featuring the one and only Q-Tip. Its insane and insanely underrated. Then after that is Sabrosa, a really solid fusion of psychedelia, jazz, and funk. Then after that is The Update which is some solid jazz rap, and then you got Futtermans Rule which is just excellent rock music. I could go on and on, but you get the point. While some of these songs are definitely weaker than others, its still not as consistent and great as some of their masterpieces. Its still an incredibly solid and eclectic piece of music.

8.1/10
