"Greetings well met fellow hail!"

Songs From the Wood - vinyl - 1977

It isn't hyperbole to claim that Songs From the Wood is the album that effectively saved Jethro Tull at the time when so many of their contemporaries were being swept aside by the onslaught of punk. While the likes of Emerson, Lake & Palmer and Yes arguably had more musical virtuosos among their ranks, Tull were arguably a more adaptable act, having already transitioned from blues rock to "small p" progressive rock act with a string of legitimate pop hits, to hard rock to "big P" Progressive Rock, albeit originally by way of a parody concept album, to a theatrical art rock act who really really wanted their material to translate to a stage or film musical. Wherever band leader Ian Anderson's muse led, Tull would morph into whatever kind of band he needed at the time, so it's not a huge surprise that when Anderson decided that Tull should go full on folk rock, the band simply adapted their sound as punk raged around them, picking up a sizeable second wave fanbase as they did so, and launching in to Jethro Tull's much celebrated "folk trilogy".

There's just one issue for me. Songs From the Wood is not only my least favourite of their folk trilogy, but its my least favourite of all Jethro Tull's 70s albums...

As musically adept and adaptable as Jethro Tull were on Songs From the Wood, for me they were just a bit to twee. Their next two albums would prove beyond a show of a doubt that Jethro Tull could absolutely rock while still embracing folk influences closer than ever, but on Songs From the Wood Tull just seemed a bit too whimsical for me.

That said Songs From the Wood is still Jethro Tull in their imperious phase, with arguably their most musically brilliant line up, with long time collaborator Dee Palmer joining the band as a second keyboard player, with his portatitive pipe organ becoming an absolutely key part of the Jethro Tull sound. Regardless of their shifting commercial fortunes, Jethro Tull were a band on top from throughout the 70s, and Songs From the Wood is still the sound of a band firing on all cylinders, albeit with a slightly lighter tone than really suited them.


Songs From the Wood - CD - 2002

The rise of punk triggered a wide variety of reactions among more established acts. Led Zeppelin nodded in approval, and continued to be Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd channelled their own disenfranchised feelings into the bleak Animals. A few other acts underwent underwent various identity crises, with some attempting to go ‘pop’ with mixed success (Emerson, Lake & Palmer failed with the awful Love Beach before calling it a day, while Genesis went on to be dizzyingly successful), while Black Sabbath struggled with their own problems, and The Rolling Stones dabbled with disco. Perhaps the most radical reaction though was that by Jethro Tull. Having already signalled that they were already anticipating being overwhelmed by the next big youth movement on 1976’s oddly under-appreciated, Too Old to Rock 'n' Roll: Too Young to Die!, in February 1977, Ian Anderson and his bandmates released Songs From the Wood, which was basically the sound of Jethro Tull that the new popular culture that would make them look utterly irrelevant was inexorably rising, and yet somehow they still came to the conclusion that it was the ideal time to go morris dancing.

Okay, so maybe it’s not quite as bad as all that, and in retrospect it was the bravest move that any of the rock establishment made in reaction to the upsurge of punk. By increasing the long-present folk rock elements in their music, Jethro Tull set themselves swimming against the youth culture current, and even non fans had to ponder the audacity of their decision. Thing is, it worked. Sort of. Jethro Tull continued to thrive on their own terms, and Songs From the Wood would prove to be the first of a trilogy of folk rock albums which saw them gain a second wave of fans who were happy to embrace the ‘Hey Nonny Nonny’ness of it all.

In that sense, Songs From the Wood was a brave, career saving, move for Jethro Tull. Long time fans thrilled at the sound of Tull bolting their hard rock riffs and progressive tendencies to folk rock, and songwise, Ian Anderson seemed to be a writer reborn. Songs like the sweaty-riffed “Hunting Girl” and the glorious title track are career highlights, and on “The Whistler” Anderson demonstrated that he could make the re-tooled Tull sound quite commercial too. It’s also home to Tull’s last bonafide hit single too, with the seasonal “Ring Out Solstice Bells”.

As well received and loved as Songs From the Wood is, there’s also a negative side to it that doesn’t always get talked about. While “Ring Out Solstice Bells” was the band’s last hit, it’s also one of the few bits of Top of the Pops footage of Tull that gets trotted out in retrospectives of the 70s, along with a frankly cringe worthy performance of “The Witches Promise”, neither of which have done much to combat the tired opinion of the tastemakers that Tull were anything more than wild-haired twee folk-rockers. Actually, one of the main things that holds Songs From the Wood back from being a truly great album is that it suffers from being just a bit more twee than is strictly necessary. Songs like “Velvet Green” and the epic “Pibroch” make Tull sound like a pale shadow of their former selves, which given that “Pibroch” features some distinctly heavy metal guitar wailing from Martin Barre, is a real shame.

That said, musically, Tull were at a peak in the late 70s. The wonderfully fluid bass work of John Glascock had kicked the rest of Tull up a gear in terms of musical performance, and his rhythmic partnership with drummer Barriemore Barlow was the finest that Tull ever had in their long career. Of course, Ian Anderson remained one of the more unique and compelling frontmen of his generation, and Martin Barre was always one of rock’s most versatile guitar players, and combined with the addition of long time collaborator and orchestral arranger David Palmer as a full time member of the band on keyboard and organ, alongside long-standing piano and organ player, John Evans, it all resulted in Jethro Tull had a much more full and rounded sound than they had previously, and as such Songs From the Wood is one of their lushest sounding albums.

While Songs From the Wood may fall short of greatness due to a tendency to lean a little too heavily on twee folksiness here and there, no one could argue that it isn’t one of Jethro Tull’s key albums, simply because of when it happened. By going against the grain of popular culture at the time, they may not have dodged the bullet of punk, but they were no more than grazed by it, and they probably would not have survived without embracing folk sounds a little more closely than they had previously. It was a brave move that could have destroyed their career if it hadn’t been done well, as it was, Anderson and his bandmates were one of the few acts of the era with enough talent for them to pull it off without it making them look utterly foolish.

Songs From the Wood [40th Anniversary Edition: The Country Set] - CD / DVD - 2017

Long held up as the very best of all the deluxe box set reissues of Jethro Tull albums, Songs From The Wood also had the highest asking price on the second hand market once it went out of print, and for reasons that no one seems to understand, there was a shorter print run for this set than there had been for previous sets (possibly because sales of the previous, Too Old to Rock 'n' Roll: Too Young to Die! [The TV Special Edition], were below expectations?). Whatever the case the relative rarity of the deluxe box of Songs From The Wood pushed prices on the second hand market to eye-watering levels, and I for one had long resigned myself to never being able to get my hands on a copy.

Good job then to those that have made the call to ensure that it has recently been reprinted and sold at its original price point of under £30 - take that money-grabbing speculative investors!

Okay, so scarcity aside, is this version of Songs From The Wood worth the hype? Well the additional CDs including a live performance is a generous bonus, and the fact they'd managed to piece together enough filmed footage from the same concert(s) means that (I believe at least) this was the first of this series of re-releases which featured performance footage of what was ostensibly a full concert on one of the DVDs. I have no doubt that some judicious splicing in of footage from other concerts on the tour occurred, but even with its ageing video artefacts, the less than high resolution footage and some now very out-of-date humour from Ian Anderson, this is a tour-de-force musical performance from the most musically adept Jethro Tull line up.

Okay, so what of the original album? For all my misgivings about how twee it could be, Songs From The Wood has always been one of the best sounding Tull albums, but even with that in mind, Steven Wilson has done an amazing job of bringing out what is easily the best sounding version of Songs From The Wood that I have ever heard. Wilson's mix has exactly the right amount of separation between the individual instruments, giving them each room to breathe, allowing it to sound huge, but at no point congested. As ever, it sounds amazing through headphones.

For the rest of the bonus material, it's all good stuff, but there's nothing here that I would take over what was included on the released album. There's interesting earlier incarnations of songs that would be later released on albums, a few demos and alternate versions of songs that did find their way on to Songs From The Wood. Yeah, pretty much what has become expected on this series of box sets, but that's not to damn it with feint praise, because yeah, in terms of content, there's any excellent case to be made that Songs From The Wood is the best of the series so far, and I can't really see anyway of them topping it going forward.

"She did wait until the pack had disappeared"

The perfect companion album to Heavy Horses (or vice versa). Never a genre purist, I’ve always been happy to call Tull folk/rock, but never moreso than here. In fact we get pure English folk and heavy metal, not just intertwined, but fused. Witness “Hunting Girl”, church-organ flourishes in one channel and jagged slashes of electric guitar in the other, this song rocks!

Ian has one of the hottest bands in rock, the arrangements consistently chockers with complexity and melody. This one could be labelled slightly more “proggy” than Heavy Horses, by virtue of the copious keyboards of many hues, organ, synth, piano, from both John Evan and David Palmer. The sumptuous “Velvet Green” is the most medieval track.

It’s fairly heavy for folk/rock, but I think that’s slightly to do with Ian’s swarthy singing; if they’d had some sweet lady folkie at the helm it would sound mellower. Anyway, a bunch of superb songs. The more I think about “Hunting Girl” the more perfectly plotted it seems. Another one for the psychotherapist.

The last truly superb Tull album. I saw Tull for the first time when this came out, and I have been a fan for life. Anyone who has seen concerts from this period will tell you, they just don't do concerts like that anymore (add Yes, Floyd, Zeppelin, Queen, Zappa, Neil Young, etc. to the equation, if you'd like). Nowadays, it's $10 for parking, $10 for a beer, and a 1.5 hour commercial. I'm surprised musicians don't have sponsor tattoos.

Anyway, back to the album. The musicianship is outstanding, particularly on 'Velvet Green', 'The Whistler' and 'Pibroch' (or Pee-break, for you Tull concert-goers). This album rocks and relaxes, sometimes in the same song. The lyrics are occasionaly very funny (read the lyrics of 'Hunting Girl' -- it has nothing to do with riding a horse, wink, wink, nudge, nudge), in other songs supremely ironic and full of regret (I mention 'Pibroch' again because of the lyrics, as well as the downright evil guitarwork of Martin Barre), and wistfully contented ('Fire at Midnight').

From an historic perspective, this will be the last time a lengthy composition from Tull doesn't come off labored and repetitive (which plagues longer tunes on 'Heavy Horses' and 'Stormwatch', although both are still very commendable albums). Both 'Velvet Green' at 6:04, and 'Pibroch' clocking in at 8:35, not only shine from a storytelling aspect, but musically the boys from Tull pull out all the stops, proving once again that the punk mantra of 'keep it simple, keep it short and spike your hair with snot and semen' is not necessarily what makes great rock music. Contrary to popular rock sentiment, I've always considered being able to play more than four chords as a mark of great musicianship. Ogres, like onions, have layers, and Tull is the onion in the rock salad -- many-layered, lyrically accomplished and able to shift from acoustic to electric instrumentation with the greatest alacrity, nuance and ability.

Tull gets back to nature and causes and Elizabethan storm.

If the track list Ian Anderson & Co. choose for their 1st real live album (yes I mean “Bursting Out”) could be seen as an indication of what these gentlemen favorite post “Passion Play” album was, then “Songs from the Wood” clearly won the gold medal: with 3 of its tracks included in their live set it is the best represented album from the 74/78 period.

Personally speaking I clearly remember I lost interest on my 1st favorite Progressive Rock band after “A Passion Play”; but with that youthful impetuosity now far away behind my back, when I go back to this album after so many years I have to admit Ian Anderson was the most clear-sighted leader of all the Prog bands in the early ‘70s, as he could perspicaciously foretell as early as 1974 that the movement his band was one of the exponents of, was clearly doomed by visions of glory, changing market tastes and let’s be fair, a lot of incompetence too; at the risk of losing a part of his fan base, he definitely won many more new adepts and kept the band’s standards of excellence at a level many would soon see as a mirage.

Without abandoning his fondness for complex and tricky songwriting, “Hunting Girl” and “Velvet Green” being a couple of fine examples of, he shunned the extended wanderings about opiate listeners carved for, or indulgent over playing and chops mastering show-offs, and instead concentrated on throwing into his cauldron of songwriting wizardry as many majority-recognizable elements as he could; these included Rock, Folk, popular sing-a-longs and Jigs, Baroque and other Classical motifs and even, oh the infamy, Pop melodies, well you name it; when I for the 1st time read Tull was considered a FolkRock group I couldn’t understand it; now I’d say it’s the immediacy of their music, and all those elements people recognize as making part of a nebulous traditional Culture, which for lack of a better definition, are behind that tag.

Moreover, even a circumstantial listening of this album will reveal why the sextet Tull consisted of at this time, is considered by many (Anderson included) as the Classical 70’s Tull line-up; although Barre, Evans and Barlow were already behind the making of the bands most groundbreaking and landmarking albums, it’s no doubt that John Glascock, versatile, competent and vices-free as he was, may have been the most suitable bottom end anchor man the band has ever had, whereas David Palmer academic musical knowledge furnished all the required embellishments, highlighters and assorted spicing a music as spanning as Tull’s was needed, or else mixing Hendrix like pyrotechnics , Renaissance chamber Music, Traditional acoustic Folk and Bach grandiloquence inside a number as “Pibroch” would have been an headache.

Songs From The Wood, which saw Ian Anderson donning the green mantle of some capricious, cavorting poacher cum Puck-like character, just sneaks it as my favourite Jethro Tull album. This is definitely personal preference rather than a critical choice. The band effortlessly slipped into the new guise as if they’d been searching for it from the beginning. It does become very Morris Dancers and Maypoles at times but mostly it manages to pay compliment to the genre without ever becoming too hokum.

The album couldn't be farther away from the prevailing musical trend of punk, thus ensuring it was immediately unfairly reviled by press and public alike. Just because a musical genre loses popularity and relevance doesn't indicate any loss of quality. Unfortunately, for albums like Songs From The Wood released during such times, they are not re-evaluated and appreciated until well after the event.

Musically, the band have rarely sounded better with a number of quite stunning interludes - "Songs From the Wood", "Pibroch (Cap In Hand)", "Hunting Girl", "Fires At Midnight" - but it's the whole concept that is admirable for its passion and ingenuity.

It's a great pity that Anderson's inspiration came at a time when the majority of people ignored it.

Lo primero que me llamó la atención fue su bonita portada, con Ian Anderson y una fogata en medio de un evocador bosque. "Songs from the Wood" nos ofrece sonidos muy interesantes (incluida música celta) y consiguen que te visualices en un frondoso bosque rodeado de brujas y duendes. En este campestre álbum de folk-rock hay momentos bastante apetecibles (The Whistler, Songs From the Wood, Velvet Green y Jack-in-the-Green), aunque no es oro todo lo que reluce y hay otras más flojas. El contenido extra de esta reedición/remix de Steven Wilson, no es muy interesante, pero incluye ocho canciones más como bonus, las mejores son Songs from the Wood (unedited master) y The Whistler (original 1977 US stereo single mix).

-Temas destacables: The Whistler, Songs From the Wood.
