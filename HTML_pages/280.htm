To tell the truth, I don't really have solidly graspable memories of what it felt to be alive during the early-mid '00sespecially not in a 'being part of an international socio-political context' way or even in a 'feeling as part of humanity' way,I just survived (and watched TV) back then. So everything I get of how the times felt arrived in retrospective, and mostly from what other people have told me, whether I do remember being told those things or not. The Iraq war was a thing, and some people were against it? I have the vague memory of seeing a banner crossing the whole street near the US embassy that was telling Bush to fuck off or something like that, but otherwise I just don't think I had much of an idea. The times were crazy and depressing? I don't know, we had worst things to worry about around here in Uruguay (and, in plain 2004, some things to be hopeful about as well).

Despite my very bad memory, I think I can say with confidence that American Idiot was the first international artistic 'event' of relevance that I actually sensed as happening in the now. It was probably my first taste of the idea of a counterculture (as dubious as it may look to call '00s pop punk a 'counterculture' as a grown adult in 2022). I noticed Green Day's image beginning to appear in magazine covers, and a friend of mine (we're talking about a 10 year old here) was quite a fan of the band. He would play the songs all the time, and he would share a couple of them with me. Matter of fact, I got my first MP3 player so that I could have a bunch of Green Day songs to play (mostly from American Idiot; those were the ones I liked the most).

Forward 5 to 7 years and I got to understand that it wasn't hip to like this album much (at least if you wanted to look even remotely serious). But I kept loving it a lot, even if in a sort of reserved way. And as I've grown more knowledgeable on the relevant facts I did get to understand its flaws (mainly the production, more of that in a sec) while also recognizing that, in spite of all that, this is a solid and brisky (even when clocking at 57 minutes!) power pop album that pretty much rocks the whole way through. The first three singles still sound electrifying today, and at most I would eliminate only two songs from the tracklist ("Extraordinary Girl" and "Wake Me Up When September Ends") in order to maintain it thoroughly entertaining.

Because this is the mid-'00s we are talking about, you should know the production sucks. The fact that the guitars still manage to sound energetic in spite of the loud lack of dynamics is a miracle. After giving a spin to this album with headphones or earphones you are forced to head to the audiologist as soon as possible, word. In retrospect, I'm kinda grateful for the fall of rock music, because this bullshit could not go on for too long or it would have become unbearable. And the guitars are of course not colorful. Their energy and personality come fully from the way they insist with their limited tricks, not because they sound good per se. Hell, it's not even the distorted guitars the ones that sound bad. The acoustic one on "Wake Me Up When September Ends" sounds thin as fuck, a sound that I honestly find kind of pathetic and worthlessly sentimental. The only guitar tone I like in the whole album? The beginning to "Homecoming". Pete Townshend-style shit!

The strength relies to a great deal on the singles. The title track is the most mid-'00s piece of music ever, an iconic rock song that genuinely sounds like it's struggling through the troubles of the world instead of getting depressed because of them. I find the chorus melody the more catchy the more time passes since its inception ('all across the aaaalien natio-ooon'), to the point it might be one of the two or three songs here that I listen to the most for fun nowadays. "Holiday" is even better, simultaneously an anthem and a parody of an anthem, or 'a plastic bag on a monument', as it prefers to put it. Chorus is another one that will live for long. My favorite moment is the end of the last one, how the melody resolves so satisfyingly, and how Billie bends the note down a little bit at the end, like the unexpected recognition that things can't be left hanging on such grace (reminds me of what Michael Stipe similarly does on the chorus R.E.M.'s "Carnival of Sorts", one of my favorite songs ever). And "Boulevard of Broken Dreams" might seem a little bit unsubstantive here and there (the harmonies in the chorus haven't aged well) but I really like how the solo breaks through the noise like lightning, and the piano touches are a very nice addition. Oh, and the ending is just a blast. It could have been better with more dynamic production, indeed, but even without that it still feels very blast-like, for it's not 'likeable', clean distortion, like the rest of the album. It's a black hole, swallowing it all.

"Jesus of Suburbia" too. "Jesus of Suburbia" rocks. Doesn't matter if Billie Joe 'stole' the melodies from like six different sources or whatever, it's a prime example of how to sustain one's attention through a whole nine-minute suite while keeping the heart beating and not evoking notes of sour pretension. Some parts of it I love: The triumphant entrance ('I'm the son of rage and love!'), Tre's drum solo by the end of the first section, Billie's demonic scream at 4:25, the Shins-esque bells on the "Dearly Beloved" section, the energetic take on "Ring of Fire" that the final section puts forward, etc.

The last two tracks are very, very solid as well. "Homecoming" is another five-part suite, a bit more one-note than "Jesus" but equally as charming ('after ten cups of coffee I am thinking!'). Serves well to illustrate the final 180 of the opera's protagonist, who still comes off as a little bit of a non-entity even though you do cheer for him most of the time. The very funny screaming during the last thirty seconds of and the subsequent "Nobody Likes You" reprise closing it all is cool, self-aware shit. As for "Whatshername", it acts as a sort of appropriate melancholic comedown, and it's always been one of my favorite cuts here. Billie Joe does manage for once to write something poignant ('I made a point to burn all of the photographs').

Something also very worthy of certain respect (and helps with the flow) is what the band got Spotify to do with the album, to not separate the tracks as much as possible. Consider that "Holiday" and "Boulevard of Broken Dreams" were both massive hits. Spotify daring not to grant them each their tracks? Crazy! But it does play much better if there's no cut at all between those songs.

Billie Joe's vocals have aged better than I would have assumed considering that the whole moanful pose of the pop-punkers of that era sounds obnoxious as hell now (I suppose it did as well back then, I just didn't notice). At worst, the most whiny parts of "Wake Me Up When September Ends" are perhaps able to irk me a little bit. And the beginning of "Homecoming" is rough too (although it's on purpose and the idea of its roughness is at least cool, so I can't complain much). In "Homecoming", by the way, Mike Dirnt and Tre Cool also get their sections to sing, and while the latter isn't interesting (his section is amusing, although it's also puzzling what the hell it is doing where it is, narratively speaking) the former sings better than I remembered; he totally pulls off the anthemic spirit just as well (or even better) than Billie Joe could have managed. (Fun fact, this "Nobody Likes You" section is already hinted at by the beginning of "Letterbomb", and the person singing a cappella there is no other than Kathleen Hanna!)

The record flakes out a little bit towards the middle. "Are We the Waiting" has one (interesting) idea with okay execution. "St. Jimmy" reignites the punk energy with a catchy melody over hardcore guitars, although it rants a little bit without direction. I like the very Ramones-esque backing vocals during the last minute of it. "Give Me Novacaine" has an embarrassing solo, but the guitar slides during the verses are lovely, and its 'songyness' holds up quite well. As for "She's a Rebel", I'd just say that 'she's a rebel/she's a saint/she's the salt of the earth/and she's dangerous' is a singable mantra if I've heard one. The idea of a cool anti-system youth still kind of existed back in 2004. Crazy!

At the 5:15 minute mark in "Jesus of Suburbia", during the "I Don't Care" section, you hear what's going on? Like, the backing vocal screaming could have sounded ridiculous, but the band still decided to risk it, and it is risk that pays. Invigorating, enraging, cathartic. It's all of those things, a primordial rock n' roll anthem excecuted with earnest feeling. Great in spite of the childlishness. The last time edgyness in the music of white young people sounded even remotely not embarrassing. 'In a land of make believe/they don't believe in me'. Fretful and immature, perhaps, but who doesn't copy that?

"When someone was looking at the album's lyrics they were like, 'you're probably going to catch some shit'. I was like, 'I hope I get assasinated for it'."B.J.A.

My previous rating for this album was 2.5. I was admittedly being a pretentious prude who was living on a whole other level of life because I had become an elitist and grown out of Green Day, the band that defined my lonely 6th, 7th, and 8th grade before I ventured out into Grunge and Hair Metal (rather antithetical, no?) and the rest is history, you know, even though its only been about two years since I last fawned over these guys. Also, there's this urban myth that you're not supposed to like The Offspring and Green Day at the same time, and The Offspring is my favorite band, so hell, why don't I just jump into the stereotype and suddenly stop liking the literal definitions of my current angst and teenager-ism?

No. My elitism was my downfall. This album isnt the best thing Ive heard in my life, but you cant deny that it is surprisingly solid for the guys who have roughly followed this timeline:

1994: Hatred, Masturbation, Laziness, and the Life of a Teenager
2004: Overdone Politics and a Rock Opera

Here, our poster boys for teen angst are now chanting pop punk anthems that climb onto the admittedly overused anti-Bush bandwagon. Then they launch into a rock opera that I used to know by heart, but I still have a vague idea of: a hopelessly confused boy who gets mixed up and lost in the city life, falling in love with a rebel with the moniker Whatsername and then being figuratively slapped in the face by her, returning home to his old life, and wondering how his first love is doing.

Wow, its all coming back. Now I remember why I like this album: they grew up and made extremely decent music. Warning will always be my favorite, but this holds its ground with hard-hitting pop punk, talented songwriting and imagery, and a friendly sound that still somehow manages to keep from selling the band out completely. They really did a good job with this album, as much as the inner elitist in me wants to say that they suck, but they truly dont, and if they keep going down this track but dont get TOO friendly and light, then theyll do some serious good work in the pop punk scenes.

There was seriously a time where this was my all-time favorite album. That time is justified. Just give this album a listen and forgive me, because even a pretentious female douche like me will admit that their 9-minute tracks are freakin' epic and will always remain in your mind as staples of your musical experience, whether you "like" or "love" or "hate" Green Day.

HIGHLIGHTS: Jesus of Suburbia, Letterbomb, Homecoming, Whatsername

NEUTRAL: American Idiot, Holiday, St. Jimmy, She's a Rebel

AVOID: Boulevard of Broken Dreams, Are We the Waiting, Give Me Novacaine (did they spell it wrong on purpose?), Extraordinary Girl, Wake Me Up When September Ends

This came out when I was in grad school for political science and pretty soon everyone at school was listening to it (grad school, seriously). Before that I think it had been Franz Ferdinand. Anyway, I am usually hype adverse and I avoided it like the plague. I longed for the day when grad students listened to daring music, like the Association for the Advancement of Creative Musicians. Those were the days. Too bad I wasn't born yet. I don't blame Green Day for my cohorts lack of intellectual interest in music, I just find it fascinating that a bunch of poli sci MAs could be blown away by...this: one of the most overrated albums in recent memory.

It always struck me as odd that a "punk" band would embrace rock opera, but in a way that is "punk" because it's attacking punk conventions. I don't think this thought process leads anywhere interesting.
What he have here is an onslaught of radio-ready rock songs and - dare I say it - power ballads (I'm sorry, when the piano and acoustic guitar start undergirding your songs, you're probably in power ballad territory). This is arena rock, not punk. Arena punk? Let's not go there.
We get a series of songs - many of which remind me of other songs, mostly from 80s pop radio, not a particularly good pedigree (example: part one of "Jesus of Suburbia" which sounds like "Summer of '69") - and some of which sound like kids' rhymes - just as good.
The pretty naive - and often, frankly, awkward - political lyrics would be more bearable if the music were any good. I can handle musicians' political naivete when they give me some music. But this arena stuff doesn't do anything for me, and it lets me listen to their penetrating critique of the Republicans - how they are rednecks - and their extremely clunky and unoriginal imagery (walking down a lonely road which happens to be the 'boulevard of broken dreams', anyone?).
But perhaps what is most amazing - shocking to me, really - is that after 15+ years together Green Day are seemingly no better at their instruments than they were in the beginning. Okay, maybe that's a slight exaggeration, but seriously folks, at least arena rock bands have chops. These guys lack chops (every instrumental section is power chords upon power chords, just like it was back in the early 90s) and every lead guitar part (save like two fills) is a statement of the melody! I could play some of these leads! (that is a real insult) Musical innovation makes pretension and/or mediocre lyrics bearable. Pretension and/or mediocre lyrics without such musical interest suck balls. And so does this record.

I lost my American Idiot CD a few years back, which, even though I was sort of in a state of apathy about the album/band at the time, really bugged me. It was the first album that I ever got, on Christmas day, 2005. That was the same Christmas that I got my first electric guitar, and I broke two strings on it from playing it to vigorously the first day. Both purchases were inspired by the music behind this album that I had heard some girl at school play during art class. Truth be told, I would not love music without this album.

Every seventeen year-old secretly loves this album. If you're seventeen now, that means you were probably 11 when this came out. Something about that age just leads to you absorbing something current and mainstream and unabashedly loving it. Listening to this album for me is like tapping into the post-innocence phase of my life, and the album's themes do more than go along with that. This album truly came at the perfect time and has basically secured a lifelong connection with me.

It gave me every 'first' feeling that I will ever have with music. The first song that got 'stuck in my head' ("American Idiot", of course), the first song I learned to play on guitar (which was "Wake Me Up When September Ends", not to mention this of course inspired me to take up musical instruments), the first song that I ever learned all of the words to and sang with (which was "She's a Rebel"), the first album I ever asked to have bought for me, the first album I actually played from start to finish, the first album that made me consider myself a 'fan' of a musical artist. It's easy to say that these things could've happened with any other artist, but they didn't, did they? Who knows what different roads I might have taken had my first band been Linkin Park? Mastodon? Slayer?

Sadly, I constantly trick myself into thinking I hate this album. It just isn't cool to like it like it was when I was in my own little circle of friends, with few band names at my disposal (whichever ones MuchMusic would provide). Now, we're on the Internet. The music community at my disposal grew exponentially, and suddenly, I was forced to question whether or not I liked Green Day. Overtime, my love sadly eroded. I remember one time I had said that I liked Green Day on the Runescape Forums (don't hate), and somebody told me that they sucked and that Nirvana was better. It started from there. I was sort of forced to question whether or not Green Day was actually good or if I just liked them. That made me want to try more artists, and see what I may be missing out on. And that's where me and Green Day kind of fell apart. But Green Day gave me the gift of music, really. This is the fucking album that started everything. Listening to "Jesus of Suburbia" gives me such a massive amount of goosebumps now, and almost moves me to tears.

So many memories, too. Whether it was pausing "Jesus of Suburbia" because I thought my dad was shouting "Hey!" (you know, cause they shout "Hey!" in that song) or when my school band played "Boulevard of Broken Dreams" and I complained about the inconsistency of the arrangement, this album is peppered with an eternal significance. Kind of pretty, really.

Anything that can make me this happy deserves to have me supporting it forever, and not just when I'm not listening to whatever tickles my fancy nowadays. Here we are, a good 5 or 6 years down the line, and this album still hits. Sure, there are a few songs that I simply can't enjoy as much as I used to (most of the singles, barring "Jesus of Suburbia"). Cool.

update June 2011: while looking for Super Smash Bros. Melee among several ancient discs in my room, I found my CD copy of this album, tucked neatly under a bunch of mixtapes I made at age 12. There are no words to explain that find - it's likely meaningless to anyone who reads this, but it made my fucking day...
