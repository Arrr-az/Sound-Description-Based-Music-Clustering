Siouxsie and The Banshees was a band that proved you could, after all, get sugar from shit. While their later works such as Tinderbox and Peepshow were excellent, their debut, The Scream, was bloody awful.

Granted they were going in a much different direction back then, more of a Sex Pistols-type sound. It hadn't been that long before, in fact, that Sid Vicious had been their drummer. And that direction just doesn't appeal to me, so it's quite obvious this album wouldn't either. Which is quite apt, as it doesn't - like i said, i find it bloody awful. Aside from two songs anyway. The single, "Metal Postcard (Mittageisen)", was decent if rather Dirk-like, and "Switch" is downright great. "Mirage" i suppose i can stomach, but otherwise, though, no cigar.

It wasn't until a couple albums later, when the band went in more of a post-punk goth direction, that they began to truly appeal to me. They're not there yet with this release, however. If you're into the earlier punky Siouxsie and The Banshees sound, though, then you'd probably love this or something. But for my money, i'd just as soon pick up a painted rock or something.

The punk movement finally allowed the girls to beat the boys at their own game, and the unsettling cold intensity of Siouxsie Sioux makes her one of the most magnetic band leaders ever. The first Banshees album is a cornerstone of the evolution of punk into post-punk. It still has the unbridled energy and constructive amateurism of punk but John McKay’s inventive electric guitar opens unexplored perspectives into it, and Steven Severin’s driving bass work and the motorik tribal pounding of Kenny Morris laid the foundations of gothic rock a few years before it actually existed. The second line-up of the Banshees will forever remain the greatest one, but The Scream is there to prove the original incarnation of the band was amazing too.

Siouxsie's jet black hair and Egyptian makeup scream a certain theatrical dark glamour so loudly that the listener is forgiven for assuming they, of all Post-Punk bands, had to be absolutely born full formed some years into that genre's development. A product of the Punk really going Post. I mean even the band's name sounds like a Post-Punk band, not a Punk band. Naw. But naw again, they were there to start with. Sure lots of famous post-punkers started in scrappy Punk periods if you dig back far enough into pre-debut material. Joy Division, the Cure etc. But what were those eh? People arriving a little late, never actually putting foot down on the Punk shores. Siouxsie herself hung out with the Sex Pistols and their posse, even figuring centrally into the Bill Grundy TV kerfuffle (where those horrible delinquent punk kids did the inexcusable act of reacting poorly to a dirty old fart flirting with a girl probably less than half his age! Scandal! Swearing!). The assumption of the time was of course that the Sex Pistols were the band, no matter how scrappy, and the chicks hanging out in the posse of course were groupies. Duh! They were good at setting some fashion ideas of course, being girls and all. But they were just ornamentation. Right?

Yeah so anyway, Siouxsie is one of those women who crops out throughout history. In a world across time and space that so often tells women "No, that isn't done". She just goes and does. And then people are all like "WHAT???" but she does the doing so confidently and naturally? The world shifts a bit in spite of it trying not to. The Kool Aid Man approach to social change, an approach I rather admire. And Siouxsie started her career arc by pretty much just hopping up on stage with a random assemblage of instruments and people, and belting out the Lord's Prayer punk style. Virtually entirely in the spur of the moment. That's punk as hell. Was it a good sit down listen? NO, oh god no! There's footage out there (probably not of that original moment) of the early early band doing on those Lord's Prayer performances and it is not exactly pleasant. But at the same time it is quite a show! There's certainly a performance going on that would have been memorable and kind of cool in a purely visceral live way, very punk once again. Honestly the idea that the debut would be anything other than "More Punk™" albeit with a good fresh group probably wasn't in anyone's head, like what else could it be right?

Something Funny Happened On the Way to the Genre

Of course we need to recall that part of what makes them truly more punk in origin than most post-punkers is that to some extent the band had existed since 1976. The process of getting signed took quite some time. Much like the Pretenders these folks were around for those early punk days, but had to fight to get a record out (both female fronted, coincidence??). Then here we are in November 1978 and the product they release is... not really Punk. It's one of the very earliest Post-Punk albums of all no less, in terms of bog standard genre sounding it may arguably be the earliest?

Of the 78' post-punk records the two that came before this are Magazine's Real Life, and Wire's Chair's Missing (it beats PIL's First Issue to the punch by a month). Magazine to me on their debut doesn't sound like post-punk even if they mechanically ...are. Not codifiers to say the least, even with John McGeoch aboard. Whereas Chair's Missing is sort of a similar situation. Two records acting as leading lights to mutating punk, getting weird with it. But laying down the genre as we know it? Putting down those pieces? That kind of first happens here. Both in the sense that the guitar sounds "the way" we like it, and the dark aesthetic thing. Sure sure, larger more accomplished titan records await us in 1979, albums like Unknown Pleasures do far more at codifying the genre. But I can't imagine Bernard Sumner scratching away as he does without John McKay here.

And what got them here? Was it just... Siouxsie and Severin's macabre interests? Was it something so simple as that which influenced everyone else (except Bono I suppose) to go dark and shady in sound? After all the lyrics very much fit the sound here, creepy subjects, off-putting subjects, rarely monsters and ghouls so much as paranoia and realistic grotesques. It's almost a doofy conclusion to the birth of the dark mystique of post-punk. This one chick who digs psychological horror, black hair dye, and Egyptian eye makeup. Directs her band to sound something like that vibe, so they do. Presto. Actually on second thought I kind of think that's an awesome reason for post-punk to end up sounding so dark. It damn sure isn't Real Life that made the genre dark, and Chair's Missing only slightly suggests that future. It's the founding goth stuff here... shit... does this mean goth rock produced post-punk and NOT the other way around??? Whoa... dude...

Let's Not Get Carried Away

Because if this is the FIRST proper post-punk album... well that means it's the first. That means it's still going to be very punk by comparison to the deeper lands. And indeed it is. Some tracks in particular really don't even sound particularly removed from the vanilla world, stuff like Carcass and Nicotine Stain for instance. While even most of the record remains infused with a Pistols/Damned sort of aggro pulse, even when dressed in strange blacks. This is bar none their speediest and most pugnacious record, I'd hazard saying heaviest but that might go to Join Hands or even Juju. That makes this a unique treat and listen, a different experience from the average Banshees record to say the least. And important to appreciating it.

With early iterations of styles like this the best way to hear them might be coming from outside from rather than inside. What do I mean? Well if you pop over here from Kiss in the Dreamhouse or some deep Post-Punk stuff like that, you will have it's simplicity highlighted. But pop in here from some vanilla Punk! Presto, you'll hear clearly how inventive it is, how exactly it twists Punk's nipple, how much SLOWER it is frankly in most places. Like a good kind of slow, it's careful deliberateness that starts showcasing mood over punch, even while punching you plenty. Therein lies the pleasures it contains, you WANT to both be able to enjoy the punky energy while noticing the mutation very clearly. Without that it falls into the cracks between genres, and not in a satisfying way.

John McKay

The ocean of Johns that worked in this band easily means not all John's get appreciated. All it takes is one favored John to overshadow the rest, condemning them to Johngatory for all time. And that means all Johns who aren't John McGeoch. But what if... we can work as one to make a better world where even the lesser Johns get their day in the sun? Let's do that shall we?

I myself have long neglected Mr. John McKay, our very first guitarist for the band. It's easy I think to fall in a fanspace where one puts their noses up at the JUDAS DUO of McKay and first drummer Kenny Morris. They who abruptly jumped ship around the time of Join Hands, they who were replaced with our queen's beau Budgie and Best John, forming the imperial period where Juju was made. So easy it is, to hiss and scratch these traitors. And this of course is stupid as all hell.

Really listen to both The Scream and Join Hands... and how do you not hear just how accomplished and even influential McKay was on the guitar? I wrote all that stuff above about how this record was supremely seminal to the tone and sound grammar of the genre, and yes yes so so much of that is the guitar work isn't it? The sort of guitar sound so integral to the genre, ask many of the other dudes to do it and they'll give the props in question. It's all so important! I'm trying to think about how The Scream would sound with someone ripping away like a standard Punk guitarist. No matter the rest of the tonal work, songwriting, Siouxsie vocals, I don't think it would work. Let ALONE birth a new genre in part. Long story short, I think Mr. McKay deserves way more recognition, and I have a feeling I'll have more to say for Join Hands on this matter.

Song Focus: Jigsaw Feeling

Jigsaw Feeling is one of those post-punk songs that clearly shows it's punk genetics, like PIL's Public Image or Joy Division's Disorder (or virtually the entire first Gang of Four album). We have something full of buzzsaw riffing, energy, punchiness, and with a primary goal of rocking out. But I challenge someone to listen to it and say they are hearing vanilla punk. Even ignoring the insanity based lyrics, the melody... yes the melody. Vanilla punk is ultimately a bunch of melodic pop songs raised in junkyards and alleyways. You know it, I know it, we all know it. This is not a pop melody. It's minor key and makes quite a point of utilizing the guitar to create these scratchy lead moments that summon up alarm. Meanwhile the bass guitar runs along below spreading a subtle sense of sinisterness. Even leaving aside the obvious gothic quality it all delivers, we have something exploring new moods and textures. Playing with uneasy melody on the way.

In a more subtle way I like the vaguely cinematic quality it has, the way it feels like a bracing opening scene, the theatric eye the band would always lean into and benefit from already making itself very apparent. As a result it really does feel like a perfect way to start the band's career, even if the Scream itself makes for kind of a poor litmus test for newcomers on if they might enjoy the career in question. Certainly one could get a feel for early scratchier post-punk through it, or the general goth music idea.

Song Focus: Switch

On the other end of the spectrum of things is this one. A definitive statement in a whole different way. When so much of the record still has Punk residue all over the place, or seems more categorizable as Art Punk or something? Which was largely still how early Post-Punk basically was? Switch comes in, right at the end, and sounds like something else entirely. Post-Punk with capital letters, rather than lower case. Something else. This is a praise I wrote a ton about in my mega review for Unknown Pleasures, how one of Joy Division's grand achievements there was codified the genre. That of course is praise for the impact of that whole record, but was never to say there weren't tracks before it that established some of that. And while I definitely would not apply that to the Scream as a whole, Switch I apply it to.

This one has guitar work clearly of Punk ancestry, but nothing else about the approach suggests Punk. Not the melody, not the song construction, nor the arrangement choices like the surprisingly subtle saxophone. That construction alone is in reference to very clearly separately written portions of the song that aren't just a banged our progression of chords, this is a really WRITTEN sounding track here, very crafted. As for the overall vibe, and yes the sax? This is one of those clear moments where we see that Post-Punk was partly born in combining Punk with Berlin era Bowie. That rich modernist urban vocabulary, the sleek latter day child of some of Glam's best experimenters, and some of the most forward thinking less Proggy of the Krautrock bunch.

It's a spectacular finale that absolutely makes official this as a minor classic record for me, and one that is too often lost in the shuffle of people typecasting this album as wall to wall scratch punk. Hell in a sense we can include preceding track Suburban Relapse in that "slower and has a subtle sax" club of forward looking material lying at the end. I wish more of this Mark I era of the band leaned in this direction, though I guess it could be argued that these best preview the Mark II splendor yet to come.

The Herald

And I think that represents what makes the Scream a cool listen. It isn't the most satisfying listen, even compared to great vanilla punk records, and especially compared to what the band would get up to once the 80's began. But it's a dynamic show of something in flux, something evolving actively. The sound of post-punk being BORN, in maybe a more true way than Wire. Wire were art school boys pretty much from the start, Pink Flag was immediately deliberately interesting in taking punk and mutating it. Which is cool as all hell? But there's something more to hearing a punk band literally transforming into post-punk. People who started out as scrappy and untrained as anyone (one of the earliest members was Sid Vicious lol), in real session time... discovering some other land through the wall. Getting audibly restless and seeing what happens if they try something new. Even more than PIL's debut or Magazine's debut, this feels like the essential early post-punk record in capturing the moment of expanding horizon. By the next year you'd have records coming out that sounded clearly like the other side (Unknown Pleasures, Entertainment, Y, and so on). THIS is the missing link of a record.

There is an arid simplicity about The Scream. A title that aptly translates to what the sound of a banshee is. The majority of the album sounds like scattered hoots and hollers. At least on the less post-punk driven material like Pure.

Lead Banshee, Siousxsie Sioux, yowls are what make the band most unique, similar to how Rotten's vocals made the Sex Pistols stand out. Although her delivery is stiff and authoritarian like she is some kind of tyrannical dictator. The environment of the late 70's was ripe for this type of atonal dreariness. The Scream is right at home with emerging Goth groups like Bauhaus (who would emerge a year later), and the infant Cure.

Their starting ground hears the band in honest form. But their approach to punk becomes flat on some tracks. Take the nearly interesting cover of the Beatles classic Helter Skelter. The Banshees drunkenly piss about the verses and struggle to appropriate The White Album staple. It sounds more like a Manson massacre.

Regardless of its many blemishes The Scream was vicious sounding, a potent concoction of spirits and toxins. But there is no doubt that, there is a vast contrast between some tracks that were clearly interesting ideas made flesh (Switch and Metal Postcard) while others were banal or insulting (Helter Skelter and Carcass). It could have been so much stronger had Hong Kong Garden and The Staircase (Mystery) had been included in the track listing. Heck I may have even raised my rating a whole half star!

You can compare Siouxsie and the Banshees to The Cure or any other gothic rock/post-punk band but it won't be sufficient, even though the music might sound similar, there's no vocalist quite like Siouxsie Sioux. She doesn't even always hit the right notes but her singing sounds like a rough diamond, right from the soul without filters or effects, and with an energy that captivates me every second of The Scream.

"Overground" is hypnotising with its fantastic guitar riff, and with "Helter Skelter" there's a rare occurance of a really good Beatles cover on this album. The delivery of "You may be a lover but you ain't no fucking dancer" alone gives this cover its right to exist. The album ends with "Switch" which is absolutely outstanding and the perfect way to end this record.

Pretty much every bass line on this album is remarkable, and it's worth listening with a good sound system just to let Steven Severin shine, I admire him more with each listen.

My favourite thing about the album: has to be the vocals. Siouxsie absolutely nails it on every track with her spine-chilling voice which adds a very eerie feeling to the record. I'd actually say I prefer her to Robert Smith (both brilliant, though) who, just like her, has very anxious sounding vocals on his records, but he never hit me quite as hard as Siouxsie.

How I discovered this album: I first heard "Love in a Void" on the soundtrack of one of my favourite films, 20th Century Women, so I listened to this record and immediately loved it.

Favourite tracks: Switch, Overground, Jigsaw Feeling
