For the Elvis Costello faithful, Imperial Bedroom is the album that displays his maturity as a songwriter. To a mere dabbler like myself who is content with owning the first four of his albums, this is the album where he starts to get boring and predictable. It appears as if he is trying to prove to the select few doubters out there that he was capable of writing "sophisticated" songs. Anybody who has heard "Alison" or most of Armed Forces should already know that Costello is one hell of a songwriter. However, here, he enlists the aid of former Beatles engineer Geoff Emerick, who helped record three of rock's greatest and best produced albums in Revolver, Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band, and Abbey Road. Oddly enough, it STILL sounds like Nick Lowe produced it (and there's nothing wrong with that). Sure, Costello's compositions have more formal grit than anything else he'd done up to this point. But his singing is generally listless (the opener "Beyond Belief" is a good example) and few of the songs are all that distinguishable from the others. The two "chamber pop" songs are the weakest things on here. Costello has to prove his chops by fusing the pomp and circumstance of the Royal Family with the ribaldry found in, say, Henry Fielding's novel Tom Jones (1749) on "...And In Every Home" and "Town Cryer." The results are stuffy, forced, and all too parochial. The best thing about this album is, without a doubt, "Almost Blue," which just might be Costello's best slower song. It mixes jazz, country, and his overall acerbic worldview into an elegant, sophisticated track that seems to have come to him effortlessly. The one interesting thing about Imperial Bedroom is its place in Costello's oeuvre. He successfully distances himself from his "angry young man" records of yore and writes music fit for a king. It also points forward to his later career as a dilettante and appreciator of the musical tradition to such a degree that his own vision is often subsumed by it.

atomicWedgie Feb 16 2005

There's a word that gets thrown around this site quite a bit that some of us are reluctant to use. Merriam's Online Dictionary defines the word as follows:
1 : a work done with extraordinary skill; especially : a supreme intellectual or artistic achievement
2 : a piece of work presented to a medieval guild as evidence of qualification for the rank of master

I think only a heavy metal act could qualify for definition number two. Manowar, maybe? It certainly doesn't apply to anything Elvis Costello has produced. Well, at least as far as I know. Definition number one, though, we might have something to work with with that definition (gotta love the double withs, eh?).

What is the most challenging pop album that you still consider to be great music, what album would it be? My answer, without hesitation, would be Imperial Bedroom. Well, I might hesitate and spell it IbMePdErRoIoAmL, but you get the drift, right? Intricate melodies, arrangements as thick as peanut butter but with a resolve to never be still, some of Costello's best lyrics and a band in top form, Imperial Bedroom has everything going for it, and none of it is spoon fed. There is so much going on that, unless you're listening intently, you'll find yourself overwhelmed by the rapid-fire delivery of sound. It's too much to listen to casually. If you are listening intently, you may be overwhelmed by the technical and intellectual prowess that is on display.

Is it Elvis Costello's best album? I don't think so. I'll take This Year's Model and Trust over this album. Those are records made for listening. Imperial Bedroom is different. It seems to have been made less to listen to than to impress. It's not progressive rock, it's progressive recording. Prog-rec? Combine great songwriting, a great band, great execution, a great singer, great production, great engineering, great mixing and whatever else there is that makes a recording great and you've got Imperial Bedroom. It's the best of many possible worlds.

You want great technique? Check out Bruce Thomas' bass lines throughout this album. Bruce was a monster, and Elvis and the Attractions relied on him to carry a massive burden. On Imperial Bedroom he plays a series of constantly moving parts, laying the foundation for some incredibly complex arrangements. Thomas was one of the best bass players I've heard, and Imperial Bedroom, as complex as it is as a recording, may showcase Thomas better than any other recording with the Attractions.

Great songwriting? Choose a song from this record. Pick one. Any one. Go on. Put it up against the anything that Bob Dylan or The Beatles ever produced. Any song. Go on. Elvis will hold his own.

Great Production? It moves so fast it almost gets lost, this record. Costello never wastes a breath, and on this album a listener can't afford to so much as yawn or scratch his or her nose without risking missing some great concept that might never again appear with such clarity as just before the yawn or nose scratching occurred. It's dense, complex and brilliant, like no other record I know of.

Imperial Bedroom is indeed a work done with extraordinary skill; it's a supreme intellectual and artistic achievement. If ever a pop record could be labeled with the forbidden word, it would be Imperial Bedroom.

La única palabra que puedo usar para describirlo es... Majestuoso.
Me volví un gran fan de Costello este año, quedé embobado por su gran talento como compositor, vocalista y letrista.
Es curioso pensar que resulta ser una joya escondida para los oyentes hispanohablantes, porque se necesita un manejo del inglés medianamente avanzado para poder entender su genio traído a la mesa por las múltiples metáforas, mensajes, juegos de palabras y chistes que hace con sus letras... Sin embargo, Imperial Bedroom es un caso aparte.
Siempre me llamó la atención porque una parte del fandom de Elvis lo ponía muchas veces como su obra cumbre, debido a un sonido sofisticado y los temas maduros que trata, junto con la forma en la que los encara.

Entré con las expectativas muy altas, y fueron ampliamente superadas.

La diferencia crucial para mí en este disco es que a diferencia de los primeros 3, Macmanus sigue afilando sus habilidades como vocalista y maneja una flexibilidad tremenda a lo largo de todo el álbum. Las melodías vocales son excelentes, la que más me gustó fue lejos la de "estilo barroco" al final de Beyond Belief.

Tengo que admitir que lo más importante, de igual manera, es lo triste y desesperanzador que es el álbum en sí. Constantemente habla de fracasos amorosos y dolores en el corazón, sobre no estar conforme y como las cosas uno no sale como uno quiere.
Ya no está ese Costello sarcástico e irónico que mira con desprecio y hace chistes ingeniosos, ahora sólo queda un hombre que está dañado, ha dañado y se arrepiente de lo que hizo... miren por ejemplo como habla sobre la infidelidad desde varios puntos de vista en Tears Before Bedtime y The Long Honeymoon.
Cómo habla sobre sentirse vacío y el daño emocional causado debido a esto en Shabby Doll.
Y así podría seguir con cada canción.

Amo este álbum y lo recomiendo encarecidamente.

More refined and full-sounding than earlier releases but Imperial Bedroom doesn't have a sure-fire winner of a track to hook me in. There's a few low points but not a killer track to offset them, though there's some goodies at least.

"You Little Fool" is probably my favorite as I like the vocal melodies for the most part and it's a pretty quirky track that retains memorability. "Man Out of Time" was pretty interesting, like a potential rock song usurped by a pop tune. "Human Hands" was on the verge of being great but the background vocals during the chorus are a bit annoying.

But there's junk like "Boy With a Problem", and "Shabby Doll" was too long and kind of a drag. The ones draped with orchestra stuff didn't really work for me either though they ain't bad.

Not much else to add; it seems to be a fan favorite so it shouldn't be skipped if you're up for some Elvis C. binging. It just didn't click for me as much as a fair amount of his earlier albums.

Half-splendid aural tapestry woven with typical unctuous Costello vocals but fewer typical Costello hits and ends up overrunning by quite a long stretch. The arrangements are very lush and ornate with a consistently shapeshifting quality to them but sadly it’s not to the greatest effect because the songs that it supports are simply not that strong of a batch, either being too long and bleh or trailing off just a bit too quick before they build into anything magnificently special. Shabby Doll is just a shabby song. Man Out Of Time works up a nice wall of sound at times and feels like the most classic of the lot. Almost Blue is a beautifully tender, smoky ballad and easily the best takeaway song. And In Every Home hones in on the Penny Lane angle with extravagant brass-heavy orchestration but winds up being the album’s ugliest track by far. The second half begins well with its two most direct, poppiest numbers in the shape of The Loved Ones and Human Hands but the songs after it don’t tend to stick as well with the vocal hook on Pidgin English being the most irritating low-point. An album riddled with rich sonic variety but where actual favourite tunes are in seriously short supply.

Elvis Costello's first few records displayed that rare synthesis of talents: the intellect and the instinct. As clever pop in a period when neither was quite appreciated, he stood out amidst his peers and crafted some of the most effective work in the entire punk-adjacent oeuvre.

The early eighties finds Costello going a different direction, applying a new-wave sensibility to the sort of studio wizardry and finely-tuned art-pop of late Lennon-McCartney. Does it work? Yes, of course, though its position within the general dialectic process is tenuous. A deftly melodious touch is applied across the board, from semi-jazz to the pseudo-baroque and so on - replete with the expected acerbic wordsmithing re: various failed dalliances.

It's all very singular, purposeful, quote-unquote 'mature': an entire record sprung from the seeds of 'For No One' by the man behind 'Sneaky Feelings'. Its incongruity amongst contemporaries is likely half the appeal - the other half is the irresistability of talent for talent's sake.

2nd August 2022.
