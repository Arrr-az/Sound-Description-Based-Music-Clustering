'Ere, what's all this then? The voice of a generation, the great poet, the master lyricist reduced to "Oh me, oh my, just love that country pie"?
Cards on the table, I abominate country music, the last refuge of the creatively bankrupt in my book. While I might accept, say, a country influence on a track (occasionally The Beatles and Stones would add a steel guitar for effect), I've always found it cliche-ridden, sentimental, cloying, maudlin and completely devoid of life or emotion and for a so-called major act like Dylan to hide behind its rhinestone skirts is as unbelievable as it's ridiculous.
Maybe it was the influence of new-pal Johnny Cash, I don't know, but with only a couple of exceptions, where Dylan gives us something approaching a narrative and a decent tune ("Lay Lady Lay" and "Tonight I'll Be Staying Here With You"), this is absolute dreck and of no artistic merit whatsoever.
Starts off with a turgid cover of "Girl From The North Country" with Cash as co-vocalist, where two sand and glue voices (to borrow Bowie's description) can't even be bothered to harmonise over a lumbering, tuneless rendition. Sounds like a first take that no-one could be bothered re-doing.
Then it's all aboard the Louisiana Hayride (me last!) for the "Rag" instrumental and it just goes downhill (bar the exceptions noted) from there.
Renowned as one of the shortest playing time albums ever, it's not short enough for me, folks.
"Is it rolling Bob?", no it's snoring...

If I wanted to give my kids a valuable lesson about the importance of putting effort and craft into things, I would play them this album and Blood on the Tracks back to back and ask them if they ever noticed any sort of difference. I mean, I like this album, I really do. A throwaway? Oh, sure, but as far as throwaways go this lords all over New Morning - it actually has two indisputable classics (you know the ones), one unknown song that is in my mind a semi-classic, and a remade classic done with one of the few straight country artists I actually do like. So I can see why the word "classic" gets tossed around a lot in regard to this album. But man, these lyrics... I don't know, this is pretty inane, and half of the melodies cut the mustard but do not do anything particularly special with it, whereas a song like "I Want You" both cuts the mustard and serves it up with some fancy garnishes, thus getting even anti-mustard folk such as myself to enjoy it.

MUSTARD.

Continuing onwards, let's get to those classics, okay? "Lay Lady Lay" is the one everyone knows, and it goes without saying that it is beautiful, with Dylan slipping right into romantic crooner mode while slipping into something a little more comfortable (and now you can't unsee it!) without any effort. Great song, easily the best on the album - those little bongos and the fantastic guitar line make it more than just a showcase for the crooner style that is, confusingly enough (apparently he quit smoking for about two seconds here, and actually is not half-bad as a technical singer, although you sacrifice the untrained charisma of the earlier classics. Life's about tradeoffs, you know?), all over this album. "Tonight I'll Be Staying Here With You" is also great, with a pedal steel line that I (get this!) actually enjoy hearing and some nice, rustic piano going on. "I Threw it All Away" is underrated and lovely, and his remake of "Girl From the North Country" with a surprise guest shot from Johnny Cash is almost as good as the original.

As for the other six songs, they are very pleasant, but nothing to really write home about or anything. "Tell Me That Isn't True," "Peggy Day," "And to Be Alone with You" are basically interchangeable, although "Peggy Day" does change things up the slightest bit by speeding things up for a few bars near the end - which, other than Dylan's newfound vocal style, Johnny Cash's guest shot, and the adequate instrumental "Nashville Skyline Rag," is the most surprising thing about this album. So that makes it fourth-most surprising, I guess. Not really all that much when the whole appeal of the mid '60s classic trilogy was how unpredictable and ramshackle it was, but all three songs (and the instrumental, I guess) are nice, so hey. Meanwhile, "Country Pie" is a goofy hoedown, and "One More Night" is a rather sappy ballad. Eh, I could do without it.

Granted, I could also do without anything on this album that isn't one of those four songs I mentioned as being classic, but you know what? It's a good throwaway. Very well-made, very professional, full of enjoyable songs. But, as I said before, it feels like he wasn't trying. And not in the cool, "I don't give a fuck about you and yours" way that made this '60s albums so great, but in a rather dull, "I don't care about songwriting anymore" way, because he very clearly was trying to write good songs on those three albums. Otherwise they wouldn't be enshrined canonical classics whose reputation even I would never dream of arguing. Now, when he finally put effort into things again, you get the aforementioned Blood on the Tracks, which is of course his best album. But in the interim between JWH and Blood, this is the best you're gonna get. Thankfully, it's a lot better than some of Neil Young's later half-assed country albums - not Harvest, of course, but more Comes a Time. Even off his game, Dylan was still capable of good things at this point. Too bad it would not last.

Track picks: "I Threw It All Away," "Lay Lady Lay," "Tonight I'll Be Staying Here with You"

Im gonna tell you what Levon Helm told me right before one of his Midnight Rambles, as he kicked the toe of one my new boots to make his point, scuffed it without knowing, and said, The last song off of John Wesley Harding could very easily have been the very first song on Nashville Skyline. We had about an hour before his set, and he spoke very freely about how Bob had gone completely back to the basics of Americana at that stage of his career, had taken some cues from Ronnie Hawks and was enjoying his swim in the currents of Country Music.

Not only was the music fresh as air, but so was his voice ... he was now singing in a much simpler, more refined tone, with a breathy feel, a fine sense of satisfaction and love for what he was doing. But dont go thinking that this was the traditional Country Music, like the music that that was coming from Gram Parsons, the Byrds, or some of the other, Rock turned Country, California Bands. This was contemporary Country Music, a style that sort of sprang up during these sessions, a mixture of where Dylans music had been, then stepped to John Wesley Harding and finally progressed to this release Nashville Skyline.

This must have been an incredibly fun and supportive session for Bob, as Johnny Cash, who had been recording at the same time stopped in and the two laid down a whole tape of duets ... and Johnny even managed to score a place on this album as a vocalist. Other talented artist like Charlie Daniels [Bass Guitar], Pete Drake [Pedal Steel Guitar] and Bob Wilson [Organ and Piano] also appeared on this surprisingly short release, that times in at just twenty seven minutes and fourteen seconds, though it was one of his best selling albums of all time, with no less then three singles being released for airplay.

The albums artwork shows a very contented Bob Dylan, but there were still major insecurities haunting him, along with pressures to become an active member of the counter culture ... but Bob, true to his nature, rather then taking a step forward, took a step back, which only created more mystery and speculation. This is an amazingly good album, one which is often overlooked as a whole, but rather seen in the context of the two major singles that came out of it. Never the less, listen to it contextually and discover where Bobs head was at ... then watch him take his next step.

This is a very personal and loving body of work ... and nothing is finer then that.

Catalog Crawl (#16)
Bob Dylan closed out a dizzying decade with what may still be the most easygoing and good-natured record in his catalog. Breezing by in just over twenty-seven minutes, Nashville Skyline is an endearing and endlessly replayable gem.

But it's also an album that drew a decidedly mixed reaction when it arrived in the spring of 1969. The country affectations undoubtedly helped Dylan appeal to a new audience, but his loyal acolytes -- already left confused by 1967's John Wesley Harding -- were troubled by its gleefully apolitical nature and slick sound.

Today however, Nashville Skyline is a wonderfully refreshing highlight of Dylan's "wilderness" era.
