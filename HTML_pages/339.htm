Overall: 57 - 58 4.42/5 88 - 89%: Exceptional; repeated listens demanded; BUY IT

This review, like all my reviews, will be rambling and incoherent. Mostly because, like the canonized Wu-Tang Clan, Bob Dylan, The Beatles, you either have to use way too many words to explain what's going on in your head or just think of some fancy phrase and hope everybody likes it. And that's for both sides of the coin - at the time of this review, someone else has a one line review for this album explaining why it's no good. For Animal Collective's Merriweather Post Pavilion, he wrote a really interesting and fucking long meandering about all kinds of things, but not always the music. And so it has to be with the truly great albums, that middle ground is so hard to reach. I think it's always important to listen to the albums that can draw such a variety of opinions in those two key ways where the language can't help but be emotional rather than truly critical.

Because white people who don't listen to hip hop might listen to this, but for those white (and black and hispanic and southeast asian) people who do, what is there to stand against? The album follows a genealogy of El-P and RZA, mixing obscure cartoon and science fiction samples with intentionally dusty beats, abstract rhythms and raps about hardcore topics and underground supremacy, and a distortion of the typical rap man brag 'n' rhyme. Granted there isn't a whole bunch of DOOM to be had here - Geedorah, as we learn from "Monster Zero", is above ground and mostly out of sight (aka a producer alias) - but all the guests recognize these beats are the opportunities of a life time and drop progressive flows with unique voices. Many of the emcees also take the opportunity to adopt alien alias' as well, which continues to add to the album's really intensely focused vibe.

I can't make any secret about it really - this album has no flaw for all 42 minutes. It is the best thing from DOOM solo moniker by a long shot. It ages like wine. Nothing comes close. A truly pantheon release that, like all of DOOM's albums, builds on the premise of Operation: Doomsday that DOOM is a villain - though like The Watchmen not necessarily out for evil - fighting against the norms of society, or rap music. And this is really where it all came together, from raps to sound. No longer is DOOM alone in his battle, he now has the power to sit in his dark corner of the rap world and send his homies out to do his bidding. It's unfortunate DOOM barely touches it with his voice since his hands were magic, but the album might have simply been too good in that case.

This album is a dream come true that no one really realized until MF came out FULLY swinging a year later. Going back to King Geedorah is like finding all your Christmas gifts you forgot about, after you took up your new Xbox to your room. You got MF Doom sharp as samurai blade, some guest MCs sharp as a 20 dollar Chefmate set (sharp enough, honestly....I have the one that spins around...you know?...its convenient..potato peeler...ya...all that stuff), and killer production and overall theme that comes from old pre-60s monster and sci-fi horror films. Its such a weird place but its still familiar to us through all of pop cultures odd referencing to this shit. All these weird sounds that sound producers made in the 50s that just don't make sense but still were awesome in their own bizarreness. Its all great really. It still feels like a starter to Madvillian but its worthy of acclaim on its own merits. Fans of interesting hip-hop production, 50s sci-fi horror, and MF Doom need to hear this forgotten album.

Buen ejercicio estilstico y experimentador que por momentos sabe mostrar de manera clara los pecados y virtudes de su creador. La ambicin musical se lleva bastante por delante el instinto comercial, por lo que la libertad en bases, produccin, Mcs y lricas es total (con todo lo bueno y malo que conlleva eso). El disco desaprovecha muchas veces eso pegando con saliva mcs, melodas repetitivas y trucos de produccin repetitivamente poco vistosos en temas que no consiguen llenar cualitativamente el minutaje impuesto, cosa que ocurre de manera cclica en el disco y no lo deja tomar el ritmo propio de los lbumes excepcionales. Los temas mas destacados son aquellos que precisamente se alejan de ese afn experimental repetitivo y abrazan ideas mas tradicionalistas del hip hop, como Krazy World, Anti-Matter o el genial Lockjaw (todos de geniales bases, delivery potente y creatividad instrumental propia del calibre artstico de quien est detrs del proyecto). Las ideas de abrazar un sonido mas oscuro y la apertura respecto al uso de mcs de delivery menos convencional son cosas buenas, pero en general el disco se quedan mas en promesa que en realidad. Los principales culpables de este ultimo hecho son el extendimiento excesivo de canciones y la repeticin de motivos musicales exprimidos hasta puntos donde no solo no aportaban sino que quitaban en la experiencia.

Nota Espiritual: 6/10

Dumile's Best Produced
"King Geedorah, take me to your leader; quick to claim that he not no snake like me neither."

To me, this is Metal Fingers' best work as a producer. Of course, albums like Operation: Doomsday and Mm..Food are fantastic, but I find this album to be a lot more consistent. Each song helps build a great atmosphere of a silly movie about powerful monsters, through peculiar sound effects and Metal Fingers' signature cartoon samples. While this is, first and foremost, a DOOM album where he steals the show whenever he decides to touch the mic, his Monsta Island peers along with the mysterious Mr Fantastik also provide great performances while King Geedorah prefers to stay in background.
