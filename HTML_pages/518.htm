Intensity. Such a flavor of word does not only serve notions of tempestuousness, but those of overwhelming, those of a slow-rolling ecstasy from which you cannot escape. In your mouth, an intense taste; in your mind, an intense drug; intense dreams, intense sex, intense sadness or happiness. I have heard intensity in one sustained note from one instrument, and I have seen intensity in the vastness of the sea. Were this an instrumental album the intensity would still last, assuredly, but most of it comes from the hollow atonal narrations of the vocals. All you find here is the wandering, the confusion, being overrun; these are observations of immersive loss, of emptiness. This is a dream through which you stumble looking downward, feeling sleepy though your body is asleep, feeling your mouth cloud open as your eyes droop closed.

The music on the evocatively titled Spiderland is morose, drifting, yet is not too bashful to scatter and then web back together, nor to unsheathe and slash with a sudden railing into distortion. As I mentioned, there is enough here in the music to be a terrific album without the spoken word emulsified into it. Discordance flows into monotone so well you'd think that each instrument was manipulated by one arm of a multiple-armed demi-god, for it slides in the directions of the composition as if by a single mind. With the base tones of the guitars and the jazzy tickling of the drum kit, the artists paint for you a bleak landscape that Isaac Brock could only formulate in a long-forgotten dream. Sewn into this sound is the expert use of dynamics, which continues to make the music interesting as each five-minute-plus song spins on into the forty-minute total.

Spiderland is the cold, desolate collective for the noctivagant one. It is not to be forgotten under the hurled detritus of genre and our myriad interpretations of what a given genre should be. Dream to this. Let it crawl over you. And for the sake of whatever god you elect, listen to every note they present for you, and to every word sung or spoken.

Favorites: 'Don, Aman' and 'Good Morning, Captain.'

As it happens, shedding their roots as an allegedly lame post-hardcore band (I haven't actually heard Tweez, but I can think of better ways to spend my 45 minutes than with an album that's supposed to kinda suck) and instead joining up with the then-nascent post-rock movement was the best career decision Slint ever made. Because this album, which would sadly end up being the band's last, is definitely one of the best albums the genre has to offer.

I'm going to warn you up front, though. This album is cold. If you're going to listen to this album, and I think you definitely should, you have to be prepared for an album almost completely devoid of emotion. Because that's what you're up against. An album so clinical and so distant that it makes King Crimson look like Arcade Fire. You're also going to get a creative, remarkably weird album that is utterly on its own in the post-rock universe, which in my mind makes it well worth the coldness.

This album is well known for its use of crazy time signatures. And you know something? Generally speaking, I don't like those. I like the idea of them, but in practice, songs in 9/4 or 17/8 or whatever just don't sound good. They tend to kind of annoy me, and be overly "ooh, look how arty we are,' because let's face it, they're hard to write quality material around. And the difficulty of that gets multiplied by a thousand when you factor in changes from one weird time signature to another. But here, they work fantastically well. Whatever it takes to work odd time signature changes into music and not come out sounding like Rush, these guys tap into it, because it lends itself well to what they're going for. The way I see it, the time signatures lend this album a weirdly suspended, off-kilter feel, and fit their alien sound quite well.

A good example of everything this album does right is "Don, Aman," which shows the band's knowledge of how to construct a fascinating piece of music without much emphasis on melody or emotion, two things I usually like my music to have. It's not like the song is a great big ball of fun happy catchiness, but man, it's fucking great. The quiet parts are suspenseful, tense, paranoid, with an ominously whispered vocal adding mystery. And it alternates between these and loud passages that betray the band's post-hardcore beginnings in the coolest way possible. "Nosferatu Man" and "Breadcrumb Trail" follow a similar formula to similarly great results.

The centerpiece, however, is "Washer." On an album as atmospheric as this one, longer songs tend to be better, at least if it succeeds in setting the atmosphere it wishes to. You probably discerned by looking at my rating that I feel this album does, and that would be the truth. It starts off with a surprisingly pretty guitar line and vocals that are, atypically for this album, sung in a nervous, Tom Verlaine-esque quiver. It isn't long before things boil over, of course, and the loud bits are shocking and - this on an album that's generally devoid of emotion - rather cathartic. It's a breathtaking song, easily my favorite here.

Sadly, "For Dinner..." has the misfortune of coming in between the album's two strongest songs, and really isn't all that interesting. Not that it's bad or anything, it just doesn't work as well as the others. Unsurprisingly, it's the least popular song on the album. But it leads into "Good Morning, Captain," the album's other high point. It's a fascinating song-long crescendo accompanied by an eerily blank story about a captain lost at sea. It's a creepy, unsettling, absolutely killer piece of music. Actually, take out "For Dinner...," and that sentence would describe the whole album.

Seems to me yet another case of the fluffy tail of breathless reputation wagging the mangy dog of minimal achievement. I've read enough rich praise for _Spiderland_ to open a Swiss savings account, so I should have been surprised when, upon listening, I found a couple of unlikeable dweebazoids plucking untuned guitars while a creative writing student in therapy reads spittle-of-consciousness prose off a napkin.

If Jandek ever needs life-threatening surgery and is provided a mike in the process, this is what he'll sound like. Then again, that might be a rare case in which he would have the good sense not to record.

I realize _Spiderland_ is one of those that may require three dozen listenings to catch a very unique wavelength, but after three spread out attempts, I'm giving up. I'd give it additional benefit of the doubt if I had any left.

01. Slint - Spiderland
When you learn that the four day recording sessions of Spiderland were so traumatic that at least one member checked into a mental institute directly afterwards and then the band broke up five seconds later, the jittery restlessness and detached tension of this record makes a lot more sense. Upon first listen, do not be alarmed if you find yourself abandoned, floating aimlessly in the middle of a calm open ocean with nothing to hold onto but your own thoughts. And then the claustrophobia closes in. Your hysterical urgency for rescue amplifies. Hushed whispers turn into scarcely audible mumbles turn into spoken word narratives turn into strained screams for help, performing an outright panic attack through agitated time signatures and manic dynamics—all very hopeless and terrifyingly quiet within the chaotic disorder of your looming respiratory failure. And once the record has played out and you are gasping for air, unable to make eye contact with your usually trusted bottle of water, you will understand why this technical progressive complex masterpiece is lauded as a pinnacle of the 1990s, often considered the primary catalyst for the whole post-rock genre, as well as the most essential math rock album ever made.

NyQuil + lung infection from a poorly-ventilated apartment + a terrible headache + this on low-volume while me, age 18/19, lay in agony on an underplush futon in my Tallahassee college apartment, 2011. This is where my mind goes when I hear the first four songs of one of the best post-rock albums ever conceived.

Shortly before deciding to solidify this album to memory, I was reading somebody's list, and he had included a praise of how terrific "Washer" by Slint was. And you know, I had heard this Slint album maybe once in my entire life, despite the truckloads of appreciation I've seen it get and the recognition it has surely earned. But only once, had I heard it. Not through any lack of interest, nor was I underwhelmed (though I guess you could say I wasn't overwhelmed in the way I expected either), but... Spiderland was a forgotten classic.

I sleep best with the music on, and after seeing the love that a certain song got from this album, I impulsively went to the album directory and put it into a playlist to be activated as soon as I walked away from the screen. Once didn't feel right, though, so I brought it back up and put it in about seven or eight times. Eying the repeating titles and feeling a little bit pleased, I went back to what I was doing, and in a matter of minutes, I let the medicine lead me away to my bed. I don't even remember turning this album on, but it was, and I only know that because I was bombarded profusely with Slint's relentlessness. Consciousness was transcended as movies played in my head over the course of falling in and out of sleep for the entirety of the listening, and after a particular four hour montage of these drowsy, suburb-laden images, I finally emerged from this makeshift trip, wondering what had all happened.

I like to relish in the 1990s-esque qualities that some bands have, and Slint is one of the bands that takes the larger piece of cake. This being the only math rock album I own, I can't say that this is a mutually-exclusive quality of Slint's music, but I can pretty fairly assess that Spiderland influenced a lot of the sound that I have been going on and on to praise. They play very disjointed melodies, in a dissonant sort of hazy, run-through-the-neighborhood vein. If that doesn't make sense to you, I don't know what else will, because I understand it completely. When songs like "Breadcrumb Trail" exist, I find it kind of hard to know that I was once not touched in some way by this music. By the disoriented race of "Nosferatu Man", with its riffs interweaving one another and Brian McMahan's whispering rhetoric spread out across the equation of chugs and squeals. It's all just so very... mental.

The weird thing is, I remember exactly as I felt before this music had touched me in any sort of way. As a matter of fact, this may be the only album that has ever given me that chance, to see both sides of a person liking an album and not liking it. What was perceived as monotony only hours ago is now something I hear as being soaked and dripping with emotive tension. The "Don, Aman" riff, for example, is one of the most simple and frustrating things I could be listening to right now, as it titillates so cruelly and changes its shade while remaining so adamant and consistent. And that "Washer" melody? The most melancholic and crushing thing I've heard in a very, very long time. The oddest and most chilling thing about listening to this song now is that I remember the guitar being stronger, broader, sturdier... in my own pathetic state, it seemed like the most reliable thing in the world... I love it so very much now in knowing that it is basically a reflection of myself. I predict a hell of a future with this song.

An effortless strife...

"For Dinner..." creeps so beautifully. There are times in this track that I think it has went away completely, but then it slithers back in and around me and makes departure again, only to repeat itself in a variety of ways. And then "Good Morning, Captain"... maybe the song that stands out the least (I typed that in 2011 but my 2023 self still agrees) and as a result, drones me into bliss the most, steers the whole affair into the crowded dock. It's like all of the culmination and fruition of the music is cycloned into one of the most painfully blissful confessionals ever expressed in music, and...

On a night like that, where my head is quivering and the room feels too hot and a relieving message or two pops through on my screen and reminds me that it's all going to replace itself with something else one day, I'm deeply enamored by how much Slint has resonated with me on this particular day. This may have barely nicked my heart the last time, but to know that an album like Spiderland can come along and ruff everything up in an instance... it makes me wonder what else might be just around the corner, waiting to strap me in for the ride of my life. Who knows...who knows...who knows...I know I like Slint.

It is a work of art that simply can never be properly explained, as it is original in every sense of the word. For many listeners including myself, it also leaves an emotional imprint that few albums ever could. Slint’s sound was one like no other, mixing together Post-Hardcore, Math Rock, and Post-Rock, and as time goes by, they remain influential in all three of these genres. The songs are usually started in a slow, icy crawl, with chilling riffs, and one of my favorite parts of the record, spoken word vocals. They are delivered in a way that is both sinister and beautiful, just as the music is behind them, and they only add to the emotional impact of every track (excluding the instrumental song “For Dinner…”), often rising up out of their quiet in a roar during some of the records most emotive moments, with the rest of the band following suite. Slint uses this quiet to loud dynamic throughout Spiderland, especially on the first two tracks “Breadcrumb Trail” and “Nosferatu Man,” which may make it seem like these songs are repetitive and predictable, but that is far from the case. Every note, especially on the first few listens, is a profound moment, and any hint of repetition is neither boring nor predictable, but completely hypnotizing.

However, not every track uses this formula, specifically “Don, Aman,” which happens to be one of the most prominent examples of the spoken word vocals/lyrics ability to drive these songs. They are soulless, almost mechanical, but nonetheless riveting and poetic. To continue to use “Don, Aman” as an example; Britt Walford’s character Don is this song. The character’s battle against social anxiety is what drives every second of it, as well as every note Britt and David Pajo pluck on their guitars. With nothing more than Britt’s vocals and the two guitars, Don’s struggle becomes one of the most intimate moments on Spiderland.

”Don, Aman” is followed by the most gorgeous track on the album, “Washer”. It is the only song here that features sung lyrics, delivered by guitarist Brian McMahan in a frail, broken voice, propelled by Britt’s always perfectly placed drum hits, Todd Bashear’s bass lines, and Pajo’s absolutely beautiful guitar picking. It is truly something I marvel at every time I hear it, regardless of how many times I have listened to this record. Every note and sound is perfect (a word I do not use lightly by any means, but easily applies in some way to every song on this album), even the sound of Pajo’s hand sliding down the neck of the guitar. It ends with the lyrics, “my head is empty, my toes are warm, I am safe from harm,” as it builds into a roar of guitar. It is one of this album’s most breathtaking moments, something it is in no short supply of. I can say confidently that if heaven truly exists, this will be the soundtrack to mine.

”For, Dinner…” a slow and tame instrumental piece, follows “Washer” as if to allow a calm before the storm that is “Good Morning, Captain.” This track features some of Britt Walford’s most impressive drumming, and it is where I realized just how much of a genius he really is (he is also the brain behind much of this album, including it’s sequencing). Brian’s vocals are arguably the most important part of this song, as the story works with the musical backdrop to build more tension with each passing second. It is almost as if the entire album was building up just to explode at the very last minute; a soft spoken, “I’m sorry…I miss you” is whispered at the story’s end, then a painful scream repeats the simple, yet powerful phrase, “I miss you.” Quite simply, it is the best finale on any album I have heard yet. According to Britt Walford, during the weekend long recording of the album (yes, only a weekend), Brian recorded those last vocals on a whim, and initially wasn’t sure if he wanted it to remain part of the song. He came out of the booth soaked in sweat, completely drained, and later checked himself into a mental hospital.

After reissuing Spiderland in a box set this past April (which I bought, of course), the band went on a tour to promote its release. Fortunately for me, I got to experience them live when they hit Paradise Rock Club in Boston. I approached Britt Walford after the show (after spending money on overpriced merch I don’t need, naturally) to personally thank him for the impact his music has had on me, as it is some of the only music that I would ever consider “life changing.” I shook his hand, looked him in the eyes, and thanked him for everything he has done. He returned my thanks and looked at me with eyes that would suggest he hears this type of thing every day, but is surprised and humbled every time.
