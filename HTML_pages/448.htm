One of those very deep, personal albums that people either connect with really deeply or think is a pretentious load of crap. My rating makes it obvious which camp I fall into, even if I understand and disagree with those who go the other way. Lennon here opened up something that hadn't really been there in rock music before - naked music. Nothing to do with the dance-lite label of course, but I mean music that's so straightforward and open in its confessional nature that it can be almost embarrassing to listen to. Sure, people wrote from the heart, but never like this: so simplistic and clear, as in "we're afraid to be alone" and "love is needing to be loved" and "who am I supposed to be?" and "I don't believe in bible." It all seems so obvious, and yet find me more music that's so straight to the point. Maybe that's the problem some people have - these ideas seem so fundamental to modern life that they want things a little more complex, and perhaps even supported by more than the basic (and repetitive) piano/gtr/bass/drums/vox that surround the words. Me, I like things simple and direct, even if in my real life I'm almost never this direct. Art is supposed to give you something to aspire to, yes? This goes straight through me like a fucking lightning bolt - it did the first time I heard it ("Well Well Well"'s screaming was what first drew my attention) and it did again today when I listened. One of my all-time favorite records.

This release is augmented by a great A-side and enjoyable throwaway B-side that's still anchored by a terrific guitar riff. I could go either way with the single, and I wouldn't make a decision to check it out based on "Power to the People" (and definitely not on "Do the Oz"), but they sound fine tacked on to the end of the contemporary material from this album, even if they add nothing to the flow.

The antidote to Beatlemania

John Lennon wants you to quit the Fab Four cold turkey, and Plastic Ono Band is the shot of Narcan that instantly cuts off the opiate of the musical massess--in this case, the blissful haze of Beatlemania.

Each song is imbued with confession and stark self-awareness in a way that no album previously (that I'm aware of, anyway) had ever attempted. Even here at the beginning of the 21st century, there are rarely examples of austere self-deconstruction as this album.

Plastic Ono Band is a stretch for producer Phil Spector, also; absent is the wall of sound he had before and since hid behind. The sparse production and instrumentation force you to focus on Lennon's lyrics--one is reminded of Alex in A Clockwork Orange-- so that you can't help but watch the veneer of celebrity and showbusiness being not slowly peeled, but ripped away in front of your eyes.

The dream is over, indeed. An unqualified masterpiece.

John Lennon creates a raw masterpiece and ends any thoughts of a Beatles reunion all on one great album. The album starts and ends with haunting songs about his deceased mom, "Mother" and "My Mummy's Dead", respectively. There are three sadly beautiful songs, "Hold On(John)", "Love", and "Look At Me" as well as two songs that are just stripped down vitriol, "I Found Out" and "Well, Well, Well". "Working Class Hero" is pure acoustic venom whereas "Remember" is piano-based hard rock. "Isolation" is a scary little tune and "God" tears down all the illusions surrounding Lennon and The Beatles. This album isn't for everyone but if you enjoy stripped down, no-holds-barred rock and roll you will enjoy this album. This was, as John Lennon put it, his Sgt. Lennon's. Check It Out!

This album is devastating, simply devastating. It may have lost some of its effect in the last 40 years, but in context with its release in 1970, everyone one knew that this album was the final nail in the Beatle coffin - which was an incredibly huge thing at the time. This was a cathartic album for Lennon, an attempt to exorcise demons: his dead mother, his stardom, his isolation, the long shadow of The Beatles, etc. It is a deeply felt and psychologically intriguing album.

It merits inclusion with the greatest rock albums of all time on the strength of the songs "Working Class Hero" (Lennon drops the F-bomb!) and "God" (the renunciation of messiahs of any sort, including Jesus, Hitler and The Beatles). This aint yer mother's Beatles, and it transcends anything except George Harrison's 'All Things Must Pass' as far as Post-Beatle solo material. As far as Lennon's mother fixation, Roger Waters dealt with the death of his father on at least three or four albums. Lennon at least kept it to one album (and the song "Julia" from the White Album). "Mother" represents the summation of the primal-therapy sessions Lennon attended prior to the recording of this album, and he lets out all his repressed feelings in anguished screams. Balance that with the simple and beautiful "Love", and you have a riveting look into Lennon's psyche.

Lennon's solo debut is wildly contrastable to Harrison's, and in a way is kind of the ultimate example of the divide I place between Part One and Two of the list chronology. Harrison, poor guy. He releases his big debut, and it's one of the best albums ever....but it's the sign of a (temporarily) dying time. The lush sort of Baroque popness of it? Was fast on the way out. That was truly a thing of the 60's, made real by people like the Zombies, Kinks, Beach Boys and of course the Beatles themselves. In a way it wouldn't revive itself until the middle of the 80's when mostly British bands began to recreate lush soundscapes albeit usually in a gothier post-punk style (XTC being a major exception). The onset of the 70's? It points instead largely to Lennon's lead here. True, not so much the sound style, but the general sense of frank earthen darkness here, is much more of what people were on to. Lennon went through a process of therapy called Primal Scream therapy. Basically a sort of naked emotional outburst type thing, no holds barred. Lennon decided to make an album of it! And that's what we get here. It makes for definitely some of the best singer-songwriter type music you're ever likely to hear, hell in so many ways it really laid the foundations for a lot of what singer-songwriters even do. Sure Dylan and his idols are probably the true pavement layers, but the sort of utter naked confession here is no doubt what truly twinkles in the eyes of the Jeff Buckleys, PJ Harveys, and Elliott Smiths of the world (yes, the 90's it seems). Lennon doesn't really let the album be about anything else either, he certainly could have had access to a lushly produced Phil Spector set like Harrison did, but this makes do on emotions and simple tunes alone. It's not all basic guitar strumming sure, there are the harsh little songs like I Found Out that are while simple... aggressively played! This works, well. So why is this only just a 4? Well I dunno, see, I guess it's the lack of enough amazing material. There's some breathtaking songs of course, but for every breathtaking one there's a pretty whatever little extra piece. Lennon is never less than great, but his songwriting sometimes begs for genius. Nothing here feels bad or wasted, rather I think it just should have been longer I guess with a few more glorious moments. As is it feels just...short of being a whole LP. About ten tracks, more than a few of which are tiny. It's simply not enough! Having said that, any record that includes the highlights you see below is nothing short of a real monster. When this album reaches it's great moments you are pretty much listening to some of the best music possible within the confessional solo guy with scant backing music...genre if we want to call it that. State of the fuckin' art. And these lyrics! Yikes! Confessional is right! We go from the funny (though they must have been distressing at the time) takedowns of the Beatles and Paul, to genuinely harrowing psychological self sketches. The first song alone is pretty damn ...wow, but the sad little skit-song the album ends on is just a punch on the guts if ever I've had one. A song like God is nothing shocking at all these days, but it must have been nothing short of scandalous back then. And in some ways declaring himself an atheist was probably a smaller shock than declaring that he did not believe in Beatles. I really do enjoy the shit-talking of Paul as well, the way he sings about him I really do feel validated to paint their relationship as that of the dictator and rebel leader, the religion of Paul, ha. It's almost like Fuck With Dre Day or something... In any case I'm almost grading this like an EP. Small in stature but far far above most 4's.

Rating: 4
Highlights: Mother, I Found Out, Working Class Hero, God

Lennon's first real solo outing is one of the most unique albums ever recorded. No other album really sounds quite like this...Lennon, Klaus Voormann, and Ringo Starr are the main musicians, and the result is a Singer/Songwriter album that sounds nothing like any other album out there. Lennon pretty much spills his guts out on this album as a catharsis of sorts, and he created some of his best music as a result.

"Mother" opens the album with the somber tolling of a bell, before Lennon goes on a rant about his parents and how they abandoned him (or died, in the case of his mother). I love how the intensity just keeps building and building in the song...at the end of the 'primal scream' section in particular. Even when the song is a faint whisper because it is fading out, his strongest screams are at the very VERY end, when you could barely even hear them!

Then there are those gorgeous ballads like the way too short "Hold On" (John), "Love" (featuring piano from Phil Spector), and "Look At Me" (yea I know the guitar line is from "Dear Prudence", screw it), that actually sound at home next to the anger of "I Found Out", "Working Class Hero", "Well Well Well" and "God".

Speaking of "Well Well Well", it is just amazing. That riff owns, and once again, the 'primal scream' section is something to marvel at. Again, the end of it, where he is screaming "WELLLLLLLLLLLL!" so loud and strong that you could actually hear his voice break, that's something that managed to scare me. Listen to the bass during the screaming section too, wow is it awesome.

On "God", Lennon denounces things such as God (obviously), Elvis, Zimmerman and The Beatles, saying 'I just believe in me'. Not afraid to let everyone know how he feels is he? This emotional and introspective palette is truely something amazing though, that's for sure.

No happy pop to be found on this classic, John Lennon/Plastic Ono Band is all heavy emotions. Lennon lets it all out on this record, and the result is one of his greatest achievements, including his Beatles work.

Fans of The Beatles and Lennon had to be shocked when they picked up this record back in 1970. Thinking they'd get an album of catchy pop tunes, instead the album opens with an ominous tolling of a bell, before Lennon breaks into "Mother". As if this isn't enough as it is, by the end of song he is screaming intensely "mama don't go, daddy come home".

With an opener like "Mother", you know right away this is not going to be a happy album. Its going to be deeply personal and full of emotion. There are softer moments however, for example the short "Hold On' (John) is much more restrained and laid back. "Look at Me", which has a strikingly similar guitar line to John's Beatles tunes "Dear Prudence" and "Julia" is another restrained moment. The real highlight of the more mellow tunes would have to be "Love". A piano ballad, and the closest this album ever comes to being accessible pop, the song is heightened by a poignant vocal from Lennon.

Then you have tracks like the stinging "I Found Out" and "Well Well Well", driven by biting guitarwork from John. "Well Well Well" has another one of those 'primal scream' moments like the coda from "Mother", where Lennon screams "WELL!" at the top of his lungs for well over a minute.

Even on the acoustic based "Working Class Hero" the anger doesn't let up. Going as far to call people 'fucking peasants', Lennon's mad at the world and he isn't afraid to show it. At the end he gives us his thoughts on "God", which he claims is 'a concept by which we measure our pain', then gives us a list of things he doesn't believe in, like Jesus, Elvis, Zimmerman (Bob Dylan) and lastly, Beatles. The last part of the song, where Lennon's voice is possibly at its greatest, is one of the best endings to a song I've heard.

"The dream is over
What can I say?
The dream is over
Yesterday
I was the dreamweaver
But now I'm reborn
I was the walrus
But now I'm John
And so dear friends
You just have to carry on
The dream is over"

Hell of a way to end a song, and barring the less than a minute "My Mummy's Dead", the album is over. Deserves all the hype and accolades it recieves, this is one of the finest albums ever recorded.
