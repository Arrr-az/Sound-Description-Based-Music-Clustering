Taking a completely different direction from his critically acclaimed 2015 album, To Pimp a Butterfly, Kendrick Lamar infuses DAMN. with a more trap and less jazz influenced sound. DAMN. also feels faster paced and more neatly packaged than Lamar’s previous two albums. As a result, DAMN. does contain several excellent standalone tracks and radio hits, but it feels less cohesive than good kid, m.A.A.d city or To Pimp a Butterfly. All in all, DAMN. is less consistent than Kendrick’s previous two albums, and it honestly has more skips than good kid, m.A.A.d city and To Pimp a Butterfly combined. This is a really high bar however, and DAMN. is still a very good album. On many of the tracks, Kendrick seamlessly combines his signature story-telling and introspection with tight production and radio sensibilities. Overall, DAMN. is a very solid album, and it demands a full listen from any music fan.

TheCunningStunt Feb 18 2024
+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
The best thing about Kendrick Lamar is that he completely wrote the book on modern Hip Hop and made two undeniable classics of the genre. Particularly with his last album To Pimp a Butterfly where he really hit on something artistically, conceptually and thematically above anything else. It felt like an instant classic even on my first listen, and 2 years on and it continues to be an absolute giant album in modern music. I'd be surprised as an artist if he ever betters it in his career.

Everyone praised its concept and it feeling bigger and bolder than anything else he'd done up until that music, and everything else in music right now. When you have the likes of Drake and J Cole immensely popular, it's refreshing someone like Kendrick is around to really set the standard. After all the praise Kendrick has received, it'd be easy for an artist to simply want to emulate it and make a tired retreat. I think Damn's biggest strength is that it doesn't just replicate that Jazz and Funk production that made To Pimp a Butterfly so celebrated.

He reinvents his sound, takes risks but he sounds so bold and confident in his abilities. He doesn't sound like he's out of his comfort zone which is the best thing you can say about it really, it's a wonderful thing when artists never lose themselves and what everyone love about them in the first place whilst still managing to reinvent themselves, progress and add another colour palette to their arsenal.

He incorporates different influences, the most notable being Trap Rap and on paper, I should dislike the album as I don't really enjoy that style of Hip Hop. I find it incredibly predictable and repetitive, there's never been a Trap Rap album that's really caught my attention and I've never felt it's produced anything other than merely "decent." There was also a lot of concern over Mike Will Made It's inclusion on production, from myself as well but the production on the album is excellent. Is it different to Pimp A Butterfly? Yes. Will people who loved that album and wanted more of that sound be disappointed? Yes. But what the album sets out to do, it succeeds admirably. By now, after the frenzy surrounding "i" and the reaction to "HUMBLE." it's about time everyone trusted Kendrick and his abilities to produce great music.

From the first proper song "DNA." you know Kendrick hasn't lost any of his edge. The song deals with the controversy he was faced with, in particular the whole publicised thing. People in high positions of power and influence such as the media who have absolutely no understanding of the art and culture they're talking about, where they said that Hip Hop has done more damage to young, black Americans than racism which is an utterly absurd thing to say and indicative of how misrepresented Hip Hop is as an artform. It's bitter, visceral, angry and is just a hard hitting banger to really kick the album off and there are so many highlights as well as 'DNA.' you have 'ELEMENT.', 'FEEL.', 'PRIDE'. 'LOVE.', 'XXX.', 'FEAR.', 'DUCKWORTH'. But honestly, there isn't a bad song on the album.

When you look at the album, it was so easy to fear the worst. Just take 'XXX.' as an example. Let's be honest, you see the feature of U2 and immediately fear the worst but it's an absolutely incredible song and I even prefer the half of the song that features Bono! Even the lowest rated songs which have contemporary r&b and cloud rap influences/production such as 'LOVE.' and 'GOD.' are still great, I particularly enjoy 'LOVE' and it's amazing that Kendrick actually pulled off a song like that.

It's an incredibly consistent album and unfortunately it will disappoint some people because for me, it's undoubtedly not as good as 'To Pimp a Butterfly' and 'Good Kid, Mad City' but for what it sets out to do, it achieves its goal supremely well. Now we wait for LP5, and as I type this, there are conspiracy rumours there'll be a second album released on Sunday, and if that turns out to be true (I'm highly skeptical) I'm sure there'll be another superb album to enjoy. Kendrick Lamar, the man can do no wrong.

I felt like I had to return to this review. While it is true that this album was one of my 5’s, it is time to acknowledge the fact that it really isn’t. I will keep this album close to my heart all the time, but there are few issues.

Giving us a dilemma that him as a black individual is or had been facing, the dilemma of weakness and wickedness, surrounded by religious themes of claiming that the minorities of the States are the actual Israelites, if we are being biblically accurate. He nails it, without a doubt.

But, come on, nobody in this fucking world can convince me that GOD. and YAH. are genuinely songs that are out of 10. They suck. Not only that but some songs here loses its replay value when overplayed, like it’s been 7 fucking years and I can’t stand HUMBLE. anymore, even though I am the only one in the middle singing that song, completely memorized it in those school parties. Same applies for LOVE. and XXX. I got in my first listening that XXX. is underrated and it is actually a perfect song, now with over 50 listens to that song, also memorized, the U2 feature is unbearable and it really is not that badass, dude.

I still love the classic moments, though. I can’t deny the legendary moments of this album. The beat switches of DUCKWORTH., the surprising but also able to make every listener question what the fuck is going on which is the intro BLOOD., the actual badass song that is DNA., that eargasmic beat of LUST. which I don’t even know how to really explain, a song that I love not only because it’s good, but it being the outro song of Snowfall’s finale.

Many others make this album a hiphop classic no matter what these redditor-wannabe RYM users tell you. See, the problem is with few songs’ few moments, not the album as a whole.
