The third Rolling Stones album and the first to be issued in stereo. It was also the last to rely on covers - it's successor "Aftermath" is all Jagger/Richard originals and 23 minutes longer than "Out Of Our Heads" 29 mins 36 seconds. However, "Out Of Our Heads" is a fine record and as the excellent "The Rough Guide to The Rolling Stones" states, this sees The Stones moving away from R & B, and while there are no horns, this is a soul album - and The Stones make the transition with "complete authenticity". And Jagger sings really well. Thank goodness, ABCKO issued this as well as the US version - because as an album, this is superior.

Me he decidido por la versin americana de este disco (en lugar de la inglesa) por dos motivos fundamentales. Primero porque es el disco que salio antes de los dos, en EEUU concretamente en Julio, mientras que en las islas apareci a finales de Septiembre. Y segundo para evitar perdernos las dos mejores canciones del disco.

Parece increble, cansados de escucharla durante tanto tiempo, que ''(I Can't Get No) Satisfaction'' se quedara fuera en la versin inglesa. Pero no contentos con quitar la mejor cancin del disco, tambin hacieron desaparecer la segunda mejor, ''Play With Fire'', una maravillosa cancin a guitara acstica con pandereta, clavicmbalo y Phil Spector al bajo. No se ciertamente a santo de que sufri semejante mutilacin el disco, imagino que problemas entre 'labels' o quiz estrategias de marketing de esas que los simples mortales no alcanzamos a entender, pero fueran cuales fueran las razones me parecen insuficientes para justificarlo.

Unos cuantos covers de R&B y Soul tomados prestados a Sam Cooke y a Marvin Gaye, junto a creaciones de los propios stones completaban el LP. En general ya estaban adoptando las races bluseras que mostraran a finales de dcada, aunque aun intentaban convertirse en los 'Hitmakers' britnicos que la discogrfica deseaba, todava a la sombra de The Beatles. Sinceramente tampoco se poda pedir mucho ms, durante su crecimiento como banda iban regalando a cuentagotas temas que 40 aos despus aun siguen siendo clasicazos de primer orden, y eso ya era mucho ms de lo que hacan algunos contemporneos suyos.

7/10

As an impressionable kid, I vividly recall watching the grainy, black 'n' white telecasts of The Beatles three consecutive weeks on The Ed Sullivan Show in February of 1964. My parents never missed Sullivan's Sunday evening variety show via their bulky Zeinth television in the living room. There weren't many viewing options back in the pre-cable era, and Ed's really-big-show was must see viewing throughout the '60s.

Pop culture changed in a major way when the mop-top four from across the pond hit the American television airwaves in early '64. Following the hysteria of The Beatles first appearance on The Ed Sullivan Show, the music scene was about to be introduced to the British Invasion.

The Beatles looked the part, but when The Rolling Stones crossed the pond and made their debut on The Ed Sullivan Show in October of '64, it was evident from the off that the Stones carried a bad boy edge.

In 1965, The Rolling Stones made it big on the strength of "Satisfaction". Driven by an infectious Keith Richards' riff that packed muscle with the use of a Gibson fuzz box, the three-minute plus track was destined for instant-classic status.

"Satisfaction" went to the top of the charts, serving as the cornerstone track from the North American verison of The Rolling Stones' Out of Our Heads LP. By contrast, the English pressing of Out of Our Heads was issued sans the number one hit single.

While "Satisfaction" stands out from the London Records release of Out of Our Heads, the twelve song recording of short cuts also features "The Last Time" and the somber "Play With Fire", plus a wealth of rhythm and blues cover songs, which includes Otis Redding's "That's How Strong My Love Is" and Marvin Gaye's "Hitch Hike".

KNOCK IT BACK!

Stones album number four shows something thats been lacking in their catalog up to this point - some change. With the album no longer mostly made of covers (its a 50/50 split with originals for once), the Stones have finally come into their own proper, and with it is production that carries more roughness and punch. Some great covers mix with the continuously maturing originals to make for what would already be a great album: add in eternal anthems like The Last Time and Satisfaction and you really have a winner.

9/10

Rolling Stones Runthrough #5 - Out of Our Heads
heres where things really start to get interesting. Stones pick up more of their iconic sound that would be more recognizable later in their career and deliver some of their greatest songs yet.

Satisfaction doesnt really need any introduction, and is certainly one of the Stones best songs to date. Additionally, Play With Fire is another beautiful song that is one of the Stones best from this early blues period, a beautiful little folky tune they definitely needed.

Other good songs are the opener, The Last Time is great and crunchy, and the live Im Alright is fun and energetic with a great riff. Some of the worst and most derivative Stones songs fan be found here too though, especially the closer One More Try, which is just fucking awful LMAO.

Certainly the stones strongest effort as of yet though, good. 7.5/10

I can't get no satisfaction...
I didn't want to write a review for this mediocrity, but I was "obligated" after seeing a guy commenting on this in the comment box:

"this is where they went from "one of a dozen mediocre british R&B groups" to "the Rolling Fucking Stones"

NOT EXACTLY! Well, this album finally delivers a great song, the ultra-legendary "Satisfaction", with catchy low-tuned guitar riffs and very catchy and charming vocals by Jagger. "The Last Time" and "I'm All Right" are solid blues rock tracks, they have a great riff.

However, the rest is dull, ultra-generic and forgettable blues covers, not properly original songs mostly, just like their prior albums, which sound exactly like this one and have only 1-2 memorable songs. This was supposed to be an improvement? Just because of "Satisfaction"? I think not! And one great song does not make an album great. Learn this, people.
