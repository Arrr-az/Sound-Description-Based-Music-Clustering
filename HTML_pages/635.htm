Lizzy Mercier Descloux began her recording career as part of New York's late-70's No Wave scene. Her solo debut, Press Color updated several songs ("Tumor," "Mission Impossible" & "Jim on the Move") and is truly a gem. However, it's unbelievable that Lizzy did not repeat this formula for her follow-up album, instead forging ahead like a true artist to explore new sonic terrain. Mambo Nassau is a deliciously sun-soaked affair, with Lizzy embracing Caribbean beats while retaining her fantastic punk edge.

I can't recommend this disc highly enough. The energy is infectious. New fans may need some time to adjust to Lizzy's vocal delivery, but after a few listens you'll wonder how you ever survived without Mambo Nassau in your CD collection.

Greenmountain Nov 20 2021
Vote downBookmarkVote up
It’s sad that Lizzy Mercier Descloux is still nigh-unknown, especially when compared to some of her contemporaries who also came out of the early 80s post-punk scene. She’s just as dynamic and charismatic of a vocalist as Elizabeth Fraser, and her music was much weirder than anything Siouxsie Sioux ever did, but for whatever reason, she never carved out the sort of career that either of those women managed. The best argument for her continued recognition is her second record, 1981’s Mambo Nassau, a fun little slice of mutant disco that still sounds barbed and alien to this day. Building on the chassis of her debut, 1979’s Press Color, Descloux found another gear and built one of the most danceable experimental records ever.

Comparisons to Talking Heads’ Remain in Light (and, to a lesser extent, David Byrne and Brian Eno’s My Life in the Bush of Ghosts) are of course apt, although it seems to me more likely that Descloux and David Byrne were drawing from the same pool of influences rather than Descloux drawing directly from Byrne. Innately danceable post-punk was far from restricted to Talking Heads; the impulse to make the avant-garde more immediate was not necessarily a unique one. What’s more, white audiences started to discover Afrobeat in earnest during the early 80s, and its first adherents tended to be musical auteurs with slightly more open minds than mainstream rock audiences. Mambo Nassau came out strikingly similar to a few other records, but the seeds it sprouted from had taken root years before.

All that’s to say that while on its face there are a handful of other records that do this sound better, there’s enough unique about Mambo Nassau to make it worth your time if you’re into post-punk. It’s simultaneously bizarre as well as catchy, which is no easy feat, and Descloux’s energetic vocal performances are always an X factor capable of elevating the track. Let’s also not forget the unbelievable cover of “Funky Stuff”, which is just as good of a reinterpretation of an already timeless song as “Take Me to the River” was for Talking Heads. The big takeaway here is that while it may lack some of the consistency and replay value of its better known cousins, Mambo Nassau is tantalizingly close to being an excellent record. Swap out one of the less intriguing tracks (why was “Five Troubles Mambo” the single from this record?) for another lean slice of avant-funk à la “Sports Spootnicks” and it could easily be one of the most enjoyable post-punk records ever made. For what it is, though - a marked improvement over Descloux’s debut and one of the most infectiously danceable albums of the early 80s - Mambo Nassau is definitely a success and a solid argument for Descloux to receive more widespread recognition as one of the more creative artists of her generation.

FUNKY STUFF!
Although initially aligned with the New York No Wave scene through her work as Rosa Yemen, and solo with her sharp and playful debut Press Color, Lizzy Mercier Descloux was to prove to be on a different track to the negativity of that scene. Especially with the release of her second album Mambo Nassau. Bankrolled by Island Records and recorded at Compass Point Studios in the Bahamas, home to the Compass Point Allstars which were a sort of Reggae version of the legendary Muscle Shoals Rhythm Section who featured on numerous soul classics. The production on Mambo Nassau was a world away from Press Color quality wise and helped to focus the energy and creativity already in evidence.

Working with Wally Badarou who co-wrote and played, often wonderful, synthesizer on Mambo Nassau, Lizzy Mercier Descloux embraced world/ethnic music that paralleled the work of Talking Heads who also worked with Badarou (checkout his wonderful Echos album). While there is a strong ethnic vibe, this isn't some kind of Paul Simon coffee table cash in from an artistically spent artist. Mambo Nassau isn't some theme tune to a bourgeois lifestyle, the sound here is too joyfully aggressive, tense and ready to burst, exploding with LIFE!

The album is anarchic too, it jumps and skips everywhere, like a kid with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder. It is an itch that demands to be scratched. The rhythms are impulsive, tumbling head over heels, dizzy with EXCITEMENT! Lizzy Mercier Descloux rides the tempest with ease. Never letting its exuberance take control. She is in synergy with the music and sounds ALIVE. NOW!

"Why live down on earth when the sky is so vast and so blue and all is pointless but desire." Lizzy Mercier Descloux
