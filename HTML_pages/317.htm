Josef K are among the vast group of late 70s and early 80s post punk that were and remain fairly unknown within the genre. However, upon hearing The Only Fun In Town, this becomes extremely hard to believe. Josef K’s debut work exudes with an energy and atmosphere so typical of the times that their mainstream failure is beyond surprising; their jangly riffs, post punk soundscapes and just general new wave-esque performance quirks make them fit in with their perhaps more popular contemporaries effortlessly. The danceability and catchiness of this release is an elevating factor of the entire tracklist, as different songwriting ideas can be heard crammed into one minuscule 30 minute record. It is almost disappointing this band stayed very much under the radar as their skill for musicianship and interplay is evident on this record, with their influence, of which can still certainly be heard within today’s post punk output, is wholly undeniable. Overall, The Only Fun In Town is a severely under-appreciated album and offers an instantly enjoyable and recognisably early 80s post punk listening experience.

4 stars

Oh no, the band that is derivative of themselves. Josef K were onto something, sure, but they weren't very smart about it. Not being able to warp their jangle makes for a 30 minute album that's surprisingly full of redundant throwaways. A shame, since it predicted one of the best scenes in indie music. Even at its top jangle best, is the riff of "Revelation" anywhere near as good as other prototypes such as Joy Division's "Disorder"? I would immediately say no. Not a hidden gem I think, but a respectable album.

Josef K is one of the more obscure, short-lived, yet exciting groups to emerge from the post-punk movement. Their jangly romps channel acts like The Fall and Joy Division through the jingle-jangle of The Byrds. The result is a sound that is far more infectious than it is dour, which is impressive given the sheer dissonance of many of the tracks on the lone album released during their tenure, The Only Fun in Town. The LP is replete with tasty numbers like "Fun 'n' Frenzy," "Revelation," "Sorry for Laughing" and "Citizens." Only the dull "It's Kinda Funny" and "16 Years" miss the mark, making this a remarkably tight set of songs. Though the group is distinguished by the guitar playing of vocalist Paul Haig, it's the angular drive of bassist David Weddell that really gives these songs that off-kilter propulsion that elevates them above other lesser acts (like their Scottish peers Orange Juice, for instance). Fans of post-punk should check this album out without hesitation.
