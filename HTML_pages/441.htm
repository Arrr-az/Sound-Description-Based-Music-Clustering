Far and away my favorite Zappa album. I won't go as far as to say it's the only one you need, which was kind of the gist of my old review and not really accurate to my current viewpoint, but it's still the only record of his I've heard where everything works. And yes, okay, part of it is because it lines up quite well with my own aesthetic (there's a seventeen-minute jazz blowout here!), which definitely slants toward a) jazz and b) eccentric melodies, but other Zappa albums do jazz and eccentric melodies but none of the others do it for me at the same level as this record does, because this is an easy jazz classic for me and not even his other jazz albums are.

Now that I've got your attention, I'm going to make a case for either picking it up on CD or at least digging around the streaming/torrenting/whatever service of your choice for the right version. See, Zappa remixed this pretty significantly in the '80s, and the remix sounds a ton better than the original LP mix, which unfortunately is the mix on services like Rhapsody and Spotify. Even if you're not an audiophile, this is still something worth paying attention to. Yes, some of it's small stuff - the violin on "Willie the Pimp" and the woodwinds on "Little Umbrellas" and "Son of Mr. Green Genes" sound better here, but they're not dealbreakers. What you're going to want is the extra four minutes Zappa tossed onto "The Gumbo Variations," because "The Gumbo Variations" smokes at seventeen minutes, rising from a loose bass groove to scorching extended solos for sax, violin and guitar, not to mention a brief but super-nifty bass solo and, best of all, a coda where everyone goes all-out. It's one of Zappa's most exciting pieces, but the short version blunts it, cutting off the bass groove and lopping off some of the best parts of the solos. Listen to it in full and you'll get a better sense of Zappa's feel for jazz.

Now, the rest. Part of what makes this album such a great work is because Zappa picked a great second lieutenant, one Ian Underwood. Zappa may have written all of it, but Underwood plays all of the many, many woodwinds on this album. Because he's the one-man horn section, he lends the album a remarkable sonic cohesion, basically by playing as eccentrically as he can. The results are terrific - check out Underwood's runs on the sneaky and unfairly overlooked "Little Umbrellas" and the skittering, avant-prog flavored "It Must Br a Camel" to get a sense of how he does things, and also check out the keyboards, also his, that dominate that track. And the bass, while we're at it, because I love love love the basslines on this record. He also brings the most famous song here to life, "Peaches en Regalia." I mean have you heard those saxes? Or the rapid organ fills? Or the mallet instruments? It's the happiest song ever and I love it to death. And if you like "Peaches," do stop by "Little Umbrellas." It shows Zappa's musical humor and ability to write a beautiful melody at the same damn time, as Future would have it.

And goddamn does he lock in with Zappa on "Son of Mr. Green Genes." It's built on one of Zappa's best melodies, a nice mix of stately and goofy, and it's also got a thrilling long solo from Frank. Not enough for you? Well, Underwood fills it out with another great woodwind arrangement, with awesome details like the sax-guitar duet segment and some tasty dramatic piano fills, not to mention that huge organ at the end. Zappa's guitar is also a nice complement to "It Must Be a Camel," throwing a blazing solo onto a bed of Underwood's jumpy horns. It's a good combo, believe me.

Underwood doesn't jump out on "Willie the Pimp," but plenty of other people do. Obviously Zappa's towering guitar solo is the main attraction - he does everything here, pulling out lick after lick over the course of seven minutes. Yet an extended guitar solo is only as good as the stuff going on around it, and there's all sorts of interest. Incessant honky-tonk piano, an ever-shifting drum part from John Guerin, unsung bassist Max Bennett (who's on fire all over this record - check out "The Gumbo Variations," or "Little Umbrellas," or "Son of Mr. Green Genes") holding down some serious chaos, and Sugarcane Harris' electric violin, which provides a strident hook in the beginning and hovers all around the mix. Plus Captain Beefheart howling! Stuff just keeps happening in this song, for a rather long time, and it's all the kind of stuff you want to happen on your song.

It took me much too long to finally give Frank Zappa a listen, mostly because his reputation for being anti-mainstream led me to believe that this album was going to be some unlistenable "avant-garde" rubbish that's glorified by hipsters. In reality, Hot Rats is an instrumental fusion of laid back jazz and experimental rock that's easy on the ears and quite complex in its structure. Zappa's production and arrangement on this release are both unconventional and spectactular, with the large combination of jazz, rock, and classical instruments joining together to create a lovely uptempo soundscape filled with strange yet intriguing sounds.

"Peaches en Regalia" is a great intro track, very smooth and jazzy, setting a warm atmosphere that grabs the listener's attention and encourages them to listen further. "Willie the Pimp" features a brief appearance of a gravelly-voiced Captain Beefheart that some may take or leave, but I find the sleazy atmosphere created by extensive use of violins and electric guitar to be really enjoyable. "Son of Mr. Green Genes", "Little Umbrellas", and "It Must Be a Camel" are more uptempo jazzy tracks, while "The Gumbo Variations" sounds like a 12 minute long jam session with prominent saxophone usage.

So - in the sixties, I was very fond of the music of Frank Zappa - and then "Hot Rats" came out in 1969 and everyone was telling me how brilliant FZ was and everyone one seemed to have it. Frankly, I was rather put OFF and I much preferred the less well-known and in my view superior "Burnt Weeny Sandwich". However, my "hipper than thou" attitude was wrong because "Hot Rats" always was an incredible album - six tracks, mostly instrumental - a mixture of amazing guitar jams and highly structure arrangements and Ian Underwood virtually getting a co-credit for his amazing contributions. And of course there is Captain Beefheart singing "Willie The Pimp". I have the 1995 CD which is slightly different from the original vinyl - "The Gumbo Variations" is over three minutes longer at nearly 17 minutes, for example. I understand that the new 2012 CD has gone back to the original vinyl masters. Anyway, whichever version you have "Hot Rats" deserved its acclaim and deservedly is the Frank Zappa album that most people know. Oh, "Peaches En Regalia" should have been released as a single.

Free from the snarky sarcasm, counterculture polemics and topical witticisms of The Mothers of Invention that, to me, are utterly hilarious and endlessly inventive, but tend to minimize Frank Zappa's musical genius and relegates many of his albums to novelty act status and comedic sideshows, Hot Rats is almost entirely without barbed lyrics (except for a brief bit by Capt. Beefheart on "Willie the Pimp"), and the brilliant playing foreshadows the rise of jazz fusion and progressive rock in the 70s.

The sound, composition, studio innovation and jazzy improvisation are entirely foreign to 1960s rock circles. Using a newfangled 16 track recorder (a technology rigged especially for the sessions), Zappa not only could use a limited amount of trusted (and extremely talented) musicians to gain the sound of a much larger band, but he was also one of the first to record a stereo version of the drums wherein every piece of percussion was recorded separately (and the sound is phenomenal for such an early release). Zappa's guitar is particularly ferocious on many tracks (he out-metals much that would later be termed "heavy metal"), and the contributions of Ian Underwood (organ, clarinet, flute, piano, saxophone), Don "Sugarcane" Harris (violin), Max Bennett (bass), and several others (like Jean-Luc Ponty, for instance) are incredible. An essential album for anyone who loves jazz fusion or progressive rock.

40 years have now passed since the release of Frank Zappas 1st album under his name; many hours of his guitar instrumentals and millions of tracks of others guitar chops and pyrotechnical wizardry have been issued since; however, few match the combined impact of intensity and inspiration that propelled Hot Rats to the rank of a case study in Fusion, one, if not The, favorite by his fans album, and a manual in guitar playing any self respecting player forcibly must have in his/her collection; one may be tempted to dig in the search of the reasons/causes that make this a masterpiece, but eventually each archeologist would give his own interpretation and reading of the facts, in what would easily amount to an endless quest;

Moreover, this album easily transcends the guitar issues, as besides improvising its also very much about writing; numbers such as Peaches in Regalia and the guitar-less Little Umbrellas, where no space for improvisation was allowed, were destined to be future working horses for any FZ tribute band worth that title, and although miles away from the intricate obnoxious pastures of 200 Motels or the mind-testing exercises of Black Pages like complexity, they also contributed to elevate the album to the status of Cult record; also, and with its mix of Mingus atonalities, Rock beats, Free-Jazz inflections and overdriven guitar passages it Must be A camel shows how FZ was as advanced as Miles in the search for new vehicles of expression.

Propelled by dynamic rhythm sections, John Guerin, Paul Humphrey or Ron Selico on drums, and Max Bennet or Shuggy Otis on bass, this album counted with a varied quartet of soloists all as inspired as distinct; Jean-Luc Ponty may be called a Jazz-violin virtuoso and its commonly accepted that some of his best work has been made under Zappas guidance, but his is a modest contribution on Camel although in consonance with his Free-Jazz work at the time; noteworthy are the involvement and energy of the Blues based Don "Sugarcane" Harris, who seldom matched the sheer intensity of his contributions to Willie the Pimp and the Gumbo Variations actively bowing or plucking his textures behind Zappa flights or Ian Underwood excursions; the latter is perhaps the huge revelation of the album dividing his skills between the keyboards, the clarinets and the saxes, either aptly delivering the complex arrangements of the shorter tracks or on the wild improvisations of the long ones, his wild sax solo on The Gumbo Variations, the way he tortures his instrument in bold Coltranesque manner and his emotionally charged rendition simply stunning,

As for FZ, his idiosyncratic, exuberant and mesmerizing guitar playing found an optimal carburant in the conjunction of the above mentioned factors and in his maestro like posture, of waiting for the right moment to introduce the tension mounting device, is served for the jaw-dropped pleasure of us simple mortals
