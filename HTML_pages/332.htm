"The best album ever made" - Kurt Cobain

Holy mother of fuck! Where do I start with Red?

I can honestly say that no album is more tight, innovative, provocative, loose, musically rich, powerful, dark, deep, and finally - completely and utterly necessary to the very last note than Red is.

Intelligent, progressive, and varied enough, King Crimson's Red is most probably the best album ever made.

From the opening track "Red" to "Starless", the album bleeds every single drop, and just enough, of pure concentrated creativity.

"Red" opens up the album giving us a taste of tritonal bliss, executed simply and viciously.

This is truly the first grunge album. While 21st Century Schizoid Man opened up the fires of what we call alternative rock today, that initial blast was silenced with the break up of the original band. Lark's Tongues in Aspic and Starless and Bible Black were the first to develop this sound further, but Red is where grunge was perfected and blue printed for future generations.

"Fallen Angel" is a masterpiece, featuring sax and oboe. The song is perfectly executed from beginning to end - the timing, the crescendos, the spacing, the use of strong riffs and changing melodic sections. Night and day are represented within one song, and with the night eventually winning the battle.

"One More Red Nightmare" is the most easily enjoyable song on the album. Once again, the tritone is king. A heavy metal riff blasts off the song, John's vocals exploding at break neck speed. The middle section is where Fripp comes in shredding a heavy funk riff only for Mel Collins to push it even further by blasting the sax. The third verse rushes through and crashes into an explosion of grunge-funk riffing and sax, burning the song to cinders via a 3 minute sax solo from hell.

"Providence" is Crimson at their improvisational best. It starts off slowly and ominously, but John Wetton's bass blasts it into life. David Cross' violin screeches and moans. John builds a heavy grungy funk groove that tears the song to shreds with Bill Bruford following and Fripp ripping his licks viciously.

"Starless" is the single greatest rock song in history. It makes Stairway to Heaven look like a pop song. John begins with a ballad, his singing at it's veritable peak, along with Mel Collins softly playing on the soprano sax. But at the final verse, his vocals explode with agression, leaving Fripp to solo a two note solo while Bruford bangs metal sheets and rattles chains. His solo peaks at two powerful guitar wailings, and then John jumps in and Fripp and him have a duel of sonic power, each one rising closer to climax, and then... Bang! Ian McDonald, God of Crimson, blasts his way onto the scene with his sax, bleeding every last drop of musical rage out of the song, only for the rhythm section and Fripp to thrash their way out of the song twice, reprising the main theme ad inferno.

Red both singlehandedly invented a style of music and perfected in album form by using concise songwriting, viciously loose playing that is so loose that it's tight, and musical variety and fluidity brought forth with the use of sax, the cello break in "Red", and the improvisational nature of "Providence".

No other album is so perfect in it's musicality, deliverance, and artistic inventiveness. Well, Nevermind is a close, close second.

I feel like I owe Robert Fripp an apology. Not that he'll likely ever meet me, but I in the past fell into the trap of dismissing the dude as a massive asshole because he so defiantly doesn't fit into the rock n' roll narrative. And it's true, he doesn't exactly come off like Cap'n Party, not with his love of ten-minute atonal jams (which I've grown to share) or his introverted stage presence (which is probably more the result of intense focus than actual assholedom) and his revolving-door of musicians (hey, why let a thing go on after you've hit your peak?) and his love of titles like "Larks Tongues in Aspic." But hey! The more I come to appreciate his music and the more reports I hear that he's actually the nicest guy on Earth, the more willing I am to just let him do his thing. So he's eccentric. Eccentric is good!

Also good is this whole fucking album, which to my way of seeing things sort of works as follows: the "song" side, and the "avant-garde/jam" side. The "avant-garde/jam" side seems to get the most attention, since it juxtaposes one of the band's most loathed songs against one of their most beloved. Yet I gotta give it up for "Providence!" Yeah, I've dissed it in the past, but there's something compelling to me about how it pieces itself together from stuttering violin-and-guitar meanderings to a clattering monstrous rocker. Shoutout to David "Not Tobias Funke" Cross for making the best on his last appearance with the group, and shoutout also to John Wetton, whose massive bass holds the whole thing together. On the other hand, "Starless" is generally cited as a masterpiece, and it's my favorite of the band's whole career. The massive ending has a way of completely commanding my attention, rendering anything else I'm doing utterly uninteresting by comparison, and it's the brilliant end result of a massive lurching queasy build-up and a surprising sax breakdown. Again, Wetton carries much of it, from his shifty bass in the buildup to his massive rhythms at the end, but Fripp also deserves credit for the world's most beautiful guitar tone, Bruford deserves credit for being the best rock drummer of his generation, and Jesus, those saxes. Plus the "ballad" section it begins with is a gorgeous sort of follow-up to "Epitaph," even though the song goes in directions "Epitaph" could've only dreamed of. It's basically a twelve-minute encapsulation of everything great about prog.

The first side is far less contentious, but it doesn't trade in any of the group's ambition. You get another of the group's great ballads, "Fallen Angel," with brilliantly realized dynamics (check how it jumps from seasick bridge back to plaintive verse without at all sounding awkward), a terrific horn part, and Fripp's heavenly guitar tone leading the charge. Want more? Okay, how about a mighty hook? How about an oboe part? It's very close to perfect, and so is "One More Red Nightmare." This is another song of contrasts, juxtaposing the thwapping rhythms and squealing guitars of the vocal segments against a ghostly sax solo that does the incredible job of sounding like an airplane in unsteady flight. But the real highlight of the side is the title track. Everyone locks in here: Wetton with the approaching-monster bass, Bruford with a hyperkinetic drum part, Fripp with the darkest, meanest guitar tone (ooooooh is that mean) you ever heard. This song dispels everything bad you've heard about progressive rock: it's tightly composed, visceral and a billion times more exciting than some AC/DC "yeaah rock n roll don't wash itself" garbage. Oh, and did I mention the bridge, where Wetton makes his bass sound like a violin while Fripp blasts out stuttering guitars? Fans of "Larks Tongues in Aspic, Part 2" will be right at home here - it struts through another smorgasbord of riffs and highlights the group's enviable skills at the crescendo and uncanny ability to play off one another.

Make no mistake, this is the real deal as rock music goes. It's calculating and complex but also with a raw appeal to it, and it's the fullest logical extension of the sound they'd been tweaking and perfecting since Larks Tongues in Aspic. I know some fans are upset with them for disbanding so swiftly after its release, but I'm honestly unsure of where they could've possibly gone from here. Once you've peaked, what else to do but break up for a while and come back with Adrian Belew in tow, yeah?

Catchy hooks in songs are nice and all, but when times reach a state of utmost normalcy, it sounds all too familiar and contemporary. With music, I don't just look to be entertained, but to also become immersed and to maybe even reach a transcendental state of awareness through this redundant lifestyle in my existence.

I can never get enough of a fix of escapism in life, which is the strongest fuel to keep me motivated to keep living my life. From movies and music, my mind is always craving for new experiences to absorb and to maybe even be inspired to break down more boundaries and apply it within the realm of art or life itself (or both).

When living in a society that sells you mental boredom, it's easy to grow too stoic, cold, distant or alienated and therefore it makes one crave something that is out of the ordinary to provoke something within us, to think outside of our normal boundaries, maybe perhaps all the while helping to keep the mind sane. That is why being open to as much music is a good thing, as well as being open to music that can make one uncomfortable or put one in a constant state of bewilderment. As I have gotten older, I have been craving weirder and weirder music and thankfully I have hit the jackpot with this album. If I would have to add to my list of 'weird' sounding records, King Crimson's album 'Red' would surely be featured on that list.

With 'Red', you get this normal, contemporary sounding production that at times make the band sound like a karaoke band, but to me that serves as the PERFECT contrast to the weird arrangements of the songs, and makes you realize that this is far from anything normal in pop music.

An example I would give is how the song 'Fallen Angel' has these almost mainstream poppy verses that would sound like they would appear in 70's greatest hits rock collection, but once the chorus hits, it abruptly eschews all the conventional sounds of the verses and shifts to much darker territory (while still having a catchy ass guitar riff!). It sounds more fascinating than me trying to describe it. It's just beautiful, but at the same time unpredictable.

And that is pretty evident with the title track which sets the mood with these really weird guitar riffs that carries the odd tone onto the rest of the album. The unpredictability is really evident here, because you don't know where the song is going to go and the way it progresses is just something that's unique to these songs.

With 'One More Red Nightmare,' it starts with this odd guitar riff that abruptly transitions into funky, dancy verses that feels like something so out of left field, but it keeps the listener guessing what these guys will pull of next. With vast shifts in tempos and time signatures, this is a very interesting listen.

All these tracks culminate into 'Starless.' I cannot find the words to do this song justice, but all I can say is that it starts off very slow and moody, until it slowly starts shifting into more darker and sinister atmospheres, until it reaches this beautiful climax that one won't be able to see coming. All I can say is that the first time I heard this, my jaw dropped and it instantly made me grow more appreciative of prog rock.

This is very great album and it is right up there with 'In the Court of the Crimson King.' Perhaps this is superior, I don't know. All I can say is that if you are into bands that create immersive experiences, this album should be listened to. 5/5

Red is an easy album to understand once the proper context is established.

That context must include the pop/prog band Yes, and the effect that drumming for that band had on one William Bruford. Yes would evolve into more hardcore prog as the years wound on, but their early efforts contain plenty of popular cover versions of the hits of the day. As a result, Yes had a strong centering on vocal melody and harmony, especially given the creative impetus of Anderson and Squire.

The English music world was puzzled when Bruford quit the successful Yes to join the fractious (and far less renumerative) King Crimson. And yet he did. Bruford was fed up playing rhythm behind the boys choir. Red is the evidence of this assertion.

Robert Fripp recounts that Bruford was an animal unleashed, especially in live performance. After playing with finesse drummers in the main since In the Court of the Crimson King, Bruford's intensity was shocking, albeit shockingly good.

As one reviews the personnel lineups of the '72 to '74 era Crimson, the trend of fewer and fewer members becomes apparent. Early Crimson is an ensemble affair. By the time of Red, Jamie Muir, Marc Charig and David Cross have all faded away, forced by the hands of Bill Bruford.

Moreso than any other member, Red is Bill Bruford's album. The drum work is astounding. China crashes, polyrhythms, odd meters and anecdotally, a trashcan lid, all drive the malevolent chaos. This was as far from Yes as he would go in the 1970's. Fripp and Wetton got larger, and larger, and still larger amplifiers to compete with the aptly-described bombast from the drum kit.

I could easily write more about the melodies, whole tone scale use, and other interesting aspects of this work. But those are all sublimated in the drums.

This is the first power trio prog-rock album. A necessity for any fans of prog, or great drumming.

This was supposed to be the end of the legendary King Crimson; after Red was recorded Robert Fripp broke up the band and they were never to be heard from again...for another 7 years. However, if this WAS their final album (IE: they never got back together again), it could've very well been the greatest 'final album' in rock history.

Now down to a threesome of bassist/vocalist John Wetton, guitarist Fripp and drummer Bill Bruford, King Crimson released Red in 1974 to little fanfare (top chart position of #66) and broke up in obscurity before the album was even released. As more people discovered the album though, they found a masterwork that is exceptional, to be quite modest. What the band has done with Red is turn the heaviness up to a level that was unheard of for them, and for most of the rock world as well (outside of Black Sabbath). As a result, was this a possible major influence on the genre of progressive metal? Maybe...

The album opens up with the magnificent title track, a excellent display of guitar mastery from Fripp. That opening riff...quite a shocking moment! The entire song is a riff fest, with Fripp throwing off one great one after another, while the rhythm section shakes the earth. One of the band's top achievements, and they follow it up with a downbeat ballad called "Fallen Angel". Lead by a wonderful vocal from Wetton, Bruford's patented polyrhythmic drumming shines. However they rev it up yet again with "One More Red Nightmare", kicked off with that monsterous bass riff. By the middle extended instrumental section, things get freaky; what the hell is that squeaking sound coming from Fripp's guitar? The saxophone flitters about crazily at the same time also. After going back to the main song section once more, it gets crazy yet again before it all of a sudden stops unexpectedly, ending the first side.

The Side 2 opener, the instrumental "Providence" was recorded live. The 8 minute piece starts off as a David Cross violin showcase, with some intimidating bassnotes grumbling underneath. The second half of the song has the full band playing, and I have to say this; John Wetton came across THE greatest bass tone on this album. "Providence" shows that perfectly. It simply has to be heard to understand how incredible it sounds. What comes next though is something that really can't be described...

"Starless" the final song on the album, is not only the greatest song King Crimson recorded, but in my opinion one of the 10 greatest songs ever, at least. At 12 minutes and 16 seconds in length (and it feels too short), the song is a musical journey that must be experienced to properly understand its power, majesty, and beauty. A soft mellotron is first heard, then Fripp's somber yet beautiful guitarline takes over, before Wetton finally starts singing in a heartwrenching and moving tone. The man is easily one of the more overlooked, and greatest vocalists in rock. After about 4 minutes everything though stops, until an omnious bass line is left playing alone. Soon a two note guitar line joins in. Slowly it starts to get louder and more intense as the drums start to play. It just keeps building and building, louder and louder...

Then Fripp begins to play an ascending guitar line. What comes next is simply impossible to explain in words, so I won't spoil it. All I can say is, it is the greatest climax to song I've ever heard, and one of the few times I'd throw around that stupid term 'musical orgasm', because this section simply DEFINES the term.

I have to thank RYM for introducing me to this album. Before I joined, while I had heard of King Crimson, I never heard of Red. This site introduced me to what is now one of my favorite albums, and for that I'm grateful. So, um, yea, not much else to say...other than check this masterpiece out, if you are not moved in some way by this album, music may not be the thing for you.

King Crimsons Red is the third and last of their mid-70s trifecta of albums (the first two being Larks Tongues in Aspic and Starless and Bible Black. As the last King Crimson album before the band broke up for the first time, Red (like their debut album, In the Court of the Crimson King) has gone down as one of the most essential progressive rock records, and in my opinion, Red is the pinnacle of King Crimsons music. At first, I only thought that Red was decent, and no tracks really stood out. The second time I listened to it, I recognized the genius behind Starless. Even now, I believe that Starless is definitely the best track on this album, as well as one of the best of all time. Soon enough, though, I learned to love all of the songs on Red.

One of the reasons why Red is my favorite King Crimson album is because of its incredible display of vocals from John Wetton. KC has made a number of songs prior to Red with wonderful vocals (for example: Epitaph, The Court of the Crimson King, In the Wake of Poseidon, Exiles). In fact, during this era, I think that the voices behind King Crimson were amazing and not used to their full potential. Thats why I love this album. Red has the perfect balance of vocals and instruments, for King Crimson. Wettons vocals are in three of the five tracks, and for each of the three, his performance is quite different. His capabilities range from the sheer catchiness of One More Red Nightmare to the spectacularly soothing Fallen Angel to the absolutely powerful Starless. Each of those songs have a few particular lyrics that I constantly obsess over (Reality stirred me, my angel had heard me / The prayer had been answered, a reprieve has been granted, Snow white side streets of cold New York City / Stained with his blood it all went wrong / Sick and tired, blue wicked and wild / God only knows for how long, Old friend charity, cruel twisted smile / And the smile signals emptiness for me). Needless to say, I love Wettons commanding vocals and the lyrics that he sings.

But theres more to Reds appeal than just the vocals, right? Of course. Lets look at the two instrumentals. The title track opens up the album, and it reveals King Crimsons hard rock side. Red is pretty much a series of a handful of heavy riffs, and its one of the bands more unique songs. Robert Fripps guitar is very admirable in that first track, as its what drives most of Red. Bill Bruford also provides much of the energy in it with his chaotic drumming display (its weird thinking of Bruford as part of King Crimson, rather than Yes). The second instrumental track, Providence, is probably the weakest song on the album, but I still like it a bit. Its almost tradition for King Crimson to have the second-to-last track on each album be a somewhat long, (mostly) instrumental track that experiments with some of their weirdest music, and Providence isnt any exception. Wetton is excellent in Providence with his bass guitar, and Fripps screeching guitar goes quite nicely with the bass.

Dont forget, though, that the other three tracks on Red also rely strongly on instruments. During Fallen Angel and One More Red Nightmare, saxophones are used in the instrumental portions. Its great to hear them, too, because Red is about variety, and those moments bring out the jazz elements of KC. Again, Fripps guitar playing is fantastic in those two songs, in addition to all of the other ones. And last but not least, the highlight of Red: the 12-minute-long Starless. Someday, I want to go into this song further, but for now, Ill do a relatively brief review on it. Fripps mellotron gives Starless its beautiful, astounding atmosphere, which lasts through the whole song. The first four and a half minutes are dominated by Wettons powerful vocals, and after that, it all becomes one insane, unbelievable buildup to the climax near the end of the song. The very end of the track has Fripps mellotron returning back to the calmness that was at the beginning of the song.

Red is such an astounding album from the golden age of progressive rock. Most fans of the genre probably already know about it, but if you havent listened to it before, you must. From how I see it, its King Crimsons best work and one that deserves its praise.

Highlights: Fallen Angel, One More Red Nightmare, Starless
