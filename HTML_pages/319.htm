I could have chosen to write about Unknown Pleasures or Closer both brilliant releases by Joy Division, and instead will mention aspects of both releases here ... but more, I would like to speak to the nature of the albums themselves and the effects they had on me.

As a veteran, one who has seen more then my share of combat, combat related issues, and was probably dealing with Post Traumatic Stress, the rise and discovery of Joy Division was as if a window had been opened in the back of my head for the first time ... the darkness of the music [as found on “She’s Lost Control”] not only flowed in, but it flowed out. No longer was I compartmentalizing, storing, hoarding, turning all of my feelings and experiences inward ... I had finally heard the music that I had been straining to hear since I had gotten back from Vietnam ... and here it was, with all of its splendid darkly haunting verse and notes. I felt that I had found a kindred spirit in Ian Curtis, and when I shared or discussed this music with other service men and women, they too felt the connection with the alienation ... yet all was counterbalanced with a sense of place and belonging.

Both “Closer” released in 1980 and “Unknown Pleasures” released in 1979, probably contributed to saving my life. I woke up with these albums and I went to bed with them, their essence seemed to float like smoke all around me, effected my outlook and attitude ... it was only from allowing Joy Division to draw me so deeply into the dark waters, that I was able to make the decision that I wanted to breath, swam to the surface gasping for air, and allowed myself to sink again, this time knowing that I could make this plunge at will ... no longer was there a driving force compelling me to venture so far from shore. Of course, just because I could see the beach did not mean that I was not drowning ... but there did come a day when I found that I was sitting on the beach looking out at the black waters, uncomfortably covered with sand, wet and cold.

I have not played these albums for years, and opening both of them for this review is actually the first time I have touched them in just as long ... perhaps one day I will have the strength or courage to again let the music contained on these discs wrap around my soul ... but not today.

The music of Joy Division exists in and of itself, it relies on nothing, seemly arriving on this planet fully formed, with no need for development or refinement ... it was perfect just the way it was. I doubt that I could list more then six other albums that exist in that realm ... they burned so brightly, that it is no wonder that Ian and his band burned out as Joy Division so quickly ... leaving us a remarkable body of work to be pondered over for years. It is said that Michelangelo received his commission to paint the ceiling of the Sistine Chapel by drawing a perfect circle, while his counterparts labored over detailed renditions of their abilities ... Joy Division drew the perfect circle ... and I lived there for quite some time.

After all, music like this is all about the emotion, “Joy Division” lets me touch the wound ... truth is often a difficult thing to face, especially when in doing so, the mask I’ve been wearing is essentially stripped away, exposing for all the world to see ... the hurt child, the angry adult, the emotionally shattered, taped together and chemically fortified tower of strength who can hold the dying at 4 P.M. and be sharing dinner conversation at 6 o’clock with a smile on my face, happy to see the company, all while knowing they can’t be leaving soon enough.

*** The Fun Facts: The album art is a visualization, or plot of data, of radio telescope readings picked up from a pulsar. Pulsars are incredibly dense, compressed, rotating stars, in which proton and electron currents moving inside create a magnetic field. The combination of rotation and magnetization form a radio beacon as the star travels through space. Each time the star spins around, a radio telescope captures that signal, which astronomers can then plot on a grid. The one used on Unknown Pleasures is a "map" of the first pulsar ever discovered. Jocelyn Bell discovered the first pulsar in 1967. At the time, she was a graduate student at Cambridge University. She was working with her advisor, Dr. Anthony Hewish, to make radio observations of the universe.

The casual listener may be forgiven for thinking that Joy Division were, well, a bit crap. On those new listening posts that they have in record stores where you can scan the CDs bar codes for a 30 second snippet of each track on an album, a Joy Division virgin may swipe Unknown Pleasures having read the hype that surrounds this band, and thought, "Gosh, that drummer's fills aren't very tight," or, "Crikey, the production is a little sparse," or, "Crumbs, the bass sounds odd," or even, "Is that singer holding the note, or is his voice not quite up to it?"

This Virgin might well be forgiven; I remember thinking much the same when I first picked up my copy of Closer and gave it a whirl on the old stereo. It took a couple of listens before the record overwhelmed me, but ultimately I fell in love with it as it overcame and seduced me.

My relationship with Unknown Pleasures has been shorter, in fact a matter of days, but I wasn't that impressed with it immediately, even though I thought that I had acclimatised myself to the JD sound. It didn't take long until the power of this record overcame me though. At the time I was walking down New Street in Birmingham with Curtis & Co. playing away morbidly in my ears. New Street is where all of the Big Issue sellers, all of the beggars, all of the questionnaire takers, the religious nuts, the animal rights activists and the Church of Scientology cult members lurk and compete for the opportunity to waste your time and steal your money money. It is to avoid confrontation with these folk that I wear earphones and listen to loud music. I have neither the patience nor the time to stop for them. I am not a good Christian after all, so why the Hell should I?

Yesterday though, even my big, shell-like earphones failed to deter one enthusiastic Church of Scientology freak. I saw her coming in my peripheral vision; she must have seen all of the other street urchins fall at my feet as they threw themselves at me in a desperate haggle for my attention, which was otherwise occupied with Unknown Pleasures. But this woman was not to be deterred, I think she perhaps saw me as an easily malleable subject. She scurried along by my side for a good 15 yards, bending and twisting her body trying to get directly in my line of sight which was fixed determinedly on the horizon of shops ahead, desperately ignoring her. When I realised that, not only had I had run-ins with this particular Scientologist before, but that she had not learnt her lesson from those encounters, I looked her directly in the eye, but was drawn to her teeth which had a visible layer of yellowed plaque encrusted on them. At that moment, Ian Curtis reached for the final note of "Day of the Lords" and yelled "When will it end?" and I felt a surge of something fizzy travel up my spine. I felt like throwing myself on the floor in front of this witch and writhing around in some kind of protest that hopefully she would gingerly creep away from.

As it was I politely told her that I didn't have the time for whatever it was she was selling and she shrank back into the crowds like a harpie, swallowed up by the Gates of Hell as it retreats from the surface of the Earth to the fiery underworlds from whence it came.

After this experience I was hooked and found myself marveling at what good songs "New Dawn Fades" and "She's Lost Control" were, both as impassioned and dark as anything on Closer, hitting all the right notes that send that chill down your legs that sends you to the doctor with concerns about poor circulation and amputation. Unknown Pleasures is satisfyingly somber, sinister and uncomfortable. For that reason it is pure gold in my book.

Anyone who has read William Burroughs' Naked Lunch will recognize the term "Interzone" and feel perhaps a little sick and disgusted at some of the horrendous acts that they occupy themselves with in the book. Fitting then that it's title sits among the other nine songs here, songs from a dark and sinister world of despair and wonder in which there is very rarely a light at the end of the tunnel. This despair is wonderfully captured by Joy Division, and is perfect for those long winter nights in by yourself, with nothing but a razor for company.

Undoubtedly one of the greatest albums ever made, and quite possibly the benchmark by which all debut albums should be measured. One thing is for sure, if there's an album being made by trench coat wearing young miserabilists, it will be compared unfavourably to this record.

At the time this was released, Britain was just coming out of the depths of severe economic depression, there had been the Winter of Discontent, the three day working week, and strikes crippling the country. The fairly extreme right wing government of Margaret Thatcher was just taking power, amidst a tide of anger at the outgoing Labour government. None of these issues are addressed by the record, Joy Division were never political, but the public attitude of those times is recreated in the sparse, solemn sound they created, a claustrophobic pessimism is present throughout.

What makes this record stand out for me, is the understated nature of it all. The drums are nearly always at the forefront, echoing, yet clean sounding, the syncopated beats moving each track forward, without ever being forceful. The bass and guitars have a restrained air, as if they exist just to occasionally infiltrate the space left behind by the rhythm. Ian Curtis croons in a almost casual, insouciant manner for much of the record, as if not quite confident in the quality of his voice or lyrics. Only on tracks such as "Day of the Lords", or "New Dawn Fades" does he seem to strain for volume and effect, adding an extra layer of emotion. The album works as a whole, for me, because it's not just melancholy, because at times, the tone is unknown. It's tense, nervy, and unsettled, the album crackles with an aura of uncertain energy, of not quite knowing how to deal with life's realities.

This is one of the few records, that has managed to win popular approval, whilst being truly original, as well as downbeat. That, alone, should tell you something of the quality of the music contained within. A genuine 5 star classic, a certain in any reliable list of 'greatest albums ever made'. Essential. Get the idea?

I played this for a friend of mine. My friend said "Ugh, this is horrible. How can people listen to this? The singer is fucking depressing me. He should kill himself the miserable cunt." I informed him that Ian Curtis hung himself in 1980, to which he replied in a somewhat upbeat manner "Oh, well, good".

Despite my friend being quite jovial about Ian Curtis' death and being massively dissatisfied with Unknown Pleasures, it's one of my favourite albums of all time. It's so bleak and hopeless, and even though it can be difficult to listen to the whole tone and lyrical content of the album always resonates with me. I find it to be one of the most haunting albums I've ever listened to. The heart-rending sorrow and pain in this album is one of a kind. There's lots of albums that try to express their pain and heartache, but no one manages to do it like Unknown Pleasures. It's one of the darkest albums I've ever listened to, and one of the most honest.

I've never listened to an album quite like it. No one comes close to displaying loneliness, isolation and depression the way it's presented in Unknown Pleasures. The whole atmosphere of this album is chilling. This album sounds like a man coming to terms with himself and his death. The album paints a picture - a portrait of a man staring into nothing, feeling nothing and seeing only death. Unknown Pleasures spawned one of my favourite lines from a song ever. - "Directionless so plain to see, a loaded gun won't set you free." I think that line sums up the entire album pretty well. I think it has that special something about it that just separates it from Closer, and I'm not sure exactly what it is because their themes are pretty much the same. I said before that this felt like a man facing his own demise, this was recorded a year before his death, I think Closer is exactly the same but those feelings are amplified and it's a much more intense listen, I think Unknown Pleasures is more accessible but ultimately, it has the better songs. Which is the most important thing.

It pays to really zoom in on 1979 and the development of Post-Punk. Unknown Pleasures comes out right in the dead center of the year, ironically gracing summer of all times with its icy self. In spite of 79' appearing to be the magnum opus year of the genre we see that most of the iconic records of the year come out more on the late side of things. Entertainment!, Metal Box, and 154 are all Fall/Winter 79' releases. Earlier 79' gives us Y, but also mostly some of the somewhat (or much) lesser offerings like Join Hands and Secondhand Daylight. Fellow Mancunians the Fall spanned the year with one record earlier than this that sounds yes arty and weird but also scrappy and uncertain in sound. Perhaps my point can best be said that in mid-1979 Post-Punk was still an emerging hard to define phenomena, not so much a genre (even a wide one). There were a collection of odd art-Punk recordings out there by now, but they seemed to be stabbing in many directions, even when awesome they didn't necessarily communicate that they knew where it was all going, or that they had anything so in common as to be centered around.

And then this came out.

Like a new formed star it suddenly inevitably seemed to provide an orbital core for everything else, from the still punk-like mutations, to the weirder avant-garde outings. Moreover it seemed to provide a firm new grammar and foundations kit, befitting a genre actually different from punk rather than just growing off of it. And perhaps most important of all? It felt absolutely sure of what it was and what sounded like. No Joy Division were never true pioneers of Post-Punk (a title I most bestow to Wire, but a factor shared by Magazine, PIL, and the Banshees at least). They were something greater, the ones who filled in the new ground paved out by others and truly laid down a new city there. There is after all a reason for this being a sacred cow to a lot of people.

And of course a great deal of that was luck, accident, and the result of differing visions clashing together. Because that's always the actual way these sort of things are behind the scenes. No one involved seems to have truly had the final product in mind. Martin Hannett envisioned some sort of urban rock statement involving bizarre production experiments, his interest in rockin' out would seem to have been less the focus. Meanwhile most of the band was interested more in rockin' out! Certainly Peter Hook and Bernard Sumner (our two axemen) were dismayed early on after hearing the final results of their studio time with Martin. They considered him to have sawed down the edges and energy of their prized live performances. This end result if anything seems to have been two forces tugging in two directions, snapping like a cartoon elastic into the middle result of a shocking hybrid.

The enduring popularity of Unknown Pleasures really might be exactly that, that most core appeal of Post-Punk itself. It feels like the absolute mathematic mean of what makes the genre up. Both sides of the deal, both things that we come to it for. While we're here talking about the genre's most famous product (aside from U2) why don't we also really lay it out there? What we actually love about it? The concept of vanilla Punk is naturally appealing isn't it? The idea of faster tempos, of energetic guitar playing that prizes power but also a scrappy simplicity, none of those lumbering vainglorious pyrotechnics that Metal plays around with. But... that is more the idea of Punk. In practice it turns out that well, just messing around with that basis gets old quick. For us anyway. Others never seem to tire of it, and even want it simpler and scrappier and faster. An alien desire that results in Hardcore. No thanks.

Instead what if we take that wonderful basis and start... I dunno... messing with it? Pushing it to do all sorts of stuff. Atmosphere? Experimental touches? Textures? Mood? But not forgetting at all to keep that raw pulse somewhere around, lest we start floating off into bloated Prog type shit, naw god keep that at bay too. Let's make this shit interesting but have it kick ass a certain way, make sure it retains something of that concrete rough. All of this description in essence is, in no purer form, Unknown Pleasures.

And the funny thing is? Really listening to it makes it's hybrid nature clear, in the AWKWARD sense almost. The songs where the aggressive Punkier spirit poke through versus the songs where Hannett really took over. At times it even seems to threaten to feel a bit imbalanced, but those are fleeting moments (or mostly just "Interzone"). Sometimes I think coming back to this as a Post-Punk fanatic has meant frustration toward those parts. How dare they not have 100% songs that hybridize! But ultimately this works out, beautifully this is one of those sacred cow records that isn't perfect... but turns out that perfection works for it.

And why? Well in the band member intros I described how a big appeal of the New Order years was an odd mix of precise professionalism with vulnerable amateurism. An endearing and extremely good thing in my book (and very Post-Punk come to think of it). But truth is I'm starting to think the band embodied this dichotomy from the very start. This is significantly less audible on Closer, but man its allll over Unknown here. The fact that you're hearing recent Punks who like slashing through live sets, as filtered through a mad genius producer, is so so clear after awhile. And it begins to charm you rather than put you off. You end up coming back around and realizing that actually this appeal was working for you all along. This glues together the disparate pieces into the whole.

Well... that and Ian Curtis. I didn't mention him in the earlier blurb regarding the album's divide and that's on purpose. From what I can tell to a degree he was with his bandmates in being unsure about what Martin was doing to them on record. But on the other hand it's plain as day that Martin saw him as they key to the whole band (and just him), the one he actually cared to work with and the one he needed to use with his experiments. And this too shows. In essence Ian is also the glue. He sounds every bit as fitting on the punky charge of "Interzone" as he does directly afterward on the glacial ink black mood of "I Remember Nothing". He gives the punky stuff a deeper mood just through his performance, and he inhabits the shadows like a natural home. He more than anything is what makes it all feel like the same aural universe.

And what an aural universe it is! This beyond a doubt is the main reason that so many people have come to regard this so highly. It is an amazing and unforgettable snapshot of a particular sonic environment, in such a way that this honestly is what makes it so pivotal for the Post-Punk genre. Part of my whole point earlier was emphasizing that they ended up producing a Sound for what was more a phenomena prior, they made it genre classifiable. Here this is accomplished by embodying at least two of the major things we associate with what Post-Punk even "sounds like".

First: Shadowplay

Dark. Darkness. Darkestness. Face it, for all the hipster posturing that comprises the Post-Punk fanbase? Most of us at the end of the day are attracted to it for some of the same reasons people are attracted to goofy Death Metal bands (in spite of their similar posturing about complexity). We like the dark aesthetics. Put another more flattering way we like the mood. The very evocative moodscapes. Which are yeah, dark as hell. For whatever reason people round the world in all sorts of walks of life are attracted to this sort of thing, in all sorts of genres. Maybe it's the old "wrap yourself in the scary thing to feel stronger against scary things" chestnut. Maybe it just seems refreshing sometimes. Maybe stylized depression and unsettling vibes makes enduring those things in real life less intimidating and more... engaging? Maybe its just cool.

But there is little disputing an aesthetic of shadows is played out with aplomb across this record, hell if most people only know a little about Joy Division its this much they know. Of course Unknown Pleasures is really more the fun dark one, less depressing (generally) than it is the cool kind of dark. More about intensity than crippling crushing despair (that's next time!). Now of course an aesthetic of darkness moved into early Post-Punk really damn fast. Even while Howard Devoto tried his hand at the upbeat sounding weirdness of Real Life, others were pushing artistic mutant Punk directly into unlit alleyways. A process largely helped along by PIL and especially Siouxsie Sioux and her gang of Banshees.

To some degree this was expected. British Punk had been largely a cynical thing, heavy on topic and pissed off in feel. If you start getting artsy and a bit slowed down with those? Well I imagine the natural thing was the music getting farther and farther away from the buzzsaw bubblegum pop of the Ramones. And one the patron saint of all things late 70's in the UK was Bowie, who had been showing off moodier soundscapes recently (other leading light Iggy Pop had always shown the dark side of Garage Rock buzz). Ian Curtis was a big fan of Bowie, but just as much it seemed he had a fascination with cold electronic things like Kraftwerk. Something I'm not sure factored in for earlier Post-Punk releases. Together these sort of things create here a new level of darkness.

Unknown Pleasures in effect sounds like Post-Punk actually realizing it was dark, and deciding "Well shoot, why don't I embrace that?". Something even the earlier two Banshees records don't even seem quite ready to do to this extent. Sure ultimately The Scream beats this to that, but it can't remotely hold a candle to how expertly this concocts a shadow. This has been so successful at this end that subsequently lots of folks have filed Joy Division under Goth rock. Now honestly I don't care at all about that label in terms of genre description. Goth rock isn't a genre in my reckoning, more a secondary characteristic that can define stuff in many genres. But if we are thinking specifically of the Post-Punk variety colored with that tone? Yeah, I do get it. But I don't go all the way on it.

Goth rock to me as a descriptor requires I think some degree of Kabuki theatre, an almost pro-wrestling degree of depression and macabre pageantry. The kind of stuff lots of goth fans would deny, but much like wrestling fans? Knowing its not real is completely beside the point or fun of it, and people who bring that up as a flaw are hopelessly clueless. Goth is stuff that ultimately is fun even when it makes you scared or sad. It's the ultimate embodiment of what I was trying to describe above, or making negative things bearable and even empowering through creativity and play. This describes the Cure for example, who even at their darkest remain a theatrical experience. For me then Unknown Pleasures doesn't fit, I don't really find this theatrical in the slightest.

And yeah probably that's influenced by Ian actually killing himself, but I just never see this as putting on a persona or character. Yeah, even knowing Ian's actual speaking voice wasn't this dirgy frog baritone, even knowing the rest of the band were a varying collection of "lads" as the Brits say. With Peter Hook being only so many steps removed from seeming like he should hang out with Motorhead. Even knowing all that it doesn't matter. This is not a darkness of pancake makeup. Which is what brings me to the other key thing.

Second: Shadowplay Inc.

So Post-Punk is dark. But like, other stuff was dark before it. Prog got dark often enough, early Metal played around that area obviously, and you had the Doors and Velvets going there in the 60's. The big deal about 1977's Punk and afterward of course was proliferation of urban styles yes, and yes that is where I am going... but I'm being even more specific. Because so much of what ended up defining Post-Punk was industrial crumble, And this too I've talked about a ton, yadda yadda. But damn it, its basically impossible NOT to discuss here. I've talked a bunch about it with my Fall reviews, and wouldn't you know it? Here we are again, same time and same exact place. Manchester.

I'd wager aside from Detroit that Manchester might be the global capital (well at the time) of post-industrial decay. It seeps into these bands from the era, in some cases front and center. This is after all the first major release of the Factory Records label, yes Factory. If you think that is a coincidental name, it ain't. It is a very deliberate reference to Tony Wilson's beloved Manchester and what lay about it in ruin or near ruin. And it annoys me I suppose that a whole genre of bizarre electronic experimentation has stolen the term "Industrial" for all time. Because Joy Division embodies the concept here so utterly.

This is if anything the only real time they would fully go there. They'd always sound urban and industrial in subtle ways, but here it was overt. And this is in damn near whole the work of Martin Hannett. I mentioned it in his bio entry but the man admitted to being straight up fascinated by urban environments. Old warehouses, alleyways, things like that. They flipped switches in his brain, reflecting things he wanted to sonically capture. And fuck yeah honestly. With so much previous rock humping rural aesthetics, it was inevitable that people whose main world was brick and steel would get tired of not hearing their world. Punk sounded of the city, but I don't think it was self-aware of this fact. Post-Punk comes together partly on increased recognition of this I think, but it didn't have a full on direct enshrinement of this until Martin came along.

In some ways the industrial feel comes through literally, it's easy to forget the use of recorded sound effects on some of these songs but they're not exactly hidden. "Insight" opens up with the metallic lurch of a factory elevator, "I Remember Nothing" opens with stuff being thrown, breaking glass. But most of the record approaches this very indirectly at time. Hannett famously did crazy things in the studio like record each and every instrument entirely separately, and a litany of semi-legendary acts of craziness particularly with poor Stephen Morris and his drum kit. The kind of stuff that must have driven the band mad, and in fact did seem to contribute to what they didn't initially like about the final results. Making the sound seem muffled and robbed of full on power... but that was the whole point.

Even at its most confrontational there is yeah, something in the way of this record. Something that keeps its pieces from coming as one, forming a fist, and clobbering the listener. Hannett denies the music every opportunity it can get. The result is vaguely alienating, partly distant, a bit automated. It's been said he made Morris's live drumming sound like a crude drum machine for instance. But this is perfect, it turns the record into a thematically sonically industrial experience. For all the simple pleasures this offers, the WAY it is made to come across has surprising depth and feel to it. You FEEL the numbing deadness of an abandoned man-made space, the claustrophobia of stacked urban living (a feature extremely well known to many Brits of this period, they suffered a damn near identical version of our own notorious housing projects). The dehumanization of all of this. Even the concept of open factories, the negatives that came with the drudgery and repetition of an assembly line job. All of this and more is carefully laced through the contours, guitar lines, and percussion of this album.

That's freakin' cool.

The Songs

The nitty gritty, let's do it. As one might expect, as a hybrid album this offers songs that filter into varying extremes and the middles.

Type One: Punky/Rocky
A loose descriptor in our case here. Basically these are the more ordinary songs on the whole, those least dipped into the shadow chemicals. Not that this means they have happy tones or anything, far from it.

Interzone is... annoying in a way. Not for any fault of the song exactly? But all my descriptions of this as a record that coalesces into a brave new mixture kind of trip over their shoelaces because of this one. Its the sole song on here that showcases their vanilla Punk origins, sounding the most like the Warsaw of old. Tucked away as deep in the album as possible without taking the place of the grand finale, it really feels almost like an obligation, maybe by Hannett. Like the band wanted there as proof of their aggressive side, but ultimately it feels like it would have been better left off and swapped with Digital or Glass.

New Dawn Fades is a favorite of many, and this makes a sort of sense to me I guess. This category of songs after all covers stuff that the average rock fan might most relate to without pre-existing taste for Post-Punk. In this regard New Dawn has always felt a bit more dull to me. It is unquestionably a nice song, but it almost feels written earlier, before they really discovered themselves and before Hannett's influence got huge. While at the same time clearly coming after the Warsaw punk days. Having said this though I do find it maybe the most emotionally sad track aboard, having stylized itself the least this is maybe to be expected. It also reminds me a bit of slower sadder Iggy Pop material, or the downer side of Glam. While also feeling like a call forward to strummy melancholy indie rock of the new 00's.

For me though the definite best of the rocky bunch is Shadowplay, which forms the ideal meeting place of a more basic rock setup and "sounding like Joy Division", short of course of any of the peculiarities of the more "produced" tracks. Of course Shadowplay in particular still gives a sense of being the kind of song they would have maybe blazed through live. It has a great steady energy, that gives the impression of being ripe for a sudden burst of speed (that never comes). And the main chorus feature is essentially little showcases of Bernard Sumner riffing out a tad. This is all assumption, but it feels like Hannett refused to let this one go off in studio. It does kind of seem a shame, but it makes sense for the album. As is it ends up embodying a similar energy to how I described Digital.

Type Two: The Dark Weird Zone

These songs are in some ways the true life blood of making the record sound and FEEL the way it does. They're the ones that focus more on mood, which doesn't always make them the standout tracks? But does make them important bit players in the experience.

On that last count I think mostly of Candidate and Wilderness, both of them dead center on the the two vinyl sides as maybe not coincidence. As songs neither (especially the former) really feel fully developed in the verse/chorus/verse sense, and feature the shortest running times outside of the predictable rush of Interzone. This hobbles them on really ever being fully memorable things in of themselves, and yet every listen has me appreciate them, and most of all their definite success at picking a texture and vibe to push into the experience. Candidate for instance feels like a sort of interlude between the opening double salvo and Insight, letting the album just kind of stop and take in the surrounding darkness for a minute. Wilderness by comparison is a much more energetic song, but still has the feeling on being more about providing another stopover in checking out the scenery.

But this wouldn't be a stone cold classic record if the interesting mood pieces just provided side flavor.

I Remember Nothing is the big finale, a glacial pitch black dirge that sees the band mostly noodling darkly as Ian seems to wander his haunted self out of the listener's sight. Slipping away into darkness. In many ways it seems to preview Closer and the sort of songs that got emphasized next time out, but just as is it makes the perfect ending to what you've just heard.

And then there is Insight. Which might actually be my favorite track on the record, definitely in my top three anyway. Of the moodier pieces this one most works as a SONG, and man what a song. It doesn't really have verse/chorus/etc either, but it doesn't need it. What keeps it from being pure mood piece is that the band provides it with an insistent underlying anxiety, that incessant bass line that refuses to relax while also never getting aggressive or loud. Playing alongside the main thick atmosphere of snowy blackness and plenty of Hannett sfx. Ian inhabits this one better than maybe any track on here, clearly recognizing that he should be singing in lower energy while matching the intense smolder. The final effect is more than just dark, it sounds wonderfully ALIEN. The farthest out in some unsettling Upside Down dimension of any of these tracks, the polar opposite of the rocky numbers.

Type Three: True Hybrids
The heart and soul of the album, including definitely two of the three elites. These are songs that either best fuse basic rockier instincts with the alien Hannett touch, or with the slower dirge-anthem approach they were starting to develop. So in sound or structure, as mixed with the basics.

So the structure example of course refers to Day of the Lords, the perfectly placed second track. After the charging rush of the opener this one puts its foot down and allows the listener to glide in more deliberately over the dark landscape (aided along by Curtis intoning "This is the X"). It lacks really any Hannettisms per say as a song, but unlike Shadowplay for instance I could never imagine this one as anything but a slow grinder. Getting by largely on some wonderful guitar noodling (and some great icy buzzsaw work at points) it really does lean toward basic rock pleasures, but never in any way other than slow. The funny thing is nearly every time I listen to it I go through a whole "Hold on why do I love this one again? It's kinda dull after Disorder" and every single time by the end of it I'm like "Fuck yeah, that song is fantastic, why did I doubt it?". Every time man. It goes to show how it demonstrates the potential power in their slower side, like I Remember Nothing it works as a great preview of next time around.

I mentioned Disorder, so lets talk it. Its simply one of those all time great album openers, a song I basically always feel down for throwing on. An embodiment of everything awesome about Joy Division all in one place, Post-Punk's appeal almost perfectly captured. Here we have a song whose Punk genetics are undeniable: It blazes along at a fast pace, uses choppy untamed guitar, and kicks ass for lack of a better phrase. Meanwhile it is also a harrowing work of anxiety and surrender to some darkness, a downer melody with a singer who sounds like he's solemnly holding on for dear life as the ride gets more and more out of control. This of course is a common theme with Joy Division, audible both in lyric and music, loss of control. Losing a grip on things.

Talking about what drives Ian and the band's sound of this period is honestly boring when we just talk about "depression", or "dark". Which I suppose makes me thus far guilty as anyone. Because really what haunts Ian, and what became an albatross around his regular life, was exactly this. Losing control, losing a semblance of order in your life, feeling like a pawn on the board with no good moves left. These were things Ian would increasingly feel all to fully in his personal life (as mentioned in the bio). What might be creepiest about the album is how Hannett's production truly taps into this.

Way back in my original Rock List review I talked about how the record has a built in claustrophobia, even when it feels spacious and darkly cinematic, it feels VERY constricted. Like you're trapped with the band in some enclosed metallic echo chamber with low lights. Words like "tinny" are often employed to describe terrible production, but as a possible sound it can be wielded on purpose to great effect. And this really is exhibit A, as a record. The hybrid songs best show this I feel. And yes all this talk of "loss of control", and restricted sound leads us up to She's Lost Control.

It isn't even my favorite song from the Joy Division period of the band, at least I don't think so. But it is THE song, of that era. Yes, more than Love Will Tear Us Apart. It's the song because it best summarizes them in sound, the quintessential awesome Joy Division track. In it's own fashion it might even be the quintessential example of the entire Post-Punk genre. The point where everything on the record I've mentioned becomes most perfectly and thoroughly mixed. It rocks. It sounds like the dark side of Pluto. It feels energetic. It feels trapped and hopeless. It kicks ass. It crushes the soul. And for the Curtis devotees it might as well be his most direct personal anthem. Creepily enough sung about an epileptic girl he met at work, when soon he himself would exhibit that same disability in the worst sense (and yes, that's the real story, the Control movie didn't invent that sequence of events).

Ultimately what makes the song haunt the most is how it sounds like it is constantly building up to something huge but never actually bursts. Because that's not the kind of loss of control here. This is Post-Punk, not Punk. The loss of control is slowly going numb and confined, going berserk while standing still.

And hey pardner', if you've liked what you heard? Welcome to one of the best genres to ever exist.

An undisputed classic for its relevance in financing, innovative production, and underlying mood and mystique, "Unknown Pleasures" truly lives up to its name and brilliantly mysterious album cover.
Even though this band has been analyzed to death there's so much about this album that remains unknown... and it is indeed a pleasure. The less you know about the band, Factory Records, and the cultural backdrop behind this record, the stronger its spell. Unfortunately, like most people, I heard this a long time ago along with the rest of the Joy Division cannon. It remains an important musical landmark and is still more interesting than anything being made today.
One year before the Unknown Pleasures recording sessions with producer Martin Hannett, an unsigned Joy Division recorded what was to be their debut album at Arrow Studios with John Anderson. The band was unhappy with the tinny and cheap sounding results, which can be heard on the "Warsaw" compilation. One can easily tell that at this point, Joy Division was just another generic punk band with very little promise. Of course, Factory Records and Martin Hannett were able to transform them into the Joy Division of legend with the Unknown Pleasures sessions.
It is well known that unusual sounds and techniques were used, but they alone weren't what made Joy Division amazing. It was Ian Curtis' lyrics and voice. Mark Hannett should be given a lot of credit, not only for the skillful separation and treatment of the instrument sounds, (especially the drums) but for nurturing Ian's artistic side. The "separation of the band members" was practical not only for the quality of the audio, but because the rest of the band clearly wanted a more punk sound. This is evident with Hannett's use of the guitar, which is basically used as a sound effect in songs like "Candidate" and "I Remember Nothing".
Hannett's encouragement for Ian Curtis to express himself culminated in the brilliance of "Closer", however, Ian's prominence as a lyricist resulted in tension in the band and his marriage. Clearly, Ian's belgian mistress Annik was more receptive to his intellectualism than his wife, whom he'd married before the band existed. These tensions obviously contributed to his illness.
Still, background aside, the album must have really blown listeners away in its time, especially if they were expecting punk rock.
Though not as concrete and confident as "Closer", "Unknown Pleasures" employes innovative sound techniques, showcasing a startling lyrical depth, apparent especially in comparison with modern music.

At the heart of Unknown Pleasures was the alarming vocal talent of Ian Curtis. His alien wails, halfway between the distorted soul of Jim Morrison and the disquieting baritone of Scott Walker, echoed expressionistic vistas of urban alienation over No Wave tribal beats and Gothic guitar impressions. And despite the breathtaking intensity of the angular acid comedown "She's Lost Control", the soaringly depraved detachment of "New Dawn Fades", the proto-slowcore "Candidate", and the epic beat poem-turned-punk chant "Interzone", the opening track "Disorder" remained the piece years ahead of its time and most immediately enduring. A simple song, the deep dark motorik snare beat fit perfectly into an epileptic bass line, which, in a mesmerizing harmonic twist, gave way to a melodic guitar motif that enveloped the whole track in a beautiful atmosphere. Then Curtis' fatalistic, apocalyptic lyrics set in, trading off with spastic guitar solos and finally imploding into an insane shouting complimented by a symphony of atonal guitars. Apart from this, there was also the timeless "I Remember Nothing", whose epic convulsions equated the sparse and the operatic. Unknown Pleasures may have been a monochromatic work, but it was a vision so uncompromising and haunting that each track was worth its length. Essential.
