Echo and the Bunnymen are now _really_ getting away from their strict post-punk sound. I don't know quite how big their ambitions were when they were making this, but my initial reaction was that this was sort of like a _Forever Changes_ of the 80s, with lots of 'majestic' string arrangements and pianos and stuff. Of course, _Ocean Rain_ doesn't have anything resembling "A House is Not a Motel" or "Alone again or" so this can't help but pale in comparison. Actually strike that last comment, this LP does have "The Killing Moon," a song that completely deserves all the acclaim that it gets and more, but no other songs can stand with that one. They've toyed with psychedelic flourishes before, but now they're integrated into lavish orchestration, and some of the time it works, other times they're just dressing up average songs with generic film score strings (like the title track). When the songs are more lively, the orchestration usually fits better. Then there's stuff like "Thorn of Crowns" which is the most blatant rip of the Doors they've done yet - Ian McCulloch whispers, coos, and shouts just like Jim Morrison, and if it weren't for the recurring psychedelic guitar hook, I'd think it was a joke that fell flat. Even the songs that are less dressed-up don't have that post-punk guitar sound anymore, in fact they feel closer to jangle-pop. Two such examples, "Silver" and "Crystal Days," both remind me of another album rated way too high on this site - 16 Lovers Lane. _Ocean Rain_ is definitely more good than bad, but I don't agree with the 'canon' that ranks it as Echo's best album.

I will always associate Echo & the Bunnymen with flight. The first time I was ever on a plane, a flight off the island at four in the morning with the sun just cresting the horizon, they were what I was listening to. It was the swoop of "The Killing Moon" that accompanied that giddy feeling of leaving the ground behind. It was a tiny puddle-jumper plane, shaky and flying low, and I could recognize the landmarks we were passing over, then over the channel to the mainland, Ian McCulloch singing of 'screaming from beneath the waves.' That December trip from Vancouver Island to Calgary, icy and turbulent into the rising sun, is conjured up every time I hear this album's elegant sweep. I put it on and I rise above the clouds.

Ocean Rain is a stunningly beautiful album, all midnight hues of celebratory heartache. It breaks from their jagged early post-punk sound, adding a vibrant, cinematic string section. 'Whatever burns, burns eternally,' McCulloch sings on "Nocturnal Me" self-examining the gothic tendencies of the band, the themes of darkness, fire, ice, religion and doomed romance, vampiric love songs. That cover art, of the band paddling a boat through deep blue water caves, McCulloch testing the waters ahead, is gorgeous and evocative. Like most of their album covers, there is a certain mythic heft to it, a sense of something timeless and vast, oceanic. They did feel that what they were making was the greatest album ever made, and that confidence saturates the music, along with cold, European rain and battering sea storms, even as the band was starting to bristle and break apart.

Those drowned, echoing piano notes in the shimmering "Killing Moon" falling away into the background are a perfect induction, everything in the song is in a state of dreamy delirium, Will Sergeant's glittering guitar feints reminiscent of Tom Verlaine's on Marquee Moon. It is the album's serene climax, from the rising power of the first group of songs and off into the incredible elegance of those that follow, the last four songs on the album the strongest run of songs the Bunnymen ever made, perhaps indeed produced by anyone in that decade. 'B-b-b-burn the skin off and climb the rooftop, thy will be done.' "My Kingdom" is vicious with fire and damnation and then, finally at end, the vast and magnificent title track is icy absolution in ebb and flow. 'Screaming from beneath your waves.' Perfection itself.

 atomicWedgie May 01 2009

All hands on deck at dawn
I've never been one to pretend to write objective reviews. Objectivity is the stuff of science and mathematics. This is music we're writing about on this site. Music should be felt. It should be lived. Analyzed and studied? Sure, but to really understand one has to feel it and experience it, not just hear it. My reviews are almost always about how I relate to the music. I may explain how it has impacted my life or how it made me feel or situations that I'm reminded of when I listen to the music. It's really about me, though.

It's all about me.

I'm making a point of noting that because some reviews, especially those for the important albums in my life, are more subjective than others. Some music is inseparable from memories, even from my identity. Would I be who I am today if I'd never heard The Unforgettable Fire or Mercury or Nevermind or Ten or Zenyatta Mondatta or Moving Pictures or This Year's Model or Slow Train Coming or ¡Alarma! or This Is the Sea? I don't know. I seriously doubt it. Are these great albums? I don't know that either. I just know that each I've mentioned, and many others, has been a big part of my life and I think has shaped my identity.

Ocean Rain is one such album. I can't really tell you how good it is. To me, it's a monumental album. I've listened to it so often I don't so much hear it as remember it when it's playing. It's part of my life, part of me.

I was introduced to the record my sophomore year of college at Western Oregon State. I'd transferred from Simpson College, which was still in San Francisco during my freshman year (It's since moved to Redding, CA). I lived in the dorms my first semester there, which was actually a bit of a drag. I'd made some great friends at Simpson, some of whom I'm still close with (including the beautiful, complicated woman I'd marry and the mighty cheeselover), but the folks at WOSC were a different breed. There wasn't a sense of community there. WOSC was full of jocks who hadn't yet figured out that they weren't the top dogs anymore and pear-shaped girls who wanted to be teachers. I spent two years at that school and I didn't make a single long-term friend. I did meet some interesting people, though, including a guy from New York who turned me into a baseball fan. There was also a little, ferrety guy who had a small but interesting record collection. He didn't like to share his music with people, which was something I didn't understand at the time. I still don't honestly, but I think it was a protective reaction on his part. I convinced him to loan me a few records I'd been dying to hear, one of which was Ocean Rain. A copy of Ocean Rain had been kicking around our floor of the dorm at Simpson, but somehow I'd never been able to get it in my grasp. Ferret Boy wasn't happy to loan it to me, but thankfully he did. The recording I made of his record instantly became a staple in my car's tape deck. The flip side of that SA-90 was The Queen Is Dead, which is another one of these monumental albums. Yeah, baby!

So, anyway, if you want a description of the album or an objective review (whatever that is), I'm the wrong guy to ask. Let's talk about the bonus material on this release, though. "Angels and Devils" is a bonus track and it's OK. It's a good Echo & the Bunnymen track, but definitely not worthy of inclusion on Ocean Rain. The rest of the tracks are interesting but unnecessary. This release has been remastered and the sound is definitely superior to the original cd release.If you have a choice, get this reissue not for the bonus material but for the superior sound quality.

Wow, could I use the word "track" one more time in that last paragraph? Normally I'd clean that shit up, but goddamn if that wasn't too awful to fix.

Ocean Rain is right up their with their best albums. With the added orchestration, this could have been a Soft Parade-like letdown, but the songwriting was almost consistently excellent and much clearer than ever before. Mac the Mouth actually made sense for a change on more than half the tracks. Is "The Killing Moon" one of the greatest songs of the Eighties?

I remember when this first appeared in record stores. Some copies included labels with the grand announcement "The Greatest Album Ever Made." The ad also appeared in music magazines. Ludicrous? Arrogant? Outrageous? Well, maybe. But if you spotted it in a truly crappy mainstream music shop, then things seem a lot more realistic.

What does make Ocean Rain great, aside from the inherently good songs, is the tasteful way the orchestration was used - and limited extent. Some of the songs are actually built around emphatically-strummed acoustic guitars, an approach the Bunnymen first introduced on Porcupine. Only then were the strings added for just the right touch. This is the case on "Nocturnal Me," "Crystal Days" and "The Killing Moon." The legendary closing title track does rely mostly on orchestration, but damn!

This album doesn't completely abandon the sounds of their earliest albums. "The Thorn of Crowns" isn't much of a song at all, but rather a sparse but psychedelic five-minute thing that kind of suggests the Velvet Underground fronted by Jim Morrison, and might have fit better on their Heaven Up Here album. I tend to care less about this cut than the others - it feels out of place and doesn't quite work for me. And guitarist Will Sergeant still gets to display his ringing lead solos on "Silver" and "My Kingdom."

 Grampus Apr 03 2011
+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
There really is no excuse. I was so enamoured of the song "The Killing Moon" I immediately went out and bought the single – and this from a vociferous Bunnymen detractor. There was something about the moody guitar and the languid vocal which completely captivated me. But it was as if I couldn't revise my original opinion of the band for fear of being called duplicitous, so anything more than the single finding its way into my collection seemed an extreme longshot. Ten years later, and with the band's CDs on special offer, I simply had to find out for myself what all the fuss was about. And I was devastated. Devastated that I could have missed something so utterly captivating and downright essential.

Ocean Rain is the album which makes sense of the band's name as their sound fairly resonates from the speakers. I can fully understand how the uninitiated cite the downcast lyrics and mournful tone as a reason for dismissing the band (I can understand because I was of exactly the same opinion) but, whilst true, paradoxically, they are also uplifting and invigorating.

I don't know whether it's because I first heard Ocean Rain fifteen years after its initial release but I've never held with one of the criticisms levelled at the album – namely that it's aged badly. The introduction of sweeping string arrangements together with a determination to focus more on McCulloch's cracked and emotive voice finally gave the band the identity they'd been searching for. Personally this kind of orchestral, almost cinematic, pop rock never loses its appeal so never sounds dated.

There is little to criticise on Ocean Rain. "Thorn Of Crowns" tries too hard to sound like a Doors' cast-off and finishes up being a rambling pointless mess but that's about it. The better tracks are those which don't hang around too long – "Silver", "Seven Seas", "Crystal Days". Where the epic majesty of the music stays on the right side of ostentatious. That said, "The Killing Moon", "Nocturnal Me" and "Ocean Rain" are all proof the band can carry their panoramic vision into something more substantial.

I'll hold my hands up to having missed a trick with Echo And The Bunnymen and with Ocean Rain specifically, but at least I belatedly managed to rectify my mistake. It's sad to think how many more bands I may have missed along the way.

All of the above was written in glowing tribute to the original album and along comes this 20th anniversary reissue and I feel like erasing everything I've written. I can only presume the people behind this rerelease were cutthroat mercenaries with little or no regard for either Echo And The Bunnymen or their fans. Some of the extra material included here is so bad it should have been strangled at birth. I have no idea what the Life At Brian's sessions were about but, if a live audience was involved, I can only presume the doors of the venue were locked once everyone was inside. I know if I'd attended the event I'd have been clawing at the emergency exits in an effort to escape. McCulloch's voice wavers uncontrollably at the best of times, here it's totally untethered. I have a cat who sometimes looks at her wagging tail as if it's some kind of alien being which has somehow attached itself to her body. She'll twist around in circles in an effort to put the thing out of its misery and I can't help feeling McCulloch is doing exactly the same with his tongue. The only difference is my cat makes me laugh, McCulloch makes me want to rip my ears off. Only "Angels And Devils" has any value and its rickety structure leaves no doubt why it was relegated to the b-side of a single.

To those people who love the original version of Ocean Rain and believe this album should be judged on those nine tracks alone, I have to disagree. The original album is both enervating and exciting and I've rated it as such in another issue, but this remastered version was released as a complete package and should be reviewed and rated as such. It should be held up as an example of how adding more material to an existing album can ruin its effectiveness. Sometimes adding more does improve matters, here more is a bloody sight worse.

Jangley and charming.
To be brief, this album is good but doesn't amaze. I think I felt the same way about Crocodiles when I first listened to it, hence why Echo & The Bunnymen hasn't really been on the top of my post-punk listening priorities. You can pretty much equate this album as most other art-pop New Wave albums of the era and is probably as interesting as early Talk Talk. Of course, that's not to say that this is devoid of any sort of value since I think it's quite the opposite. There's a lot to like about this, though I prefer a little more edge and experimentation in this sort of stuff.

Nocturnal Me might be my least favorite on the album, though I will admit it's pretty darn catchy and is certainly not a bad song at all. It's just... eh. Most of the lyrics on this album are kinda cheese but at the same time aren't entirely unpleasant to listen to. All of my nitpicks are layered onto the bright, poppy baroque instrumentation that I don't find bad at all. I wouldn't call it experimental but it is complex enough to warrant more attention than just a casual, background listen and is certainly something to be admired. It's pretty much just a Beatles-y New Wave janglefest, which isn't the worst thing in the world for a post-punk slut such as myself. Personal highlights of this album would probably be The Yo Yo Man and The Killing Moon.

Overall, I liked this album, as I expected, though as I explained it isn't a flavor of post-punk I divulge into often. I feel something like this is better done by Talk Talk or The Stone Roses, but no disappointments from me here. There's a lot of complex instrumentation in here besides all the usual jangley stuff and I always appreciate that in any album when its done right. However, if you're like me and expect your post-punk to be a constant gut-punch of silliness and depression, look elsewhere. I may regain interest in this band if I bother finding an album by these guys that I think I'd like more.
Rating: 7.5/10

In essence, the stars aligned. By just doing the same thing but better, the Bunnymen produced a quality studio album. 'Ocean Rain' continues along the same dissonant, jittery lines: the emphasis on texture, the 'organic' song structures, the vocal idiosyncrasies. What has changed, it seems, is the care taken in the material's genesis. After a few spins, the interplay of various subtle elements becomes rather symphonic: perhaps the orchestra helps with that.

As a last hurrah of sorts for the psychedelic revival that never really happened, this record is flirting with mundanity and transcendence in equal measure. When everything sort of clicks, the spectral melodrama is a real aesthetic achievement. Theatrical post-punk melancholy sans jangle was rare by '84.

21 October 2022.
