+7 Vote downBookmarkVote up
A very old review I once wrote for Amnesiac described it as the moment that Radiohead disappeared deep up their own behinds. Others have described it as the Radiohead album that sounds like what people who hate the band think they sound like. Half the people who like the album still call it "Kid B" and consider it as a selection of outtakes not good enough to be on Kid A. So, that should give you an idea of what we're dealing with here.

All of that's a little bit true. The Kid A/Amnesiac sessions were very fruitful: Radiohead tore apart their sound, spent an awful long time reconstructing it into something new and once the pieces started falling into place the band found themselves with a deluge of new songs as they kept experimenting. Kid A was the spearhead of the revolution, and it was carefully constructed as a statement of intent. The plan was always to release two albums and Amnesiac was intended to be the wild child: while it's not made out of discards as such, it does act as a catch-all for everything that didn't suit the more controlled environment of Kid A. Some of it is Radiohead at their least compromising: the backing track for "Like Spinning Plates" is a backmasked version of another song that wasn't even out yet, "Pulk/Pull Revolving Doors" is the skeletal backing track to an attempt to record the already-fabled "True Love Waits" but with all its melody stripped out it sounds like a robot bashing its head against the wall repeatedly. Most of it, though, is Radiohead jamming in the studio without borders - attempts at jazz ("Life in a Glasshouse"), indie disco floor fillers ("I Might Be Wrong"), anxious full band rock music that now sounds like a strange revisit to their imminent past ("Knives Out"), inwards synth pop ("Packt Like Sardines in a Crushd Tin Box"), et al. This is Radiohead at their most self-serving, where almost everything is a love-it-or-hate-it deal bound to split opinions.

Regardless, Amnesiac isn't really a challenging album as much as it is just disorganised and dense to get through as a result of it, flipping itself around and over its head at every turn and running through the whole gamut of Radiohead at the same time. The disquietingly haunting and depressingly beautiful "Pyramid Song" is a Radiohead all-time great and one of the key tracks in their entire discography, sending shivers down the spine everytime it descends with its ghostly falsettos, ethereal strings and Selway's most legendary drumming part; "I Might Be Wrong" and its razor-sharp groove is a personal back catalogue favourite, Yorke & co interpreting dance music as something just as nerve-wrecking and anxiety-inducing as anything else in their ouevre and blessing it with one of their best guitar riffs which jerks and jangles with near-delight. It's mad that those career highlights are on the same album as the superfluous re-recording of "Morning Bell" (less drums, more plaintive atmosphere) which mainly just serves as a flimsy bridge between the two records without ever threatening to be the definitive version of the song, or the nondescript jazz of "Life in a Glasshouse" that might just be the most throwaway composition the band ever released on one of their albums; and yet, the likewise jazz-tinged "You and Whose Army?" and "Dollars and Cents" pull the album back into focus after losing its track along the way, their crescendos having the same gloomy power as OK Computer but reshaped. "Pulk/Pull Revolving Doors" in no way justifies its four-minute length, but then "Like Spinning Plates" turns out to be a surprisingly powerful song despite its "lazy" backdrop, where Yorke's rising vocals in combination with the abstract soundscape behind him sounding apocalyptic and strikingly moving. All this back-and-forth has been gathered equally around the sides of "Knives Out", an olive branch to everyone who thought Radiohead had abandoned guitars forever and because of its relative normality it sounds the most alien out of it all in this context, despite still being an odd endless rush forward without a chorus. There's no running thread through any of it; the ball of yarn has unravelled across the floor.

The reason for listing all these songs one after another is to make the point that Amnesiac is a collage. It's a group of individual pieces that pull the listener from one extreme to another without a break, apart from the interlude "Hunting Bears" that effectively acts as this album's "Treefingers" as an offer of a moment's quiet from the maelstrom around it. The band once described the two albums along the lines of Kid A being the equivalent of staring at a forest fire from a great distance and Amnesiac was the center of the blaze. There is no convenient cohesive harmony, but a collection of screams colliding into one another and forging a new context through that cacophony. And, perversely, I enjoy it because of that. As frustrating as it can be in places, so much of it is positively bewildering and continuously revelatory; I've always enjoyed "Packt Like Sardines" but I don't think I've truly found it as exciting before as I have discovered it to be while going back to the album for this review, its clanging production hiding underneath it a pogoing dance number, like a nightmare version of Hot Chip. The first half of the album is miles better than the second to a degree it's not even funny, but even at its most alienating or meandering it sounds interesting, and the only time it genuinely falls apart clumsily is "Life in a Glasshouse", ending the album with the twiddling of thumbs rather than a befitting closure.

Amnesiac absolutely deserves some of the criticisms it gets: it's an unhinged mess that swings wildly in quality, endlessly stuck to be compared against its big brother and losing in very obvious ways. In the Radiohead rankings, it's always close to the bottom because it is one of the few times when the band's consistency slipped. But Radiohead at their messiest - loose and uncontrollable but in control of their own goals - is still an interesting prospect to listen to. Amnesiac isn't a grower so much as it is a blind grab bag that offers something different each time you put your hand in it, its contents a selection of fragmented ideas which would fuel the rest of the band's lifetime. It's altogether a fascinating flipside to Kid A's more focused vision of the band's future.

To sit in a lecture hall for ten minutes before the beginning of an exam, one must have a lot of trepidation while looking around the room and seeing everybody going furiously over their notes while he himself has nothing to skim. He was never much of a studier, making nearly straight B's throughout high school and landing himself a rather fine gig here at the community college of the town he hadn't grown up in. But he wasn't a great student. His 'overachiever friends', who had taken AP classes and dual enrolled, were sitting with free rides to universities and room and board included. He, on the other hand, had scraped his grades together to earn himself his first two years of schooling free, though he'd signed a $5000 lease. As the students made their way into the structure and the professor undid the rubber band around his manila folder of test copies, the student felt no fear. He was going to knock this test out of the park and he would do it with more pizazz than any other of his surrounding peers.

The above excerpt is something that I myself encountered mentally as I heard "Packt Like Sardines in a Crushd Tin Box" for the first time in over three years; though I'd actually printed up a meager study guide to help me with the classes of astronomy I had missed, I felt pretty okay just barely checking them over. Once I had the test in hand, I felt I was doing fairly well through its entirety. Still, this was not what was even remotely on my mind. What was on my mind was how Amnesiac, an album I had often lambasted as Radiohead's most rancid hour, was suddenly starting to open itself up to me. It's like a once-scorned enemy of past years coming 'round to make her amends, and the irony of the present day is that she's actually madly in love with you and will do anything in her power to serenade you. That is what Amnesiac was to me, and listening to the opener of this album before the test was like getting a text message about meeting her under the bleachers for some post-exam recreation.

My mindset in distant years was centered around rejecting non-rock music, and the electronic that pervaded my once-beloved Radiohead was an irksome acceptance for me. Kid A, the successful business owner brother of this record, was mildly tepid to my ears, but I made do with its likes and actually found a plethora of redeeming qualities in its music (and things have only went upward from since its personal debut). But Amnesiac was a different flavor of icing for this act; what was once creamy and sweet had curdled into a chunky, sourdough sludge. Where were the moments that made me want to cry and lose my mind like the miserable entity of "Let Down"? That raw, rocker's vigilance that emanated so madly from "Vegetable" and "Stop Listening"? That British charm that came from The Bends masterpieces "Just" and "High and Dry"? The assimilation towards electronic music was something that I endured before I was ever truly ready to, and the ending result was a several year delay of Amnesiac being anything more than a stain on the face of a previously untarnished group. Apparently, this is how many people feel about Hail to the Thief, one that has captured my heart and soul since day one; I may not understand how one can attribute these feelings to such a record, but I at least know the feeling in regards to Radiohead.

Have you ever ordered a sandwich consistently, only to one day find that the bacon was overcooked? Or the mayo had spoiled? Or the bread was unnecessarily soggy? And even though you've ordered that sandwich countless times in the past, you can't shake that gross flavor of the fail sandwich out for quite some time? I feel like Amnesiac was that sandwich, and I'm just now overcoming any and all grievances with Radiohead that have spawned since I've first heard this. When I experience this album, I'm still remembering what I used to, but I'm slowly getting over it. Still, there are always parts of this record that were never evasive of my enjoyment; "You and Whose Army?" is a thesis of somberness, fit to join more movie trailers than "O Fortuna" and "Lux Aeterna". "I Might Be Wrong" is a romp through the newly-opened rape tunnel of the town, all terror and unsettling allusions contained. Later in the game, you've got a fantastic alternate side to "Morning Bell", whose 'Cut the kids in half' line is perhaps even more chilling and ominous than before.

I may still find this to be towards the lower-portion of Radiohead's offerings, but that streak of calling this names and lambasting friends who don't hate it as much as I do has come to an end (but then again, that latter one has been dead for years). Maybe it's the buzz of The King of Limbs and the breath of wondrously cool air that Thom Yorke exhaled on that record that has me succumbing to this band as much as I have been. Radiohead has somehow become the only band on my mp3 player for this last full week of February, and I have a hankering suspicion that I'll be hitting 'next' on shuffle until something from Amnesiac surfaces. I love. Oh, I love how the tables can turn.

I'm a reasonable man, get off my case
So this album is supposedly made up of tracks that were made during the recording sessions of Kid A, so I initially was very apprehensive about this release. However, after listening to it, I can safely say that I believe this album was what Kid A should have been, because the band definitely improved upon the type of experimental electronic sound they were trying to achieve with the previous release. Amnesiac fills the vacant, desolate void that is Kid A with emotion and substance, albeit very harrowing and characteristically offbeat, as is Radiohead's style.

"Packt Like Sardines in a Crushd Tin Box" is my favorite Radiohead song, and one of my favorite intro tracks to an album ever. The hollow percussion and Thom's droning, repetitive voice in addition to the synths provides a cold, desperate atmosphere that demands attention in order to be truly appreciated. Other great tracks include "I Might Be Wrong," "Knives Out," "Like Spinning Plates," and "Life in a Glasshouse."

As my deadline for a review of Flying Lotus' 2010 Warp LP Cosmogramma keeps creeping closer, all I can think about is Amnesiac. This album and/or collection of b-sides has always had kind of an interesting position in Radiohead's discography. At times I get the feeling people couldn't care less about it, and other times a sort of grassroots campaign to champion it springs up. It's strange that a collection of songs from recording sessions many listeners of this generation argue produced a Best Ever long player receives such a mixed reaction. Because nearly ten years down the road, I feel like Amensiac feels a lot more relevant than it may have in 2001.

"Pyramid Song" is still one of my (sometimes very most) favorite Radiohead songs, but the stuff that I let myself be convinced of just doesn't feel like it rings true anymore. Amnesiac certainly feels purposeful, in that it's supposed to sound cool and tightly coordinated and, perhaps most importantly, eliminate barriers of genre. KID A already did this, we say, but Amnesiac does it in a more aggressive manor that feels more at home in the context of 2010, where this kind of audible schizophrenia is more normal.

This is the release that most explicitly states Radiohead were done being a rock band, or a pop outfit, or anything other than some guys trying to make the most interesting music for themselves. The key difference from KID A is that most of these songs are catchier, and a little looser in how they go about their business. Like Hail to the Thief, it's more like the best songs Radiohead had available at the time than a Radiohead album. So there's that. Otherwise, this is another stupifying release from Yorke, Greenwoods & Co.
