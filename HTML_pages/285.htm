Nossa bicho, eu daria um 6/5 pra "Wah-Wah" se fosse possível. Musicalmente é um negócio MUUUUUUITO bom, adoro os vocais, adoro as guitarras, adoro tudo nela, é incrível.

A versão alternativa e mais crua de "Beware of Darkness" que tem no remaster de 2014 no Spotify me lembra um pouquinho de "Where You’ll Find me Now" do Neutral Milk Hotel.

A letra da maioria das músicas aqui é puro "zzZzZz" pra mim. Acho que na maioria das vezes não curto muito o George Harrison como letrista. NO ENTANTO eu não presto tanta atenção assim nas letras e gosto da instrumentação das músicas, então no final eu acabo gostando da maioria.

Cortei o "peso" do tempo de toda a seção 'Apple Jam' pela metade. Porque é uma Jam afinal de contas, é algo que você ouve mais descompromissadamente do que o resto das músicas, sabe? É como se fosse um "extra" depois da apresentação principal.

VR090: Really the Only Way I Coulda Done This Thing, a Tremendous Movement
Before listening...11:29AM, June 21st, 2010

For years, I've known this to be one of the truly worst albums of all-time. I reviewed it many years ago, but for some reason I deleted the review. It's a shame that I have, because that now opened this record to possible selection in the "Go Review That Album Game!", and guess what...it was picked.

I will first open up to you about my reluctance to listening to this album. If my memory serves me correctly, this is an abhorrently long record. Any album that requires me to listen to it for more than one and a half hours better be 69 Love Songs, Final Fantasy IX, or some other outstanding collection of music. There wasn't a single song that I was able to take away as being cool or hip or catchy or beattastic. It was all garbage. It was all shit. Adding on the fact that it was such a mind-numbing and long listening session, this was one of my most well-deserved 1/10 ratings.

But now that I have been faced with this unavoidable task (I could always quit the Go Review That Album Game for a little bit, but where's the pride in that?), I have decided that today's listening session to All Things Must Pass will be very different from my initial first hearing. Rather than listening to this cohesively as one ordered album, I have taken the tracks and shuffled them. I will be listening to the songs in the order that fate allows them to, and I will evaluate this record NOT as a record, but as a collection of George Harrison songs.


During listening...11:35AM (starting time)

I'd Have You Anytime - I'm starting with the first track for cohesive purposes. It's important that I start with record in the way it's meant to be started, though I don't necessarily care. Right off the bat, I think that George sounds a little meek, but I'm finding myself a little bit interested with the music. The music actually sounds like it has a little bit of soul to it, but in a heartfelt, slow latter-career Beatles song way. Not abysmal; we're off to a good start.

I Remember Jeep - Starting with an obnoxiously painful burst of noise is no way to begin any track (even when you're listening to noise). After this inconvenience, it is revealed that a rockin' jam was actually underneath it. I've never been a fan for jams, and there isn't anything about this one that is particularly grand. At the same time, this sounds like something that would make excellent background noise if you're chilling with friends and want something to put on at low volume. That's not much of a compliment, but it's damn better than what I would've said ages ago. Still, at eight minutes, the variation here is a big problem.

Run of the Mill - The acoustic guitar that opens this song is a very nice touch, and is probably the most exciting thing to have happened thus far. George sounds more like George in this song than on the first one, which is a big plus considering that I like George's songs in the Beatles (not as much as Paul's, but...). For such a short song, this one is leaving a favorable impression on me. The first success thus far; keep 'em up, Georgie.

What is Life - Is that a statement or a question? Who cares, because all I can think about is the insanely good power pop opening that this song has. THAT was a big surprise, but what's an even bigger surprise is that George's entrance doesn't kill this song's momentum. By the time the verse turns into the chorus, I'm amazed at how well-crafted this whole thing is turning out to be. The chorus is something that might be considered proto-'80s rock? It's like if you took the mediocrity out of a typical Foreigner song (no offense to the band, of course) and replaced that lukewarmness with a Beatle. The last song is completely overshadowed by how much I enjoy this one.

Behind That Locked Door - This song has a country-era Bob Dylan sound to its melody. I love Bob Dylan, but his country era completely bores me (and when I say 'country-era', I'm talking about Nashville Skyline and New Morning). If even the great Dylan can't satisfy me with that sound, George Harrison surely won't even come close. At the same time, I can recognize that this simply doesn't float my boat (rather than it being the dishonorable 'ass salad' that I currently have this album labeled as...perhaps it's time to make a change?). Moving on...

Beware of Darkness - There is a subtle fluctuation in this song between major and minor sounds. I typically don't dig this sort of thing, but George makes it interesting for the beginning of the song. This song has a very light but noticeable swelling as it marches on; visualization is, for once, possible on this album with the inclusion of this song. I imagine a desolate suburban landscape, but there's an atmosphere of hope. Very desaturated image. This mental picture makes the song a little bit more special to me, although my ultimate disappointment comes in the fact that they do nothing with the song-long crescendo. It's still decent.

Ballad of Sir Frankie Crisp (Let It Roll) - I wish that these songs were actually next to each other on the album, because this is the exact climax that I was hoping the last song would reach. The song picks up from where the last one left off, and it finally gives itself release with its proclamation: "Let it roll!". A desperate and sad-sounding vocal is in the background, yet it's at the same time upfront. It's hard to explain, really. But no matter: this is the second song thus far to have impressed me. What's even stranger is that it feels shorter than the preceding song. Odd.

Isn't It A Pity (Version One) - It's a pity that, once again, we omit question marks from our questioning titles. It's cool that the first version got selected before the second version by shuffle. I'm rambling. This is kind of a lengthy song, but that gives it room to grow. I can't complain about buildup in this song, because it definitely has it, but I also can't say that it's the kind that I typically look for in music. Likewise, this is the most Beatlesy song here, which gives it a few extra candy points. I wouldn't be surprised if this song was an outtake from the Let It Be sessions. Not fully my cup of tea, but (like an earlier track) one that I can't call bad by any means. In fact, none of the songs so far have been bad.

Plug Me In - This is another from the series of jam tracks that plague the end of the record, but luckily it's much shorter than its earlier counterpart. If I were to characterize the feel of the music here, it would be 'harder, psychedelic Beach Boys'. I can see Brian Wilson and the gang sitting down in the studio and writing something like this, although they'd have a poppy tune going over the top of it. Like the other song, this sounds like cool background music for social events. Not too shabby.

I Dig Love - It sounds like somebody has placed a thick veil over George's mouth for this song, because I can barely make out his muffled lyrics as he sings this one. Aside from the parts where he begins to sing out, I think I've finally found a song that lives up to the way the album formerly made me feel. Vibes are important to me when it comes to music, and if I'm not diggin' it, I won't return to it easily. This song is not a good example of music from All Things Must Pass. I'd go as far as calling this filler (even if it does have momentary bits of heart).

If Not For You - Say...what a coincidence that I should earlier bring up country-Bob Dylan, because this happens to be a cover from one of those very aforementioned records (the latter, to be exact). It's no secret that Bob and George were self-identified colleagues (I would hope they were, considering their role as bandmates in The Traveling Wilburys). The George Harrison cover of this song COMPLETELY slays the original, which was mundane in production and trite in spirit. While the previous song took me down a slope, this one has led me to a geyser. Will it erupt?

Art of Dying - The title made me think of Led Zeppelin, which makes it eerie that its opening solo is completely reminiscent of something that Plant would've been doing at around this time. As the song settles down and George enters, a lower guitar plays a very driving bluesy riff. It's pretty clear that the ground under my feet has propelled me back up to dry land. There's so much emotion in the way George goes about this one. One of the absolute best songs so far.

Isn't It a Pity (Version Two) - While the first version of this song made a point to be driving and out there, this one lays itself down and laments softly. I honestly think that this is the sound that George should have independently went with for the tune. It just works better (and I actually like this one). Though it's still lengthy when compared to song content, it's overall a much more manageable and enjoyable affair. It also winds things down after the last amazing track (this and the last are side-by-side on the record), only to slowly bring it back up again by the end of the song. Nice.

All Things Must Pass - There isn't a whole lot for me to say about this song. It's a standard folk rock ballad of sorts with a very nice jazzy accompaniment brooding in the background. Like in earlier songs, George sounds very sad. I feel like this song itself might be about the liquidation of the Beatles. Could it be? Perhaps (since the whole album is named after this song, I see no reason to assume otherwise). It's a nice little tune, even if it's a bit forgettable).

Hear Me Lord - Starting with a banging explosion and morphing into a heartfelt pop/rock '70s tune, this piano-driven piece of Harrison borders on the line of being impressive, though there is something about it that I can't quite place my finger on as being off about this. And when I say off, I don't mean bad or mediocre. I mean off; it's a small little nagging something that is making me feel like this song isn't reaching its full potential, but what it does present is really cool and musically pleasing. These are the songs that I should have paid a little bit more attention to years ago.

Let It Down - Either I know a song that is like this one, or I've visited this one secretly after having declared hatred for the album. Flowing like smooth jazz and swirling softly and freely, this song is a very gentle and almost pristine piece of pop. I feel like this is cliched slow dancing music, and while it sounds a little bit dated, George manages to make it something worth listening to.

Out of the Blue - Here it is: the single longest piece from one of the longest albums I own. And it's a jam track? Ugh. And yet...from the opening licks, I feel like this one is a little bit more special than previous improvisations. The saxophone in this just DOMINATES. This is how the jazz-rock bands of the 1970s should've done it; there's groove here; eleven minutes of groove, mind you, but what a feel this is. While one cannot argue in its favor for the song's length, it certainly can't be criticized or insulted for its content. In a way, this is one of the most definitive pieces of the album, since it plays a biopic role in how I've viewed the entire album thus far: too damn long, but having moments that makes the whole thing bearable. Has my view changed? Am I beginning to like All Things Must Pass?

Thanks for the Pepperoni - Such a silly title. Having the lovely robot of iTunes pick two jam tracks in a row must have been really funny to it, but the joke isn't on me after all as I rather enjoy myself with this one. I mean, there's not a single shred of original sounding material in this song, but the fact that I don't really have much old rock n' roll makes a track like this entertaining enough. Chuck Berry means Jerry Lee Lewis meets The Beach Boys meets George Harrison. Surf's up, anyone?

My Sweet Lord - After the last sixteen minutes of listening to guitar improvisation, I've almost forgotten what album I'm listening to. Harrison resuscitates the lighter, folkier pop/rock of earlier songs on this disc one side A track. It doesn't jump out at me, but it's got that nice George sound to it, so I mean...can I really complain? When it picks up in velocity during the second minute, it becomes a little bit cooler to hear. The backing of "Hallelujah" is the highlight of this song.

Wah-Wah - I want to hear The Flaming Lips cover this, because it sounds so perfectly like a song that they would write. The song's structure, the vocal melody, the psychedelic sounds, the short occasional breaks of percussion...all of it! I wouldn't be surprised if Wayne Coyne admitted that the band wrote all of their music after being inspired by this very song. Really cool tune that's even cooler upon the realization that I've just had.

Apple Scruffs - Folky, poppy, and...oh come on, how many times do I have to say the same thing? This is a song that is exactly like the others on this album. For that reason, I don't think too highly of it. Repetition doesn't work well in gaining my favor, and this throwaway song is one that I'll be forgetting promptly.

It's Johnny's Birthday - Could I rate this? Of course I can, because this is one of the best fifty-second segments that the album has to offer. Apparently, somebody named Johnny is having a birthday on this day, and George decided to honor him here. Forty years ago...I wonder if Johnny is still celebrating birthdays.

Awaiting On You All - It's a proverbial blessing that my makeshift album should end on such a resolving and fun song. I thought a song much like the opener would end up ruining the momentum that I struggled so hard to achieve on this long Monday morning, but I'm delighted to know that one of the best and catchiest songs to come has been waiting for me at the very end. The finish line. This is the exciting finish that I think this album should have been granted. A parade of approval in its honor.

After listening...1:21pm

Opinions of mine have been shattered before. I've had albums that I've hailed as my all-time favorites make a very unfortunate and dishonorable fall from grace, and records whose likes were lifeless and hollow make their way up to unimaginable favor. This album has fallen somewhere between the two: I admit it, on this day, that this once thought awful album is now so much more. It's decent. It's fair. It's pretty good. It's neat. It's likable. It's LONG AS HELL, but there's too much good material here to downplay this record's spirit. George put a lot into these songs; who am I to think anything bad of them?

I close this horrifically long (but terribly informative) review with admittance to my nearly three-year blunder. I'm sorry, George, that I have thought so ill of this release of yours for so very long. Your role as a Beatle and as a solo artist has been granted my utmost respect from this day forth. Though I know you'll have not a chance or body to read this, I sincerely hope that your spirit lives on in all of the music you've given the world. Though I'm nine years late in saying it, you will be missed.

I can't believe it's this late in the day...
