The Kinks were a band that flared, spectactularly, in the mid 60's and forged the template for the heavy rock sound that was still a couple of years in the future. But with a change of direction they began to resemble something akin to an eccentric novelty act and whilst rock music exploded around them, seem to lose their street credibility. True perhaps, but quietly and unobtrusively Ray Davies was reiterating his own singular vision through a series of albums that improved gradually and peaked here on this magnificent testament to the power of rock music and story-telling. Along with its predecessor, Arthur is another masterpiece of pop-rock perfection with a text more cleverly contrived than most anything around at the time. The fall of the British Empire was a subject that lent itself perfectly to Davies' imagination and the result is a satire full of biting sarcasm and genuine pathos. He pushes his writing and arranging skills up a level creating a suite of songs that have multiple parts, delicious tempo changes and memorable hooks by the bucket load.
The band gets a little heavier than on Village Green and are augmented by a horn section, the stereo is authentic and a little more care and attention has gone into the production. One could argue that Village Green (best heard in mono) sounds like a 60's album whereas Arthur sounds more 70's. Some say it approaches a Beatles sound but to me it sounds like a band with confidence enough to play it live, this is not an album saturated with studio overdubs.
Side one is simply breath-taking from the soaring Victoria, through the ingenious modulations of Yes Sir No Sir, the poignant Some Mother's Son, the comical shuffle of Drivin', to the R&B riffing of Brainwashed and the crafted complexities of Australia, Davies' musical and lyrical message is unwavering. Some criticize the final songs' extended jam but it's the perfect way to wind down and Dave Davies' guitar playing is clean and inventive.
Side two is only slightly less compelling but every bit as clever. Shangri-La is Davies at his inimitable best as is the unique Mr Churchill Says. On the other hand, She's Bought A Hat Like Princess Marina is a little light relief that helps sets up beautifully the emotional impact of Young And Innocent Days, a moving ballad of bitter-sweet nostalgia. Finally we are brought back down to earth again with the cruel and heartless Nothing To Say only to be washed clean by the closing hoe-down, Arthur, an emotional home-coming. All is forgiven and all is too late, such is the fate of our hero.
The late 60's and early 70's witnessed a proliferation of bands producing inspired and original music, it's no surprise that the Kinks were somewhat over-looked but listening to their best work it's more than obvious that they belong up there with the greatest.

What can I say? It can be hard to review unqualified gems. When I got into the Kinks, I had no idea they had something like this in their catalog: something that vies for 10 greatest albums ever. A gush of superlatives is boring, so suffice it to say that this is their Abbey Road or Who's Next: the band is firing on all cylinders, and everything -- lyrics, melodies, arrangements, playing, production, dynamics, diversity, vibe, concept, sequencing, synergy -- is as good as it gets, in Kinkdom and indeed all of rock music. They move confidently through multiple styles, especially chamber pop and guitar-driven melodic rock, sometimes within the same song.

A lot of the songs have extra parts beyond verse/chorus/bridge, which is just great; I imprinted fully on "Uncle Albert, Admiral Halsey" as a wee lad, and love multipart pop-rock opuses. Back-to-back anti-war songs "Yes Sir, No Sir" and "Some Mother's Son" feature this to excellent effect -- the former sardonic, the latter elegiac -- and pack an extra wollop coming on the heels of the rousing, intentionally patriotic-yet-goofy title track. Likewise for "Shangri-La", a paeon to the meagre pleasures afforded working-class retirees, and "She's Bought A Hat Like Princess Marina", for the currently-humbly-employed. Despite its gravitas, every track on the album retains a light touch. Ray Davies' lyrics are both compassionate and ironic, and his ability to pull off both with such panache is an important part of what makes him such a great songwriter. Not to mention his deftness in putting them to music, which on this album combines the subtlety of mid/late-60's Kinks with the driving power of their earliest work.

The result is an album that's rocking, rebellious, incisive and introspective in the best senses, both substantive and a fun, exciting listen. Rueful, given the subject matter, yet musically so uplifting -- such a masterpiece of juxtaposition. If Village Green Preservation Society is a full-blown 10/10, this one's gotta be the proverbial 11.

Arthur also features Dave Davies' best guitar work, with all kinds of little flourishes and countermelodies both acoustic and electric. Reissues include three of his B-sides that normally would've been album-track standouts: "This Man He Weeps Tonight" and "Mindless Child Of Motherhood" are among the band's most exhilarating rockers, and "Mr. Shoemaker's Daughter", with its wonderfully plaintive vocal, sounds like a classic folk tune. Outstanding complement/coda to the main course.

1969-1971 was probably rock music's peak, and these guys rode the wave as high as it could possibly go. What else can I say? For awhile, my all-time favorite.

While a bunch of hippies got stoned in fields and believed they were changing the world, The Kinks were truly in touch with the reality of the 1960s. The world they saw wasn't full of love and peace, it was a place where the average man lives a miserable life in which all his dreams are crushed and all he has to offer some consolation are material possessions he is supposed to believe will make him happy. That is the picture The Kinks paint with Arthur or the Decline and Fall of the British Empire. Though the band sticks to their traditional upbeat pop/rock, there is a strong emotional undertone to Arthur, as their was on their previous album Village Green. But whereas there I detected a great deal of sadness beneath the surface, here there is barely concealed anger. Ray Davies is rightfully outraged at the unbelievable state of Britain, a country where the elite 'give the scum a gun and make the bugger fight', teach people to give up their hopes of a fulfilling life, and brainwash them to convince them they are happy with their vacuous suburban existence. The only way out? The fabled land of Australia, where (supposedly) there are no problems! So basically 'if you don't like it, fuck off!'

The strength of the message holds this album together, and I think gives the songs an extra edge. The highlight of this album for me is the opening four song stretch, as the songs are of great quality to go with their great subject matter. Victoria mocks the patriotism that ran through Britain at the beginning of the 20th century. Yes Sir, No Sir and Some Mother's Son are the angriest songs here, and the contrast with Drivin' is brilliant. What are you told to do about the world's problems? Absolutely nothing; instead, shut up and distract yourself! It really emphasises the outrage running through the prior songs. Obviously, though, the song this album is most remembered for is Shangri-La, which basically sums up what The Kinks were about. It is certainly the album's centrepiece, and a great song with more solid lyrics. The second half of the album isn't as strong, though it ends very well with the title track, but the continued strong lyrics and continuation of the theme means the album remains solid and engaging.

Arthur was another great success for The Kinks, and is probably their best album, just beating its predecessor Village Green. Both are equally essential.

Cool points to Ray Davies. Why, exactly, am I giving cool points to Ray Davies? Besides the fact that it's Ray Davies, and he deserves them? Well, he managed to follow up the great The Kinks Are the Village Green Preservation Society with an album that's at least as good, maybe even better. A big part of this is how this is a lot less old-timey and more rocking than Village Green. Not that I absolutely need all my music to be rocking - my favorite album of all time, A Love Supreme, is nowhere near rock in even the broadest definition - but I'm not a big fan of the jaunty Vaudeville-type stuff that Davies was working with on Village Green. The fact that's an awesome record anyway - probably one of my twenty favorite albums of the '60s - is a testament to Ray's undeniable songwriting skills, but this is even better than Village Green, although once again dragged down by a little piece of annoying old-timeiness, in this case "She's Bought a Hat Like Princess Marina." But even that one's better than "Sitting By the Riverside!"

But right, rock. Rock opera, actually, but believe me, the first half of that sentence is just as important to the album as the second. So you get these big arrangements with lots of strings, but also some heavy (for '69, of course, this isn't exactly Slayer with brass we're talking here) guitars. I think it's a great combination, and helps make songs like "Brainwashed," "Yes Sir, No Sir," "Mr. Churchill Says," and especially the fantastic bit of satire that is "Victoria" all the more memorable. Because the melodies are as top-notch as they were back on Village Green! Great melodies, awesome arrangements...

And on top of that, biting, brutally sarcastic lyrics! "Australia" has got to be the centerpiece of this album, a viciously funny advertisement for the country that was a penal colony during the Victorian era which I believe this album was set in, although the Winston Churchill references make me doubt this. It starts off as this big, anthemic rocker, but it isn't long before it movies into a Beach Boys-style harmonyfest, only to turn around and move into a jam for guitar and horns. It might seem awkward on paper, but hear it for yourself. It works. I guess this makes this the second consecutive Kinks album that I end up loving the tune everyone else hates - dammit, "Big Sky" rules! So does "Australia!" And so does "Shangri-La," which does something similar: moves from quiet, contemplative ballad to big triumphant rocker, all without losing Ray's signature sarcasm. What a great one-two punch!

The second side's a little bit weaker than the first, but it's still great. The aforementioned "Shangri-La" and "Mr. Churchill Says" are definitely highlights, as is the not-yet mentioned but completely awesome "Arthur," but I'm not a HUGE fan of the other songs here. I already discussed my dislike of "She's Bought a Hat Like Princess Marina," so I feel no need to explicate that further. Meanwhile, the ballad "Young and Innocent Days" is good, but not as good as the album's other ballad, "Some Mother's Son" - it only really picks up at the end, with some rather pretty classical guitar noodling, but of course that ends just as soon as it begins, as such things often do. "Nothing to Say" is fun, but it lacks the spark that makes the best tunes here work. And "Arthur" just rules all over the place. It has got to be the best song ever written about dragging a crotchety old man away to a retirement home, and don't let the fact that it's also probably the only song ever written about this topic keep you from loving it.

Oh, and the concept is cool! Much better than Village Green's. It's about England right at the end of the Victorian period (or, y'know, somewhere), when the British Empire - indisputably the world's dominant power throughout the 19th century - fell apart. I think Arthur the guy represents Britain, sitting around his crumbling mansion (the British Empire) as it falls apart and getting songs with cool riffs (like the title track!) written about him. The result, then, is the Kinks' second consecutive very-near-masterpiece, held back only by Ray Davies' false and completely baseless belief that old-timey British music hall is honestly something people like to listen to.

Track picks: "Victoria," "Some Mother's Son," "Australia," "Shangri-La," "Mr. Churchill Says," "Arthur"

Back when my uncle was in college- I'm guessing this would be at some point in the '70s- he had this one class called "Urban Polity", and his final assignment was, unsurprisingly, to write a research paper. Well, the day before the assignment was due rolled around, and he, perhaps equally unsurprisingly, hadn't actually done any research. So instead of writing a research paper, he wrote a five-page review of this record album. His teacher read it and gave it back to him, noting on the front "This is NOT (underlined several times in red ink for emphasis) a research paper", I guess because he felt the need to state the obvious, and then went on to say "...it is, however, a very well-thought-out record review", and gave my uncle an A. Quite naturally, the immediate effect of this amusing anecdote was to inspire me to brood about the mistakes I've made in my life. Too often in my life when I am asked to do something, I would do precisely that (which is fine), and if I couldn't, for some reason, do that, I would do nothing (which is not). It never occurred to me to bend the rules in my favor, to, rather than doing exactly what they wanted, at least do whatever I could.

Back to my point: This record is OK, certainly better than their late '70s junk, but I prefer the stuff from around the Kinks Kontroversy- it is very hard to do a concept album that is good to listen to, as opposed to writing five-page essays about, and I don't think the Kinks ever got the knack.

Now if you will excuse me, I am off to write a letter to the President. And maybe also my senators. I don't know.

Ray Davies' inspiration for this, the Kinks seventh studio album came from a subject very close to his heart, for Arthur was his and brother Dave's brother in law. The story of Arthur Or The Decline And Fall Of The British Empire is of a very patriotic, driven young man who strives to improve his status, his disillusionment with Britain and his eventual emigration to Australia (this had already been partially covered on the song "Rosie Won't You Please Come Home"). However, as you might have guessed, things don't really turn out like Arthur had dreamed. This is told through a collection of very strong compositions, many which have become Kinks classics. The albums Something Else and Face To Face had shown Davies to be one of the most gifted songwriters of his generation but The Village Green Preservaton Society was their first truly consistent album. Arthur would be as consistent but also shows a more experimental side.

We begin with the single "Victoria" which I must admit, was a track I largely ignored for a long time. Thinking it was just another cliched love song featuring a girl's name for the title, (how could I have been so naive?) I hadn't delved deeply into the theme of the song. The title, is of course, a reference to Queen Victoria and her once powerful empire. Also mentioned is the class divide and the poverty stricken majority who are continually robbed by their superiors but are first in line to defend their country when war occurs. That theme is examined more closely on "Yes Sir, No Sir". The first of a trilogy of war songs it finds Davies at his most cynical, scathing best. Dealing with the dehuminisation that comes with military service "What do I do sir, where do I go sir, how do I behave?" really drives home the point. Also impressive is the wonderful bridge which features the lines "Give the scum a gun and make the bugger fight" and "Be sure to hurt deserters shot on site".

The deeply moving "Some Mother's Son" moves away from the training ground and straight onto the battlefield. In this Davies lyrically goes straight for the jugular with intro line "Some mother's son lies in a field, someone has killed some mother's son today" which describes it in a very detached way. However, later the harrowing line "they put his picture in the frame, but all dead soldiers look the same" shows that this is a real person but in the end, just another statistic. "Mr Churchill Says" is the least impressive of the trilogy, complete with famous quotes and air raid sirens. The theme of escapism is celebrated in "Drivin'" which is obviously inspired by the Davies' childhood. The sentiment is summed up by "the rent collectors they will be behind us but they'll never find us". While not being a classic, it is breezy and cheerful. The opposite could be said of "Brainwashed". A much rockier affair, the grinding bassline at the beginning ensures this isn't another "Sitting By The Riverside". "Get down on your knees" is the repeated line as Davies vents his fury at the people that have nothing and aspire to nothing but still vote to maintain a system where the wealthy few rob their very beings.

"Australia" is a fabulous track and describes the promise of a land with "no class distinction" or "drug addiction". A place where dreams can come true with non-stop sunshine and surf boards. This track really looks at it from Arthur's viewpoint but the line about "perpetual smiles" across their face is somewhat cruel on Davies' part. The Beach Boys style "ooh, ooh oohs" just add to a hugely enjoyable number. The outstanding "Shangri-La" unbelievably bombed as a single. Initially beginning as a folk flavoured track, it then goes on a fascinating musical journey complete with synths and horns while the only slight downside comes form the production of the chorus where it sounds particularly thin. Again the class system is highlighted here from the man who "need not worry" to him who has "a mortgage hanging over his head". However, the quest for material wealth appears to be nothing but a smokescreen for the rich man who resides among his ever jealous neighbours.

"She's Bought A Hat Like Princess Marina" is very similar in sentiment, describing social climbers who will spend their money on un-necessary extravagance while living in a state of semi-starvation. The nostalgic ballad "Young and Innocent Days" is a welcome addition. More akin to the previous album theme wise, the gentle acoustic guitar work is joined by added keyboard and strings very effectively. The sense of serenity is only briefly broken by the line "I see the lines across your face". If earlier tracks "Drivin'" and "Australia" deal with Arthur's fantasies then "Nothing To Say" describes his reality. "How is your rhumatism, chillblaines?" appears to describe two people content to talk in cliches and not discuss their broken dreams. The title track really gives a summary of Arthur's life and while the summary is one of failure, it also acknowledges that he was brave and willing to improve Ray's sister's standard of life while his kids will perhaps do the same thing. With a strong country feel to it, it concludes with the line "We love you and want to help you". Perhaps a slight apology from Davies for using his brother in law as a musical guinea pig?

The Saddest Record

I don't know where it came from or how it came into my possession. Honest to God. I certainly never paid any money for it. I don't recall ever acquiring it. One day, as I searched through my records, I noticed it.

It's hard to miss, actually. The album cover spine was once held together by packing tape. The tape is old and no longer capable of holding the sleeve together. I didn't tape it. I'd never do something like that. It looks like the tape is older than I am, but that can't be true because I was born a couple of years before this record was released. I certainly feel like the tape must be older than I am. It now acts as a thick, dull-yellow marker in the stacks of records. "Here I am! Look at Me!"

Pulling the album off of the shelf, the wear on the cover is disarming. This poor, sad record. Wherever it came from, whatever type of home it had before finding its way to my collection, it was clearly the target of some strong emotions. So hated? So loved? One or the other, surely. A neglected record would have been left alone. This one's had some hard days and nights. There's a large worn-down drop of some kind of thick blue-gray paint on the front cover, and more packing tape in the top right corner. The bottom right corner looks like it's been eaten by mice. The cover is so flat and frayed on the edges that it's a surprise to find that it's actually a gatefold cover. Open it up and what do you get? A two foot tall drawing of a boxing kangaroo with an arm holding an umbrella beneath the marsupial's behind, that's what.

Alrighty then.

The back cover has fantastic liner notes. I'm a big fan of liner notes. The back cover is so worn and dirty, though, that it's difficult to read the small print. Hell, the ring wear is so bad that it's hard to read the large print at the top. The large print in the middle is fairly clear, though, it says or the decline and fall of The British Empire.

The record itself is in pretty bad shape. I've seen records that are worse off at thrift stores, but this one may be in the worst shape of any in my collection. The fact is, if my needle wasn't already old and ready to be replaced, I wouldn't even try to play it. However, my needle is old and I'll either be replacing it or my entire turntable soon, so what the hell.

The first song is "Victoria". It has about a half-dozen skips within the first twenty seconds. I'd stop listening, but the song is pretty good. I've been around the block a few times. If any part of a record is going to develop a skip, it's most likely to be the first part of the first song. The outer edge of the record is generally the most exposed, right? So, I don't panic. It's a good thing, too, because there's only one more skip in the entire record. Snaps and pops aplenty, no doubt, but only one more skip.

And what a record it is.

I'm not a Kinks fan, or at least I haven't been in the past. They've had some great songs, no doubt, and they were a great band as far as performing goes, but most of their output lacks originality. Arthur, however, is a different story. This record must be the reason that people put up and even rave about a lot of their less-than-inspired output (not that all the rest of it is crap, mind you). The Kinks produced a lot of songs, of which a handful are brilliant. Likewise, they recorded a lot of albums. I haven't heard all of them, but of those that I've heard, Arthur is the only I consider to be really good, and it's more great than good.

So how did Arthur (or The Decline and Fall of the British Empire) become part of my collection. I don't know. I think it was drawn to me, like Lassie. It sensed a need. Here I was, a man in love with rock music who'd never heard the great Arthur. I dismissed the Kinks as a greatest hits band. I had a hole in my life and I didn't even know it. This torn and frayed copy crept into my collection and waited patiently for me to play it. Since I've now been enlightened, its deed is done. I expect one day soon to find an empty space on the record shelf where that twelve inch tall strip of yellowed packing tape used to be. This copy of Arthur will have moved on to help some other lost soul.

Thank you, my friend. My sad, worn copy of Arthur, you'll always have a place in my heart.

Just like on "The Village Green Preservation Society" The Kinks use catchy riffs, humour and tongue-in-cheek lyrics to actually make statements and criticise society. This all leads to a perfectly crafted album that's equally good to TVGPS. It's never boring, every track is incredibly entertaining and it's full of great little ideas, be it the riffs, melodies or lyrics, that make it a great experience to listen to. It's also a great and heavy rock album, the heaviest the Kinks had made up to that point. Recommended to everyone who likes The Kinks or simply a great rock album that makes you feel the fun the band had playing it.
