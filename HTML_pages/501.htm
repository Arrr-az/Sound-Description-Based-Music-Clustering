"OK, Bobo. You're in for a treat", I said to the rockin' housecat..."I'm adding Zappa to the review rotation and we're starting tonight". "Oh no!", cried the cat as he jumped-up and bolted for the door. I was quicker and caught him before he could escape. "Now stop that silliness and let's review the '66 debut from the Mothers of Invention, titled Freak Out", I said as I returned the cat to the sofa. The cat knew he was licked..."It ain't that I don't like Zappa. Man, I dig the dude. He did it "his way", to quote the well known cliche'. It's jus' that, jeez Louise, can that feller wonder off into left field and get lost in the tall weeds. You know we've been listin' to rock records from the day Mom and you brought me home from the ASPCA. (Best $20 you ever spent, I might add). I rarely quibble with your selections, it's that I jus' feel that a little Frank Zappa can go a long way; or, to put it differently, Zappa is good in small doses. An acquired taste that I don't have yet". Unsimpathetically, I retrieved the CD and directed the rockin' housecat to play the disc. The cat removed the disc from the case and inserted it into the changer and pushed play.

After listening to over 60 minutes of the Mother's debut, I asked Bobo for his take on the album. The rockin' housecat replied...You know, those idiots over at Rollin' Stone Magazine say that Freak Out is one of the top 100 best rock albums ever recorded. All I can say about that is they haven't listened to many rock albums. Nevertheless, I'll give Zappa credit for writin' an' recordin' somethin' that was totally different from what else was recorded back in '66. Frank was all over the freakin' place. From doo-wop, to whacked-out blues and psychedelia, to sound collages (Say, have I ever told ya that I don't like sound collages). The dude was not afraid to speak his mind. Whether it was his commentary on the Great Society, "Hungry Freaks, Daddy", to the '65 Watts riot, "Trouble Every Day", to scenesters, "You're Probably Wondering Why I'm Here", and to authority in general, "Who Are the Brain Police". Those tracks are interestin' to say the least. Zappa's not afraid to tackle the vagaries of love with "Go Cry on Somebody Else's Shoulder" and "How Could I be Such a Fool?". It's apparent that Zappa is never gonna be a chick fav. I know Mom hates 'im 'cause you keep singin' the lyrics to "Dinah Moe Humm" from a subsequent Zappa album release. Yep, Frank rounded-up all his freak bud's and recorded his skewed vision of rock-n-roll. Some parts are jaw droppin' good. Whereas, others make me wanna scratch my furry little head. Suzy Creamcheese indeed".

"OK, Bobo. What's the call on Freak Out?", I asked the rockin' housecat. The cat replied...."Lets give this album four-stars for historical significance. A recommended purchase. Young rockers are advised that a lot of patience is required with Zappa and that this dude has a huge body of work to wade thru. Only you can make the determination whether you should travel down this road. Freak Out is a good place to ask yourself that question".

Right out of the gate, many of Frank's career-long musical styles, personality traits, quirks, talents and turn-offs were already apparent here. If every artist in history was given points for both talent and obnoxiousness, Frank would clearly be at the top of the combined points list.

Amazingly, he and his band were given a double album with which to launch his ludicrous vision onto the world. The whole shebang has a 'catching up' feel to it, as it showcases everything from then-trendy West Coast rock to old-style doo-wop ("Go Cry on Somebody Else's Shoulder") to pointless psychedelic tape loop noodling ("The Return of the Son of Monster Magnet"). Musically, Zappa had a long way to go before achieving his tight Seventies musical virtuosity and production technique, but familiar instrumental touches were already in his repertoire here (xylophone, kazoo, vocal gibberish, etc.).

Part of the madness in all this mess is the fact that the songs veer randomly from one of several levels of stupidity/seriousness to another. This probably got the ball rolling on the eventual formation of the big pool of Zappa fans who are mainly in it for the music (okay, and occasional humorous tidbit). Frankly, Frank never really outgrew this tendency. But if anyone could bust open the limited pop universe, he could.

Despite all the zaniness, about half of the songs tend to fit a passable contemporary rock format. This makes sense, as The Mothers were at this time a common staple on the Los Angeles club scene. If things got too weird or out of control, they could at least play it straight (on the surface) with a decent catalog of songs to choose from, depending on the mood and need of the times. Still, Frank never sang a song in his life in a conventional, emotional way. He always had that sarcastic tone in his (frequently spoken) voice, as if to mock the entire culture, which he sort of was. He could never commit himself to it completely, often to the point of annoyance. But another side of him was always craving respect as a budding musical composer, and sporadic songwriter. There are many weak-to-passable songs here, and they could have been left off to leave behind a tremendously strong (or at least effective) single record. He's often funny or hilarious, and often nails down a catchy song. And even when he's commenting on society in a more serious way, they can still create stunningly good cuts, such as on "Trouble Every Day."

You can't really imagine the turbulent times of sixties without this album. The freak show finally arrives into the cultural spheres of music and obliterates every existing convention on its path. People were waiting this body of work - even if it was more of a subconscious expectation.

What i think of this album is the fact that there's definitely a lot of intellectual brain food around the spheres of the work. Free love manifesto is already in the frames of "Motherly Love" or one of my favorites of the album "Any Way the Wind Blows". The playful and naughty nature of these tracks is one of the best examples Zappa's love of the grotesque, where the conventional is vaporized by the contrasting weirdness coming out of the pen of a satirical behemoth. The sharp social nerve of Zappa also appears whether on the more straightforward Dylan-esque blues-rocker "Trouble Every Day" or on more interesting tracks such as the disturbing garage-psychedelic piece of "Who Are the Brain Police", asking do you favor the content or the "package"?

"Hungry Freaks Daddy" is probably the most popular track on the album, catchy, witty, cynical and brilliant at the same time. Definitely one of the highpoints of the album for me. Kinda reminds me of the fantastic "Peaches en Regalia" some years later, and he's already using the vibes very cleverly here! On the other hand, some of these arrangements are also quite dated. What's up with these silly Doo-Wop and Boogie numbers here? I'm not really getting the vibes from these. In addition, the sixties blues-rock standard is kinda archaic as well. I think Zappa's compositional skills improved vastly over the years, that enabled to churn albums that dated better than the one I'm dissecting here. I would also prefer that the other guys sing more, since Zappa's voice kinda pales against the other two, but i guess the quality of the vocals is part of the artistic vision here. The elevated levels of avant-madness of the last two tracks is kinda what i wanted to hear more, though at times these feel a bit over the top as well. But the last one can really groove in its strange avant-garde territory that defies all common sense. Yeah, the age of conservative innocence ends here and we start to question the values established around us again.

This is influential for sure, even blasphemous, ahead of the competition, but somewhat lacking the proper punch, where everything falls into place. There's loads of brilliant satire here, but the musical aspect is often lagging behind.

El monstruoso debut de The Mothers of Invention (Frank Zappa desde ahora, ya que es imposible pensar en el grupo sin centrar la figura en él) fue pionero en muchos aspectos musicales, pero también lo fue fuera de ellos. Primer LP doble, uno de los primeros en buscar la cohesión del álbum de una manera casi conceptual, y con una actitud critica y ácida no vista hasta el momento en la música.

Se hace raro que en pleno movimiento ''Flower Power'', cuando se proclaman desde todos los escenarios del planeta actitudes como el amor libre, Frank Zappa se presente como un líder satírico que muchas veces parece buscar la simple mofa con mucho más ahínco que mostrar su indignación hacia los temas de los que se burla. Una actitud bastante punk (¿freak?) en la época, pero que al contrario que otros contemporáneos, sí que venia de la mano de un virtuosismo en el instrumento y una habilidad fuera de toda duda para la composición.

Canciones a la vieja usanza como la inicial ''Hungry Freaks, Daddy'' o la tremenda ''Any Way The Wind Blows'' muestran el lado más ''clásico'' del rock que Zappa era capaz de desarrollar. En el otro lado de la moneda se nos presentaría un Zappa experimental con el clásico ruidoso ''Who Are The Brain Police?'' o la bizarrada de ''Help, I'm a Rock''.

El eclecticismo del álbum no se queda solo en es binomio, sino que va desde palos como el jazz, el soul y el amago progresivo que es la cara D del disco, dedicada por completo a los 12 minutos de ''The Return Of The Son Of Monster Magnet''.

Es difícil separar al personaje de la persona, y a veces es muy normal sentirse perdido entre la telaraña de ideas por minuto que es capaz de desarrollar. Hay momentos de verdadera locura, y para mi el intento de abarcar tantas ideas en tan poco espacio acaba pasandole factura al final. Todo esta lleno de buenas ideas, de momentos de inspiración tan brillantes que uno puede ver los 45 años siguientes de rock a través de sus sonidos, pero sin llegar a cuajar con la suficiente fuerza para ver el disco redondo que debería ser, cosa que si que llega a alcanzar con algunos de sus siguientes discos.

Porque no hay que olvidar que aun estamos en 1966 y esto era un ''simple'' debut...

Welcome to the land of do as you please, also known as the world of Frank Zappa. Where one man with immaculate hippie meets dictator facial hair and shaggy top does anything and possibly everything. Zappa is considered something of a (literal not just hyperbole) genius by his fans and music critics and I'm definitely seeing the possibilities already. He gets off the ground here proving himself to be an accomplished pop song writer with the nice little rock ditties that make up the first bunch of songs, and also successful experimental stuff on the second side. And by sides I mean double album, ambitious guy no? The hint of dry humor and quirkiness that pervades the thing acts as a definite precursor to the likes of The Talking Heads and They Might Be Giants to some degree, it's post-modern before that word meant anything. Paired with well written fun and adventurous tunes? And it's pretty easy to fall in love with this, now I know why some pals of mine are devotees. Overall what impresses me most is that Zappa balances his satire with genuine love of both the music he plays, music in general, and the talent to prove it. There's a deluge of satirical music these days usually made up of annoying idiots mixing random pop culture references with ska or ironic rap, all of them can fuck off and make way for REAL satirical musicians like Zappa.

"You found a candy bar?!"
"Yes, my son. Sit back and I shall tell you a tale!"

Even though I can't pinpoint the date, I remember exactly how it felt to listen to Frank Zappa for the first time. I was 14, hadn't discovered downloading yet (or communism, incidentally), and my musical interests were still narrow enough for me to be content with dipping into my mom's CD collection. Finally, I picked up Hot Rats. I was kind of confused. I knew the name Frank Zappa; I had once idiotically debated with some girl about how I thought Led Zeppelin were like, ten billion jillion times better because "Don't Eat the Yellow Snow" was stupid (I still feel like punching myself- I can't listen to Zeppelin anymore). I guess I thought I was getting some goofy guitar guy, and with the name 'Frank', I threw in some crooner elements. But I went upstairs and put it on anyway.

I didn't totally get it- the songs were reeeeaaaaaallyyyyyyy long, and I didn't know what to call it. Was it jazz or rock? Where's all the hippie shit or drug god wankery? But I kept listening. It felt like a cartoon. I subconsciously decided that Frank Zappa was my kind of dude.

Some time later, I was hanging out in Borders with my mom and I decided to get a CD. I was torn between two choices- The Transformed Man by William Shatner and Freak Out! by Frank Zappa and the Mothers of Invention. I don't know why I didn't go with Shatner, but I chose the latter. And yeah yeah yeah, it blew me away. Help I'm a Rock! and onwards were too weird for me, but what was all this other shit?! My idea of density came from Pink Floyd, and they didn't have xylophones and horns, or kazoo, or joy. It was funny! Music wasn't supposed to be funny unless it was in a comedy-rock ghetto! If I had known how much this album would come to affect me, I would have fainted. But it happened organically. I was getting a bit older and too cynical for Jefferson Airplane and company, so I turned to Zappa. And this album grew with me:

When I needed to feel superior to high school idiocy, I focused on the lyrics, which painted a grotesque picture of 1950s adolescence. When I became a full-on Zappa cultist in tenth grade I held this up as the perfect clash of high and low culture. And I'm still a Zappa freak, although I've mellowed out. I don't read The Real Frank Zappa Book weekly anymore or anything, but there's a certain Zappa attitude (Zappatude if you need to be cute) that permeates my artistic consumption- I probably wouldn't be a snotty dada fan without it. When I was more into complexity, the dense instrumentation became my focus. And when I got into noise and more abstract forms of music, "The Return of the Son of Monster Magnet" finally made sense. Now I can't believe that I could have skipped it.

These days, this album acts as security. I don't even know if I would call it my favorite Zappa album anymore (hi, Uncle Meat!), but it has to be my FAVORITE ALBUM OF ALL TIME because of what it created. Zappa is still everywhere I look in some way. His name is on the Nurse With Wound list. Merzbow cites him as an influence. I started reading Kafka because of him. I took everything less seriously after this album. It's the genesis of my current persona, the starting point of my love affair with the avant-garde and 'experimental'. Sure I was a snarky, smug, shithead for a while, but that was bound to happen anyway.

"No Commercial Potential" are words to build a life around. And I did, so suck it.
