The songs of Funeral seemed to be set in a post-apocalyptic winter, a suburban world buried in snow as the former world is gradually forgotten and all those who remember what life used to be dies out. The Suburbs feels a lot like its precursor, that last hazy summer before everything went to hell. It feels like the exhausted end of youth, and while it seems on the surface positive, it is frantic beneath and there is a sense that maybe nothing exists beyond what is being left behind. However, where Funeral was very much an emotional outcry, The Suburbs is much more nostalgic and self-reflexive. There is a lot of talk about hip kids and boredom and wasting time, and in a way much of the album is directed at the self-conscious cult of irony, a suggestion that it might be time to abandon poses and legitimately feel things again.

A lot of critics have referenced Bruce Springsteen as a major touchstone of the songwriting on this album, and there is definitely a resonance with his Born to Run-era tales of youthful abandon and all the complicated emotions and mythology of growing up. However, where Springsteen's songs were a loving chronicle of blue-collar living on the cusp of adulthood with cars, girls, parties, and hot summer nights, Arcade Fire is dissecting the suburban life and its insularity. The car belongs to the parents, and the driving is aimlessly around the sprawl of neighbourhoods of endless identical houses and dead malls. That fiery passion that drove Springsteen's protagonists is here a numb ennui. 'The emotions are dead, it's no wonder you feel so strange.' The kids here are not craving adventure, they just want to feel something real and genuine. 'I need the darkness, someone please cut the lights!'

I very deeply relate to this album. 'Spent the summer staring out the window,' 'The music divides us into tribes,' 'I would rather be alone than pretend I feel alright,' 'Late at night the feelings swim towards the surface.' I am part of a generation that has grown up in a weirdly plastic suburban environment numbed by mass media. The kids of the sixties grew up in the same environment, but they protested and rebelled, they spoke out. When the revolution was commodifed by the greedhead eighties, it stopped being cool to care about anything and all the plastic music and big popcorn movies so falsified emotion that no one seemed to be allowed to publicly feel anything anymore. The Suburbs is an album looking at all the desperately real emotion bubbling under a million identical facades trying to conform and asking how and when exactly conformity and being void of personality managed to become cool, asking where the unending circle jerk of ironic detachment and distaste for everything gets us.

Must it be so terribly wrong just to love something unabashedly?

Concept, people, concept...
Yes, yes Arcade Fire are divisive; any band of their stature unavoidably is. And understandably: Win Butler's prim posturing and Régine Chassagne's self-serious antics wear thin quite quickly. Yet I find myself compelled by Arcade Fire's ambition more often than not-- as they demonstrate on the Suburbs, they are one of the few contemporary acts willing and able to produce a thorough concept album. Radiohead is the only other group immediately comparable in this respect, although In Rainbows was a step away from the high concept album for them. The Suburbs is flawed, certainly, but it also achieves a coherence and development from song-to-song unmatched by any other 2010 release thus far. Even if it compares somewhat poorly to Funeral and Neon Bible, it is challenging and never less than interesting; at its best, it contains some of the most timely and memorable tunes of the year.

I've always been a member of the small but distinct minority that prefers Neon Bible to Funeral, precisely because Funeralamounts to a collection of fantastic songs tethered together while Neon Bible is a coherent, powerful whole. On this score, the Suburbs is much closer to Neon Bible--like Neon Bible, it explores themes of alienation and paranoia. Unfortunately, the concept is not nearly as interesting on this occasion--the Fire retread a lot of well-worn ground here. Neon Bible was an urgent, deeply troubled response to an atomized, belligerent society; the Suburbs is its lesser sibling, somewhat nostalgic, somewhat critical, somewhat blase, as fits its guiding concept and subject matter. On the other hand, there is also evidence of improvement here--most notably, the bloated production that occasionally marred Neon Bible has been scaled back significantly. And there are a handful of songs that match anything on the first two albums--notably, the title track, Modern Man, City with No Children, We Used to Wait, and, especially, Suburban War, which is downright beautiful.

What to conclude, then? The Suburbs is a step down from Funeral and Neon Bible, but not by much. It wanders into cliché frequently yet manages to remain compelling. And this is perhaps its most important lesson--by demonstrating that even a less-than-perfect Arcade Fire album demands attention and contains ample pleasure, the Suburbs establishes the Arcade Fire as one of the very few A-list rock bands of the moment. Success in perfection is easy, but success even when a band is somewhat off of its game is the true mark of greatness.

The Suburbs introduces you to the shiny new Arcade Fire that you’ve always known.

This idea is so relevant to this album I felt it would unfair of me to not include it when it’s been ringing in my head the entire time I wrote this very, very lengthy review. I realize that opening a review with a quote is an awful thing to do to you, but closing with it would probably be far worse.

"People can inhabit anything. And they can be miserable in anything and ecstatic in anything. […] We all complain that we are confronted by urban environments that are completely similar. We say we want to create beauty, identity, quality, singularity. And yet, maybe in truth these cities that we have are desired. Maybe their very characterlessness provides the best context for living." -Rem Koolhaas


Part I: What They’re Playing

When I wrote my review of Arcade Fire’s Neon Bible in 2007 I said that, compared to Funeral, what it lacked in emotional intensity it made up for in technical execution. I still say that holds true, mostly, but I also wrote it with the understanding that Arcade Fire would always be chasing Funeral… always. The blend of vitality and craft the band exhibited on Funeral was and still is so striking that if they were to confess to you that it was a complete accident you might be inclined to believe them without any sort of cynicism overlaying that knowledge at all. You might then convince yourself that they only pulled off Neon Bible because the feeling of Funeral had seeped into them just deep enough to make a good follow-up the only natural reflex. It would be easy to convince yourself of that, too. However, if you did that, listening to their latest effort, The Suburbs, would throw you. This new record delivers in a way that Neon Bible did not; in a way closer to what made Funeral so magical for so many people. What Neon Bible gave us, the fans, was what we thought we wanted from Arcade Fire: bombastic arrangements, recognizably meaningful (if not occasionally overbearing) lyrics, and at least the image of a stage full of tense emotional chaos. However, The Suburbs seems to give us back what we’ve missed.

What is it we’ve missed exactly? Well, it’s that sense of intangibility that makes a song seem bigger than what it “is”; the idea that the song is somehow greater than the sum of its parts. This abounded on Funeral on songs like “Wake Up” or “Neighborhood #3 (Power Out)” that, despite being relatively straight forward in their structure, still seemed to rise above themselves. On Funeral that phenomenon seems to come from the energy of the performers. The delivery on Funeral was so tense it crossed a sort of sadomasochistic threshold and made you willingly submit to it. On Neon Bible, however, the band let their skills speak for themselves a bit more. There were definitely big songs like “Keep The Car Running” and “No Cars Go”, but their effect wasn’t surprising. To be fair that might be because we were a bit jaded by that feeling of bigness from Funeral, but Funeral didn’t need to be as obvious as the blaring organs on “Intervention” to catch our attention and neither does The Suburbs.

Not that The Suburbs is at all minimal. The sound of this album is possibly the most expansive and exploratory the band has been to date. While those always obvious influences like David Bowie and Talking Heads are still present, The Suburbs sees Arcade Fire drawing from a much broader template of sounds. Electro-pop, shoe gaze, punk, country, noise pop, and even new wave influences all appear on this one album, but never seem at odds with each other. This broadened pallet is arguably why The Suburbs is able to seem fresh and alive the entire way through despite clocking it at over an hour in length, and with 16 tracks it is an appropriately sprawling album.

With The Suburbs, Arcade Fire seem to have scaled themselves back to just themselves again. Instead of trying to manifest their songs as some orchestral splendor, a large majority of the songs on The Suburbs seem full of life without having to make a point that there seems to be a 7-person minimum for the band. The Suburbs feels less worked, dare I say a little dirtier and maybe even somewhat home-grown in a Phil Elverum sense, and is all the better for it. The emphasis of the band’s songwriting seems to have shifted from showing off all they have to offer all the time to only showing what they need to show when they need to show it, and nothing more. In the process their craftsmanship actually becomes more apparent than ever.

While Neon Bible seemed to want the entire band up front and center, The Suburbs isn’t afraid to let some of them stand out of the light more often in favor of highlighting the strong points of each song. “Wasted Hours” could work well enough as just Win and his guitar and the melody of “City With No Children” would pass as a highlight for most other artists, but when you think they might only be good-enough songs one of those less-prominent performers inches forward to give just enough of a kick to keep them going higher without having to keep kicking or even carry them the rest of the way. “Month of May”, which seems like Arcade Fire’s take on “White Wedding”, has enough attitude in its punk-inspired sound that it could easily carry itself, yet here and there behind the brash guitars you hear little howls and moans of other layers that transform an otherwise straight-forward rock song into something aggressively unsettling. The success of this selective approach to composing has the effect of making something special out of songs that, by nature of their basic structure, could have wound up very generic or garishly ornate in other hand; songs like “The Suburbs” and “We Used to Wait” whose melodies are safe enough to have been possibly boring, but in the matured hands of Arcade Fire let their strengths sing out.

Despite its length, The Suburbs still feels like an album packed with highlights. While “Deep Blue” feels like alternate-universe Elliott Smith as opposed to a fully Arcade Fire song, only “Rococo” really feels distinctly out of place. While good enough, following the slow-rocker “Modern Man” it just seems haphazard. However, despite being the black sheep of the album, “Rococo” makes for an excellent lead up to the driving “Empty Room”. Channeling Ride’s more up-tempo moments, “Empty Room” feels like a shoegaze anthem passed through Arcade Fire’s showmanship filter with its swirling strings against wailing guitars. “We Used to Wait” rides a coldly persistent piano chord, only to erupt into dense and richly textured choruses. “Half Light II (No Celebrations)” builds and soars on top of a synth line that oozes new wave appeal. I could go on about a majority of the songs on this album in the same way. Yet, despite the outstanding quality of all of those songs, they all seem to build up to the phenomenal “The Sprawl II (Mountains Beyond Mountains)”. On “The Sprawl II” Arcade Fire have breathed their unique sense of wonder into The Knife’s ever-lauded pop masterpiece “Heartbeats”, while maintaining the sense of yearning found in the original and the rest of The Suburbs as well. After an album dominated by Win’s lamenting, Régine steals the show at the last second to shout out what Win seems to have wanted to say the entire time… and she has never sounded more beautiful.



Part II: What They’re Saying

To make a brief and under-detailed history lesson even briefer: post-World War II urban development patterns have been short-sighted bullshit, to say the least. If you’ve been looking in the right places it’s no secret that discussions about the failures and impending disastrous consequences of suburban sprawl have been going on for decades now. A majority of metropolitan area growth is outward and unorganized, creating swaths of loose urban fabric with little to no character or soul. Yet, most of those discussions have been about the quantitative effects on the environment and such in the not-so-distant future… but that’s the future. People have been living in these places for generations now. What effects have these places had on the past few generations who have lived in them? This is what The Suburbs explores.

Funeral was an album focused on internal uncertainty and finding hope where it was hard to find. Neon Bible looked outward and was worldlier with overtones of war and religion. The Suburbs, much like its namesake, is somewhere in the middle. Drawing from his upbringing in the Houston suburbs, Win’s singing about all the time wasted and waited seems to be as close to him as anything on Funeral, but also says something about all of those who “can never get away from the sprawl” no matter how badly they may want to, or how far they drive.

A sense of helplessness builds throughout the album. The album opens with Butler singing “In the suburbs I learned to drive / and you told me we’d never survive” and what follows is a sort of gradual sinking realization of just how true those words might be and how “grab your mother’s keys, we’re leaving” might only be a teasing hope. Driving, the thing that initially offers a form of escape, comes to represent a sort of inescapable bind to the sprawl. The seemingly endless repetition of road and driving images seems to mirror the endless and repetitive nature of his surroundings. Win sees himself driving endless through The Suburbs and people, maybe even himself, locking themselves inside and ignoring the cost of their outlying lifestyles. The Suburbs is riddled with poignant images that paint a bleak picture of the American dream: childless cities, cars, more cars, homes as prisons, new stuff, war, modern this, modern that, and everything is lit up and trying to stay young… but inevitably falling apart.

Arcade Fire hope they don’t live to see the end of things that are truly wild, but they can’t help but keep driving through the very fabric of society they see destroying it. Along the way they cheer themselves up a bit with more fleeting hopes and cynical people watching of their fellow suburbanites, but always wind up back where they started: driving through a lot of nothing… and more nothing… and more nothing… until Régine finally lets out that simple desire for escape lingering behind the entire album: “There’s no end in sight. / I need the darkness someone please cut the lights,” or i¬n other words, to escape the monotonous haze the “the grind” casts over suburban life.

Yet, the album closes with Win telling us that if he could get all that wasted time back he would just waste it again. Why? We don’t know, but it’s safe to infer that between all the dying gardens and clock punching in Arcade Fire’s version of The Suburbs there must have been something genuinely enjoyable intertwined in it all. There are a lot of places you could go with that, but I think the safest place to go is to say that people actively make the value of their lives. After all the lamenting and distracting, maybe what the album is really trying to say is that in the hands of the right people anywhere can become enjoyable and even beautiful. In this case the place is The Suburbs and the hands are those of Arcade Fire.



Part III: Wrap Up

So where does The Suburbs leave us with Arcade Fire?

(This is the part of the review where I play the “let’s talk about the band moreso than the album” card that seems to rear its head in so much music criticism… and is therefore shorter than the previous two sections.)

On one hand you could say Arcade Fire are still chasing Funeral. It needs to be said that this album is not Funeral. It doesn’t best Funeral, either. While that may be a bit of sentimental bias showing through, there is a certain intensity to Funeral that makes it somewhat more catching than The Suburbs. It almost seems unfair to say any of this since the band’s image has been so directly linked to Funeral’s intensity that even their live performances seem to get built up as the “Funeral experience.” While understandable, it’s unfair that the intensity of Funeral came to represent Arcade Fire’s intensity as a whole to so many people. That intensity doesn’t necessarily make Funeral musically better in any way. I think The Suburbs is at least Funeral’s musical equal. Yet, despite Funeral having set itself on a rather high pedestal, Arcade Fire never really slipped that far behind it, as far as their music goes. Even though Neon Bible was a bit obvious at times, it was still a very good album. The Suburbs, however, is tremendous.

On the other (stronger) hand you could say that The Suburbs finally allows Arcade Fire to say that they don’t need Funeral. The Suburbs more clearly establishes itself as an independent body of work. Neon Bible, even though it is respectable in its own right, was “the follow-up” and in that sense seemed to have been a distillation of what people picked up in Funeral. The Suburbs goes so much further than Neon Bible that it should finally carry Arcade Fire beyond being Funeral: The Band, as they seem to be portrayed so often. Comparisons will always be made, but after The Suburbs people should be able to anticipate new Arcade Fire albums for their own merits and not for how they weigh against Funeral.

Hopefully after The Suburbs people will be able to fully appreciate Arcade Fire for everything they do and how they do it, and not just the image they’ve come to represent.


Overall: A+

The Suburbs is a palate cleanser for Arcade Fire. Neon Bible took their crescendo-driven songwriting to its absolute limit, and I loved every orchestrally exploding, choir-drowned, organ-bellowing second of it. But, there is a limit to just how big you can get and after that you either start repeating your tricks or you can choose to move to a different aisle. It wasn't just bigger in sound but also in its message, Win Butler shifting his sights from the snow-covered neighbourhoods of Funeral to doom-mongering over the entire world. A few years later and those sentiments had moved to self-doubt, or as The Suburbs at one point puts it so aptly: "you never trust a millionaire quoting the sermon on the mount / I used to think I was not like them but I’m beginning to have my doubts”.

The Suburbs is a return to smaller stakes, to some extent. It's still very much an Arcade Fire record with all the epic moments that come with that name attached, but in the context of this particular group it's scaled down, stripping away a lot of the extra bells and whistles that they themselves had introduced to the 00s indie rock scene. It's more down to earth and firmly fixed in the personal scale of its titular nostalgic suburbs - Butler shrinks the record’s aim into the microcosm of the neighbourhoods and faded teenage memories, personal stories and introspection from when the rest of the world was just a blurry image in the horizon. Alongside that, the music pulls back as well: the production is more straightforward, even compared to Funeral, and the album frequently takes its time enjoying the stillness rather than ramping up to the finale, as evidenced by numerous two-parter songs with extended introductionary sequences or outros. The Suburbs still layered in multitudes - the band is a septet of multi-instrumentalists after all - but its approach is, in lack of a better word, less show-offy about the wide range of talent the band hold.

The greatest trick The Suburbs pulls is hiding its own expansiveness underneath a back-to-basics exterior: it's an experimental, transitional record in disguise. Crowd-inspiring anthems were Arcade Fire's signature move, but third time doing them in the same way and you could start wearing the concept thin - but at the same time, the band weren't seemingly sure where exactly to steer their ship either. Thus, Arcade Fire aren’t reinventing themselves here and the stylistic concepts of the first two records are running throughout The Suburbs - “Rococo” carries the maximalism of Neon Bible and the close-to-heart warmth of e.g. “The Suburbs” and “Modern Man” descend from the homegrown intimacy of Funeral. Many of the album's greatest moments have their feet stuck firmly in the band's own musical history, particularly with the hazy melancholy of its marching title track and the stunning "Suburban War" which is one of the band's most gorgeously arranged pieces with most arresting melodies - both of which still sound like familiar Arcade Fire by this stage. But those recognisable siybds are are intermittently scattered in-between the rest of the sprawling 16-track record, serving as the center for the ideas board the rest of the album represents and used as springboards to new areas. It's why it's not quite accurate to call The Suburbs simply an Arcade Fire record that got toned down, because a good two thirds of the record sees the band pulling in new ideas or executing old ones in different ways.

How that manifests most notably is in just how dynamic The Suburbs sounds - or in other words, how much it rocks. Arcade Fire have never exactly been close to the 'rock' part of 'indie rock', but for The Suburbs they channel their characteristic zeal and arena-sized energy into giving their songs a right kick under the rear. There’s a world of difference between e.g. the stadium fist-pumper “Ready to Start”, the noisy punk brattiness of “Month of May” and the baroque shoegaze of “Empty Room”, but what they all share is the sheer show of force in their sound and in the playing. One of Arcade Fire’s greatest assets is their passionate intensity and throughout The Suburbs they use that to be loud, fervent and exhilirating. Many of its most deftly arranged, gorgeously performed songs lie in its less frantic corners, but it's that punchiness of its most electrically charged songs that really sticks out when you actually play the album - and even during the subtler moments, there's a liveliness and strength to the band's play that comes naturally when now-seasoned live veterans return to bashing things out together within the same small four walls. In Arcade Fire's context The Suburbs is an intimate record, but only in the sense that the sweat in the player's brows is palpable through the sheer power of their playing, as they bring the songs to life in what is for them a relatively low-stakes production environment.

It's why “Sprawl II (Mountains Upon Mountains)” works so well at the end of the album. The other stylistic undercurrent cutting across The Suburbs is that it features a couple of songs where synthesizers dominate the soundscape - which, if you're aware of the wider discography, is in retrospect a clear test run for the ideas that would start popping around the next set of records. The twilight rave of "Half Light II" (which marries beautifully to its more Suburbs-like first half) is an exciting jolt of surprise already, but "Sprawl II" gets the fabled penultimate spot: the tracklist slot where Arcade Fire always bring out their record-defining highlights. Compared to most of the earnestly self-serious The Suburbs, it’s a complete 180° - a perkily pop-flirting number lead by Reginé Chassagne, all bright synth leads, bubbling synth bass and a light-footed, shuffling rhythm eager to hit the dancefloor. Where Butler has been searching for meaning in adolescent experiences and got lost on the path between the suburbs and where he is now (and as an aside, this is probably Butler's peak as a lyricist and "Suburban War" in particular is sublime and nails down the record's entire concept beautifully), Chassagne brings the colour back into the world. “Sprawl II” is a fantastic song, radiating with so much genuine warmth and fun - it's still caught in the anxiety of growing up somewhere so small that you felt trapped, but it's bursting with defiance and pride about escaping into the wider world. It subtly shifts up from its quaint beginnings into a veritable giant and even still it sounds so lightweight it could practically fly off. As the de facto closer of the record (the reprise of “The Suburbs” at the literal end is really just an outro), it not just brings the album's various threads together, but it turns them into the proud declaration of intent that the rest of the album shies away from - and it does it with a massive smile on its face. That contrast at the end works so very beautifully.

"Sprawl II" also draws a line on the ground as it starts pulling the curtains to a close: it's the most out-and-out diversion from what had been established as The Arcade Fire Sound, and after The Suburbs the band took it as their main inspiration to move away from that sound. Which makes The Suburbs somewhat of a pivotal record for the band because while on one hand it takes a step back towards a tone closer to their roots, it ultimately represents a desire to change, for the band to start shifting shape. Yet, its stylistic experiments are still done with an overall cohesion in mind as part of the Wider Concept of the record, and they're tucked in-between songs which basically sound just like more great Arcade Fire songs - so you might never even realise just how much it's started to move away from the expected. That's why it's not necessarily one of their more stand-out records, because it feels like a slight retread unless you really start paying attention to it - and it definitely could have shaved off a few songs and the "Wasted Hours" / "Deep Blue" couplet has always been the section that comes to my mind - and I for one certainly underestimated as such for a long time after its release. In retrospect The Suburbs' true nature becomes more clear though, both as a hint towards the future and final farewell to the past, and from a completely personal angle it took me a good many years after its release to realise just how good of a record it was and why. If it's possible for Arcade Fire to release a slowburner then this is it, but its thrills are many and various.

The Suburbs: the album that anyone who is anybody is reviewing here on Rate Your Music. This is pretty expected, since Arcade Fire is one of the most agreed upon bands of this community. Well, at least they were. When Funeral was released, it seems that nearly everybody was taken aback by the stunning beauty that the record contained. Three years of speculation led to some of the highest hopes that anybody could have concocted, and when the sophomoric follow-up Neon Bible came to, a few very minor schools of thought came about: a good majority of people saw Funeral to be the better of the two, and the new album was just good. Another group seemed to think that this album was of the exact same quality, or even that it exceeded this record. No matter what you thought of either album, if you liked Arcade Fire, camaraderie was afoot. Some have went as far as calling them one of the most definitive bands of the last decade (which, in the realm of indie, this isn't too grandiose of a claim).

Two thousand nine, two thousand ten. A new decade emerges and with it comes the announcement of a new Arcade Fire record. Slated for late summer, the anticipation surrounding The Suburbs really took off at the beginning of the season, with the release of the album art, tracklisting, and a few songs to boot. The songs left a certain sense of ambiguity amongst Arcade Fire fans; some professed disappointment, but were still excited for the album. Some were enamored by what they heard and couldn't be happier. There were even a few people who declared the band to be dead from the moment they heard the title track single. Things were beginning to get a little tense in Arcade Fire's fanbase, but it was nothing that seemed to be too big of a concern.

The album drops, and the entire internet goes wild. Many no doubt torrented the album while others were able to score legitimate copies early. A good handful of people insisted upon waiting for their preorder to arrive, but many ended up succumbing to the temptation. Thousands of Arcade Fire fans had this album from the moment it could be had, possibly making it one of the most demanded records of the decade so far.

Flash forward to about two weeks later. The stronghold of excitement has mostly been dismantled, and the sentiments of this album being their OK Computer, their The Dark Side of the Moon, their IV, have dissolved. A lukewarm, bitter tension has swept over the fanbase of Arcade Fire, leading people for the very first time down two distinctive roads. Down one path, you have the people who truly found something to enjoy in this record and were happy with what it was, even if they thought it wasn't as great as the others (though a select few might have even thought this). Down the even more populous other road, however, is a newly formed sprawl of individuals who were not satisfied with what Arcade Fire had brought to the table. Cries of uninspiration, pretentiousness, and poor songwriting can be heard by anybody who ventures down this road, and amongst the naysayers are even those who had a profound affinity with Funeral and Neon Bible.

For the very first time, Arcade Fire has made something questionable. They've made a record that people can't agree upon, and they've made it so that all future records will now not be perceived from the same mass collective, but rather two singular distinctive ones. It's really quite interesting that this would be the album to do that, though I applaud them for successfully keeping a cohesive bunch of fans for up until their third album. Most bands don't get that chance even.

So what's my take on The Suburbs? It's probably fair to first give you my take on Arcade Fire. Arcade Fire is a band that I like. They are a good group of musicians who write interesting songs, but they are simply that: a band that I like. I do not love Arcade Fire. I love a few songs that they've made, though. A band I like making a few songs that I love. Simple (and here, pedantic). There's also a lot of sentimentality in Funeral (since earlier this summer I associated it with the loss of a friend), so I do fall into the group of people who are always holding their work up to that first record.

When The Suburbs first got its listening in my house, I was ecstatically joyous and excited for what would come. Within the first five songs or so, I maintained this sort of awe in the content of the record, but I was excited for what was to come. Only a few tracks later, I found my attention slipping, and by the time it was nearly over, I realized that my excitement had faltered a great deal. What was the problem, exactly? Things got off to such an alluring start, only to be a bit crushed in the long run. I still liked that which I heard, but the intensity that showed its colors early on was never truly regained, save for a song near the end.

One of the biggest criticisms I've seen of this album is that people are calling it uninspired, and I just want to take this moment to call out such accusations. First of all, how can anybody know whether or not a song was written with inspiration? To say that something is not inspirational is solely a view of the self. It didn't inspire any emotions in you. How can you even dare say that Win Butler wasn't inspired when he wrote these songs? Maybe the fact that he focused less on hooks and aspects that we love and more upon other things is proof that it was just as, or even more, inspired than past efforts. I do not for one second believe that this is a work of filler, and despite what my ear and mind may tell me, I don't believe that there's a single song here that wasn't meant to be.

But I'm being droll. There are certain albums who simply need to have its tracks selected and talked about, and that's what I'm going to do here. Normally this is a criticism of mine when an album is not cohesive, but I actually think this is one of the more (if not the most) cohesive Arcade Fire records there are (though Neon Bible could give this a run for its money, maybe). I'm going to explain to you what I have discovered in each of these sixteen tunes.

The Suburbs...from the moment I first heard this song, I told myself that I liked it, but even then, I think I thought I liked it more than I did. Armed with a steadfast beat and a feel that is, as all titles may imply, suburban, the title track acts a bit like a moody overture to the music that's coming. It's not a song that pushes any boundaries, and when compared to openers of their past, it lacks that sense of drama that Arcade Fire has always instilled. Nevertheless, this song is entertaining and nice to have on. A little thing that bugs me is the line "Grab your mother's keys / We're leavin'". I understand that it's probably a callback to the memory of not having your own car and whatnot, but the line just comes across as being a little obnoxious and, dare I say, pretentious. Not a big deal, because I like this song, but just a nitpick that I need to throw in there.

Ready to Start...this is where the drama kicks in, and the Arcade Fire that we all have grown to know show their selves. When I heard this song at its own early release, I hailed this to be one of the most exciting things I had heard all year, and it definitely propagated an interest in me that I hadn't really found before. For the first time, I was truly anticipating an Arcade Fire album, and it felt good. Going by this song alone, it's no surprise that some people were let down by the album, seeing that this is one of the strongest songs it has to offer. The guitar kicks off and sends the listener into a torrent of that indie bliss we've all come to know. Win's dramatic wailings, as annoying as some might find them, really sit well with me, and I'm very deeply satisfied with the stay that this song has. One of their highlights as a band, no doubt.

Modern Man...this one throws the listener off with its obscure time signature, making a few people more than likely believe that their torrented version had a glitch in it. It's a rhythm that seems to diminish as the song goes on, but it's cool nonetheless. This was one of my first noted highlights of the record, and I really enjoy the part where Win's vocals perfectly match the guitar melody. I usually don't like bands that do this, but they've done it with a lot of charm here. At this point in the album, one might be expecting the thing to carry on this immensely satisfying charm. However...

Rococo...alright, listen: every band was bound to have its blunder sooner or later, and any band that has never truly written a fluke of a song needs to come forth and show their work, because even some of the most beloved groups have their dark moments. This is Arcade Fire's very first (and probably most hated) bad song. It's about hipsters. A friend once told me that he doesn't like songs that refer to indie rock, which I thought was a little odd ("At the Indie Disco" by The Divine Comedy was playing at the time), but hearing a song about hipsters made me cringe a little. It doesn't help that the melody and vocals are incredibly obnoxious, with its chanting of "Rococo-coco-coco-coco" et cetera. It's a song that serves as a set back for the record, but it's not where the album begins to lose its steam, for next you have...

Empty Room...guys, let it be known that violins are one of the most beautiful instruments that a man or woman can master. Listening to the furiously spinning violins of this song have damn near brought a tear to my eye, and I have yet to show any sort of disenchantment with this song because of it. Win and Regine sing together on this one, and the violin never lets up. As it relentlessly crafts what is to be one of the best moments on the entire record, I've begun to completely and distinctly evaluate what it is that draws me to this band. If they would ever make an album whose songs matched the intensity of this one, they would no doubt become a band that I love.

City With No Children...things calm themselves down quite a bit after the last song, and it was at this point that I thought a winding down song might be exactly what I needed. This one has been compared to the songwriting of Bruce Springsteen, which I could kind of see (though had it not been immensely brought up, I would have never made the connection). It's one of the more reserved songs, trucking along in the same way that "Windowsill" does. It's one that I think I'll come to love more with repeated listens, though it was admittedly my second least favorite of the album at this point. Still, I persevered.

Half-Light I...on my first drive through The Suburbs, this was one that didn't leave the biggest of impressions on me. Hell, even right now, I have to turn the song on for a little while just to get the feel of it again. Regine softly sings her heart out on this one (have I ever mentioned how much I love that voice of hers?), drawing a near comparison to a less hectic Bjork. Win comes in, though I kind of wish it had been left up to Regine. You know, I wouldn't even mind it if the next Arcade Fire album saw just Regine as the vocalist (as much as I love Butler, she sounds so much more stunning to my ear these days). This song is arguably one that you need to pay attention to if you want to successfully crack it emotionally, but it's not too bad of a track. Maybe better than the one before this.

Half Light II...way more exciting than its former counterpart, motivated throughout by a steady kick drum and a calmed Win. When I listen to this, I feel like they were trying to retain the same vibe that they had on "Rebellion (Lies)", between the drumming and the background chamber music. It's really a nice sound for them, but I definitely like that song more. This one sounds like its holding back its temptations to just let loose; a prolonged release that isn't ready to come out. Nothing wrong with that, of course. When Win and Regine sing together about halfway through the song, it's one of the most pristinely beautiful things that the record has to offer. Very nice stuff.

Suburban War...of all the titles that the tracklisting had presented to me, this was the one that I was most excited about. A really good title (see:Astro Coast) can mean the world to me, and seeing a title like that reminded me of stories my dad used to tell me about his neighbors across the canal. On the fourth of July, the kids across the canal and him would play pranks on one another, which escalated to aiming and firing fireworks across the water and at each others' houses. I'm pretty sure people got hurt, which makes me wonder how he didn't get himself in a shitload of trouble, but eh...it was the 1970s. Anyway, this song musically also gives me the feel of that story he told me, and while it's not the most immediately mesmerizing of songs, it's one that I like more and more every time I hear it. I kind of wish it had more of a climax to it (I like the build-up it has regardless), but what it is can be good enough even for now.

Month of May...some people love this song and can't get enough of it, while some find it to be dreadfully horrid and lacking. It's a garage rock song, which places this as being completely and utterly out of place, but hey, who really gives a shit? This is a fun song to me and it's one of the most rockin' they've ever done. Power to them for experimenting. I liked this even more than the A-side title track, so I guess you could say that this was one of the songs that set me up for the anticipation of The Suburbs.

Wasted Hours...this and the two songs that comes next form what's probably the most uninteresting of album segments. As a matter of fact, it was in the sea of this part of the album that I realized that I hadn't gotten truly excited since "Empty Room". Don't get me wrong, as I've expressed my liking for most of this album so far, but I was a little sore at the fact that they couldn't maintain that sense of wonder that they had kicked themselves off with. This song is a pretty neat one, but a slight problem I have with it is that it continues (and sort of helps along) the one-two-three-four song structure that quite a bit of The Suburbs's songs have. There's nothing wrong with that, but it's kind of unimaginative. I've always liked their crazier compositions, and this shows that they seem to be laying back more. Still, lyrically, this fits in perfectly and is one of the most relaxed things you'll hear from them. Worth listening to.

"Deep Blue"...on my walk through the Port Richey suburbs earlier (I was listening to this, I should add), I noted that aside from "Rococo", this is my least favorite that the new album had to offer. There's just such a sense of nothing emanating from this track. It's got a few key aspects that are cool, including Win's occasional slight of falsetto or the climax cymbal crashes, but for the most part...I really can't say that this one left much of an impression on me. Setting it inbetween "Wasted Hours" and "We Used to Wait" probably doesn't help it along, as it's now an island between two decent songs, allowing the shallow dryness of mediocrity to mound itself at the surface. Oh well, at least this is the last truly lukewarm song of the release. You've still got a little bit before anything wonderful comes back, though...

We Used to Wait...I probably made it seem like this song wasn't very good based upon the above writing, but it's quite the contrary. I actually think that this is the best of the desolate threesome that is pre-Sprawl The Suburbs. The steady piano and gradual percussion, tied together by Win's kinda pretentious rambling (once again, it's actually meant to be a good thing), is kind of charming. It's a very steady song, but not in the way that the title track or "Wasted Hours" was.

Sprawl I...and just like that, the album picks up where the beautiful "Empty Room" had left off. This is a dark, lonely and misery-laden track that seems to tell the story of nostalgia gone wrong. I really like the dark atmosphere that this brings, but despite the fact that I like this song, I want to point out that it contains the most dreadfully painful of things: a pretentious sort of pseudo-intelligence that even the most regressed of hipsters would shudder at the mere mention of. I'm of course talking about the part of the song where Win is speaking to the cop. The cop asks "Do you kids know what time it is?", and Win gravely responds: "Well, sir, it's the first time I felt like something is mine." *vomit*. Try saying this to a cop whose caught you out past curfew in real life. His response will be somewhere between a baton in the face and a taser to the temple. And rightfully so, as this part is one of the most contrived things I could have ever expected from Arcade Fire. The police officer goes onto asking Win a question once more, "Well, where do you kids live?", and Win dramatically informs the officer: "If you only knew what the answer's worth / Been searching every corner of the earth." Meanwhile, Officer ORLY has had about enough of this bullshit, and he takes you into police custody. A night in your hometown's jail might do you some good for mouthing off to him like that. God...despite my qualms with the lyricism displayed at the end, I really do like this, and I would think it's one of the best if it wasn't for...

Sprawl II...if there's any one song that has been claimed as a favorite more than the others, it's this one. If there's any one song that people are more divided over than the rest, it's also this one. Arcade Fire turns about one-hundred eighty degree and introduces a Regine-driven synth pop piece of bliss. Hearing her voice in this completely furthers my thought that she should have her own album, and as this song progresses, I realize that I've encountered my favorite song of the album. There's just something so beautiful about the hazy way she sings this one. Her reply to pretentiousness almost seems to be scolding her hubby for his verbal blunders of past, and seems to be letting loose the sort of joy that he had misconceived before getting into that car. It's one of the happiest things I've heard in a long while, and it's exactly the kind of music I need to be listening to. Though its poppy setting might not be for everybody, it's a song that you can't write off for being different (like with most bands). It's simply a multitude of all the things that they should have had in this album, saved for the final hurrah. I guess that's something, right?

The Suburbs (Continued)...and as mysteriously as it had appeared, it was gone. Reprising the theme that the album had kicked itself off in, this sorely and miserably shows its disappointment and horror as it slowly slinks from the scene of the suburbs. It's actually quite disheartening. It's as if Win tried really hard to gain something he wanted, but he just couldn't and now he feels dejected. This track could be best compared to a whimpering dog, scaredly trying to find his exit so he can cower somewhere in peace. It hurts my stomach just a little bit to think of the kind of misery it would take to produce something of this sound. And for that, I really am touched at how things end.

So let's recap, shall we? Tracks one through five (minus "Rococo") are a brilliant sign that the album will be gorgeous. Tracks six through thirteen, minus a few sprinkles of delight, are good but they can't quite get it up. The album closes with a regained strength in tracks fourteen through sixteen. Creamy moist brilliance with a dry center, really. That's what The Suburbs is kind of like. Luckily, the center isn't anything I would even remotely call filler, and it's still music that I hear as being worthy of my time of day.

I can see entirely why some people are crying nay at this CD. It's one that has a tremendous segment of hope fronting a pretty standard album. Had this been their debut, though, and Funeral had ended up being their number three, I wonder how things would have ended up. Perhaps things would be just as they are now? Maybe people are just tired of the sound, and whatever the band had released would have been received this way. Who knows...

All I know is that I like it. It's a good album and nothing more. Much like Arcade Fire.
