Grampus May 08 2010
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
It's been quite a while since I heard a Fall album and somewhere along the line I'd lost the knack. I'd forgotten you don't just listen to The Fall, you submerge yourself in them. Failure to do so will quickly see you floundering for support, disorientated by the combination of rhythmic dissonance and a guy with a vocal style comparable to the scruffy drunk found slumped in a corner mumbling to himself at the end of a heavy night on the beer. So it wasn't until the glorious clamour of "Barmy" with its sparkling scythe of a guitar riff that my senses began to regain their equilibrium. As a result "Bombast" remains an impenetrable mess, as if my mind gave the track a chance, couldn't make head nor tail of it and now refuses to acknowledge its presence. Not so with instrumental opener "Mansion" which comes across as a warped sixties theme tune for a TV show to rival The Munsters or The Addams Family. But "Barmy" was my true entry point into This Nation's Saving Grace and remains my favourite track.

Thankfully, however, this is no one track wonder. In fact I'm happy to throw my hat into the same ring as those who believe This Nation's Saving Grace is the place for the uninitiated to begin their exploration of The Fall. Part of the reason for that is down to accessibility. Besides "Barmy", "Spoilt Victorian Child", "My New House", the patchwork "Paintwork", the oddly angled rockabilly of "Rollin' Dany" and the repetitive and abrasive "Cruisers Creek" have an immediacy rarely heard from the band up to this point which I guess can be attributed to the increasing influence of Brix Smith. It's a stretch to announce The Fall have made a concerted effort to court popularity but there's no denying This Nation's Saving Grace is a distance away in style from debut album Live At The Witch Trials.

There must be an explanation for why the bonus material has been added in the way it has – two tracks inserted where side one of the vinyl issue would have ended and three at the end – and I'm guessing this would spoil the flow for those who grew up on the original, but personally I don't have an issue. All I will say if these were outtakes, or B sides, or standalone singles The Fall found themselves in a rich vein of form during this period.

I came to The Fall very late and, as such, I don't really have a favourite album but This Nation's Saving Grace is right up there with their best. It manages to be both acerbic and irresistible, inharmonious and accessible, raucous and welcoming, combinations very difficult to pull off but, here, almost effortlessly achieved.

Step One: Turn up late and noticeably hungover, dump your bag and coat at your desk and disappear immediately into the bathroom for a fifteen minute paid poo.

Step Two: After returning from the bathroom, fix a little breakfast and talk about the previous evening loudly using vulgarities that can be heard by anybody calling the office by telephone.

Step Three: As the stares from your colleagues intensify and their frustration begins to boil over, pipe down, finish your breakfast and then go and wash up the bowl and make a cup of tea, offering nobody else a drink, assuming that they all made one for themselves when they arrived at work half an hour before you.

Step Four: Log in to rateyourmusic.com to see which album you have been assigned to review on the "Go Review That Album" thread. Discover that it is The Fall's obnoxious classic This Nation's Saving Grace and realise that you are not very well versed in it as you have only owned it for a few weeks.

Step Five: Realise that you have This Nation's Saving Grace loaded onto i-Tunes on your company owned computer and decide to give it a spin. You will notice that it is having a visible effect on your colleagues; "Barmy's" incessant, catchy-in-that-annoying-way riff will draw sighs from the accounts department, and you can increase their annoyance by drumming the two beats on the table each time the riff comes back in. "What You Need" has a similarly repetitive riff that sounds like a drunk walking unsteadily home and will get on your colleague's nerves, especially if you jig around in your seat. Turn the volume up as loud as the computer speakers will tolerate and enjoy the rest of the ride, swirling noise, sudden hooks that verge on pop, then vertical descents into shouty punk, taking in a bit of doo-wop along the plummet. When you reach the end of this glorious cacophony, allow the dust to settle in the office, take in the relief on people's faces, then double click on the eerie "Mansion" and play through it all again.

Step Six: Spend the remainder of your paid working day flicking between a projected cash position for company funds and a six paragraph review of This Nation's Saving Grace, the latter of which will no doubt be the more fulfilling of the two.

This sure is a catchy record coming from a band that doesn't have mainstream appeal. When I do a mental run-through of all the people I know, most of them would react with violent dislike toward the Fall. Yeah, okay, it is a wonderful and frightening world. Most comments made would probably be regarding Mark E. Smith's vocals. "This guy can't sing. Who cares about seeing his new house? What is this? What is he even rambling on about?" Well, truthfully, I almost never know what he's talking about but you've got to love someone who utters lyrics like "feel the wrath of my bombast" and "I Am Damo Suzuki" in its entirety. Damo Suzuki would probably marvel. Punk rock that's too arty for the punkers. What a great lineup they had at this time anyway, huh? I love Brix Smith. Some of the Fall's finest is stashed on this record. So, Fall fans are a weird and strange breed. People don't get us, do they? Still, I don't know what's wrong with you if you can't dig the basslines at least.

I don't know how, but there was a locked groove in "I Am Damo Suzuki," where Mark E. Smith got stuck saying "drums" over and over again. I don't know if it was on purpose or not, but I thought it was pretty crazy. After all, the drums in krautrock are famous for their repetitive drum rhythms, and the song is about the singer of the most famous krautrock bands out there; Can. Greatest coincidence ever? Probably.

Also, another fun little fact, the record's sleeve is a collage of pictures of the group and newspaper articles, some of which concern the group. But there's one article that seems to be a review of Sonic Youth's Bad Moon Rising, or a review of a gig from that time. Overall, it seems to be a pretty negative review. Curiously, the clipping never mentions Sonic Youth by name, but there are hints; "'*obscured* Moon Rising'," musical descriptions similar to that of Sonic Youth, and a mentioning of New York. One part that isn't obscured is a comparison the author made, saying the band is "strung somewhere in between the Fall and the Doors." I thought that was pretty cool.

I fear the beer is making sludge in my head
When he's not in the pub getting bladdered or doing endless lines of speed or insulting members of his own band Mark E occasionally gets round to writing some songs and on this album he has produced some of his best.
Who can fail to warm to such outlandishly eccentric lyrics as:

"May we go back to days pre-virgin?, Cannot get on clear vinyl, The handle that was brass is now brass evil, The rock that was an egg is in wrong cradle, The hand that cradles the rock makes egg gooey, I am damo suzuki"

And as if that wasn't enough this was probably the strongest band line up The Fall ever had and together they created a perfect marriage of poppy guitar hooks and cacophonous confrontation with that dishevelled sounding Salfordian snarl of disapproval peppering every note with effortless attitude.
It's easily up there with 'Dragnet' as one of the two greatest albums they ever produced!
