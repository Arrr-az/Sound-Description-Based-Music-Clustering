A great classic album of it's time and genre. Great punk rock with fast catchy songs that actually make you think, unlike most pop music, which probably makes you less intelligent; not all of it, but some indeed. Some say that this album (and other BR releases) are repetitive, which I can understand, but if you listen to it enough each song begins to stand out on it's own. I think it's because of it's tight production; there doesn't seem to be any blemishes or change in guitar distortion and things of the such. Even so, it's still a great album. The standouts here for me are "You are the government", "Suffer", "What Can You Do?" and "Do What You Want". Can't go wrong with some good smart Bad Religion style punk tunes. Great stuff.

This album's regarded as a punk classic, yet when Bad Religion was listed as one of my top 5-10 favorite bands, along with X and the Adolescents, I got mocked rather heavily for it. Hmmm. Anyway, this is good, hooky punk that you could consider rough if you haven't listened to BR's own How Could Hell Be Any Worse? or any of the really garagey Dischord Records stuff, and it's a solid taste of what this band has to offer: Easy politically-aware power chord punk that the average Joe Schmo factory worker can understand. Take it or leave it, it's been around for awhile and ain't goin' away.

MWE 2024, Day 13
For a genre called Melodic Hardcore, I would hope for the melodies to properly stick with me, but with the first in a trilogy of acclaimed Bad Religion albums, what I actually walk away with is acceptable, sustainable punk rock with the only thing sticking to me being the fact that the singer sounds so generic. Then I learned that the reason Greg Graffin sounds like a college professor is because he is one and more fittingly for natural sciences. The production doesn't wow me either, but it's certainly way better than I would hope for in the late 80s, the worst decade for mixing & mastering. Overall, the fact that it exists proves that it exists.

Classic milestone
Considered to be the first in their trilogy of Melodic Hardcore classics, this album is great. For many people, they would say this is where it all truly began for this band. After the flop that was Into the Unknown, BR came back with a stonecold classic that defined this style of music and influenced several bands and the genre as a whole. And it has what you would come to expect for a great BR album, it's poetic, political, energetic, rebellious, pessimistic, philosophical, etc etc. You Are (The Government), Give You Nothing, Forbidden Beat, Suffer, What Can You Do?, and Do What You Want. Are especially fantastic songs, "What Can You Do" and "Do What You Want" has to be one of my favorite pairs in all of punk for sure. A perfect comeback honestly, it's however less engaging than No Control and doesn't hold up as well as it does. However this album is still a jam

"Did you ever see the concrete stares of everyday?"
People may complain that Suffer is one note. I say that it struck a chord, and, like any punk guitarist worth their salt, hammered that power chord into the groundand then some.

The intelligence behind the lyrics, the propulsive instrumentals, the sheer charisma of the songs; throw on any track today and I guarantee it remains compelling. How can you not love those slides? Suffer is foundational to the evolution of punk rock through the decades following its release, and it still holds up as a pinnacle of achievement in punk songwriting.

It's Good Old Melodic Hardcore
Not much can be said about Suffer, in my regards it's feels a bit less what I expected. Though I do understand their structures and lyricism - most of the songs have that sense of nice uses of words via elegance, honestly there's just not much to prove about it. I know it's a baseline problem to say that most HXCX is "too fast to understand", but to me the problem that I do understand, just kinda felt uninterested at the end by the lack of bite, or maybe that's just me.

Still, I like the riffs, and I'll say it again Greg Graffin has a nice way of saying stuff, but still it's another slightly good Bad Religion record, and that's all that matters.

I do like chanting of counting on "The Numbers Game" though, they should do that more often.

Bad Religion is a no-frills punk rock band with a sense of melody and very clean playing. Their sound is extremely samey, but their good songs manage to sound different from their inferior songs. This album has almost no stand-out songs except in the way of Best for You, Suffer, and Delirium of Ideas, and Suffer is the only truly really good song here. The other two are good to pretty good. Otherwise, this album is so consistently okay that it becomes a slog to listen to. Your mind starts to melt listening to this album because it sounds like 20 minutes of nothing but the same thing over and over again with very little to try and revive you aside from those mentioned songs. Go ahead and worry about the albums after this, you don't need to visit this one unless you're a big fan.
