In 1967 Rock music changed forever, at a very fundamental level. Rock went from being a most Pop medium of jingly accessible three minute tracks meant to please young ears and make the record companies money, to being a true form of artistic impression, and is so doing it became Rock, what we know as Rock today. The catalyst for the change had been the release of The Beatles album Revolver the previous year, but everything broke wide open in 1967, with an entire pantheon of albums that changed music forever. Are You Experienced? was one of those albums. The Jimi Hendrix Experience burst onto the Rock music scene with this, their debut, with all the blazing intensity of a neutron star. No one sounded like this in 1967. This album sounds nothing at all like any album that preceded it. Jimi Hendrix' Guitar sounds like something from another distant, strange and beautiful galaxy, not like something any human was capable of. The roots of at least 70% of what Rock is today can be traced right back to Purple Haze. I would consider Are You Experienced to be, in fact, the second most influential album of all time.

Yes yes Brits, I am listening to the North American version of this album. A change from my usual policy. I mean I've broken this a few times before and after, I bet it's annoyed you that I listened to the versions of the first two Smiths albums that have those awesome singles on them! But here? I feel at least to the 300 Million (plus Canadians) this really is the icon. This is the album. And how the hell can I review Jimi without some of these songs? You already won the Beatle battle, let it be man. So Are You Experienced? I almost feel silly writing about something so overwritten about. But really it's scary that it's hardly that much of an exaggeration that a good 50%+ of modern guitar playing sprouted directly from this album. It really does seem to be the case. In some ways it's even way ahead of it's time. Man, where do I begin. Jimi takes a starting point from most of the basic guitar playing of the time of course, but attaches rockets to it and just goes hog wild. I'll be honest, to some extent the difference between the acoustic and electric guitars on these early decades of rock sometimes actually blurs in my modern ears. For example, the difference between acoustic Dylan and electric Dylan is there obviously, but it's not really all that jarring. And back then? It WAS super jarring. Frankly to modern ears, electric Dylan more often than not still sounds pretty stripped down. It's one thing to play electric guitar, it's quite another though to actually make that guitar sound electric. Electric guitars can truly do stuff the normal kind definitely can't, that should be taken advantage of! Enter Jimi Hendrix! Just listen to pretty much any track on here and you can here him just thrashing out bold new avenues on that thing. You can hear spurts of pretty much all the harder edged rock genres if you listen closely. Metal, Punk, Noise, even down to specific style. Tell me in the middle of Manic Depression you don't hear a flash of Eddie Van Halen even? We take all this for granted now but hell, it had to come from somewhere. And usually these things form over much time, and over many different artists. But so many huge leaps are taken all at once here. It's not just guitar playing either, these are some thick and juicy melodies if ever I've heard them, funky as they are rocking. And what a vibe it all creates! A hazy sort of fuzzy other world experience. Perfectly ideal psychedelia without all the bells and whistles to begin with. It really does feel like that awesome North American cover art! There's decent variety in sounds too. There's the songs that rip the roof off (Purple Haze), those that take it sexy slow (The Wind Cried Mary), and some that have a more beautiful spacey feel (the title track). And Jimi's voice! My god, the final touch in why this is a masterpiece. What a set of pipes he's got! Injecting that real deep soulful shit into this thing. Blows Jim Morrison out of the sky. In some ways I'm not sure Rock has ever really recaptured exactly what Jimi had to him. Party this had to be cultural, black and white culture meeting as it did with his band. It's a combo that has rarely surfaced since (Prince honestly might be the closest thing we've gotten). White and black music has remained so hugely segregated, and I think only just now are some of those borders finally really dissolving. I say this because there's a fuckton of energy straight from the Soul genre here that you damn well won't find coming from Cream. Heck, maybe that will be one of the new around the corner trends in the 2010's? I sure hope so. Until then we'll have to sustain ourselves with Jimi and his rare ilk!

Being the pretentious snob that I am I have great difficulty truly appreciating cultural icons whose image outstrips their creative output in terms of popular appeal. You know the ones, the ubiquitous "bumper sticker" idols.....Pink Floyd, Led Zeppelin, Bob Marley, and yes, Jimi Hendrix et al. Setting all of these prejudices aside I am able find the artistic merit in all of these names but none so much as the beamed down from outer space genius of Jimi Hendrix. What matters to me most about Hendrix is not his immense guitar prowess or his flamboyant image so much as his commitment to innovation in all that he attempted, his exploration of sound, and his desire to express something mysterious and powerful with his natural gifts. _Are You Experienced_ is like an eleven step demonstration of the myriad possibilities that Hendrix was working from. There is amped up R&B, heavy psychedelia, distorted blues, lighter than air balladry, and cosmic freak outs all carried out with an acutely inquisitive playfulness intermixed with a serious burning urgency, perhaps inspired by an awareness of his impending mortality. A landmark album and an hugely entertaining one as well.

Don't know The Beatles, wonder what all that hype was about? The White Album would be my choice as a crash course in Beatles 101. It was released in the second half of their career, so you get a bit of everything in this brilliant double album, the first of the "coloured" disc as well. From simplicity to intellectual, the lyrics are genius as are the melodies and subjects. Despite the lack of continuity, you look forward to the next one and so forth; all of these songs are addictive. There isn't one bad track, including number nine; hey it was the 60's.

Some consider this album the first sign of the band falling apart, as they offer up more than the norm when it comes to individual pieces. If you know their music, you will know who wrote what on where, after you've spent some time with this pure white The collaborations are quite evident at this stage, still, Lennon & McCartney kept to their bargain of sharing equal rights to all their songs, whether they had anything to do with it or not, they both got equal credit.

This is double treat of ensembles and solo works, hence the supposed beginning of the end rumours. To date George Harrison had only been allotted two songs per album, and Ringo was lucky if he got one. Not so here, you finally get a taste of Harrison's talents, which went on to inspire the eastern influence that was so popular at the end of the 60's. There is no secret here, the live performances had ceased and session work was the new game. During this break, they each sought out there own interests and for the first time, they did so as individuals and not as a whole, thank goodness too, as they returned with an abundance of musical gold. So pay no heed to those silly rumours, no one buried Paul, you can't hear jack if you play it backwards, but that is what "number nine" is for.

This is an important album, just as much as any of the others. Here you can see how individually they each are and what gives The Beatles that, oh, so special chemistry. It's pure creativity in every form, raw, harsh, poetic and melodic, it's one ride you will find worth taking. After a few listens, and you will be able to spot who wrote what and where... even if it's just the middle eight.

The diversity is immense, like opening Pandoras Box, you will find that resistance is futile, The Beatles in all their many forms come out to play and per usual they don't let you down. Enjoy!!

The album's highlights are its bonus tracks: Hendrix' first 3 singles and its B-sides. "Hey Joe" is the best version of that song ever recorded (even though Willy DeVille's version is also quite good). "Purple haze" opens with one of the greatest guitar riffs ever, while "The wind cries Mary" remains Hendrix' finest ballad. Its B-sides "Stone free", "51st anniversary" and "Highway chile" are brilliant as well. Those 6 tracks make the album worth 4.5 stars alone. Then there's also the original album, which gives us "Foxey lady", "Manic Depression", "May this be love", "3rd stone from the sun" and the title track. Hard to pick a favorite from Hendrix' 3 regular studio albums, but this one remains special because it was the first and therefore the most surprising.
