Many rock acts like to pretend how they're dangerous, out on the edge, putting everything at risk. Rowland S. Howard is all that, and he's not acting. There's no pretence here, it's singing with a mouth full of whiskey and broken teeth. Sometimes it's even hard to take it all because of that. It's a bit easier to go with guys who pretend a bit, at least you have a feeling that you might survive the ride. Not such certainty with Rowland, he throws his heart out on the asphalt and the traffic rides over it. "Sleep Alone" is probably the best song he's ever done, the real and true grinderman (no offense, Nick).

Crown Prince of the Crying Jag
Teenage Snuff Film is absolutely stunning. A definite modern classic in my eyes. Both terrifyingly dark and miserable, but near constantly breathtaking. His cover of "White Wedding", while it's a good one that fits in thematically and stylistically, is the only thing I would consider skipping over, just because everything else is on a different level. Of course, Mr. Howard is one of the best guitarists I've ever heardthe sounds that come out of that Jag are fearlessly demonic, sadistic, sexy, and beautiful in the most twisted way. The bitter "I Burnt Your Clothes", "Silver Chain", "Undone", and "Autoluminescent" are favorites that'll probably haunt me forever. For shame, individuals this gifted should be immortal.

Fuck this is amazing! I can't believe that ex-bandmate Nick Cave is so huge whilst Rowland is still playing dives in St.Kilda. Not that there's anything wrong with dives in St. Kilda, but, anyway this album is a miserablist masterpiece, lots of love-gone-bad songs, mostly slow in tempo, lots of squally guitar bursts that compliment Rowland's flat vocals that are like a slightly cheerier (not much) Michael Gira of the Swans....really emotional and kind of sarcastically jaded. The songs are very simple and direct for the most part, and are dripping with a thick sludgy pathos that is completely effective and engaging.

An excellent display of dark/depressive blues rock (or however you'd call this style) - big recommendation for Cohen, Daleka Obala, Morricone and (obviously) Nick Cave fans.
Perhaps a bit uneven when it comes to quality but Dead Radio, She Cried, Silver Chain, Autoluminescent, Exit Everything and Sleep Alone are worth hearing for anyone who ever spent a night in a dark room smoking and thinking they're the most profound piece of shit ever - and I'd wager that's a lot of us.

Howard really manages to hit that unique balance of the goofy pomp of cabaret with the genuine downer sound of 'trve' blues. It's rather insistent in creating an engulfing atmosphere but it never feels novel. Besides, how could it? Something so brilliantly arranged and catchy can only be a product of a man completely aware of the bounds of aesthetic reach.

A relatively overlooked and lost 90s classic. Relative in the sense that I've never heard it being mentioned in any forum or real life music discourse. Recommended.

Maybe I should drop the straight edge thing if this is my reaction to pure alcohol-fueled seediness.

Rowland S. Howard delivers a night out on town full of regretful boozing, unending loneliness, dread, and gut punches. Howard always had one of the most distinctive guitar tones in rock, and here it sounds like he's bringing straight razorblades to the strings at points. "Sleep Alone" in particular is a great showcase in the noisy fire-starting nature of his axe, with the song's rootsy riffing eventually collapsing into screeching, unhalting noise that drones on for a few minutes on end. The music drawls and bobs along to the heavy, circular basslines at a lowdown country pace, and this accentuates the dark, quasi-goth atmosphere.

It helps too that Howard is delivering unmatched tales of misanthropy and hedonistic overindulgence to the point where all pleasure is lost to begin with--brimming with sadness and sympathy. The "characters" (this album seems pretty autobiographical I gotta admit) are pathetic drunkards who can never hang on to love or happiness or anything tangible except another pint, drowning their crumbs of hope in more substances and cheap sex in lieu of real human connection, and Howard's monotone retellings of these sad saps is punctuated by crashes of guitar and cymbals after every cutting line--you can really feel the crimson on those accentuations.

What really separates this album though from others like it is the genuine emotion. The lyrical themes seem par for the course, but Howard's rich poetics are consistently heart-wrenching, "A powder burn to the face / To remember you by / A powder burn to the lips / It'll make you cry." Even in stone-faced confessions, Howard's tangible remorse elevates this album greatly, "She cried / When I told her / When I kissed her goodbye / Oh my baby / Oh my God what have I done / What have I done." Howard flirts frequently with death, both through violence and suicide, but it never feels cheap--even if the album itself feels like a tour through a cheap, bedbug-ridden motel.

These lyrics mixed with the country stylings and undertones make a genuinely emotional, and I'll say it, often beautiful punk record in a tragic kind of way. Despite the lyrics of the straight up gothic country track "Autoluminescent" showcasing full-blown narcissistic mania, with Howard claiming to be bigger than Jesus and greater than God, the song manages to be remarkably tender with the minor organ drones hanging in the back, and Howard's sneer bringing the slightest tinge of melody.

If you're having a truly miserable night, perhaps partaking in an adult beverage or two (something I do not do so take anything I say with a grain of margarita salt) then why not pop this on and commiserate with the self-proclaimed "Clown Prince of crying jag?"
