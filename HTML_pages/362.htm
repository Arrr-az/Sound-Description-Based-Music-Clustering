Zephos Apr 04 2011
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Each step taken by Wire in their classic period (really only period, who cares about anything they made after?) is a quaking moment for Post-Punk. Pink Flag was the clarion call to mutating Punk at all in the first place. Chair's Missing was the moment when the idea of Punk being a careful arty thing that needn't speed by or be full of energy was best first communicated, not to mention the inclusion of textures like synths and really strange guitar sounds. 154 is such a huge forward step though compared to Chair's Missing that it's a little jarring at first! Pink Flag to Chair's Missing is a logical very natural feel of a transition. When you first put 154 on though? You'll probably swear you're hearing a new band! There is a second singer now (who opens the album) but really just the sound of it is a huge change up. The synths and guitar sounds that were subtly layered on their Ramones buzzsaw last time have pulled a coup and are now firmly in charge of the joint. Most of the songs here are dominated pretty hard by frigid freaky synthesizers that add a metallic cold sheen to everything. The Ramones buzzsaw is still around more or less but even it sounds much more alien than before. This is really one of the chief records I'm mentioning in this year's intro paragraph when I say people were walking on the moon. The songs here are among the best the band ever made, the last two albums work better as wholes than they do if taken apart (bar a few). 154 has a bunch of tracks that are just straight up EXCELLENT and are great by themselves, and they run a sort of gamut too. In spite of the very set sound this has it really runs with a high level of variety. There's the electronic upbeat punk On Returning, the crunchy and incredibly catchy The 15th, the gothy opener I Should Have Known Better, the long creepy Sonic Youth like A Touching , and even some catchy New Wave style stuff with Map Ref! Each of these approaches is worth the time given, all together it's nothing short of a huge pleasure. But not everything is perfect here. The Other Window is the worst song the band has in this period by far, a meandering pretty bad spoken word thing that smashes the spectacular momentum the first three songs had going for them. And as far as Wire being the kingmakers of Post-Punk development they are no longer out in front. As excellent as their execution is here they were among peers at last with these motions, Joy Division and Gary Numan for instance were making similar advancements on this sort of martian sound. Still this stands tall among those peers nonetheless. Like Gang of Four Wire is among the more important influences on the group of bands who rediscovered all these sounds in the 00's, and 154 is the Wire album to make that really so. And lots of Post-Punk bands would follow up on the synth take this has heavily within the next couple of years. Groups like the Psychedelic Furs, Simple Minds, The Sound, and even Joy Division itself (whose wonderful Movement album as New Order is a clear younge brother to this) all owe a great deal to 154. That much is sure. And sure 154 itself owes quite a lot to Bowie's Berlin albums, but there's sounds on here even Bowie dared not to touch, some fearful off color melodies and so forth that point also to American bands rather than British ones. Sonic Youth I already mentioned, but Steve Albini (particularly his Atomizer album with Big Black) and Fugazi were surely listening as well to the more harsh moments this includes. Another crown jewel on the already bedazzled 1979.

Rating: 5
Highlights: I Should Have Known Better, The 15th, A Touching Display, Map Ref. 41°N 93°W

1979 was really a great year to a new kind of music. Like Gentle Giant to progressive-rock, Wire were (beautiful word-game) the post-punk aestheticians. Nothing there is blurred, there's no guitar solos destroying our patience, there's no forced rage, there's no horrible cloths or haircuts (green, yellow, pink) - they were just like perfect assertive boys. Like Velvet Underground or Silver Apples they knew the exact time for a melody and invented new sonic textures, eventually cold at the first sight yet strongly humanized. We couldn't say this isn't civilized music. But it is appart from everything we knew at that time. They were musical surgeons.
1979 was a great year, "Pink Flag" started the kick two years before with 21 songs/moments in 38 minutes, an arty scream compared with other dull punkers. I still enjoy Buzzcocks and some Clash, dug Dead Kennedys in my youth (not now) but nowadays I haven't any patience to punk rowdiness and lack of catchiness. And I still praise Wire above all, they were tricky and eagle-eyed composers. I once loved their recordings, especially this "154" and "The Ideal Copy", in my opinion the most mature works. "Chairs Missing" is also terrific, I say.
In fact, I even consider "154" better than "Unknown Pleasures" - Oh, God, what a sacrilege I've said! Probably it is really better. Architectonically speaking, think of "Unknown Pleasures" as a ruined factory and "154" as a modern cathedral, ruined as well...
1979 was a real great year. "154" with glassy walls and creepy open spaces was certainly an influence to many post-punk groups (XTC, Echo & The Bunnymen, The Cure - yes right!) and stood light years away from many contemporary bands. A masterpiece!

Grampus Jun 07 2008
+5 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Three albums in and Wire have become positively loquacious. Compared to their back catalogue thus far a 13 track album is something akin to an EP! 154 – named after the number of gigs the band had performed up to that point in time – sees Wire continue to develop by delving in some very strange places – as if Pink Flag and Chairs Missing weren't bizarre enough! I know that 154 is considered inferior to the previous two releases but I've never been able to see that. For me, 154 is simply the next stage of a very weird journey.

There's something almost tactile about Wire's textural landscapes which, even though dissonant and abstract and grisly in detail, make you wish you could bathe in their resonance. Who would have thought that cartography ("Map Ref 41°N 93°W"), the alienating effect of love ("I Should Have Known Better") and the struggles of a horse caught in a barbed wire fence ("The Other Window") would be so compelling?

The album resounds with a sense of separation and lack of understanding. That events are happening to people around you but somehow passing you by, or happening to you but you don't have the ability to interpret them, or happening in front of you but you are incapable of or prevented from intervening. The death throes of the horse are viewed through the window of a passing train. A relationship splintering through the pressures of being apart but no-one knows how to fix: "will she save me from what or who I do not know" ("A Touching Display"). A sense of voyeurism: "it's not hard to see another unique event when you miss the beginning and you miss the end", that suddenly changes to one of anxiety over the same detached state: "I never know which version I'm going to be, I get the feeling my mind is deceiving me" ("40 Versions").

The opaque, sometimes ambiguous, lyrics, the deadpan vocal delivery, the bleak nature of the music itself, all contribute to an uneasy album and I can understand why people have problems getting through it in one listen. But, in a strange perverse way, 154 speaks to me very clearly because, when I was younger, I was always that impartial observer, that onlooker that didn't know what to do for the best, that shy kid that was always worried about what people would think of him so never took the plunge. And for those reasons this will always be an important album.

I find it amazing that, after the release of 154, Wire temporarily disbanded. I suppose that eight year gap is the reason I lost touch with them. I realise now that I may have missed out on something really worthwhile.

Too many fade outs are the only blot on this near perfect album's book
Utterly sublime in most regards, 154 features perhaps the best production I've ever heard on any album. Unfortunately the album can feel somewhat incoherent at times due to the repeated use of studio fade outs at the end of songs, which robs the album of some of its momentum.

Otherwise though here we have the band who just a couple of years prior had broken punk down to its bare essentials doing the same thing to the likes of Pink Floyd.

An album which still sounds futuristic 40 years after its release, combining ominous and psychedelic atmospheres to an often powerful effect.

In amongst all of this one can find a surprising amount of pop prowess, in particular on Map Ref. 41°N 93°W, a track whose sound managed to point the way towards much of the pop music of the next decade.

Indeed many parts of 154 can arguably be seen as pointing the way towards the alternative music of the decades that have followed it. One can't help but wonder how much influence it had on many new wave, shoegaze, dream pop, post-rock, and art rock acts in that time.

An essential listen for any alternative music fan.
