Man, this is one of the absolute most perfect records ever made....I wouldn't change a single note about this record, start to finish, not a thing. I love every note, every nuance and am surprised just how quickly Ray made the leap, as Face to Face showed something was coming, but Something Else fulfilled it all....

Heck I love the sarcasm in the title. I'm sure Ray knew he had something cooking at this point, he'd had a ton of hits in England, and was starting to write songs for Cher, etc at this time making them hits, and was at the peak of his powers....

Then he got into some surprisingly very little written about silenced altercation (I heard perhaps he attacked a music industry related press agent and was banned from touring that states for a while because of it, though little is mentioned, I'd LOVE to know if you know for sure...

Anyway, I got this record used, finding it for next to nothing in the late 90's a good few years before the Kinks became cool again....this was the first album I dove into after their greatest hits, and I didn't really know a lot about them at the time...I traded in some 4 disc Beatles interview cd set and the music store at the time (a great music store btw that is now defunct that I spent too much of my then next to nothing college income at) surprisingly overpaid me in credits for that interview set (I think I paid like 18 dollars for the 5 disc set, and the guy at the store gave me 35 for it used (store credit) or would have given me 17 for it cash....I of course jumped on the credit....that store was awesome--relax I more than made up for the one time overpayment to me). But I had an amazing finds day that day as I snatched up this record--real introduction to the real stellar kinks; got my first ever PJ Harvey record (To Bring You My Love--fairly new at the time and I loved that one and have bought everything sans Dance Hall and the Rid of Me Demos since); I snatched up the imported Come On Pilgrim/Surfer Rosa (which was my intro to the Pixies; nabbed my first TOm Waits record, and got the Rykodisc Ziggy Stardust, all without paying a dime over the $35 store credit....best single shaping music day of my life...I think and it cost me nothing.

Anyway aside, asides.....back to this record.

Not a single note or ounce out of place. Every song is classic kinks:

David Watts has the britpop punk thing going for it. Who didn't know a David Watts kind of character growing up...I wish I coulda been David Watts meself.

Death of a Clown is one of those songs I sing to my wife whenever she's nagging at me to do some total wife-y thing like see "Ghosts of Girlfriends Past", etc....she doesn't quite get the quote just yet...and no I did have the gumption to put my foot down on that seemingly crappiest of the crappy movies, but she did try forcing me to see it....

Two Sisters, wow married life down perfectly, unisex two, Ray switched the genre, but it works for both genre's. He is as Pete Townshend said the should be poet laurette of Great Britain after all.

I totally dig No Return, one of those classic Ray lyrics. So much loss and yearning in his singing that it just strikes a huge chord with me.

Harry Rag, come on catchiness meets cigarettes....music hall beauty.

Tin Soldier Man, I've got Sand in my shoes every time I think of Tin Soldier Man....but is there a catchier song in rock and roll? especially a catchier song that is actually so ridiculously awesome as Tin Soldier Man? I don't think so, most of the time uber catchy songs become ridiculously annoying after a few months....Tin Soldier Man just gets better and better as the years go by, and I've had this album I think for 11 years now....not that long for those alive when it came out, but I wasn't....that's my entire adult life....

Dave's Love me til the Sun Shines, is another "Come On Now" perfect proto Punk tune (Dave's bonus tracks on the bonus disc--which make a ridiculously classic album even that much better as the bonus tracks are all perfect) are great too, Suzannah's still alive is as good as anything he wrote (And I'd take that over Funny Face, but I love Funny Face too). Dave was on a major tear here, and wouldn't touch it again sadly (he was good afterwards don't get me wrong, but this was easily his peak). I'm glad he didn't go solo then though, but am shocked he didn't because he hit the big time as a songwriter here with these 4 tunes.

I can't get over the overtly 60s keyboard riff of Situation Vacant. The Lyrics too are another married life to a T kind of thing (I love my inlaws, but I can relate)...

Lazy Old Sun, is Ray's best ever psychedellic track--a style he didn't utilize much despite arguably being one of the founding fathers (I know Paul McCartney was listening as you can hear Ray's influence all over Paul's Revolver-Mystery Tour output)....

Afternoon Tea, another ridiculously catchy tune mixed with classic lyrics....

Ray's singing on End of the Season is another one of those tunes that he makes classic and that none or a very select few could ever cover to get the same effect.....

And then so much has been said about Waterloo Sunset, but sadly in the US, not enough still has been said as it's not known enough to be the classic it is.

Unfortunately for me I had a cd eating car a few years ago and it scratched up a couple tracks on here, deeming to me the album almost unlistenable as if I know a cd is scratched it'll piss me off to a degree that I have trouble listening to the record....so I tried buying this album again shortly thereafter and paid the premium for the cd with the add'l bonus tracks, but amazon couldn't find the inventory so they had to send me just the main album....the main album is still my 3rd all time favorite record, and has had moments at #1 and #2 throughout time too, but with the bonus tracks it's even better, yet oddly isn't better--can't get much higher, if you get it. Still worht tracking down in whatever form you can track down, but if you see it with bonus tracks, they're more than worth the extra few bucks.

This album is pure genius through and through...unlike my current all time fave record (THe White Album) I wouldn't change a second on this one....I just like the White Album with it's flaws a bit more I guess right at the moment...

Buy immediately. Sorry that the Kinks have become almost trendy to like the past 4-5 years, they are worth the trendiness though, a GREAT band.

In the year of our love 1967, the psychedelic movement was in full swing. The Kinks had, with their skin-flaying early garage sound, been associated with the mods, which couldn't be further from their true faces. So when the psychedelic took over in this year of Pepper, it was time for Something Else. Decked in the least psych colour imaginable, London sky/Thames water gray, this was the Davies brothers' response to those damned hippie sheep.

Elements of the psych sound are here, generally coupled with a decidedly mundane or downbeat lyrical content. Jangling harpsichord on "Two Sisters" backs a tale of dishwashing and envy. The flowering ecstasy of opener "David Watts" is also centred on envy of a sort but, in early shades of "Lola" more as a Lindsay Anderson-esque schoolboy crush on a dashing older boy. The tape-warped and very druggy "Lazy Old Sun" is about the closest they get to a pure psychedelic song. The Kinks were sly satirists, and their small concessions to the psych sound were contrasted with the very Englishness of their ideas and interests. "Harry Rag" would appear to be a marijuana song, but its reality-escaping characters have none of the cheer of those enjoying their "Afternoon Tea" a few songs later.

Something Else feels rather like a dry run for the very English pastoral escape they would go on in Arthur and Village Green, a last look at glum, gray modern post-Empire London, doomed and misguided youth movements aside. The best bits are songs like "Situation Vacant" about a man quitting his job and ruining his life looking for a higher-profile position at his mother-in-law's behest, and of course, that most British of all their songs, "Waterloo Sunset" as the ideal closing song.

This edition adds a healthy eight extra songs, mostly singles and b-sides from approximately the same time, including the excellent "Susannah's Still Alive" and classic b-sides "Act Nice and Gentle" and "Polly" - great stuff all, that really adds to the overall effect of the album.

1001 Albums to Listen To: album 97
The Kinks' least successful record by sales at the time, this album sees Ray and co. embarking on a more quaint expedition into British life via song. Musically this album utilizes elements of Bossa Nova (Which was at it's height in the 60's), chamber pop, and most prominently baroque music. Another new experiment on this record was having Dave Davies try his hand at songwriting and performing as if he was a solo songwriter.

The album begins with the quite homoerotic song David Watts. A tale about a jealous schoolboy who is enamored with a fellow student who is just perfect in every way, This tale was inspired by Ray's own experience in school, and the character's namesake was a real person who was actually gay, and had a crush on Dave. The song is a rousing number, and quite catchy. The reason I like it is because of the mundanity of the lyrics. The homoerotic nature of the story isn't portrayed as anything weird or detestable and I commend it for that.

Dave Davies was having a rough go with the touring life, and the push to make him a solo artist helped him whip up the surreal and exhausted Death of A Clown. One of the best songs here, compares life as performers to being a worn out circus act, playing for peanuts and becoming jaded and disillusioned at the show-biz world. It's wonderfully performed song for sure.

Two Sisters is allegory for the two brothers Ray and Dave. A song played on harpsichord, is about one sister being wedlocked and the other living life a bit more freely. Jealousy is expressed in the lyrics, but when Priscilla looks at her children she becomes grateful for her life and no longer feels jealousy. It's a quaint tune but lyrically powerful and is exemplary at how good of a songwriter Ray Davies is.

Harry Rag is possibly the album's catchiest song in my opinion. A silly song about the dependance on cigarettes, that hook in the chorus is just such an earworm but one I can relate too as I sell cigarettes during my job. An underrated song for sure.

The middle portion of this album goes by without a hitch mostly, the song's lyrics all pertain to various British things such as business conformity (Tin Soldier Man), taking tea (Afternoon Tea), and the upper echelons of society feeling down about their life (End of The Season).

The record's closer and possibly The Kink's best song ever written Waterloo Sunset is a gorgeous song. Ray Davies expressed that "it was like an extract from a diary nobody was allowed to read", meaning that this probably the most personal song he's ever penned.

It's a slightly melancholy, yet upbeat song about being content with doing nothing and being a shut-in, because the sunset that casts over Waterloo Bridge is enough to keep him satisfied. The brilliance of the lyrics is in how the Narrator is able to connect himself to the outside world via a couple he observes from his window. Terry and Julie (based on Ray and Dave's sister and her boyfriend) meet up at Waterloo station to get away, but come back to watch the sunset, from a similar vantage point of our narrator. The melodies, the composition, it all comes together wonderfully to create a truly beautiful song filled with many similar, but ultimately different emotions.

So all in all, I enjoyed this record for what it is, and I feel it has much to offer in the simplicity of life presented in the lyrics. It all feels so close to home, but clever in presentation that it is all fascinating anyway. The music is classic Baroque pop through and through, just ever so adjacent to rock music that it still has it's 60's charm to it. If anything though, to me that's where the record falters ever so slightly. Apart from the hit singles and highlights, the record suffers from a couple mixing decisions that I felt were unnecessary. The vocals are very up front in the mix that they clip sometimes, and the vocal channels also drown out the singing sometimes. The bass is near non-existent. It can be kind of messy, but when the record hits it absolutely knocks it out of the park.

I can see how this record might be a bit overrated, especially because of Waterloo Sunset overshadowing the vast majority of the record's songs, but I feel the lyricism and attention to detail is enough to still give this album high marks, and repeated spins.

"As long as they gaze on Waterloo sunset
They are in paradise
Waterloo sunset's fine"~
7.9/10
