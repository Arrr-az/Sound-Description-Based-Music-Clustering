Half awake in a fake empire, you get mistaken for strangers by your own friends.

I don't know for certain that The National have taken their name to heart and set about becoming political, moving from their inward-looking self-contemplation into the larger world of political and social discourse. However, if it is not directly political as a statement, Boxer does use politically charged language in its accounts of relationships between people and reflects on a larger social reality.

I maintain that Alligator dealt with the details of living in the hangover malaise of the violent and insane 20th century, The American Century as post-modernists say. Boxer is a turn of the page, going forward into the energized thrust of the new millennium taking flight from the broken shackles of old. It is about current events, shaking off the dust of bad chemicals and an existential migraine, getting moving, starting the new day. In the passenger seat wondering where the hell we are going. In Alligator, we are in the black of the club with only our thoughts to keep counsel, the dim outlines of others feature only as a projections of ourselves. For Boxer, the house lights are brought up and looking around, there's no one we recognize here after all. As the music speeds up on the first song in growing agitation and awareness of surroundings, the 'I' of Alligator transitions to 'we' and 'you.' The calm of inner life fractures into the bustle and mania of outside.

In terms of relationships, Boxer is outside of self. No more concerns about personal identity, of self-definition, but about other people, the struggle to know others. Now that we know who we are, how do we relate to the people we encounter? Disoriented among strangers whose names you know but who you cannot connect with or truly understand. There is political allegory in there somewhere. 'This isn't working, you, my middlebrow fuck-up.'

Musically, instead of the circularity of Alligator, the album rides its rhythms (magnificent rhythms by the by) ever forward with complexity and crescendos of instrumentation, with songs after the first few slowing, capitulating to the night. It travels in a straight line, ducking obstructions and working through knots. It is a mature perspective, a concerted motion beyond self-involvement to wrestling with the cognition of real knowledge. 'Stay down champion stay down.'

So in this fake empire, a kingdom of the blind, we are lost among people we cannot pretend to understand, reading into others what we want to see, following ideals and idols. No one watches over us and why would they want to? Our leaders don't know where they're going, just following patterns of their own idols and narrating their own legacy, celebrating whatever broken icons are available for lack of something worthy. We try to understand others as if we are surgeons, alienating them from us with our images of what we suppose they are and distressed by their nonconformity to that sketch, then we fake it, try too hard to acquiesce and come off as reaching. So we hide within ourselves and break down, lashing out at our shattered expectations. Our facades give way to meaningless activity and nostalgia, we become inverted and palindromic with the outside world, grappling with it like an enemy and just maybe find a way to be happy and separate from the past.

Damn. 'They're going to send us to prison for jerks.'

Out of The National's albums, Boxer is the quiet one. The more intense or high adrenaline cuts are left to the other albums and even at its most energetic this still sounds drawn inwards, moving through the empty streets with the hazy mind of an insomniac. It's an album that sounds like it was recorded in a practice space at 4am when everyone but the band have gone to bed: much of the album was in fact recorded in makeshift studios in people's homes, which makes sense (and if you wanted to, you could imagine they were trying to be politely quiet so as not to disturb others). Where the lyrical personas on Alligator were growing people in urban jungles experiencing the first signs of unelegant adulthood creeping up behind them, on Boxer the ship has already sailed and its narrators are now lost in the vastness of suit-and-tie dinners, soulless offices and fading relationships, trying to hold onto some deeper meaning while pretending to be more grown-up than they are. It's an album of mid-age anxiety, emo for the sad dads in the audience, but every description (cheeky or otherwise) doesn't quite accurately reflect on the subtle devastation of sudden directionlessness in life that the lyrics convey. Nor does the music underline it or highlight it, instead it delicately orchestrates the backing for it. Boxer doesn't wield the same snappy hooks that Alligator did, it lets the notions linger and eventually take over naturally. It's in no hurry.

One of the frequent conversation pieces around The National (which I'm not sure is quite as widespread anymore) is that they are or were the epitome of a grower band: that no one ever really madly hit off with them at first sight but something compelled people to keep returning to their music and before they could realise, the album was speaking to them on a personal level. It wasn't even used as a dismissive remark, everyone just seemed to carry an anecdote around about how it tooks weeks or months and suddenly it was like they were living through every note like the albums had been there for them for years - and somehow it was a surprise for every newcomer. Boxer is where the bulk of that reputation stems from. The band haven't changed and the dynamic interplay that was all over Alligator is present and accounted for, but utilised differently. Devendorf's fluid and propulsive drumming still leads the songs - essays could be written on his drumming and I probably will in the course of all my National reviews - and both Dessners have now become multi-instrumentalists as the guitars are no longer the de facto lead on the songs, which leads to an increasingly varied range of elements across the songs. But what's not playing is just as important as the impressive instrumental chops everyone wields. Boxer lets its construction blocks breathe and the songs seem to be made out of a number of small and unconvoluted details that come together to form a deeply intricate musical backing. Not coincidentally, the songs from Boxer's songs have gone through the greatest transformations when the band perform them live, with extended outros, new or expanded instrumental sections and other changes which make the songs more obviously powerful in a way, lighting a new kind of fire in the songs' engines, and many of those elements feel so integral that it's like the songs were composed with them in mind - and given these alternative versions began to appear as early as the album's release year, they likely were. But on the album, everything is pared down to the absolute essentials - and it works and it's the right thing for this cohesive selection.

By placing equal emphasis on the quiet parts and small details as much as it does on grander gestures like, let's say, a triumphant horn-carried finale ("Fake Empire") or a massive heart-swelling chorus ("Mistaken for Strangers"), Boxer becomes a hypnotic experience. Part of its grower appeal is in how each listen you discover something new and those little revelations then reconfigure how you approach these songs going forward, turning the whole album into a chase to look for further rewards and sinking deeper into your old findings. It's an album to get lost in: to close your eyes with your headphones on, to soundtrack moments of stillness or times when you need to find 40-odd minutes of stillness to help make sense of the world. Boxer isn't all hush-hush and placid even though I may give that impression: "Squalor Victoria" is a rousing, fist-pumping call to arms, "Mistaken for Strangers" is full of vigour and is one of the band's greatest individual stand-outs, "Apartment Story" has a brightness and looseness that temporarily brushes off the album's post-midnight haze, "Brainy" is a tight and tumultuous post-punk cut that somehow sounds so at ease, and the melodic runs and swiveling choruses of "Ada" are vibrant and full of life, almost a classic pop song which has gone through The National's filter and become integrated with their world. But the same sonic principles carry throughout the album and including those songs, and these intervals where the band deliberately stand up for attention are utilised carefully, to blow new wind to the sails without taking away from the tight mood that Boxer constructs around itself.

All these changes, these shifts in gears, build up to a 12-song experience that I've pretentiously titled as one of my all-time favourite albums; there's a perfect score right there, next to the cover. You can break down why exactly that is by focusing on three particular aspects of the album - and the first and most obvious one is that it's packed full of great songs. It has a number of tracks that would comfortably fit into the band's top 15, and easily even into their top 10 greatest cuts. Out of those, three in particular are above everything else. "Fake Empire" is at the top of the hill - a simple, weary ode to whatever you want it to be, whether it's for the last drinks of the night, the conflicted feelings you have of the country you live in (very relatable in the 2020s UK, a true fake empire at this stage!) or the merciful suggestion to forget all of that for a brief second because every hummingbird heart needs to rest at some point. In either case it's such a powerfully gripping statement that it's the antithesis to any of the claims the album takes a while to grow on you, and in its soaring elegance it's probably my favourite song by the band. "Mistaken for Strangers" I've already hinted at being one of the band's most defining statements, a rock anthem that's a direct evolution from Alligator's centrepieces but drenched in atmosphere and quiet rage and sorrow: "another uninnocent, elegant fall into the unmagnificent lives of adults" may as well be the band's motto. "Slow Show" just a few songs later arguably pulls the biggest emotional punches of the entire record, its uncharacteristically direct and (tragically) relatable anxiety being lifted by a heartfelt appreciation of finding someone you can brush off those intrusive thoughts with. Its directly stadium-adjacent grand build-ups are not just wonderful in their own right, but finds the band foreseeing their future and preparing for it in advance - on the often unassuming Boxer, it's the big tearjerker torchlight song of the night.

Beyond those three, it's generally classics all the way down. The already-mentioned "Ada" and "Squalor Victoria" are easy runner-ups for the prize places, the gentle closing lullaby "Gospel" channels so beautifully and effortlessly the fragile peace that the album ultimately leans towards and leaves behind a downright cleansing feeling as it quietly ends the album, and "Green Gloves" and "Start a War" most accurately represent the album's approach to production and arrangement, patiently building their respective soundscapes until they fully dominate the air with their sudden grandeur. The piano intermission "Racing Like a Pro" and the propelling "Guest Room" are the underrated duo of the lot, appearing together to guide the album towards its end and often getting lost in the noise but they're strong compositions, where "Guest Room" has some of the most immediately effective choruses of the album and the stark power of "Racing Like a Pro" is particularly poignant. It's a cohesive, consistently engaging set of songs with a surprising amount of variety in tone in-between them despite the shared aesthetics.

Another key factor is that despite having such a heavy heart, it's by no means a deeply melancholy album. Or, well, it is, but it's found a way to channel that into a kind of comfort - that if you're feeling the same, here's a shoulder to lean onto. Berninger plays a heavy role here and his mumble is an incredible asset: it's a voice that can just as easily make a line hit deep in the nerve as it can strip away the edge from any such message and maybe even raise a wry smile while at it. His vocals receive even more of a central spot on Boxer than it has on previous albums and while that's partly attributable to the overall arrangement decisions made with the instrument, it does also feel like the band had properly realised at this stage the power that Berninger's charisma brought to the band and so gave him an increased spotlight to capitalise on that. The music is wonderful, the songs are great - but Berninger is what makes them sound so personal, even welcoming. The insecurities, conflicts and hard decisions - and the moments of incidental happiness in between - that flicker through the lyrics are told with the warmth of a friend who's here to keep you company. And so Boxer stops being a downer and becomes downright soothing. It's a little strange to use a term like "cosy" from an album that on the offset really doesn't tick the kinds of boxes you normally associate with that term, but there's a very intimate quality to Boxer and it's comforting to get lost in that space.

And as you may be able to infer from that alone, Boxer has become a stalwart companion over the years - that's the third aspect I referred to. I love Boxer so of course I have my grower anecdote as well: how I discovered the band through "Fake Empire", how I then spent literally the next several hours looping that song over and over again because something about it struck me so strongly, and how my first listens through the actual album left me disappointed because nothing in it had the majesty of "Fake Empire". We know how the story goes on from there, and during all those subsequent listens where I kept unlocking parts of the album and discovered new depths, it became a personal fortress of solitude to retreat into where the songs became close friends. Somewhere along the line its mixture of melancholy and warmth became something to hold onto and seek out whenever I've needed to take a breather from things for forty-odd minutes. Boxer has long since ceased to be just an album of great songs, it's a friendly refuge and a state of mind. It's also what - once it all finally clicked - made me fall in love with The National, so it's special in that way as well. There's no real "objective" way for me to describe why it's so great, but albums as magical and personally close as this are few and far between. If The National's growth across their first four albums has been a state of constant reconfiguration and starting over every time with the teachings of the past fresh in mind, then Boxer is where they figured out their unique essence and how to go about building something that is uniquely, distinctively them - and it scored them a masterpiece.

Considering that the two albums - yes, Cherry Tree counts as an album as far as I'm concerned and you should follow suit, get with the new world order people - The National released prior to Boxer have what I see as a conceptual bent to them it would follow that I'm on the lookout for similar throughlines here. After all, Boxer is by far the most unified album in the band's discography in terms of overall sound and feel; no real explosive moments la "Mr. November," or fully stripped back moments la "About Today," just a dozen lightly orchestrated pieces of post-punk/cabaret tinged alternative rock with measured vocals and far more involved drumming than was strictly necessary. Considering the unified lyrical concerns of the band's previous album it makes sense for the album that solidified The National's sound as it were would have a similarly unified lyrical outlook. So is this another installment of Brent Reads WAY Too Deeply Into the Albums He Loves and Ascribes Them Thematic Unity That May or May Not Have Ever Been Intended But Fuck It It's Happening Anyway? Yes and no...

See, the thing about Boxer is that it has a lot more to say than previous The National albums but it does so in a less narrative-adjacent way. There's not a real story uniting the songs here like I managed to read into Alligator but they are all of apiece with each other nonetheless. If you want the quick version, Boxer is about the unease one can feel as they drift between social circles. It's not one man's journey from one clique to another but it's a reflection on what it means to be a different person than you were five years ago and how the more you think about that disconnect the less you feel at home with anyone you considered a friend, either present or past. It's feels more like a series of vignettes from the perspective of the nouveau-riche questioning their place in high-ish society when they know in their head that they should not be there than one rich white dude's problems being explored for 45 minutes. I don't think it's all from one perspective (aside from Matt Berninger's, of course) and I think that's what makes Boxer stand out as maybe the most meaningful set of lyrics on any The National album; after all, if there are a dozen people who have similar but not necessarily overlapping issues within this millieu it carries more of a universal appeal than a single story from point A to point L.

'People questioning their place in society' seems like a pretty vague theme to build a concept album around, but I'dargue that that vagueness allows for more possible points of connection. Maybe you've never been in a situation like "Brainy" where a man being dragged up the social order by his partner starts to realize that he's as useful to her image as she is to his, but that's not the only stroy being told here so it's perfectly fine. Maybe you'll find more identification with "Guest Room" where people too concerned about being 'found out' try to approximate the behaviour of their new clique but it only brings them misery (I'll just say that as much as "Mistaken for Strangers" has the album's best imagery, and we'll get to it later, the image of people who 'throw money at each other and cry' in the room they don't need but have anyway because nouveau-riche has stuck with me ever since I first heard this album. Such a neat, tidy little picture that conveys so much in its simplicity) or with the people from "Fake Empire" going through the motions of a charmed life becoming slightly more aware of how precarious it all is but ignoring it in favour of retaining the comforts afforded to them. Maybe you don't even need to look at things through this whole nouveau-riche angle for these conflicts to make sense; at the end of the day Berninger's lyrics are just specific enough to come across as "real" but general enough that anyone could theoretically latch onto them. I'd argue that the only person who pulls this off better than Berninger is Taylor Goddamn Swift, so he's in good company there (also one of the reasons I wasn't shocked to see her collaborating with The National on her last couple of albums, they're far closer in approach than it might appear on a surface level.)

Perhaps the most universal moments on the album are the songs that center around losing your connection to previous acquaintances and trying to understand how to deal with the feelings that brings out. "Mistaken for Strangers" is the song that initially sold me on The National and it still hits just as hard now, fourteen years later, as maybe the most effective picture of the banal horror of being an adult I've ever heard. The imagery of 'a feathery woman carry[ing] a blindfolded man through the trees' contrasted with the notion of angels not wanting to watch 'another un-innocent elegant fall into the un-magnificent life of adults' (I'm in this picture and I don't like it) is such a vividly horrifying picture, recasting the beings that are often seen as protectors as the vectors responsible for dragging you forward in life without your knowing where you're headed. All of that in a song whose main hook is just about not being recognized by your friends on the street, a banal occurence that nonetheless is the exact kind of thing that might strike a little bit of fear into you as it happened. 'I am not who they think I am, what does that say about who I am and who they think I am?' As someone prone to over-intellectualizing mundane things that happen to me and reading far too much into quotidian interactions (as if you couldn't tell my deal by now) "Mistaken for STrangers" is one of those songs that just cuts right to the heart of my own anxieties and offers no comfort at all but at least offers some level of understanding. The other track in this vein is "Green Gloves," which deals with this sort of lost connection by imagining a scenraio where you can slip into these people's lives to gain a better understanding of why thay've drifted away from you. It does come off as a bit intrusive and almost stalkery from certain angles but it always read to me as a rumination on how curious one might be when faced with a new acquaintance and not being able to figure out their whole thing. It speaks to the fascination that we have with the unknowable and it's probably the track on Boxer that I've warmed to the most between its release and now, my initial coolness towards probably stemming from its less-than-dynamic instrumental more than anything else, but it adds to the overall themes I've chosen to read into the album really nicely.

Speaking to the instrumentals though, what a fucking tour de force this is for Bryan Devendorf. It's clich at this point to say it, but any band that counts a drummer as subtly inventive as Devendorf in their ranks couldn't make a boring-sounding song if they tried. He's always doing the work to spice up the more measured and static moments of Boxer with these shifting, intricate drum patterns and flourishes that never distract from the core of a given song but always catch your ear just as it starts maybe sliding past you. I said up top that this is maybe the most unified-sounding album in The National's catalogue up to this point and part of that comes from Devendorf's insistence on doing just that little bit more than he needs to to make a good track great and a great track untouchable. The rest of the band isn't that far behind him all things considered - witness the constant 6-against-4 polyrhythm of "Fake Empire" and hte double-picked arpeggios that serve as "Racing Like a Pro"'s main driving force, plus all the orchestral elements that the Dessner brothers layer into the album - but Devendorf's drumming will always be the thing that takes the band into that next tier as compared to their immeditate peers and that even holds if you're less concerned with the lyrics than I am at this point.

I remember first hearing this album back in 2007 after ignoring The National for a couple of years for spurious reasons and immediately being enthralled by the overall vibe of the album. In 2007 I was going into my third year of university, a fuckin' baby all things considered, who hadn't really lived a life that allowed me to connect with the ideas at play here, but I very much dug it on a sonic level and it was a mainstay in my stereo through the rest of my college days. In 2021 I'm more than able to find some added resonance to the scenarios presented here. I've become a tranger to former friends, I've questioned my belonging in certain situation and I've completely fucked myself over by overthinking how to act in unfamiliar situations. I'm not some nouveau-rihe douchebag who's scared his new high class friends will find out that he's a phony, but I can see a reflection of my issues in these littel vignettes. They're so elegantly drawn and no universal in their appeal that even when the specifics don't match up with my own experience they at least hold up a mirror to them and give me an opportunity to find some degree of comfort in the fact that I'm not hte only one who's dealt with these things. The image that people will paint of The National these days is of a cold, boring and most damningly bourgeois band who can't be related to, but here I am in the year of our lord 2021 finding some degree of comfort in their work. Maybe some of you out there who've been sold a particular bill of goods about them might be curious to look a bit deeper into them and find the beating, anxious heart underneath the suits and ties and remoteness. And even if you don't you'll at least hear some of hte best drumming in alternative rock history, so that's something I guess.

Willy Wonka on a stick.. I'm starting to wonder if there's a sort of magnetism taking place between me and the bands that I like, yet do not know. I say this because The National has alluded any gracing of my collection for the past few years of indie rock inception, and I'm having a very hard time explaining it. When I think about just how charismatic the contents of Boxer are, even if I've not heard any of its songs all day, I am pervaded by a warm giddiness that does not find its third law until a few scrobbles have been met. New York City once again rears her cold, dampened head, only this time her reach has pulled our Cincinnati friends out of their Midwest abodes and into the torrential shitstorm that is her cold, desolate state. To wonder of the spirits that Brooklyn has contaminated, and to try and relate from all the way down in north Florida, is a challenge I find myself excited to face.

The confidence in depression that the National conveys is their highest peak of interest to me. Considering the unoriginality of an indie rock band riddled with post-punk influence, Boxer showcases some rather fine attributes and melodies that ultimately come to a refreshing head. Marquee stealer Matt Berninger does that which helps distinguish his band to the fullest extent: he has a recognizable vocal (and a terrific one at that). Imagine if Ian Curtis had survived to do a rendition of "I'm On Fire", but he had just listened to sixteen Johnny Cash records before emerging from backstage. This is what Matt generally sounds like, and that alcoholic begrudge of a voice is what helps captivate the most basic essence of the National's sound: the emotion. The heart-rotating, soul-shattering depression that the words and vibes of these songs contain... there's really nothing out there like it. Interpol, Editors, Franz Ferdinand, what have you... they're all trying their damned hardest to do what this band succeeds in. It's a shame that victory spells such an estranged lethargy.

The people sang about over the course of this record, some named and others left to the follies of the human imagination, have a striking sense of realism in the way they are portrayed. It could just be that Matt is so choked up and lambasted from the sheer thought of these people that I come to bitter contempt at their very idea. I wonder of what sort of wretched subhuman could bring their friends to not recognize them under the silvery, silvery Citibank lights. I brood in earnest over the caretaker who ejects his patrons from the guest room. I want to grab our narrator and shake him until he confesses the mistake that he's made in his life today. It's a feeling I haven't experienced since childhood, and I'd only ever get it when watching TV, or a movie; I'd have trouble distinguishing the piece of dramatic fiction in front of me, and I'd begin to become agitated and concerned for the people before me. Boxer does a pretty phenomenal job of rekindling that feeling.

Did I mention that the drumming is also spectacular?

I'd probably have been able to love and cherish Boxer as early as two years ago, when the musical extremities of my past were dying down and the demand for gratification was first starting to dwindle. Now, I find myself unregretting the hold-off that the National has ended up taking. I truly believe that this was an album that needed to come when it did, and perhaps I'll appreciate it to an extent I could have never possibly achieved during any other time period. I go now, pondering this fact complacently. This really stands tall against the pale.

At times, I think that this is a landmark piece of musical satire, with the lyrics pointing out what modern society has become, that is, a society plagued with political grandstanding and ideas of empire building, but crumbling from within with the masses lacking the self-awareness to see what has gone wrong.

An album serving as entertainment but also serving as a political statement, this becomes clear from the start with the fantastic "Fake Empire." Beginning with a mellow piano intro but finished with a horn laden rock flourish, the lyrics are a none too veiled attack on the pseudo-imperialistic notions that many politicians seemed to cling to in this modern age. Subtle it is not. "Mistaken for Strangers" seems to be an indictment of what modern man has become, superficial, narcissitic and self-aggrandizing. I, for one, find way too many parallels in that song to my own life. I have always enjoyed "Green Gloves," with the great line "Falling out of touch with all my, Friends are somewhere getting wasted." I always feel nostalgic listening to it, yearning to go back to the not too distant younger days of getting wasted with friends. It was such a better time then. As it says to the end though, "Now I hardly know them." "Apartment Story" is another fantastic track, along the same vein of "Mistaken for Strangers." The foolishness and capriciousness of public opinion and the vanity and stupidity of people are the subject here. Really this song can sum up all the lyrical themes: "We'll stay inside 'till somebody finds us, do whatever the TV tells us, stay inside our rosy-minded fuzz for days...So worry not. All things are well. We'll be alright, we have our looks and perfume"

The lyrics in this album paint a harsh yet seemingly truthful reality, saliently stated, without a misplaced note in the background. The drumming is particularly good and I really like the vocalist. It is catchy at times, but always with a foreboding undercurrent. Another reviewer used the term existential emptiness, and that is exactly the feeling I get after listening. In almost every song you can find something discussed that is played out in the daily news or observed firsthand just by turning on the TV. As someone who has zero optimism for the generation that I am lumped in with or in the US as any more than a decaying "fake empire" in the coming decades, I could see this easily being the soundtrack to tougher times ahead. At least people wouldn't have to look too far to see some of the reasons why.

"Oh, you wouldn't want an angel watching over, surprise, surprise, they wouldn't wanna watch."
