A suitable description of this album is almost beyond the capability of mere words. One of the myriad things that makes Zeppelin so great is that the subject matter of their songs is generally about as far from the mundane as is empirically possible, and Led Zeppelin has the ability to completely capture your imagination in a way that few other bands are capable of. Houses Of The Holy is Led Zeppelin's greatest manifestation of that ability. The album starts with The Song Remains The Same, which takes You on a journey across the globe, in space and time, but all in your head, and seen/heard through the medium of John Bonham's crushing Drums, Jimmy Page's soaring Guitars, and Robert Plant's sirenesque Vocals. No Quarter drags you, willingly or unwillingly, along with some brutal viking death squad on an unholy mission through the fog enshrouded mystic ancient night to an unspecified but terrible destiny. Dancing Days leaves you in awe of it's magisterial mystique. Over The Hills And Far Away may be the most perfect example of what a Zep song should be. And just when You think it's all over, The Ocean simply bludgeons You into utter submission with it's entire hallelujah-chorus exploding Krakatoa of Guitar riffs cascading upwards to infinity. Many people consider Zoso to be Led Zeppelin's masterpiece. I say Houses Of The Holy is the one, Zep's monument to the ages.

A wise old fansite publisher noted that Led Zeppelin's first few albums show the band paying tribute to their idols, and their last few show them trying to glom onto the musical fashions of the present. Seems about right.

But before we get any further: a huzzah for John Paul Jones! And a huzzah for John Bonham's microphones! It's Jones' synth beds that lends these tracks any kind of pathos... without him, "The Rain Song" would be Robert Plant humping his bedroom wall, murmuring about "mystery of the quotient". On "No Quarter," his swampy licks (reminiscent of the Sabs' "Planet Caravan") merge well with Page's fuzz guitar. Even Plant gets into it, slurring his vowels and coming off, like, bravely empty. This is the kind of atmosphere the band ought to have summoned more often, as rehabbed as their rep is. Oh, and his palpable sideways fills liven up "Dancing Days" and "The Crunge".

Not that "Crunge" is a total drag. It does, after all, have the best moment on the record that isn't Plant numbly calling down Thor*: it's the very end, when Plant mutters "which way to the bridge?/where's that confounding bridge?" - he sounds like proto-Axl. And it's funny! Lord knows Zeppa could've been more like Zappa. But no, they stuck to sluggin' riffs with abysmal lyrics/vocals. "The Song Remains the Same" could have been an all-time galloper (let's dream stoopid: could've merged Krautrock and Tom Petty!) with some poignant chord changes. But Plant jumps in nads-first and slows everything down. "Over the Hills and Far Away" is a C-grade "Ramble On" rewrite with the usual "I'm-jes-strollin'-down-thee-road-looking-for-some-legs" sunshine hard rock crap. The whole band's at fault for the plodding, sleepwalking cod-reggae "D'yer Mak'er" - I used to think Bonham's drums made the song tolerable, but it ain't even that: the way his drums are recorded bring the song to a needle-mark just under bearable. If they really gave a damn about current styles, couldn't the Zep have cracked a cookie jar for a horn section? Or some female backing singers? Uf.

Thankfully, "The Ocean" crushes. It's got a touch of the psych-blues in the "na-na" part, there's a nice recurring background "ooooo" - oh, and the riffage is fucking thick, so thick Bobby does the right thing and makes his words unintelligable. The rock'n'roll breakdown in the last minute is also inspired - honestly, it's the best style these guys ever mimicked.


*For the record, th' Otis Redding references would place higher if there'd been more than two.

Full disclosure right up top: Houses of the Holy was the first non-compilation non-classical album I knew I truly loved. After 15 or 16 years of listening exclusively to classical music - including a fair bit of bad classical music - and just starting to dip my toes into the 20th century when I started hearing mom wax nostalgic about "Dancing Days" a lot, I borrowed her CD, and...well...yeah. This stuff made those groggy afternoon bus rides and long summer evening walks feel like poetry. I mean, when I first heard that electric guitar rip into "Over the Hills and Far Away", it was like, 'Okay, I've gotta get my shit together and do something.' High( )schoolers, take note: this album is fuckin' perfect for the last day before summer break. And to all you people who've sat on your roofs and listened to the last 25 seconds of "The Rain Song" over and over and over again: marry me.

How's the album objectively? Flawed. But marvelous.

Flawed because:

a) "The Rain Song", while gorgeous and generally terrific, also shows the band's limitations the chord changes just kinda amble around without really doing much (i.e. not going anywhere out-of-the-ordinary), and the climax isn't handled/paced as well as it could've been; the strategy was apparently just to let Bonham start banging around and swell everything else up around him.

b) The clumsy, quite undanceable "D'yer Mak'er" tries to be an enjoyably corny reggae pastiche but just ends up being corny. Okay, it's mildly enjoyable if you just focus on the guitar. But even then, the song still gives you plenty of time to realize that Bonham was a one-tricker albeit with a great trick.

c) The verses of "Dancing Days" don't really follow through on the invigoration of the song's main (great) guitar hook or for that matter, the lyrics.

d) "The Crunge" stinks. While you might be able to pull off some sort of half-hearted shuffle to "D'yer Mak'er", you can't do jack shit with "The Crunge", because "The Crunge" doesn't do jack shit. I don't care how strange the rhythm is for Zeppelin, or how many time signatures they go into the song's a James Brown imitation with absolutely no groove whatsoever. Which...kinda defeats the point, no? And the clueless synth and keyboard tones, which sound like they got lost on their way to a totally different song, just add insult to injury.

But the record's generally superb. On a minor note of goodness, Plant's lyrics alternate between his most easily-ignorable - thankfully - or his warmest. From beginning "The Song Remains the Same" with the line 'I had a dream...' (as the guitars arpeggiate like clear, clean rippling water) to 'dancing days are here again, as the summer evenings grow' to the part where he sings '...you really oughta know...oh...oh...oh...oh...really oughta know...' as "Over the Hills and Far Away" seeps into its soft, mellow harpsichord outro (a seepage that's probably my favorite Led Zeppelin 'moment'), the guy never sounded friendlier than he did on Houses of the Holy he's like a benevolent guide through sunny days.

It helps that the music itself is so warm. This is the more major achievement of the album: the production is fabulous. I don't think I've ever heard electric guitar tones that sound quite like what Page was doing here; it's a sound that's at once hard and crystal-clear, rocking yet chiming. Interestingly enough, I credit the influence of Yes: an airy, buoyant sound that clearly imitates the twangy bounce of Steve Howe, and yet also sounds inimitably pure. Just listen to those chords in "The Song Remains the Same" and you'll hear how the band (particularly Page) was at this point deviating from their earlier prog-influenced sound from the fourth album a sound that recalled the darker, more brooding prog of bands like King Crimson. Listen to Page's rolling blues lines as "The Song Remains the Same" enters its last minute (around the 4:30 mark) and you'll hear just how much happier they are here. And that's not even to mention how good everything else sounds, too the cello and mellotron fills in "The Rain Song" and the keyboards (and guitar licks) in "No Quarter" are tones of uncommon beauty. This is unquestionably Zeppelin's prettiest album.

Not only that, but the songs are pretty kick-ass. I've already mentioned the adventurous glory of "The Song Remains the Same" and the slow-building beauty of "The Rain Song" (flaws and all), but don't overlook the breezy fills of the (almost Celtic-sounding) acoustic guitar in "Over the Hills", and the way the electric solo turns the song into a bluesy barn dance before climbing up perfectly to lead into the final verse. (I hope the upcoming CD remasters re-establish the aforementioned crystal-clear hardness of the electric guitar's entrance in that song.) As for "No Quarter", the token 'dark' track on the album, it's terrific, a Doors-y trudge (in the best sense of both 'Doors' and 'trudge') with the keyboards dripping like stalactites in a cave before deep, grotty guitar lines snake out as you march on through the dim torchlight. I've noticed some people say that "No Quarter" doesn't fit in the context of the rest of the album, but I disagree I'd say it's sequenced just right. Coming as the second-last track, just when another blast of summer might've seemed overbearing, the track slows you down and drags you into the depths of a dark and wintry room (and/or cave) for seven minutes, whereupon "The Ocean" brings you back out once more into the glorious sunshine. And glorious sunshine it is the album's final minute is so fucking joyful, so pitch-perfect a goodbye (listen to those vocals in the left channel!), that I've always taken it as an unofficial farewell to - ahem - rock and roll itself.

Houses of the Holy is my favorite Zeppelin album. Beyond that, in its best moments it really does feel like you're going, well, over the hills and far away. Into California sunlight and sweet Calcutta rain. Across the ocean. Along the open road. Have a great weekend, guys.

A.

How do you follow up an album as legendary as Zeppelin IV? Well, you release an album that matches it in quality song-for-song. Houses of the Holy, much like the album before it, excels in it's ability to have great songs that captivate the listener in different ways but it also takes a very different approach, all but abandoning the classic styles of hard rock at the time.

Instead, this album is almost as though the band's hippie side is coming into view, with happy go lucky songs like "The Song Remains the Same" and "Dancing Days". Again, the band takes you on a journey from one very unique place to another, with "The Rain Song" like a light shower in a meadow in spring or "Over the Hills and Far Away" having you thinking of what life on the road would be like.

Truly, though, Led Zeppelin's most interesting three song combination comes with the final three tracks. "D'yer Mak'er" is a strange combination of reggae and hard rock before the album takes a turn for the unexpected with "No Quarter". A depressing, dark tune that still makes a lot of modern metal songs seem like a sunny day, this is my all time Led Zeppelin song and surely one that, on paper, doesn't fit with the rest of the tunes on this album. However, that is part of the magic of the song; it somehow manages to fit. Of course, Zeppelin get back to their hard rock style to close out the album with yet another memorable song with "The Ocean".

Whether or not this is my favourite Led Zeppelin album seems to be reliant on what day it is or what my mood is, but there is an argument that can be made, an a very good one, that IV and Houses of the Holy might be the best back to back album combination of all time.

It is hard to go along with the idea that a band who continue to sell millions of albums, decades after they split up, as being under appreciated, but that is kind of the case in regards to Led Zeppelin. Lennon/McCartney rightfully get praise for their consistent and eclectic songwriting, but rarely did Plant/Page. At least not as much as they should.
Houses of the Holy, Zeppelin's fifth (and best) album is a collection of diverse styles, tip-top musicianship and a strong argument for the reason why the 'album era' was rock music's strongest period.
It starts where Led Zeppelin IV leaves off, with a straight ahead rocker, "The Song Remains the Same", that works in spite of Robert Plant's sometime 'Chipmunk' vocals. What follows is one of Zeppelin's finer moments, "The Rain Song", a gentle and touching love song with gentle strumming from Jimmy Page that builds into a string filled crescendo that is truly beautiful.
Page was born to create guitar riffs, and Houses of the Holy has them in full. In fact nearly every song has a classic riff, but it's the way Zeppelin plays these riffs, so stylistically that makes the album so great. Not just straight ahead blues-rock either. There's reggae in "D'yer Maker" and some jazzy-funk in "The Crunge." Surprisingly, the only thing that seems to missing is the blues, and when you think about Zeppelin's first album, it's quite surprising. Like I said, the boys were eclectic.
Page and Plant cannot get all the credit though. John Paul Jones, (who plays like, a dozen instruments on Houses) really holds the fort down. His organ on the Tolkein-esque "No Quarter" make room for Plant's lyrics to sneak by under the corny radar.

Groups like this attract a lot of knuckleheads. It's just the way it is. You know, I hate that guy who yells "ZEPPELIN!" all the time too. But I do understand him. I have more respect for fans who are unashamed of their likes than those who are too cool to like what they should.
