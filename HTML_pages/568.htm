Best punk album ever. Despite The Clash, to whom I have a psychotic teenage emotional attachment, being my personal crown princes of punk for the long time, the Lost Years, when Inflammable Material was absent from my heart; despite really stylishly snobby punk purists insisting that real punk was only made between three-sixteen and a quarter to four on an overcast Thursday afternoon in 1977, thus categorising Inflammable Material as post-punk; and despite the only punk thing to claim under the circumstances being that Sandinista!'s triple-album pig-headedness is the best punk album ever, or, if someone out-punks you and claims that before you can, that something secretly iconoclastic by Abba is, actually, Inflammable Material is the best punk album ever.

Existing a step removed from Jake Burns's material experience of occupation and the paramilitarisation of civil life, it's difficult not to feel anything other than juvenile exhilaration at the righteous fury of his response to it. Special punk 'over-understatement' abounds -- "They take away our freedom / in the name of liberty / why don't they all just clear off?" ... "I won't be a soldier / I won't take no orders from no-one / Stuff their fucking armies / killing isn't my idea of fun" -- and I genuinely believe the working class will unite around its interest and smash the state in a way that somehow neither subsumes emancipation in violence nor leaves me grubbing around the base ideological filth of 'creative destruction'. Then I open my eyes and see doe-eyed newly-weds Paisley and McGuinness grinning coyly as the British Army finally fucks off to make way for the neoliberal solution to Northern Ireland's particular wonderland of 'peace' walls and economic irrelevance.

I can't lay claim to any of that contemporaneous unheard-of-punk-noise-shifting-my-music-paradigm stuff, but holy shit it sounds good.

What we need is / an alternative Ulster!

From a society at its lowest ebb, one brought low by institutionalized bigotry, a trigger-happy military presence, the impetuous embrace of guerrilla tactics and the endless fuel of religious rhetoric, came precious few voices of reason with both the will and the strength to be heard.

Instead of Gandhi, we got Adams. Instead of de Klerk, Thatcher. Instead of M.L.K, we got I.R.K. Paisley. The inheritors of the high moral ground, those who could have continued to legitimately cry injustice into the world's camera lenses as they did at the Derry civil rights protest, declined the opportunity to humiliate the British into action. They took the dirty war underground, out of sight and out of mind. The troubles. What a fucking thing to call a war, as though it was a daughter's pre-marital pregnancy or a rash in an intimate area. An embarrassing little skirmish best not discussed in public.

Not in context, anyway. After the daily roll-call of mortar bombs, shattered knee-caps and suspect devices, one could enjoy the Other News. Football scores, fatal car crashes, cats stuck up trees, the usual local news stuff. There would never be a bridge between the two, though - the war and the people. No mention of the cumulative impact of a couple of murders a week for thirty years on the minds of those remaining. No inkling that the people in the second half of the news bulletins were victims, every one, of the events of the first. Certainly no talk about the fact that the economy had slipped into a coma since our heroes on all sides had begun frightening off potential investors.

Nobody talked about it - apart from the actors voicing Gerry Adams's list of grievances, the ones rendered so comprehensively moot since he and others decided to start exploding innocent people. Nothing from unionist politicians, happy to conceal behind outrage their long, personal histories in a de facto apartheid administration. Nothing - absolutely nothing - and STILL nothing from their loyalist attack dogs. Nothing from the British, much more concerned with winning votes in a nation that long ago tired of the mentally defective cousin living in their attic.

Nothing either from those I'll reluctantly call our public figures. Those achieving national fame in their chosen field - Van Morrison, George Best (uh, I can't think of any more. Eamonn Holmes?) - stayed well below the radar on the matter of local politics. It was oddly absent from Morrison's art and Best, content to spew racism and xenophobia in his newspaper columns and after-dinner speeches ("Pele wasn't bad for a nigger"), was always struck conspicuously dumb when it came to the state of his hometown. The mutes are always well rewarded. Keep shtum and you'll have an airport named after you, even if all you ever managed was a couple of goals in those six weeks in 1968 you managed to stay sober. We were left with John Hume, a man of noble intent and action ("Northern Ireland's only statesman" - Robert Fisk), and one of desperately few politicians from this locale whom history will treat kindly, but whose oratory style ultimately became a byword for aural sedation.

This, my friends, was a job for rock'n'roll. This was the perfect field for some barely competent four-piece to come roaring out of the ghetto and scream everything that needed to be screamed. And what did we get? The Undertones. The studiously apolitical, eternally teenaged media darlings of the northwest delivered their twee ditties from some hermetically sealed Penny Lane in the middle of Derry, and were paraded as the local success story, our wee country's own mini-Beatles, the proof that You Too Can Make It. Another four blank public faces for NI, content not to mention the elephant in the room like all their moderately successful compatriots. Hot on their heels, God be praised, came Stiff Little Fingers.

Inflammable Material takes so few prisoners over its blazing 35 minutes that the Ulsterman's traditional pursuit of discreetly figuring out the background of everyone with whom he communicates becomes impossible. "Suspect device" is its first track and crown jewel, a storm of protest against the self-appointed community guardians who take away our freedom in the name of liberty. Jake Burns's warning shot as the chorus detonates underpins the whole album: Don't believe them, don't believe them. Don't be bitten twice. This is an instruction, repeated elsewhere on the album, by a young man living in Belfast to tell the sociopathic footmen of a paramilitary organization where to stick it. The people he walks among every day are their open or tacit supporters. For this to be the sole voice of reason in a country deserted or screwed over by everyone who should have been committed to its preservation is testament to both the infamy of the latter and the courage and intelligence of the band.

I could be a soldier
Go out there and fight to save this land
Be a people's soldier
Paramilitary gun in hand
I won't be no soldier
I won't take no orders from no-one
Stuff their fucking armies
Killing isn't my idea of fun

- "Wasted Life"

Elsewhere, "Alternative Ulster" is an anarchic vilification of the security forces, and a lament for the emptiness of life in a city economically hamstrung by strife, while "White noise" is a truly disturbing mockery of the No blacks/No dogs/No Irish mindset then pervasive in urban England. Outside of polemic, there is the ludicrous, across-the-barricades, black humour of "Barbed wire love" (featuring bad puns galore ...it was love at bomb site...you set my arm alight...blasted by your booby traps... and a Ramones-style doo-wop interlude), as well as the two staples of any punk debut: the reggae cover (Marley's "Johnny Was" relocated to Belfast and poignantly delivered) and the fabulously scathing takedown of a money-hungry record company. Burns insists in the reissue notes that the song "Rough Trade" is not about Rough Trade records (sample lyric: We were betrayed, betrayed, betrayed, betrayed, betrayed by Rough Trade lies. Betrayed by lies). I'll reserve judgement there. "Closed groove," the last track, is the only misfire - gimmicky, cute and failing to make a point - Burns admits regret at its inclusion on the original album.

Inflammable Material made the charts (the first record to do so on an independent label) on his 21st birthday, and 21 years to the day before mine. The idea that it could spark a youth-led cultural revolution like the hippies in the States or the punks in Britain was, however, strangled at birth. At that time, the IRA and UVF fought the battle for hearts and minds very early, and the delinquent, jobless teenagers of Belfast were fertile ground for indoctrination by clever men who could capitalize on their feelings of worthlessness, or spin them romantic versions of history, or simply tap into their latent psycho. Most of SLF's target audience were already way beyond salvaging, and engagement with other demographics was scuppered by the one thing the two sides of our cultural divide share: a spectacularly entrenched social conservatism. These young fellows with their f-words and impertinence could never get past the front gate with most people, and the limited exposure of their music to the mainstream meant that absurd allegations of 'glorifying violence' (congratulations again, John) went unchallenged in most quarters.

So where are we now? SLF are back on the circuit after a lengthy break-up. Their past glories remain off-limits for the likes of the BBC, who prefer to join in the beatification of "Teenage kicks" as some kind of watershed in music history. The country continues to slumber as the puppeteers enact their latest stop-gap policy. The current one goes under the name "wait until the most intransigent 100,000 people die off and then maybe have an election or something" in Westminster white papers. Local politicians have not sat in office for going on four years, yet they continue to pick up wages for running the place. People don't shoot each other quite so much, which I suppose is good, yet the same faces, greyer and balder, vomit the same rubbish on the news every night as they have done for generations. That's hard to take. Even in the US, the devil gets a new face every eight years (or four. Theoretically. One wonders what magnitude of fuck-up it now takes to get booted after four).

I enjoy very few albums as much as I enjoy Inflammable Material, the debut, and all around best album from Northern Irelands Stiff Little Fingers.

Stiff Little Fingers first and foremost are one hell of a great little punky rock n roll band. I always felt that this and the Clash's Sandinista album were what U2 have been trying to create their entire career, but have never really hit the mark.

While technically the album and band really, would fall into the new wave slop, or even post punk, maybe, but Inflammable Material is true blue first wave of punk to its core. Lyrically, aggressively, musically, sonically, vocally, and whatever other way you wanna cut a slice of this cake it will still taste like punk n roll my friends.

As I was sitting listening to this just now I could remember the very first time I listened to it and thought the scruffy vocals embalmed in those tasty melodies like on 'Barbed Wire Love' were the coolest thing ever, and still hit me the same exact way. Or even how down to the cover of the album adds to the record. I remember looking at the cover for the first time and saying "with a cover like that you KNOW this is going to be an incredible record." Obviously I was right.

I've recently acquired a number of albums which I already own in one format or another. A number of these are different issues complete with bonus tracks, bonus disks and, in some cases, a different core tracklisting. Rather than write whole new reviews from scratch my intention is to copy and paste my original into the new issue and add a few words about the extra material.

I've always considered it typically and quaintly British that sectarian divides, knee-cappings, masked men breaking down doors and shotgunning fathers in front of their children and the mass slaughter of innocent and guilty alike by random bombing can be termed "troubles". But, when Inflammable Material was first released, Northern Ireland had apparently been having a spot of trouble for years! Even in music, bands from the province always seemed to steer well clear of any mention of the violence and bloodshed as though it was just how life was and nothing could be done about it. So, when Stiff Little Fingers released Inflammable Material, there was something almost seditious about the flagrant, brutal, stark manner in which the bitter conflict was brought to the fore. The music and lyrics are explosive, angry and passionate and Jake Burns stinging, venomous vocal perfectly compliments the mood.

Tracks like "State Of Emergency", "Law And Order", "Alternative Ulster", "Suspect Device", "Wasted Life" and "White Noise" blast past on waves of hate that are difficult to stomach but hit the bullseye with unerring accuracy. "White Noise" is especially dangerous treading a razor thin line between racism and righteous indignation with the grace of a ballet dancer. Most will read the lyrics and dismiss the song as white trash supremacy not realising it's that short-sightedness the song is railing against.

Inflammable Material isn't all political - after all SLF were still teenagers right? So "Here We Are Nowhere" is about boredom. "Breakout" is about trying to find your own way in life. "Barbed Wire Love", which contains a great doo-wop break, covers trying to find love across the troubled divide. "Rough Trade" is about the disillusionment of getting a record deal.

This remastered reissue also includes the single version of "Suspect Device" whose language was toned down for the radio, "78 rpm" whose neat wordplay on revolution makes for a entertaining listen and an eloquent interview with Jake Burns retelling the story of the early days of the band up to and including the release of Inflammable Material which went on to become the first fully independently released album to break into the top twenty.

Inflammable Material remains a raw, powerful statement of intent with the political songs remaining the most memorable of all and, even though this inflammatory, vitriolic, ardent rant will not be to everyone's taste, everyone should listen to it - if only to try and ensure that the little spot of trouble that Northern Ireland suffered doesn't rear its ugly head again.

Some of the most abrasive, pissed off and hatred fueled punk rock to come out the original '77 explosion in the UK, except unlike the Sex Pistols, this is the real deal and exposes them for the panzies that they are. Every track is a classic with excellent riffs and Jake Burns acidic vocals, with excellent raging lyrics that still say something today. "White Noise" ironic racist lyrics "black wogs, you ain't no Brits" are way edgier than "God save the queen and her fascist regime". "Suspect Device", "Barbed Wire Love", "Law and Order" and "Alternative Ulster" are instantly accessible classics, although every track is equally a masterpiece. "Johnny Was" is arguably the greatest Bob Marley re-imaginings and one of the most epic punk tracks ever. This album is recommended highly whether you are a fan of the UK punk rock explosion, US pop punk like Green Day, or the much more extreme stuff like Minor Threat, Napalm Death and Discharge, I think you will be able to hear all of those styles on this album, for the actual music is way more influential than most of their rivals. It embodies the best of punk explosion, and everything that came after. Simply put, THE GREATEST PUNK ALBUM. EVER!

Known as Clash copyists due to the similarity of song choice in the middle of side two of both albums Inflammable Material and the Clash hosted lengthy cover versions of Reggae artists in their debut albums. Thats were the similarity ends, Johnny Was a song penned by Bob Marley was so much different to the Clash infused Reggae of Police and Thieves. Further Johnny Was was hardly in the style of Reggae at all.

I took my copy of Inflammable Material to a party, the vinyl was saturated with Cinzano Bianco though undamaged after cleaning the black vinyl when i got home. I remember this vocal debut to be a popular album in my final year at schoolin 1979. A corroboration of defiance of Civil Rights movements, "Provos" and Loyalist paramilitary groups such as the Red Hand Commando battling for Ulster and against free Derry. A time of remote control IRA detonations, secret English information gathers know as The Debt and people driven to murder at the front doors of working class streets.


Suspect Device.

A smashing introductive display of simple chords with aggressive sand paper vocals from Jake Burns. Words of despair against an occupying British Army, or more generally a thrashing soundscape against all violence in the island of Ireland. This song hits you in the face like a hurricane and sets the tempo for the rest of the album. "Were gonna blow up in their face" Jake shouts, a smell of revolution in the air with ordinary kids arming themselves to rid the spotty Irish youth of the shitty law of force from the British occupation.

State Of Emergency.

Life in the Irish provinces was crap to say the least. Catholics had no chance of work, same white skin pigment different religion are or were the display signs of confrontation between indigenous populations and the Protestant hordes. This song like the opener full of lyrics concerning teenage depression no solution because as the song lyric goes hate has made you blind.

Here We Are Nowhere.

And the anger continues in this fast paced 1 minute ditty. Friday usually the day to look forward too at the end of the working week. But the only thought in the minds of (SLF) is from here to where to have a good time. It is only there a nowhere of misery no Youth Clubs, no Boys Clubs, no Cinemas the only solice for the disaffected youth are the squallid surroundings of closed down local shopping centres.

Wasted Life.

A great slow opener to a nexus of massive in your face simple punk guitar chords. All there is to live for estranged teenagers is to be a martyr for an impossible cause in an ongoing political confusion, hostility and turmoil. Yet, violence has to be questioned what a waste of life, this song is driven with powerful guitar riffs and guitar solo that are a highlight of side one of the album. Jakes sorethroat vocals must have been painful after the recording at Spaceward Studios by the leafy streets of Cambridge.

No More Of That.

The only song to be delivered by the Rythmn Guitarist Henry Cluney. More of the same fodder in an undemocratic land that resembles more of a timearchy with the gun and the bullet being the decisive factor of settling the argument from the spoils of civil war. Training to fight for freedom or to fight for occupation whatever way the hopeless youth wants no more of that, it is as simple as that to say but difficult to implement with peace. All the youth are saying is give peace a chance.

Barbed Wire Love.

In all of the squalor of war and hate a little bit of love at the centre of conflict will relieve the stress for young love. Love in despair is the remedy in this rare love song. Probably their most poppier song as the sandpaper vocals are rested in most of barbed wire love. Vocal barber shop harmonies are prominent in the chorus of the song. A nice use of the drums especally the snare with the added resonance which compliments the structure of the recording. There is a nice clapping ingredient in this love song that adds to the catchyness of this harshless melody.

White Noise.

A tongue in cheek racist lyric that in 1979 SLF could get away with because the whole subject of race was thought of by white people as something to be joked about. White people were not disposessed to these racist insults and jibes so does that make it alright? I doubt that very much!. Race was embedded into the English psyche and its foulness permeated society especially through television. SLF were making an anti racist statement but looking at the lyrics they could be interpreted as pure racism. They used racism to make a joke about themselves calling the Irish as green wogs with faces not fitting into this white world. Even though the Irish skin is whiter than white life was cheap in Northern Ireland at the time.

Breakout.

The end song of many songs on side 1; 8 in all is a thought provoking discharge of dropping out of society. The troubles are too much for one to bear so what can one do? Powerful guitar punchy chord shapes emphasis from despair to where! This guy is leader if i can breakaway so can you! Nihilism is the word, if you can win you win if you loose you loose. He dont care if he is on the dole or not, no worries because life in the Irish province is really very worrying indeed.

Law and Order.

Great opening delivery to side 2 "HA". "Were doing nothing wrong we are only hanging around". No justice. The RUC are picking on innocent kids because the kids have nothing to do and the law have nothing to do but pick on their own citizens. Nice guitar solo breaks up the song well. The vocals are recorded low in the production which adds to the intensity of Inflammable Material. Rough justice and great lyrics " kicked him in the head and then laughed when he bled, spat in his face and pulled his hair call that justice just isnt fair". Tremendous writing by these pioneering Irish upstarts.

Rough Trade.

Another record that accuses SLF of being Clash copyists. The Clash wrote a song called Complete Control about the meddlings of a selfish record company. So did the Sex Pistols write a song about the anti heroics of EMI. So why cant SLF write a song about a record company, their record company ironically Rough Trade. Very fast punky beat as fresh as it is now than when the day it was recorded all of 30 years ago. Punk classic could and should have been a single maybe the record company were scared of this anti record company baiting. Strange thing at the time both factions need each other in recording market. Betrayal being the buzz word for an untruthful record company lying about false promises.

Johnny Was.

A nice deathknell drumbeat interpersed with guitar sets up this Bob Marley cover to punk perfection. This sad drama on a Belfast night was all about Johnny who was killed by a stray bullet from an aggressor. The beat was done on the old snare drum with mighty resonance. Also at the start is a nice piece of melodic guitar that is menacing to the ear. This attack on the system for the cause of the good Johnny is by far the longest song at over 8 minutes which has comparisons with the Clash but only in length and song order.

Alternative Ulster.

Classic guitar lead at the start, one of the best political songs of the punk scene period. Mandatory in anyones punk singles collection. If Ulster is the source of the problems in this troubled society, then, there must be some kind of solution needed to help both sides. This was to be heralded later in the 1990s, with the Ireland peace declaration aided and abetted at first with the republican party called the SDLP. Freedom at last, however, there are still some hardcore radical groups like Continuity IRA who still are at war with the British State. They want the sovereignty of all Irish counties in Ireland to be a commitee under control of the Republic of Ireland. Yet the power of the Good Friday agreement is still intact. Back to Alternative Ulster a classic thats all i can say covered by many punk bands since its inception.

Closed Groove.

A poor ending this song to a great debut album. I did like the drum beat again simple snare drum. The vocals are spoken in the format of an answering machine. The voice is similar to a robot speaking in fits and starts which is rather irritating. The voice machine talks about racism, amnesia, nostalgia and history. The end is a repeated lyric "scream whenver you hear this tone" with an unanswered ring of a telephone which eventually fades away.

I liked the rough production on this record. It works with the overall content of the music and lyrics. Most of the concepts are about war and nasty use of the law by a foreign invader who has no right to rule in the province according to disaffected youth. The aggressive sandpaper of Jake Burns frosty vocals are a perfect accompanyment to the despair of the situation the kids of Northern ireland are in at the end of a violent decade concerning the Irish "Troubles". Interestingly enough Jake was careful not to sing too much about sectrarianism in the province concentrating more on the awful situation the youth have to endure in a place were violence, unemployment, lack of investment and the like cause a never ending despair to the people that suffer most.
