With the dawn of MTV, many well-established bands adopted a distinctive "look" for the very first time. A growing reliance upon television audiences then convinced many of those artists to move in a poppier direction, softening the artistic impact of many once-adventurous headliners. Talking Heads exemplified this career arc as well as any band of the New Wave era. Despite beginning their career with a self-consciously arty sound, by the mid-80's they had (largely) ditched their youthful pretensions in favor of MTV-friendly pop rock. 1983's Speaking in Tongues was the album where Talking Heads made their first big step toward the mainstream- it's not nearly as poppy as Little Creatures, but it's a clear departure from the experimental disposition of Remain in Light. Sadly, it's also the first Talking Heads record that's less impressive than its predecessor. Still an audacious record that I'd recommend to any New Wave fan, but a descent from godlike status into music that is "merely excellent".

Speaking in Tongues was the first Talking Heads album to appear after the band's split with Brian Eno, as well as their first album to be written after a pair of successful side-projects (Tom Tom Club, Byrne's The Catherine Wheel). This predictably resulted in a record that was more eclectic and less "nuanced" than its immediate predecessors. In particular, it expands upon the burgeoning funk tendencies of Remain in Light while reigning in the bizarro art punk and the moodier, synth-heavy arrangements. "Synth funk" and "dance punk" are acceptable genre descriptors for the first time in the band's career, whereas "post-punk" is suddenly indefensible. For better or worse, Speaking in Tongues also ditches the world music component that made Remain in Light so unique. This is complemented by what is undoubtedly David Byrne's most conventional vocal performance up to this point. Byrne is still absurdly quirky, but he avoids stream-of-consciousness blurting and even throws in an unprecedented number of singalong choruses. In short, Speaking in Tongues follows the relatively accessible example set by "Once in the Lifetime".

Personally, I didn't obtain Speaking in Tongues until I was already intimately familiar with both Fear of Music and Remain in Light. I always enjoyed "Burning Down the House", but for whatever reason I assumed that the record was unnecessarily quirky pop rock. This made the album an extraordinarily pleasant surprise when I finally decided to take the plunge: despite my complaining above, Speaking in Tongues is far more challenging than your typical New Wave release, and it never succumbs to dated, mid-80's tropes. I've still never shaken my initial perception of the album as a case of "style-over-substance". Without Eno's lead, Byrne's often sounds like he's trying way too hard to be weird, and when the record does take a genuine chance ("Swamp", "Moon Rocks") it feels extremely forced. The end result is the first Talking Heads record where tracks rise or fall almost entirely upon the strength of their hooks. Fortunately, there are tons of vocal great hooks to be found here, and the grooves are nearly as toe-tapping as those on Remain in Light. Speaking in Tongues may be "disposable" relative to earlier Talking Heads releases, but it's nearly as enjoyable on a track-by-track basis.

Speaking in Tongues features just as many great tracks as Fear of Music and Remain in Light, even if the standouts here are less brilliant than those off its predecessors. The only two tracks that I'd classify as truly iconic are "Burning Down the House" and "This Must Be the Place". "Burning Down the House" is arguably the band's best-known track and epitomizes the album's mix of infectious hooks with funky art rock. "This Must Be the Place" is a stunning slow-burn and nicely anticipates Little Creatures without succumbing to any of that record's pop cliches. Among the remaining tracks, I'd rank "Making Flippy Floppy", "Girlfriend Is Better", and "Slippery People" as minor career highlights, although "Girlfriend Is Better" is goofier than it needs to be and I could've done without those soulful backing vocals on "Slippery People". That leaves a four-track stretch in the middle of the record that I'd categorize as merely good: the strangely ZZ Top-esque "Swamp" is the only thing here that feels like filler, but I'm shocked that Talking Heads could release such a middling stretch a mere three years after Remain in Light.

Although its back half is too underwhelming for the album to reach classic status, Speaking in Tongues still features enough highlights to earn an "excellent", 4.0-star rating. A clear step down from Fear of Music and Remain in Light, but still better than anything that the band would subsequently release. At least we got one more great album out of Talking Heads before their newfound accessibility would begin to negatively impact their music.

8.3/10

In my mind, the only blemish on "Speaking In Tongues" is that many fans may be more familiar with the charismatic live versions of these songs featured in Stop Making Sense. The studio versions of some tracks ("Girlfriend Is Better","Slippery People") initially feel a bit stale in comparison to the "live" concert film while others ("Swamp","Making Flippy Floppy") have their own unique life on the record.

The production of this album (again, only initially) struck me as a weak point. The reverb-heavy kick drum sound and distanced mix of the vocal tracks serve to date this record to the mid-80s but that stopped bothering me after several listens. The mix is far different on this album because the songs, accordingly, are a departure for the band and would not be as well served by their previous studio sounds. The drum intro to "I Get Wild/Wild Gravity" (which is a fucking brilliant song) wouldn't be as effective without the drum echo in flowing with the delayed guitar David Byrne is weaving over it.

The quartet of songs comprising "I Get Wild/Wild Gravity", "Swamp", "Moon Rocks" and "Pull Up The Roots" may be the best sequence I've ever heard on a record. A good friend described the Talking Heads to me as "the closest white people get to black music" and it seems fairly true on this record. The more authentic Brazilian percussion of Remain in Light is toned down here in order to meld more with a spacey, New Wave feel but the swing and white-boy funk hold true.

I will start a fight with anyone who doesn't think "Moon Rocks" is killer. It absolutely explodes with energy. How can a four-some of caucasian art school nerds get so funky?

Best Track: "Moon Rocks"

This album always reminds me of that camp I was in when I was eight or nine. It was an acting camp my parents enrolled me in to keep me from spending the summer pouring buckets of water onto my father's head from my treehouse because I thought it would be good for a laugh (I read a lot of Calvin and Hobbes back then... I mean, I still do, but it was a bit more of an influence on me than it should have been back then) and other such things, or at least that is what I have concluded, because I have never had much interest in acting. Anyway, I recall finding it rather boring and full of annoying people, so I (probably subconsciously) decided to shake things up a little when it came time to audition for the performance we were putting on, a rushed version of You're a Good Man, Charlie Brown. I was supposed to sing a song, so what I did was I put on my best David Byrne voice and belted out "Swamp" to the best of my ability. This did not strike me as even remotely odd, but I can imagine that the camp councilors came to the conclusion that they had an odd one on their hands somewhere around that point.

That pointless anecdote does not at all segue neatly into my review, except perhaps to the "Swamp kicks ass" portion of the show that I will get to in good time, but I thought it was funny and therefore worth sharing, so I'm keeping it in there. Anyway, this was the big shift. Story goes like this: the Heads got sick of making records that, while massively acclaimed, were not exactly tearing up the charts although they did make respectable showings. Coupled with their apparent struggles working with David Byrne, whose coke habit had rendered him rather insufferable at this point, they broke up after Remain in Light and focused on solo projects for a few years before getting back together, reemerging as a funky but robotic new wave act. From a commercial standpoint, it was a brilliant move - "Burnin' Down the House" cracked the top ten and gave us one of those classic music videos - but it's just not as consistent or as memorably weird as what came before it. It is hardly even weird anymore, in fact - the strangeness of previous albums only really comes through on "Swamp's" insane hook, which provided my younger self many hours of amusement. I also thought the police siren on "Highway Sixty-One Revisited" was hilarious. I still do, but that is more because I'm an idiot than anything else.

So instead of hitting us up with arty lyrics, dense arrangements, and all the polyrhythms in the world, the Heads take the hook option here, and they pull it off in style. As I mentioned above, "Burnin' Down the House" was a gigantic hit, and it's also a gigantic bundle of super-catchy fun. And it has a drum hook! Not many songs have drum hooks, so I appreciate them whenever I can get them. Even better is the closing track - the wonderful, adorably awkward love song "This Must Be the Place" is honestly my personal favorite Talking Heads song, by virtue of being a string of well-placed hooks coupled with an endearing vocal and charming lyrics. To me, it's one of those "why music exists, and why I prefer it to most things" sort of songs. Needless to say, nothing else here lives up to it - but then, it made my top hundred list, so there you have it, I guess.

The remainder of the album does not quite live up to the standards of its opening and closing tracks, unfortunately. I for the life of me cannot put a melody to "Making Flippy Floppy," "I Get Wild/Wild Gravity," "Moon Rocks," and "Pull Up the Roots." All four songs are quite well-done when taken as part of the album, and they certainly contribute to the "massive, nonstop '80s dance party" feel this album has (I'm listening to "Flippy Floppy" right now, and it's got some cool squiggly synths, if nothing else), but coming off the four albums it did, it is disappointing how forgettable four of these nine songs are. I mean, that's a little less than half of it! Dammit, guys, I just expect better!

And thankfully, I get better from the rest. As I already discussed, "Burning Down the House" and "This Must Be the Place" are both first-rate, the sorts of songs I would give my left arm to write, especially if I could express love as well as Byrne does on "This Must Be the Place," even though the only thing I would express it to would be a Coltrane album, dedicated bachelor that I am. Anyway, "Swamp" is pretty cool as well, and as far as '80s dance tracks go, you cannot possibly outdo "Girlfriend is Better" and "Slippery People," because both of them have AMAZING choruses. And while "Girlfriend" seems to have secured a place as a minor Talking Heads favorite, "Slippery People's" gospel chorus gets a far amount of hate. This is a shame, as it is awesome. I guess I just wish there were more awesome touches like that on the lesser tracks. But on the other hand, this is just as good as the earlier records when it's on its game. They have just changed how they go about making great music.

Track picks: "Burning Down the House," "Girlfriend is Better," "Swamp," "This Must Be the Place (Naive Melody)"

This album retains the kitschier elements of TH's earlier releases in a...uh, convenient way: by turning its' brand of art-funk into nonsense genremash disco. David Bryne's songs are a far fucking cry from his work on any of the first four albums. He literally just jammed some phrases together, a move inspired by various early 20th century art movements and such, blah blah blah, but still not as interesting.

The one song that seems more focused, and coincidentally recalls their earlier chitnzy sound the most, is the closer "This Must Be the Place". The main difference here is that this song is a love song...hearing Dave go "tweet tweet" is a trip, and it surprisingly works. It's a pretty dumb 80's song though, and "works" doesn't always mean "is a good song".

The only honest-to-goodness awesome musical moment here is Bernie Worrell's killer synths on "Girlfriend Is Better".
The rest has been pounded into all the most annoying parts of post-funk/disco, the Head's earlier sounds, and 80's pop rock.
Brian Eno, we miss you.

Byrne's nonsense and presence can be compelling sometimes. Clearly some of these songs are better than others.

"Burning Down the House", "I Get Wild", "Pull Up the Roots" and parts of "Girlfriend Is Better"...good stuff.
The rest? Blech. Move on.

Why Speaking in Tongues isn't held in the same esteem as earlier Talking Heads albums is beyond me, but I guess it's just one of those things I'll have to learn to accept. The album picks up right where Remain in Light left off, but has a more poppy feel to it, with quirky synthesizer being used more prominently than ever, and maybe this is what throws people off. It's their loss I guess. The other thing about the album is that all but three of these songs are on the Stop Making Sense film, and those versions are mostly better, but these are hardly weak in comparison and one or two songs actually work better here. The songs are slightly worse than ones on the last on the last few, but those were some of the best albums ever so, it's hardly a big drop in quality. Byrne's still being cryptic for the most part, but a few songs are more straightforward such as Girlfriend is Better, a catchy song with a great synth bass. Moon Rocks is the sole song to hearken back to the punk funk of the early days and it's tons of fun, but the true highlights are Burning Down the House, a big hit for the group, and This Must Be the Place, a nice love song, though still with the Heads's ever present quirks. Swamp sounded a bit messy live, but it works surprisingly well on album, and Making Flippy Floppy is another highlight with a great groove. Not everything here is outstanding, but all of it is good, and it's a highly enjoyable album. It is certainly a lot more commercial in sound than the one preceding it, but that's not the end of the world. Speaking in Tongues is still Talking Heads doing what they do best: having fun, and there's nothing wrong with that.

This is by no means a bad record. It's quite good, in fact. But imagine, if you will, the very idea of writing a follow-up record to Remain in Light. This band could have stopped after Remain in Light and thus forever remained in its perfect light. The page turned with Speaking in Tongues is the beginning of the band's weakest chapter, but a strong beginning none the less.

Eno is nowhere to be found on this record. Belew is nowhere to be found. Also absent, any sense of experimentation and revolutionary ideas. We we DO get, however, is Talking Heads becoming a huge pop entity. On the horizon is Jonathan Demme's Stop Making Sense movie, which cemented them firmly in the public eye and made David Byrne a pop icon. A Renaissance Man. Central to this cultural transformation is the brutally catchy, unavoidable blockbuster hit Burning Down the House. Ironically one of the band's weaker tracks, it doesn't even fare well in comparison to the rest of the album. Strange.

The rest of the album is all kinds of awesome. This is pop music filtered through Talking Heads take on music, arguably one of the weirdest bands of the time. Standout tracks like Girlfriend is Better, Swamp, Making Flippy Floppy make for an enjoyably funky and quirky listen, with Byrne at the heigh of his confidence as a vocalist, and new trendy effects and synths used with intelligence and restraint. Most importantly, despite inner struggles and tensions within the band at this point, they sound like theyre having a fucking blast.
