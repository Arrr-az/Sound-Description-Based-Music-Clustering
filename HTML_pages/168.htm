Dark_Elf Jan 15 2012

Eric Clapton was a "guitar god" of the 1960s, and nowhere is this more apparent than the album Fresh Cream. The particularly heavy sound punctuated by the eccentric drumming of under-appreciated Ginger Baker, the improvisational bass and howling blues harp of Jack Bruce, and the chunky hard rock riffs of Clapton were a revelation for 1966. Yes, I didn't stutter, 1966. It is a very mature blues rock album for the time, and the sound was revolutionary (along with The Paul Butterfield Blues Band release of 1965, the most important of the era).

Listen to "N.S.U." or "Sleepy Time Time" - this aint the mop-top Beatles or the tinny-sounding Stones, but a hard rocking blues band in heavy-duty mode. And, of course, it is a powerful set of blues, driven just as much by Jack Bruce's wail and howl as Clapton's guitar: the ominous "Spoonful" (which does ol' Willie Dixon proud), the absolutely crazy "Rollin' and Tumblin' (just try to keep up with Bruce on blues harp for the full 4:42 - I dare you), Muddy Water's "Four Until Late" (Clapton singing), and the quintessential rock version of "Cat's Squirrel". Then there's the anticipatory psychedelia of "I'm So Glad" and "Dreaming". One of the greatest blues rock albums of all time.

Establishing the template for a rock 'n' roll trio, Cream debuted in 1966 with the release of _Fresh Cream_. Eric Clapton, following his gig with the Yardbirds, garnered the glut of the attention, but it was the sum of the parts that pushed Cream during the late sixties trip.

Clapton may have been viewed as God by many, but from my humble perspective E.C. wasn't even the main six string force to emerge from the Yardbirds, as both Jeff Beck and Jimmy Page captured my fretboard frenzied attention. That is no knock on Clapton, as both Jeff and Jimmy were in a class of their own.

Bassist Jack Bruce and drummer Ginger Baker teamed to form the remaining two-thirds of the Cream machine. Together, Bruce and Baked locked down the rhythm for the tight arrangements that consume the eleven song _Fresh Cream_ LP, which opens with the short "N.S.U.", and adds a six-minute-plus cover of "Spoonful", plus "Rollin' and Tumblin'", "I'm So Glad", and "Toad", while "I Feel Free" was reserved for the North American pressing which was issued in '67 by Atco Records.

KEEP IT FRESH!

Cream rode the psychedelic wave, they had the psychedelic look and trappings, but no band had ever been so mislabeled. They would certainly venture deeper into the psychedelic sounds with later explorations, but here, here on Fresh Cream, this was Blues, mixed with a bit of Jazz and Rock sensibilities taken to the next power and squared. Eric Clapton had learned all he could at the feet of John Mayall, Jack Bruce, with his six string bass has to be one of the finest bass players to have ever walked the planet, and Ginger Baker [?], Ginger Baker wasnt just a drummer ... trying to draw a line of demarcation between where Gingers drums ended and he began would prove a challenging task for anyone. And what did all of this produce? Why some of the best power pops, blues and rocks songs ever written.

Songs like Im So Glad, and I Feel Free stopped people cold, it didnt matter what you were doing, who you were talking to, or what your intentions were ... for the next three minutes or so, these numbers owned you, heart and soul. And seeing them again for their reunion tour knocked me flat, when I had to remember, and come to terms with the fact that it was Jack Bruce who was doing the vocals. For all of the Heaviness hype, and there certainly was an extraordinary amount of heaviness, there was a lightness in the melodies and the harmonies that tied it all together and made it palatable. What Cream achieved, what made them so special, was that they almost single handedly opened the door, demanding that rock music be taken seriously ... and they achieved this because they had no choice ... it was all they could do ... it was who a power trio of this magnitude were.

I was there when this album came out, and I tell you true, it's one of those albums that only gets better with time. The more one understands about music, structure and form, the more important this record becomes. Fresh Cream is a seminal album, an album that is responsible for so much to so many, and should never be taken lightly ... less of course you dont mind being bitten when you least expect it.

Cream Takes Hard Rock To A New High Level
Cream's debut is a high energy bazooka blast, followed by armored tank divisions, navy guns, and B-2 bombers.

Side one has the great original songs:

- I Feel Fine
- NSU
- Sleepy Time Time
- Dreaming
- Sweet Wine

Side two has the explosive cover songs, harmonicas, and drum solos. Put the two sides together and that's quite an KO.

Overall, the album could use some better song writing (which would get better on future albums), but it's the unique high energy heavy metal sound that stands out here.

Consequently, not only was Cream very popular, but highly influential.

It might've been fresh in 1966
I've never been a huge fan of Eric Clapton. And yeah, I know he's hailed as one of the greatest guitarists of all-time etc., but I just find him incredibly boring. Apart from "Layla" I don't really rate any of his output. I just find that blues is a harsh mistress, and it can either be raw, gritty and passionate, or it can be incredibly boring. While a guitar player like Hendrix falls in the former camp, Clapton unfortunately belongs in the latter.

However, Cream was not a Clapton solo project, so it was more to it than his guitar playing. Jack Bruce was the band leader and primary songwriter, and for me Ginger Baker is the main attraction. Unfortunately I never get carried away by any of the songs, and it almost just sounds like a long jam-session without anything special happening. Great musicians, but not any great music.

A very uninteresting album, and simply not my cup of tea. Saved by some excellent drumming by Baker, but still a very dull collection of songs.

Track Picks: "Sweet Wine", "Spoonful" and "Toad"

Still some nice songs to listen to: "I Feel Free" (can't get it out of my head!), "N.S.U.", "Dreaming", "Sweet Wine". I like the voice of Jack Bruce.
