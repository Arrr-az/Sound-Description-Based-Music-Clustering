Known as the 'clean' form of _In Utero_. At the time of it's release it was distributed to chain-like stores (e.g. Wal-Mart, K-Mart, etc.), with the "Rape Me" title converted to "Waif Me" and the back artwork altered slightly. The real reason to own is for the Scott Litt remix of "Pennyroyal Tea," however the exact same version can now be found on the band's 2002 greatest hits comp, Nirvana. Regardless, it remains a fun Nirvana artifact.

"Teenage angst has paid off well, now I'm bored and old"

These are the first lines you'll hear when you first listen to Nirvana's "In Utero". Full or sarcasm, and irony. Kurt Cobain half-jokingly relates that he has cashed in on the angst-ridden teenagers around the world and that now the whole idea is boring to him. Kind of ironic that In Utero is a record that is chalk full of angst, much more than any previous Nirvana album.

There was also alot of controversy surrounding the release of this album, such as their record label, DCG records objecting to the sound of the record claiming that it was "unlistenable" and "unreleasable" due to the recording style of indie-legend Steve Albini. There was also quite a noticeable lack of potential singles, and catchy hooks on the record too. Nirvana didn't want to release "Nevermind II" no matter how much their record company wanted them to. They also caught flack for their song "Rape Me" ..for obvious reasons.

Nirvana makes much more of an artistic statement on this record though... not that Nevermind wasn't, but Nevermind had a sound that was much more easily consumable to the masses than In Utero. In Utero is much noisier, angrier, and more emotional than Nevermind. It takes a bit more time to appreciate it's intricacies. But that doesn't mean there aren't any pop gems hidden within the chaos.

Nevertheless, Nirvana manages to create an album that is superior to Nevermind. It's their best record, and quite frankly... it's my favorite album of all-time.. by any artist.

It starts with "Serve The Servants" a moderate rock song that might catch you by surprise at first. Definitely a stark contrast to "Smells Like Teen Spirit". It's one of the few songs that features some blunt, personal lyrics by Cobain "I tried hard to have a father, but instead I had a dad". What follows is the aural assault that is "Scentless Apprentice" which is much heavier and louder than just about anything they've recorded before. After that you have the big hit off of this record "Heart Shaped Box", a great song that you have undoubtedly heard dozens of times. Then comes the aforementioned "Rape Me" (which is actually an anti-rape song) and despite all of the controversy that surrounded this song, it's 3 minutes of pop-alternative-grunge rock bliss. Then you have "Frances Farmer Will Have Her Revenge On Seattle" which it's intro will make your ears bleed if your wearing headphones with its screeching, siren-like wave. "Dumb" is a Beatles inspired softer song that fits exceptionally well in the middle of the record as a relaxing change of pace song.

"Very Ape" is a crunchy 2 minutes of goodness. Then we have "Milk It", with its exceptionally loud, abrasive, pummeling chorus. "Pennyroyal Tea" is next and was slated to be the next single before Cobain's death, and with good reason, the song is catchy, has a great chorus, and is great in all facets (you must hear the Unplugged version). "Radio Friendly Unit Shifter" is the one song that even the band themselves admit that it was never truly finished, as indicated by the final 2 minutes of noise, nevertheless it does not feel like a filler song at all. Then "Tourettes" comes in and punches you in the face, 95 seconds of furniture breaking inducing chaos. Then the album ends nicely with "All Apologies" a sad, yet touching ending to the record.

We will never know what the follow-up to this record would've sounded like, if there even was going to be one. But one thing is for sure. Their final studio recording will be a collection of songs that will be treasured the same way most treasure albums like Led Zeppelin IV and Abbey Road. It's unfortunate that what happened with Cobain happened, but one thing is for sure, they left in a blaze of glory, burning out, instead of fading away.

Kurt Cobain's story is extremely publicised and it's full of mystique, mystery and even a little bit of romance and possibly the most interesting period of his story was around the making and release of In Utero. His drug use had increased, his marriage was tempestuous to say the least, he was accused of being a bad father, he couldn't deal with the fame and his life was in absolute turmoil.

As I mentioned in my review of Nevermind, Nirvana were one of the most important bands to me and I actually became a little bit obsessed with Kurt Cobain's story. I watched every documentary I could get my hands on, I bought their albums, band t-shirts, posters, I listened to their songs all the time, sang them in the shower. It was an obsession, and even now I absolutely adore them and I sometimes think this is better than Nevermind. Though it usually passes, because of how important that album is to me. After the release of critically acclaimed and revered Nevermind, Nirvana were the voice of a generation. They were the most popular band on the planet, and Kurt couldn't deal with the fame that came with it.

In Utero is Nirvana's last album before Kurt Cobain killed himself, and I think it was designed to be a completely different album to Nevermind. I think it was designed to alienate some of their fans, because they went for a more natural and abrasive sound, though I still think they wanted to keep their artistic credibility and make the album they wanted to make. A lot of people (including the band themselves) think Nevermind's production is extremely polished and mainstream sounding, and although I do disagree to an extent, but when you compare it to this - it definitely is. This is incredibly raw and powerful. Steve Albini as a producer is brilliant, and he really got the best out of Nirvana. Even though rumour has it they weren't completely satisfied with the sound he got out of them, but I think it's brilliant.

In Utero is definitely more challenging and I'd argue that it's more interesting than its predecessor, especially lyrically. I think as a lyricist, Kurt Cobain definitely hit his peak - but he always said that lyrics came second, the music came first. I don't know if he put more effort into the lyrics, or if he put more focus on them, or whether they just came out naturally - but they're incredible. It really shows how messed up Kurt Cobain was, but as always they were very abstract and cryptic. As a songwriter, he was always brilliant. Even though he wanted to make an abrasive album, his ability to write catchy melodies were still there and it makes for a brilliant juxtaposition. But this is one of the most angry and intense albums I've ever listened to.

A devastating statement from the hesitant leaders of a manufactured counterculture. It lacks the universal punch of a "Smells Like Teen Spirit," but more than makes up for it in waves of blistering feedback, the like of which was never (and almost certainly will never be) found on a #1 record ever again.

I would argue that In Utero has a broader appeal than even Nevermind. I can't help but notice that my friends always skip the abrasive tracks I love, yet I could just as easily go the rest of my life without hearing "Pennyroyal Tea" again, which they can't get enough of. There's something for everyone. People always talk about the anger here, and yeah, he screams the word "rape" a whole lot, which is all fine and good...but the NOISE, man. The same stuff that scared off the "Teen Spirit" frat boys keeps bringing me back.

It's especially amazing in context: In Utero knocked Garth Brooks' In Pieces--an album that went platinum eight times--from the #1 Billboard spot, if only for a week. Then a month later, Pearl Jam released Vs., an album that also fell under the absurd "grunge" genre, yet probably has more in common sonically with Garth Brooks than In Utero.

This is an album completely out of time that had no use being anywhere near the pop charts, nor did it belong with the fading grunge fad. It's a profoundly unique death rattle from a band that only becomes more and more fascinating as a piece of pop culture history as time marches on.

Revisiting this, I've had to dock my rating a little.

This was a 5-star album for me for a long time, and I still feel like it's a perfect-sounding album. It's been really bizarre to me to see Albini's production take a lot of knocks from music nerds on the internet lately. The sound of this album is its greatest asset.

And some of the songs are pretty great, too. I've always loved the two openers and still do. When I was a kid I thought "Heart-Shaped Box" was a disappointing single, but I came to prefer it to most of the ones off of Nevermind in the ensuing years. The string of closing tracks from "Pennyroyal Tea" to "All Apologies" is of course great as well.

This brings me to the point of why I no longer place this album on such a high pedestal. It really drags in the middle. "Rape Me", "Dumb", "Very Ape" and "Milk It" are just not that strong. I appreciate the need to have a little variation on an album and not just pummel your way from start to finish, but Nirvana was never really great at slow/quiet/moody songs. I guess I'm contradicting myself a little, though, in that "Milk It" isn't really slow - not even as slow as "Pennyroyal Tea" which I do like - but the lyrics are some of the more bothersome to me on this album. Just kind of gross and nonsensical, and to me it really sums of the kind of empty cynicism that was always lurking behind a lot of Cobain's lyrics. It's something I relate to but also find tiresome.

But man, the sound of this record is just glorious, especially on the opening and closing tracks.

"In Utero" foi o sucessor de "Nevermind", lanado em 1991, um dos mais importantes da dcada de 90 e que havia sido um soco no estmago na cena musical.

A produo de Steve Albini recupera o som mais abrasivo da banda e o resultado das gravaes desagrada editora e correm rumores de que nem a prpria banda ficou totalmente satisfeita.

O disco mais diversificado do que o seu antecessor e alterna grunge e noise rock com as melodias de "Dumb" ou "All Apologies", que viriam a ganhar belssimas verses acsticas no concerto gravado para a MTV, apenas dois meses depois do lanamento de "In Utero".

Oh my fucking god...what is there to even say about this?

Nirvana's In Utero is something of a bastardizing record (if you've heard Nevermind, you know what I'm talking about) but that's the beauty to it. I think as a whole it showcases the sound Nirvana was striving towards their entire career. I really do.

The record opens with the lyrics "Teenage angst has paid off well / Now I'm bored and old" and they single-handedly set the tone for the entire thing. Kurt's vocal performance is brutally despairing and you can really hear the pain and emotion in his voice. It's a bare, honest, and raw-sounding album but retains its emotion, which combines for an unforgettable collection of songs. You've got to give Steve Albini major props for his production skills which shine so vividly on this album.

Love them or hate them, Nirvana was a special band with one hell of a drummer and one phenomenal bassist, and Kurt Cobain was truly a gifted man, not to mention a genuine poet.

I am a huge Nirvana fan. I've heard just about everything they've done, from the studio albums, the b-sides and other rarities, and even all those questionable quality demos on With the Lights Out. Even still, I generally think that even the biggest fans of an artist don't like everything they put out. There is a lot of Nirvana material I would not mind much at all to never hear again other than for the reason that they're one of my all-time favorite bands and I feel inclined to own their whole discography.

In all honesty, I didn't really like Nirvana that much at first. Just one of the singles off In Utero, Heart-Shaped Box. I did what many people seem to do these days and neglected delving deeper into their work under the assumption that it would be similar to all the heavy, screamy, overly-stlyish emo rock music Nirvana would (unintentionally) inspire. It was a stupid and presumptuous thing of me to do, I now realize, but that didn't change until I listened to In Utero, my first ever Nirvana album and was so intrigued I had to hear everything the band had ever done. Because little did I realize, I had now become the emo, emotionally and sexually frustrated, alienated teenager that Kurt Cobain's music speaks to. When you're in that confusing, rebellious stage, finding anything you can relate to is a miracle. So I became a big Nirvana devotee for a good while and learned to appreciate just about everything they've done.

But I don't want to drag on too much with my personal history, just giving you a little background into what In Utero, and Nirvana at large, mean to me.

In Utero was an attempt by Kurt Cobain to intentionally alienate his fanbase that had come to idolize the band through their first huge success, Nevermind. Whereas Nevermind was a tastefully produced, though heavy and honest pop/rock album, In Utero promised to be more abrasive, getting back to the band's "rawer" roots in garage rock, noise rock, and even a bit of sludge metal. Kurt Cobain had gotten tired of writing new generation Beatles-influenced pop songs, and like The Beatles, he didn't want to be cute anymore.

So does In Utero deliver the abrasive, alienating, shocking sound that its background story and mastermind hinted at? Well, yes and no. From the moment Steve Alibini was revealed to be the album's producer rather than Nevermind's Butch Vig, it was clear to anyone familiar with Albini's work that In Utero was going to sound a lot different from Nevermind, at least on the surface.

While the guitars grind and screech more than ever, and reverberate never, this is still token Nirvana. Every song has some kind of strong melody, and a definite structure with most songs displaying the typical soft verse, heavy chorus the band is known for. Listening to the opening track, Serve the Servants, one could easily make the assumption that In Utero is just about as accessible as Nevermind.

And they would be right. Because In Utero is a pop/rock album. Unless you are very easily offended, in which case In Utero still has the same roots in pop/rock that Nevermind has. The only consistent trait separating it in structure from Nevermind is that In Utero is slightly noisier, slightly angrier. The verse-loud chorus-verse format is still there, the token, endearingly sloppy guitar solos are all there, and there's still a lot of attention paid to melody and catchy riffs.

Serve the Servants starts off the album with a clangy, grinding guitar verse, then regularly shifts into a melodic, relatively soft chorus that would definitely be more accentuated by Nevermind's production, and with very slight tweaking fit on said album, structurally, if not thematically. Steve Alibini's production values may make the pop aspects of In Utero seem less apparent but they are still there.

The album continues with Scentless Apprentice, which I believe was the first ever song credited to all three regular members of the band. While in the scope of Nirvana's entire catalog, Scentless Apprentice is a decent song, on In Utero it is easily one of the worst individual tracks, featuring a prominent and very generic scaling riff insisted upon by Dave Grohl. Kurt Cobain himself even commented that the riff was very typical and unimpressive, and primarily went along with the song as to not hurt Dave Grohl's feelings. And when Kurt Cobain criticizes your guitar riffs for being too simple, then you know it's a problem. It is well-documented that during In Utero, Kurt Cobain was losing a lot of passion for his songwriting career and wanted less pressure taken off him writing songs, and encouraged the other band members to help write songs. However, neither of them were as good of songwriters as Cobain, and it shows. Even the lyrics are supposedly inspired from the horror novel, Perfume. but even considering the book's theme, the lyrics are mostly nonsensical and impersonal. Lacking a great melody, riff, or emotional lyrics, Scentless Apprentice lacks all of Nirvana's greatest traits (yet for some reason is often claimed as one of the best tracks on the album).

Continuing, Heart-Shaped Box is the most recognizable song from this album and one of Nirvana's biggest hits. Like I said in my introduction, it's the song that got me (and many others) into Nirvana. And it's just as gripping today. The delicate riff in the softly-sung verse is a perfect mood setter as the song gets increasingly angry and explodes into a roaring sing-along chorus. Heart-Shaped Box lyrically details some of Kurt Cobain's feelings on his failing relationship with his wife, Courtney Love. Powerful both musically and thematically, Heart-Shaped Box is deservedly the star track on In Utero.

Rape Me is a decent song, but strikes me as a deliberate attempt to be lyrically abrasive, almost attention-seeking (which is ironic because in a way it's supposed to insult oppressive-minded people who respond to that behavior). I do like the bridge, however. Speaking of bridges, Dumb very possibly has the best bridge Nirvana has ever done and the prominent backing cello adds a lot to the song (previously used on Something in the Way on Nevermind), but unfortunately the bridge is by far better than rest of the song. I guess I would call Dumb the "Polly" of In Utero, comparing it to the song on Nevermind. Frances Farmer Will Have Her Revenge on Seattle and Radio Friendly Unit Shifter are heavy as hell, very monolithic, though both songs go on for about a minute too long. Nevertheless, both are great songs, especially Frances Farmer, being one of the best on the album.

Pennyroyal Tea is another fairly conventionally structured song that at its best sounds much too similar to a lot of other songs Nirvana have done that are far better than it (see Heart-Shaped Box, which is on this very album), and at its worst reveals another weakness of In Utero in that the album's songs are often too reliant on their choruses. Radio Friendly Unit Shifter displays that same weakness.

Very Ape and Tourette's are two songs that when taken individually are the worst on this album (practically throwaways where Nevermind had none), but in the context of the rest of the album contribute far more to it than Scentless Apprentice, primarily due to their short length and aggressiveness, further driving home the almost schizophrenic feel of In Utero. Milk It is the closest In Utero comes to straight-up noise rock, and despite being a bit too long, it's another highlight and one of the most memorable songs on the album. And then there's the fitting closer, the near-acoustic All Apologies. A very mellow and heart-wrenching song, often considered to be one of Kurt Cobain's final "goodbyes" before his eventual suicide. Intimate, like all the greatest Nirvana songs, but more confessional than any without the usual contentiousness of the heavy guitars and mangled screams.

The few problems I have with In Utero are that it's often very repetitive lyrically (see: Dumb, Rape Me, Radio Friendly Unit Shifter) and the noisier instrumental segments thrown in by Kurt Cobain at random simply and bitterly to alienate and annoy his fanbase are unpleasant, bothersome, and rarely do anything more than drag a song's playing time out longer than it should be.

Nirvana were always a band that valued pop songwriting above anything else, and no amount of noise feedback or screeching guitars can disguise that simple fact. For the same reason, Nirvana were at their best when they had an album like Nevermind that capitalized on their songwriting talents, and Nevermind definitely contains the band's best songwriting of their short career, containing none of the weak moments in its tracklisting that In Utero sometimes comes across. And again, noise feedback does not make up for a poorly written song by a band that are noted for their skill at writing memorable hooks as well as dynamics that can take you wherever the band please, where In Utero often sacrifices dynamics to be able to thrash out for the majority of the song. It's for these reasons that Nevermind is rightfully viewed as the band's defining and greatest album.

Nevermind and In Utero are like two sides of the same coin. One side favors melody and concise song structures, while another side favors the heaviness and unrestrained energy underneath. But all of those qualities are in both albums, just with a different amount of focus. Whether you prefer Nevermind or In Utero depends on which of those qualities you generally prefer in your music, and to completely devalue either one of the albums while enjoying the other strikes me as narrow-minded, presuming that it's only over an aesthetic difference, because structurally they belong with each other. In the end, I'm happy we got two fantastic, memorable alternative rock albums that both show off the strongest attributes of this seminal band before their short career was over.
