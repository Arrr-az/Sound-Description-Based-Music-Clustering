Album 111 of 1001: Low-Life by New Order
The story of Joy Division is a tragedy too many bands face. But the story of New Order is an inspiring one about conquering that tragedy. And conquer they did. They are hailed as one of the great bands of the 80s, and played a large part in the growing success of the synth pop genre, which at this point in the decade, was only getting bigger. This is a good time. You can dance to these songs. This is very much New Order, and not Joy Division. But it also has an underlying layer of beauty with massive synth layers stacked on top of each other, and amazing chorus-soaked guitars. This feels right. This feels like the way all new wave and synth pop albums should be written. Sunrise is actually a masterpiece. One of my favorite songs Ive heard from the 80s ever. The only issue I have is the vocals. I dont know what it is, but vocals never really seemed like New Orders strongest characteristic.

An Amazing Genre-Defining Album
Not only is Low Life my favorite New Order album, it might be my favorite New Wave/Post-Punk album. This album has some of my favorite New Order songs on it, and I love the darker, melancholic tone that carries throughout the album. This was the last New Order album which I felt had some of the post-punk sound that carried over from Joy Division before that aspect of New Order's sound was left behind with albums like Brotherhood and Technique. I really appreciate that aspect of Low Life. My only complaint with Low Life is that it has a terrible closing track, Face Up, that doesn't fit the tone of the rest of the album, which causes the album to end with a whimper.

Who at the time would have predicted such a renaissance for these boys, when the start of the decade left them without a vision and short the visionary? The slap-bang-middle sees them all amongst it in the dialectic, wedding post-punk sinewy rock with a chart-friendly synth-i-ness. New Order are probably (certainly) not the eighties Beatles, but they could very well be the eighties Beach Boys. With such a string of impressive stand-alone singles ('Ceremony', 'Temptation', 'Blue Monday'), they didn't even need to be an 'album band', but 'Low-life' is a striking and confident pop statement regardless.

The ensuing years have seen the adolescent romanticism of the sophomore album overtake it reputationally, but this disc still is likely the group at their most imaginative; amongst others, we are provided a silly melodramatic ballad, a Gothic stomper, and a beep-whirr-buzz synth-strumental tribute to poor Ian. It's always difficult to write about good pop, because good pop is efficient and it tends to speak for itself. I leave you with clichs: solid, confident, strong, definitive, etc.

I've never really bothered to look up New Order other than their singles. I found their previous album, Power, Corruption & Lies, quite passable and with the obvious lack of Blue Monday on the original tracklist, the album didn't stick with me. And with no obvious hits on Low-Life, this album seemed even more daunting, especially with that non-descript album cover

And wow what an awkward beginning. Love Vigilantes is an awfully awkward ballad, with Bernard Sumner proving that yes, he had to be coaxed to be the singer. Add in some awkward stomp rock drums and I fear some really dated music is coming up. Which is really most of this album: Bernard Sumner singing like the guy who had a few too many drinks at the karaoke bar, blaring synths, and I guess there's some drumming. The album tries to convince you its melancholic but really the fragrance I would give it is cheddar.

The only highlights for me are in the middle, when the album sounds a-typical. Sunrise sounds like a sudden The Cure-cut and instrumental Elegia is nicely moody. This Time of Night is the only song where their melancholic mood works.

Perhaps New Order isn't the band for me. I only really listen to two or three singles from them (guess which ones are the latter two because we all bloody know what the first is), and even with Joy Division I only play Unknown Pleasures.

MrT503 Jun 18 2023
+1 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Coming off of the clean, immaculately produced Power, Corruption & Lies, Low-Life shows a much rougher side of New Order. Where Power, Corruption & Lies's opening track, Age of Consent, glistens with its layers of synths and chorus effects, Low-Life starts off with the thudding drums, and slightly distorted synths and bassline of Love Vigilantes. This grimier tone is present throughout the lyrics, too. New Order/Joy Division's previous troubled, passive narrators are replaced by the accusations of neglected children in This Time of Night, declarations of hate Face Up, and the descriptions of the misfortunes of others in The Perfect Kiss (although this latter track's album edit removes a key verse). The narrators' crises don't stem from their own moral failings, but rather out of anger from others' failings. This is a thirst for vengeance coupled with moral indifference. This is the picture painted in Low-Life.

In terms of songwriting, Low-Life is more of the undercity's nocturnal soundtrack rather than the usual "sit in your bedroom and be sad" of their early outings. For example, tracks 2-3 have very busy rhythms and thudding kick drums. which cool down for Sunrise, a dirty The Cure impression, and the palette-cleansing instrumental, Elegia. However, the pace is quickly picked back up in the closing few tracks, where relatively flimsy synths carry the melody rather than Peter Hook's bass. Combined with Sumner's flat, dry vocal performances (especially on Sub-Culture), this is clearly the weakest stretch of the album, as there's relatively little holding the cheap sounding synths (imagine if you took the dreamy qualities out of The Cure's Disintegration and you'll get a good idea of the synths on these tracks) to the underlying percussion. Still, there's enough going on in these songs, that they're relatively enjoyable. In fact, I would go so far as to say that these tracks are saved by the songwriting calling card that New Order develops on this album: the very dense synth- and melody-driven chorus that cuts out for an airy, percussive breakdown. There's often so much going on in these songs that it can sound like a mess at first, but I've found that repeat listens have only allowed me to enjoy this style of songwriting more.

Overall, Low-Life does a fantastic job capturing a tone and delivers a plethora of highly danceable songs. While it's less catchy and the crescendos are less bittersweet than other New Order outings, the added initiative in the lyrics and more energetic songwriting synergize to more than compensate for these losses.
