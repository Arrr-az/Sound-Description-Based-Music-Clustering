wago Sep 15 2009
+8 Vote downBookmarkVote up
This isn't no perfect album. It is:
- badly recorded;
- full of too long instrumental digressions;
- spoiled (!?) by questionable tape cut'n'paste experimentations. Very short, luckily.

But I don't care. I really do not. This record is just too fun to waste any time complaining about this and that.

What kind of stuff is this record which is just too fun and so on, you'll ask. Well:
- it sounds like This Heat;
- it sounds like Stereolab;
... Isn't it enough? Ok, it often sounds like The Flying Lizards or Ludus too, sometimes like The Residents, a couple of times it even sounds like The Police and The Police.

Wait, we're not getting the point. "Monkey Banana Kitchen" actually sounds like nothing else. What else has this rickety guitar sound, these clumsy piano chords, English/French/German pseudo-nursery rhymes as lyrics and such an omnipresent dub influence? What else combines Jamaica, Art & Language and Dave Brubeck?

Let's summarize:
- This album is quite bad for a series of reasons;
- But in fact it's quite (a lot, actually) good because it sounds like many other fantastic things;
- But after all it's unique because... Well go and find anyone who sounds like that!

So what. I'm still missing the main point. This is a wonderful pop album. A collection of songs which won't get out of your mind easily (they won't get out at all, much probably). Playful, light-hearted, funny, polychrome. Beautiful. What to ask more?

The ideas Family Fodder presents on their debut LP are truly strange and creative, but fail to keep my interest over the course of the song runtimes. Though I’ll say “Monkey”, “Wrong”, “Organ Grinder”, and “Bass Adds Bass” mostly hold my attention, particularly “Monkey” and “Wrong”. “Monkey” is an incredibly disorienting psychedelic rhythmic barrage of what seems like samples, and “Wrong” is a super strange tune with great existential lyrics — they play with sampling and psychedelic lyrics quite a bit — and they also dabble in reggae and post-punkish stylings. It’s certainly a curious listen, but not a very engaging one. Maybe I’ll come back around to it someday.

There is a lot of things happening, all at once, at all times. Every song could have been a separate single from different albums but no, they all belong to the same album. I don't know anything about the multiple genres within this album except for the heavy dub influence which is great. I know dub has influenced the post-punk scene but Family Fodder went as far as to make two full dub tracks in this album, so for me, those are the standout tracks: Symbols and Bass Adds Bass.

There were no transitions that made sense, honestly, it's a huge mess but for some reason, it works REALLY well. I liked this a lot, so many different moods and feelings all compressed into 46 minutes. Sometimes the vocals switched from English to French, then it was a heavy guitar, I think bagpipes came in at one point, there's too much to unpack.

I highly recommend.

8/10

Zephos Sep 14 2016
Vote downBookmarkVote up
Lets dig deep in that 77-82' soil and see what we can find. Increasingly I think effort is going into exploring the more "lo-fi" products of the period (see also Young Marble Giants, TV Personalities), which is fine and well? But I associate "lo-fi" so strongly with self-consciously lo-fi 90's American stuff that I kind of cringe at the use in this case. A band like this is scruffy and simple out of that just being what they had to work with, it doesn't describe the artistic interests and thank god. Not that Family Fodder isn't relishing in their DIY own way artistic ideas, because its not like this is "budgetless Banshees" or something. But actually categorizing it against the other music of the period is kind of hard as hell! It DEFINITELY belongs in this period of course, but it doesn't really fit under the already fairly broad umbrellas of Post-Punk or New Wave. I guess it would be Post-Punk? But in the extremely vague sense of the word, and not the genre sense I tend to use. The closest band comparison I can come up with is the Raincoats, which this sounds like a more muscular version of in some ways. The Raincoats also sounded thin and low budget, but were happy enough sounding small. This sometimes does too, but also has no problem trying for more energy and scale. It also shares with the Raincoats a omnivorous feel, taking in various ethnic music elements. But most notable is the constantly reoccurring reminder that these guys love Dub! And well...who didn't in 1980. Dub was an omnipresent fascination among Post-Punk bands and even some Punks. Family Fodder love it enough to have a couple tracks devoted to it as a style, and also adding in Dub inflected extensions to some otherwise non Dub songs. Lots of risks are taken here, in fact more than the Raincoats did given the chemistry mixing is much more reckless. What worked in the intimate alleys of the Raincoats might collapse in the high flying songs here! But somehow it never really does, and the results are absolutely charming as hell. Yeah, thats right, charming. Music that prides itself on being experimental can often be covered in the slime of seriousness and ego in the audible sense. Music that wants you to look at what it dared, not what it did. These guys tap into that sacred Tago Mago zone of creating wacky totally non radio friendly work that is....FUN. This is fun! Its fun to listen to and the band sounds often enough to be having fun. The track that springs to mind most is Monkey, six minutes of repetitive kind of sunny melody orbited by a swarm of vocals and production effects. Centrally inhabited by the female vocalist chirping something like "with a BANANA!" in yes, a cutesy fashion. This may sound terrible, but hell I like the song! Why? Well principally its FUN. It has a fun energy, a fun tune, is fun to follow along with its twists and hijinks...whats not to love? And don't worry out there, other tracks are not anywhere near as cute, but they share alike the sense of exploration and finger painting level freedom. But they hey thing! The key is that underneath it all are good songwriting chops, strong central melodies that keep the generally jam like songs together (don't really expect verse chorus verse here). Yeah jams, jams with strong fundamentals, jams that bow to Dub in how they take and twist and contort the song every which way. Oh man, its just cool. Total lost gem this.

Rating: 4
Highlights: Symbols, Savoir Faire, Monkey, Organ Grinder

Tooooooooooo much to say about this album! It really flipped me out when I belatedly discovered it two years ago. Since then I've returned to it time and again, developed special relationships with individual tracks. The songs are so different from each other and sometimes I'm in the mood for some and not others. It takes a lot of exposure to get inside weird songs sometimes, but when the melodies click into place it's such a thrill. For an album this important it's good to at least give track-by-track ratings because I'm too lazy to write all my thoughts about them and this way I can at least express my feelings about the album a little more clearly. (I rate on the 15 point Gnosis scale and then convert to the RYM scale. 10=3.5. 11=4. 12=4.5.)

This group was so unique, so inspired, so awesome at so many levels! I recommend this album to everyone in the world! Except all the dumbasses.

I think I've alternated between 10 and 11 on "Bass Adds Bass". Theoretically each individual listening experience has a separate rating, so there's inherent variability and statistical indeterminancy to this kind of data. I'm a little surprised that none of the tracks have climbed up to 13 yet, but it's extremely hard for a song to go that high, the threshold of transcendental experience that is more often achieved via abstract non-song music.

"Cold Wars" is the most recent track I've paid attention to. There's a magical little melodic fragment in the way his voice keens during one of the lines that pushes it pretty close to 12 territory and the ending is amazing.

"Savoir Faire" is of course THE most legendary, important proto-Stereolab song in history.

"Love Song" has been my favorite the past few months as far as getting specific FF cravings. I could see that one climbing to 13 before the others someday.

Actually, I think "Savoir Faire", "Love Song", "Wrong" and "Cerf Volant" are all pretty darn close to hitting 13. These songs totally DESTROY me!
