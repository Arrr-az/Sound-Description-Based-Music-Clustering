TheImpossibleMan Jun 13 2007

Certainly the strongest album of Cream's all-too-short existence. Even if you discount the mind-blowng second disc (which, in a lot of ways, isn't a fair comparison to the other albums, as Cream was far better live than in the studio), this is definitely the best album by the band.

First, the studio record: While, as expected, the instrumentation is some of the best of the 1960's, it is the fact that Cream has hit their song-writing stride that makes Wheels great. Cream's three other albums suffered from great tracks surrounded by filler. While some of the songs on this album are less than fully formed (Passing the Time, Politician), the album is very solid. I think this is typified best by As You Said: a rare acoustic track by the group, it is utterly unlike any other song on the album, lacking a solo and showcasing almost no jazz or blues influence. Yet, it is still an enjoyable, interesting song. It is a song that fills out the album - it is not filler. Much of the album functions this way.

There isn't too much to say about the studio side, other than that it is very good. Cream was a straight-forward blues rock band that placed a supreme emphasis on instrumentation. You get the blues-rockers you expect (Sitting on Top of the World, Born Under a Bad Sign), as well as the psychadelic tracks that the group was so good at crafting (White Room, Pressed Rat and Warthog). The album is helped by Pete Brown's superb lyrics; they are clever, sure, but also have a serious depth and are very much poetry. Deserted Cities of the Heart is probably the best set of lyrics he ever wrote for the group.

While, studio-wise, Bruce's bass playing takes a backseat, Baker's drums move to the forefront. His drumming really stands out on cuts like Passing the Time, White Room, and the excellent Those Were the Days. Those Were the Days is one of the few songs I've ever heard where a drummer completely takes over a track without soloing. In my opinion, it's the best song he wrote for the Cream and his best studio performance for the band.

If the album only consisted of the studio songs, I'd probably give this 4 stars and move on.

But.

It's doesn't consist of just studio songs.

Most people, when talking about the live album, complain of it's length. 'Oh, Crossroads is good, but the rest are just too long.' 'I think this is a great album but I really don't like the live record.' 'They're just fooling around for fifteen minutes.' I am not one of those people. The live album is simply fantastic.

Cream consisted of three musicians who excelled at improvisation. They effectively combined blues, jazz, and rock-n-roll in all their live pieces. They could be inconsistent live, but since this is not a full live concert and has the best tracks culled out of two separate live performances, it is damn near perfect.

I don't know, maybe it's because I'm a musician myself, but I love the tracks here, length be damned. Crossroads is, of course, one of the two or three greatest live tracks ever; we all know that. Clapton is firing on all cylinders and Bruce is at the height of his counterpoint-powers. Spoonful is the band in full-on 'jam' mode, as the middle section has the trio all going off in opposite directions while still keeping an overall structure. Traintime does drag a little bit, but the harmonica work is a lot of fun. Toad is the one cut I hear people badmouth the most, but it is just as strong as the other tracks. Baker's drumming is incredibly dynamic, as he manages to shift through tons of different playing styles, while still keeping a sense of rhythm and the music moving forward. Live at the Fillmore is an absolutely indispensable record for anyone who is a fan of live music.

This album's singularity pushes it into the realm of truly great albums. There is a wealth of material here (around an hour and fifteen minutes) and it is consistently excellent. I think it's one of the definitive albums of the Sixties.

Eric Clapton's, Jack Bruce's and Ginger Baker's blues/psych rock supergroup Cream with their third album. Wheels of Fire is a massive double LP which has a studio album as the first disc and a live album as the second disc. Both are impressive in my opinion. Wheels of Fire reminds me a lot about Pink Floyd's Ummagumma because it has both studio disc and live disc too. These kind of double albums are more effective if you listen to them as two separate albums.

The first disc includes some well-known Cream classicks like the opener "White Room" and "Politician". There are also some cover songs which are all just unbelievably great and made with that special Cream touch. I don't have anything bad to say about any of these studio songs on Wheels of Fire.

Disc number two has only four songs performed live but two of them are very long. "Crossroads" is Clapton's arrangement of Robert Johnson's "Cross Road Blues" and it's also one of the best known Cream tracks. "Spoonful" is just fantastic live cover too. "Traintime" and "Toad" are once again Cream's own material. "Traintime" might be the weakest part of this whole double LP. Not very convincing at all. "Toad" is a lot better with it's jam band feeling and awesome drumming by Ginger Baker.

I can admit that Wheels of Fire would be a bit more balanced package if it had only the first disc. The first disc is flawless. However I enjoy the live disc a lot too (except "Traintime"). 4,5 stars out of five.

The Disc 2 of this two record set is amongst the most boring records I have ever tried to listen. If it was a single [live] album, I would possibly give it 2.0. Fortunately, the studio album is so good I would even consider giving it four stars – that means, on an average it feels better than Disreali Gears. No "Sunshine of Your Love" here of course, but "White Room" is a classic too, "Passing the Time" and "Pressed Rat and Warthog" are really interesting psychedelic experimentations, "As You Said", "Those Were the Days" and "Deserted Cities of the Heart" being simply fine. Of course there's a couple of useless electric blues covers ("Sitting on Top of the World", "Born Under a Bad Sign"), and "Politician" is actually quite dull stuff. There may be better songs on Disraeli Gears, but it also contains more tracks to skip, say. Except if you count the live album in. Oops!
