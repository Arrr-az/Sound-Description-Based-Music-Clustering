Geez, I wish I'd been exposed to this album earlier in my life. I think I would have really been into it in high school and college.

The Cure were a little under the radar for me back in the early to mid eighties. I knew their name. I knew goth kids listened to them. Goth kids wore black, smoked clove cigarettes and hung out in coffee shops and pizza parlors in Salem, Oregon. Well, not just any pizza parlor. Old Town Pizza. It was kind of a hip place. They sold Italian sodas, Samuel Adams beer and cappuccinos. I worked there for about six months when I was in college. I was listening to R.E.M., Echo and the Bunnymen and The Smiths back then, you'd think The Cure would've fit nicely into that cluster. For whatever reason, I never really heard them until the late eighties. My first Cure album was Kiss Me Kiss Me Kiss Me. I was playing in a band with a bunch of Cure fans and they turned me onto to them. Even then, I wasn't listening to Faith. I liked Kiss Me Kiss Me Kiss Me and Disintegration and a few years later Wish, but it wasn't until maybe ten years ago that I started to look at the Cure's back-catalog.

My loss.

The sound of Faith is dark and moody, but it doesn't give up on hooks. Everything about it is simple. The bass lines are simple. The guitar parts are simple. The keyboard parts are simple. The vocals are simple. As always, the Cure takes these simple elements and creates something complex and interesting to listen to. There's a rich, dark atmosphere that envelopes Faith. The sound is rough and raw, a little like U2's Boy but moodier.

Side one is definitely the stronger of the two sides. "The Holy Hour" aptly defines the tone of the entire record. "Primary" is an up-tempo song that somehow stills seems to brood, likely due to the dark tones of the guitars and bass. It's followed by "Other Voices", which is one of my favorite songs by the band. Again, in "Other Voices" a bunch of simple parts are pieced together into something much bigger than the sum of the parts. Smith's liberal use of echo on his voice definitely adds to the overall murkiness of the tone. "All Cats Are Grey" closes out the side, and it's another Cure classic. A keyboard part that would probably have sounded cheesy just about anywhere else works some serious magic on the song. Side one would have been a five star EP for me.

Side two is another story. The keyboards, which were low-key and atmospheric on side one, are out in front leading off the back side. Unfortunately, the corniness that they eluded in "All Cats Are Grey" swamps "The Funeral Party". The wheels just fall completely off the wagon on this track. "Doubt" is a rocker without a lot of charm, but it marks the end of "The Funeral Party", so it's not all bad. Mood and credibility are regained by the final two tracks, "The Drowning Man" and "Faith". Not great songs, but they bring the album back around to the tone of the first side and aptly close out the album.

Like I said, I wish I'd heard this album in the early eighties. There's no doubt it would've been one of my favorite records from the era. As it is I can definitely enjoy it, but it's from a time gone by.

 ArtificialEnergy Apr 30 2013
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Well, it looks like their flirtation with a more symphonic brand of post-punk proved to be short-lived - once again, and not for the first time, The Cure's personnel changed after their first keyboardist Matthieu Hartley quit the band due to incompatible differences between him and Robert Smith, because he didn't like the band's shift towards moody and suicidal music and Smith wanted him to play single notes rather than complex chords, and neither could be persuaded to the contrary. So, it's back to their power trio format and a leaner and more compact sound - ah well, nice while it lasted.

Actually, don't be fooled. This album continues and refines the advances of its predecessor with the band attempting to carve and establish a clear sonic identity, through which this time it wholly succeeds. Seemingly unperturbed by Hartley's departure with him simply taking over on keyboards, Faith confirms Smith's assumption of an autocratic role having attained more creative control on Seventeen Seconds - this is his band, The Cure, and as the lead singer, multi-instrumentalist, principal songwriter and lyricist, his albums would continue to bear the stamp of his artistic personality, even those credited musically to all band members. Moralists who believe in the rock band as a proponent of democratic values, team building and bonding, interactive group spirit, and unique and relatable personalities may deplore this kind of instability, egotism not to mention cavalier attitude towards fellow musicians that included long term friends of his, but when it comes to achieving accomplished musical product, why should anyone care about this? Great music is rarely if ever achieved by being Mr Nice Guy and being prepared to compromise your ideas all the time, and this album succeeds in delivering this precisely through Smith's vision, integrity and unwavering belief (or, appropriately, "faith") in his ability to realize and justify his thoughts in compellingly strong musical form, whatever obstacles had fallen his way (and still been successfully overcome).

Because, right from the arresting eight note bass line that opens The Holy Hour, it's immediately apparent that we are hearing the work of a master, as something smarter and still more substantial. Faith throughout breathes a greater air of confidence, depth, coherence and dynamism, scaling unprecedented heights of textural beauty through its eight, this time all vocal, numbers, with often long drawn out and sparse instrumental passages but without any of the padding and somewhat facile spooky moods that had occasionally marred its predecessor. In addition to a number of reasons already alluded to if not overtly stated above, a bigger recording budget was clearly a factor; having broken into the top 40 on both the album and singles charts with Seventeen Seconds and A Forest and embarked on their first world tour in 1980, this enabled them to afford much more time in the recording studio - two months as opposed to merely a week - to put their songs together in a more finished and fully conceived manner. The title and the lyrics of many of its songs suggest a loose concept album, as an existential meditation on the purpose of faith amidst the absurdities and trials and tribulations of life, concentrating still more on dark, to the point of plain disturbing (Doubt) and often abstruse subject matter with subtle and obscure literary allusions, infused with a melancholy, tending towards tragic, nostalgia. This is unquestionably monochromatic music, with the kind of "shades of grey" subconsciously prophesized in one of the more characteristic songs in Three Imaginary Boys, Another Day, in addition to sparkling white, pitch black and occasional splodges of brown, but like with Joy Division in the best possible sense, of a kind of beauty that evokes classic romantic black and white films, foggy winter landscapes, the moonlit countryside, frozen waterfalls, old ruins and ornate architectural exteriors. And altogether it's an album of quite startling maturity; lest we forget, the band members were still very young, with Smith and Tolhurst being just 21 and Gallup only 20 during the recording sessions.

The brooding and almost religious sounding Holy Hour signals the album's serious intent, continuing Seventeen Seconds' prominent use of flanged guitar, distant plainchant-like synthesizer melodies, insistent snare drum beats, long but never dull instrumental intros, with Smith's desperate and despairing vocals pushed noticably back in the mix and a haunting tolling bell at the start and end. Like so much of the rest of Faith, the lyrical writing is brief and free flowing with no clearly defined verse and chorus structure; the only time we actually hear the title's words are towards the end, but they are sung with such power and conviction that it feels like a proper chorus, resounding in the listener's head even after the album is over. The more uptempo Primary, the album's sole single and the second of The Cure's to chart but which this time failed to crack the top 40, isn't quite as impressive as The Forest, but it provides a much needed contrast of pace and some added aggression, with some marvellous oscillating quasi-harmonic effects through more flanged guitar over a pulsating rhythm section with its most memorable hook provided by its disquieting echoed "Further we go/And older we grow/The more we know/The less we show". The sadder and quite scary Other Voices sees the album progressing towards the more grandiose production and richer textures that reach their culmination on The Funeral Party, with the drums and especially vocals given more reverb, adding a psychological layer of paranoia and horror that wouldn't be surpassed until the closing stages.

However, the greatest is reserved for the middle two songs, All Cats Are Grey and the magisterial The Funeral Party, which after A Forest are the finest songs The Cure had written to this point. It's hard to decide which of the two is more masterly; the latter is more immediate, richer and more awe-inspiring, but the former is subtler and more moving in the long term, with a wealth of quietly intricate yet understated detail which continues to reveal itself over multiple listens. Inspired by the fantasy of manners Gormenghast series by Mervin Peake, All Cats Are Grey creates its enigmatic and otherworldly aura through its combination of a hypnotically pattering circular drum pattern, its sublimely warm synthesizer line conjuring up a wonderfully ambivalent mood in its neither quite major nor minor tonality, its pithy and vaguely impressionistic lyrics well complemented by the added sonic layer of Smith's perfectly timed and distantly delivered vocal entry over two minutes in (which never feels that long, such is the spell-binding nature of the music), and with the music becoming more stripped down and desolate towards the end, the profoundly haunting outro with the tolling piano having the final word. The Funeral Party, written about the death of Smith's grandparents, is as evocative as the title suggests, conjuring up the stately magnificence of a gothic (no pun intended) cathedral, or perhaps the beautiful ruins of Bolton Priory from which the album front cover was taken, its majestic hymn-like melody strongly suggestive of Smith having recently attended a funeral or some other religious service which had an inspirational impact on him, with a sound that looks forward to the gorgeous and lushly layered textures of Disintegration a full eight years on.

Then with Doubt, a kind of evil twin brother to Primary and the most obvious precursor to the more tortured and demonic world of Pornography, we are rudely awakened into the more overt emotional turmoil of Smith's inner world - with lines like "Fury drives my vicious blows/I see you fall but still I strike you", "Screaming through myself in fury/Over the edge and into your blood" and "Knowing I'll murder you again tonight" you have to wonder just what nightmares or real life personal problems had tormented him to the extent of inspiring him to write such a grisly song. Yet whether sincerely felt or faked, it's convincingly and powerfully enough performed, especially vocally, to make it compellingly believable without being ham-fisted, offensive or just plain laughable, thus avoiding the trap that so many lesser rock artists working in the same vein fall into, with an especially catchy wordless vocal chant two minutes in. The closing two songs see the album becoming gradually more introspective, forlorn and bereft of all hope (except perhaps faith), with the chromatically descending harmonic progression of the obsessive rhythm guitar and the schizophrenic dynamics and omnipresent nature of Smith's vocal, rebounding all over the studio, of The Drowning Man establishing its despairing mood. If perhaps not the greatest song on the album, the title track certainly represents the emotional kernel, with Smith's voice barely ever rising above a desolate moan but all the more profoundly effective for it, accentuating the deliberately robotic drums and the despondent bass and lead guitar lines that crawl up and down without appearing to want to go anywhere, leading to harrowing and increasingly mournful repetitions of the line "Nothing left but faith" - yet another supreme example of The Cure's genius for deeply affecting and resonant outros - not until Sinking would we see another Cure song that matches, let alone exceeds this, in its dark poignancy.

So, The Cure's third album less than three years into their official recording career, and their first masterpiece. Is there room for improvement? For sure in light of what follows, but it's hard to discern just how this could have been bettered; if there are any very slight nitpicks to be made, compared to the two leading contemporaneous albums of the post-punk genre, Wire's 154 and Joy Division's Closer, it just lacks the colour, diversity and pop sensibility of the former and the gut-wrenching plunge into the emotional abyss of the latter - The Cure still seem more concerned at this stage with mood and atmosphere than readily assimilable hooks, and Disintegration would prove consistently that the three were by no means mutually incompatible. It's not an album for everyone, as it does require some measure of undivided attention and patience for a certain state of mind - a record to be enjoyed by those who appreciate the album as a self-contained work of art with its subtly variegated layers and timbres and its predominantly dark but certainly not monotonous sentiment, to be turned up fairly loud to let its beautiful sonic textures slowly envelop the listener. But it's a wonderful, fascinating and rewarding listen that is capable of leaving a deep impression on anyone in sympathy with The Cure's early output or this genre of music in general.

All credit to The Cure for making being glum into an art form. Of course no-one at the time called this 'Goth'. I doubt I even heard that term before 1983. But with The Cure's albums of 1980 and 1981, the popularity of Siouxsie and The Banshees, Bauhaus, Joy Division and, on a lighter note, The Cramps, there sure was something melancholic and morbid in the air in early 80s Britain. Hip kids of the time thought a good night out was reading Kafka novels by a full moon in the local cemetry (and that's a fog-bound Bolton Abbey pictured on the LP sleeve in case you were wondering). Had i-Pods been around Faith would have been the perfect soundtrack to some late night crypt kicking (actually they were; we called them Sony Walkmans).

Faith starts on cue with the toll of church bells and ends with the line "I went away alone with nothing left but faith" and in between you get, er 'party favourites' such as "The Drowning Man" and "The Funeral Party". Bring your own razor blades. There's a continuation here of the dysfunctional mood music of their previous album Seventeen Seconds, only to these ears the tracks on Faith have more sophistication and contrasts and a nice atmospheric use of layers of synth. This time the music is more emotionally engaging. There's a haunting sadness to it.

Although Faith sees The Cure sleepwalking further into depression, it was the album that followed, Pornography, that was the real dark one. There's no epic here like "A Forest" on Seventeen Seconds but to me Faith is a more satisfying listen; I can hear the progression. And yes the music is mournful but it doesn't actually drag you down. I was 17 when this came out and, along with a mountain of similar musical miserablism, it chimed perfectly with my self-pitying adolescent self. Now I'm older and with real reasons to be angst-ridden listening to Faith is almost comforting, like a soothing, slow motion aural soma. Odd how that The Cure can be a real tonic.

Until recently, very few people had heard this piece, or even knew it existed. It was written as the soundtrack to an animated short film Simon Gallups brother Ric directed. This short was used in lieu of a support band for the Picture Tour in 1981, and could only be found on the B side of the Faith cassette tape release.

I stumbled across it a number of years ago in a friends collection. I asked him about it and he gave me the back-story, and needless to say I was intrigued. Fortunately for me, I had the capability to do a tape rip and burn to CD (ah, the good ol days). At the time I didnt dig it too much (but then, at the time I didnt dig Pornography either, and now its one of my all time favorite albums). It was too minimal and too repetitive.

Fast forward a few years, and I again stumbled across the song, this time as an MP3 in my music library. Curious to see if it was as boring as I remembered, I put it on while reading. This time through though, it really clicked. I realized there is a lot more going on under the surface of this piece than is readily apparent. While it is indeed minimal and repetitive, there are a plethora of subtleties and hypnotic textures present that seem to just pull you in and hold you under. This is quite the sinister little composition. Bleak, dark, stark and I can only imagine that the film meant to accompany it was equally unnerving (something to do with toys and inanimate objects), though I still have not been able to track it down to see for myself.

Alas, my humble tape rip is no longer a rarity, as Carnage Visors was released on the recent Faith remastered deluxe package, and easily available to the masses. And while Im sure the sonic integrity is much improved, Id rather listen to my own dubious rip, quality be damned. There are some things that are better when left in obscurity, if for no other reason than to preserve their mystique. The forgotten and unknown often possess peculiar sway when discovered. Thats the way this was for me. I hope thats the way it is for you as well.
