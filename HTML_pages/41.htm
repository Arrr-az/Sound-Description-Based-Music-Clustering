When Surf's Up was released in 1971, it got the best all-round reviews of The Beach Boys' career. Some 35 years later, it feels a whole lot more problematic to me. Yes, there's no denying it's a damn fine record: more ambitious and more adult than Sunflower, more in tune with its times. There's also no denying, however, that a lot of its finest moments aren't exactly new: they're more Smile outtakes, dusted off and revamped. And the album as a whole is kind of uneven (a word I reach for a lot when reviewing Beach Boys albums).

Let's start with the good: the legendary "Surf's Up" is one of Brian's most haunting songs, and "Til I Die" is just a shade less fine. "A Day In The Life Of A Tree" is a slice of weirdness that could only have come from Mr Wilson's pen, and is rather endearing. While Dennis is strangely absent, Carl's "Feel Flows" and "Long Promised Road" are two really great songs that show the sort of directions a more united Beach Boys could have moved in. "Don't Go Near The Water" is light breezy fun too.

Now for the bad: On "Disney Girls (1957)", Bruce Johnston dives straight into the schmaltz bucket for something that Barry Manilow might have thought too saccharine. And as a response to the Kent State killings, "Student Demonstration Time" is trite at best, if not insulting ("Ohio" it's not). "Lookin' At Tomorrow" is just plain dull, and "Take A Load Off Your Feet" stupid.

Ah, those fabled swings and roundabouts. Beware the bad stuff, but the good stuff is just so good -- pretty much essential -- that it's worth braving the bad stuff for.

I have always considered Surf’s Up to be the best Beach Boys studio album of the 1970s. Surf’s Up may not have the consistent production wizardry of Sunflower nor its pleasant flowing feel but it is a better fit for the societal background against which it was released. It also has an edgier feel and tends to rock a bit more in places. Whatever you’re feelings about Sunflower vs. Surf’s Up, it would be the last consistently excellent Beach Boys studio album.

The Vietnam War and the resulting protest movement, plus the fact that Led Zeppelin and like groups were taking music far away from the Beach Boys sound and image, would cause Surf’s Up to be another commercial failure. This was a Beach Boys release that definitely deserved better from the buying public.

There are only two songs that are out of place on Surf’s Up. “Don’t Go Near The Water” and “Take Good Care Of Your Feet” both have good production but lightweight lyrics. “Don’t Go Near The Water” is a song about conservation and “Take Good Care Of Your Feet” is about health. It’s nice that some of the Beach Boys were developing a social consciousness but there were a lot more important issues around in 1971. These songs were naive and simplistic in 1971 and today are just odd relics.

Carl Wilson contributes two excellent, if somewhat depressing, songs to Surf’s Up. “Long Promised Road” features a fine lead vocal by Carl that runs counterpoint to the harmonies. It contains sophisticated lyrics about life’s struggles and perseverance and are some of the best Carl Wilson would write. “Feel Flows” has a soulful, spiritual feel to it. The vocals ride along on a combination of instruments that almost defy description.

Bruce Johnston contributed three pretty but inconsequential songs on Sunflower. Here Bruce Johnston creates the song of his career. “Disney Girls” is one of the best pop songs of all time. This is a song that transports the listener to times that have passed. It evokes an emotional response to those times. When this song was released in 1971 I would think of my childhood and adoring such Disney icons as Hayley Mills and Annette. The song has held up through the years as now two more generations can think back upon their “Disney Girls.” A laid back lead vocal by Johnston set against gentle harmonies explore a long gone world that is now only fantasy. Student Demonstration Time” is either a song that you love or hate. Personally, I have always liked this song. Mike Love took the music from “Riot In Cell Block Number 9” and re-wrote the words to fit the chaos of the early 70s. The Beach Boys use brass and rock a little harder than usual here.

Brian Wilson reaches back to Smile for the last three songs on the album. “A Day In The Life Of A Tree” is underpinned by a somber dirge like organ. Brian Wilson’s vocals float above harmonies as he sings about life passing by. “Til I Die” is another depressing song about death and killing of the soul, but is hidden in muted harmonies. “Surf’s Up” is a brilliant creation where a simple piano note sets up the song structure. The use of textured harmonies and lyrics only serve as another reminder of how unique a creative Smile would have been.

Surf’s Up may have been a product of its time but it continues to hold up musically 37 years later. Few albums can make that claim.

Most post-Pet Sounds albums were kind of a mess due to the vacuum left by Brian Wilson. Surf's Up is the companion to the bright and optimistic Sunflower.

The album has a few environmentalist songs like
Don't Go Near the Water and A Day in the Life of a Tree, which are both pretty good actually. The latter was sung by Jack Rieley, their manager, on suggestion of Brian Wilson because, why not, this is still better than being co-produced by your psychiatrist I guess.

This album also has two fantastic songs by Carl Wilson, Long Promised Road and Feel Flows, the first songs he ever wrote for the group. He writes much in the style of Brian (both were blessed with perfect pitch) and deliver interesting harmonies and vocal lines.

Take a Load Off Your Feet stands in the tradition of Brian Wilson's health songs but was mostly the brainchild of Al Jardine.

Bruce Johnston provides the FANTASIC Disney Girls, a slow baroque pop ballad that stands as one of the best songs on the albums, and overall one of the best non-Brian Beach Boys songs. And oh man, I love his voice here and the nostalgia.

Overall most songs every very somber and melancholic. This album is a nice group effort, albeit with a lack of Dennis Wilson who was more present on Sunflower. The Boys worked well as a true band, and they had talented songwriters that tended to be overshadowed by Brian Wilson's once-in-a-century talent.

Of course, we have to mention the worst song which is Mike Love's Leiber-Stoller garage rock Student Demonstration Time, a strange and violent song that breaks the mood of the record and is painfully shallow. This song further tarnishes the reputation of Mike Love, the most hated Beach Boy, and yes, for every Big Sur, this man gave us a hundred Student Demonstration Times.

But the best songs of the album sit at the end, and both are among Brian Wilson's masterpieces.

'Til I Die is easily one of Brian Wilson's best songs, but also his darkest. It's a thinly veiled suicide note about feeling insignificant and powerless facing the might of oceans, the roughness of land and winds. It also has a phenomenal composition, and wave-like jazzy chord changes that stand among Wilson's most original movements. Its instrumentation around a psychedelic vibraphone and a powerful organ provides a backdrop for the Boys' amazing vocals.

It closes with the SMiLE leftover Surf's Up, an enigmatic and complex piece with incredible vocals by Carl Wilson. It may not be as emotionally powerful as Til I Die, but the three-piece composition is breathtaking, around dissonant chords, and a spellbinding fugue-like ending.

Despite these UNBELIEVABLE masterpieces at the end of this album, it suffers from the same inconsistency as many Beach Boys albums. It would also be the moment Brian Wilson moved largely out of the picture until the mid-1970s when it gets really wild.

7/10

Just because I've always thought Pet Sounds was overrated doesn't mean I don't think it's a great album. It is. I just don't think it's as spectacular as everybody else does. But then there's Surf's Up, an album many Beach Boys fans think of as their best post-Pet Sounds long-player. Now it's just plain overrated because it's NOT VERY GOOD. Coming after the delightful and underappreciated Sunflower, Surf's Up shows the Beach Boys falling back on some of their weakest tendencies: beyond-corny novelty songs ("Take a Load Off Your Feet," "A Day in the Life of a Tree," the well-meaning but horribly dated "Student Demonstration Time"), a fascination with state-of-the-art musical technology that often undermines the more powerful tracks (see "Long Promised Road," probably the best song on the album, which is almost overwhelmed by the dated keyboard solo during the coda), and an embrace of tranquil musical patterns, ones that eerily foreshadow the nadir of late 1970s pop music: Adult Contemporary. The final two cuts on the album are probably the highlights. "'Til I Die" is a beautiful song, and one of the group's best at placing the listener on an instant vacation. "Surf's Up" is always going to be disappointing to those familiar with the solo version Brian Wilson cut during the Smile sessions (which, for my money, is the best Brian Wilson/Beach Boys performance). Despite these unfair comparisons, the updated arrangement, complete with Carl Wilson on vocals, a sharp horn arrangement, and an assortment of chimes, the track is still fairly breathtaking in its hallucinatory, Wordsworthian embrace of Romantic natural solitude. Listeners interested in the arc of the Beach Boys' music in the 1970s will find this of absolute interest, but the album is more of a chore to listen to than it is rewarding.

After a stream of albums that ranged from heavy experimentation with synths to classic rock, The Beach Boys arrived back in a room of ghosts the brothers' couldn't shake off. Cocaine and trampled dreams have finally become mutual things amongst the band members and there was no place left to look except back. The heaviness and desperation for something better can be heard all over the record, as a haze of drugs and depression swirl around pop of the most magical qualities. All the band have a voice here, even Mike Love attempts a hokey classic rock track ("Student Demonstration Time") and Brian comes out of the sheets to make the most mysterious song of the record ("A Day in the Life of a Tree"). The greatest surprise here, though, is Carl Wilson; While he is certainly the voice behind the Beach Boy's greatest songs, Brian was always the writer. Out of nowhere Carl Wilson writes two of the darkest, most vivid songs of the 70s that have become lost in time and, of course, get no recognition from non-Beach Boys fans. Over the most incredible use of synths in a pop song, perhaps ever, Carl Wilson confronts his inner demons of childhood and losing connection to his brothers. "Long Promised Road" asks time if wounds can ever be healed, and when time says "yes" Carl denies it to himself in "Feel Flows". If this album was only those 2 songs, I'd have to say its a contender for greatest single ever recorded in the 70s but we also have 8 other tracks that range in style and substance. Perhaps Pet Sounds will always be the favorite because it hit all the right notes in ways expected and ways unexpected, so it's understandable why Surf's Up won't get the same amount of love as it only hits on the unexpected ways. This is the Wilson brothers suicide note that they never made good on (Thank God!)

Some excellent songs including some of the greatest things the band has ever done, namely Brian's contributions at the end, but a bit inconsistent. There were a few Dennis songs that should have been on here that weren't because of band politics which is a shame. 4th of July and ...Live Again we're done during this period and would have fit the album perfectly.

The stuff I'm not too crazy about: I am not a fan of Disney Girls. It's saccharine and overwrought and not the biggest fan of the melody. It's not terrible though and still a decent tune. Like a lot of other fans, Student Demonstration Time I feel is very weak and lame as well. At the time they needed something a bit louder and heavier to compliment their live shows, so I get why it was decided to put it on here, but it doesn't work and just drags the album down. Take A Load Off Your Feet is really silly but ultimately fun and catchy. Kind of like the stuff on the Friends album. Very lightweight, silly, cheesy but fun, catchy and the best of what sunshine pop of the era offers.

But Til I Die and the title track are absolutely brilliant songs. Of course the title track comes from Smile and was finished by Carl and the other guys for this album and it came out sublime. A Day In The Life Of A Tree is underrated and has made me tear up. I prefer to think of it as a cry for help from Brian ("there's nothing left for me".. it comes off like a poetic suicide note. God damn is it heavy) but Brian didn't write the lyrics and it was intended as an ecological disaster kind of song. Either way it's gorgeous and sad as hell. Til I Die is the actual cry for help from Brian and it is very beautiful but also very heartbreaking.

Then you have Carl's songs which are really good and fit the era well. Don't Go Near The Water has silly lyrics but instrumentally and melodically it's a bit of a beauty as well. The instrumental version on the Made In California box set shows some depth in the production and it is a well arranged song. Al contributes a really cool light psych folk Looking At Tomorrow which I also find very underrated in their catalog.

Ultimately an excellent album that has some of the best songs of the post-Smile era but could have been better as well which is slightly disappointing. Either way, I like the slightly darker sound here and the ambitious sounds of several songs here are unlike anything they would ever do again apart from some of Holland a few years later.
