Happily playing hookey today, sitting around listening to _Hex_ for renewed pleasure and geeky evaluation instead of laboring in the economic trenches.

I've always rated _Hex_ tops among the studio albums alongside _Grotesque_, a hair ahead of _Dragnet_ and _Slates_, and leagues beyond anything from 1982 onward. But while _Grotesque_ has always seemed to me the more immediate and accessible of the two (relatively speaking, though there's plenty of grating fangy aggression on _Grotesque_ too) , I sometimes wonder if _Hex_ is as electrifying as I remember it, or if I'm swayed by the popular opinion favoring it, plus the fact it was among the first Fall albums I heard.

"The Classical" and "Jawbone and the Air Rifle" make me think I was right. This _is_ their best. The noisy, viciously rambling, darkly hewn fragments couch compelling nightmares. "Jawbone" is a noisy Grimm's Fairy Tale on a damp northern European afternoon treading through marshes, a goblet of grog in one hand, a hunting rifle in the other. Smith's voice roams violently between supercilious and psychotic.

The sarcasm of "Hip Priest," the putdown character sketches in "Fortress/Deer Park," the hostility of "Mere Pseud" are followed by the faux-balladic, dreary-by-prescription "Winter," which drags you out into the compassionless cold of its storyline with it for nine tortuous minutes.

"Just Step S'ways" is nearly a hit. "The Eastern Bloc rocks to Elton John." Heh.

"Who makes the Nazis": "Remember when I used to follow you home from school, babe? Before I got picked up for paedophelia." Like a felony version of Frankie Valli's radio travesty "My Eyes adored yuh," which whimpered "carried your books from school, playin' make believe you're married to me."

Then, to be humbled in "Iceland":

"And the spawn of the volcano
Is thick and impatient
Like the people around it."

200,000+ Icelanders just got dissed.

"And this Day" thuds the curtain down on our antiheroes for ten agonizing minutes. A tepid background synth sounds like the Zombies under a jackboot of percussion and a frontal assault of ad libbed shouts.

So then, what sets _Hex_ apart from other Fallaciousness? On the surface, it's not _that_ different from dozens of other Fall albums. But the narratives Smith grumblegabs and wobbleweaves, and the putdowns and complaints he registers, are more focused and tightly wound than ever. The martial drumming, the splinterjab, late Beefheart guitars and warehouse arrangements wrap the package with such nastiness a pitbull wouldn't venture. It's the Fall album that most compactly defines what blisses me about the band---paradoxically, I suppose, since it sprawls like a sewage spill.

_Hex Enduction Hour_ is the rambling pastiche of a stand-up tragedian refreshingly lacking in caution or perspective. While rock abounds with predators, the Fall are the alpha scavengers of the medium, fulfilling a vital organic function on the foodchain of rock as Marquee Smith picks the flesh off the swollen carcasses of decomposing rock mythology with his vicious beak. If rock is the carrion of music, _Hex_ is the baddest buzzard circling in a gray sky overhead, unsentimentally devouring leftovers while gale winds pummel and erode the rock on which the stenchy corpse of a once-vibrant art form lies.

"This is the home of the vain! This is the home of the vain! Where are the obligatory n*****s? Hey there f**kface! Hey there f**kface!"

No false promises on the cover; no misleading lyrics on the first track. From the first second of looking at and listening to this album you know what it's about: one hour of music, and a couple of slaps in your face every minute. As to what "Hex Enduction" means, well, have a bleedin guess, but once you have been induced into this album, there is no way you could not appreciate the greatness of The Fall, even though Mark E. Smith claims that "he is not appreciated". But he is the hip priest alright.

This is The Fall's legendary two-drummers, two-bassists line-up, including the extra-scratchy guitars of Marc Riley and Craig Scanlon, and Mark E. Smith in ber-epic mode as he recounts tales of unprecedented strangeness, like the tale of the rabbit killer who damages a gravestone and is subsequently haunted by visions of jawbones, or the secrets about who makes the Nazis (they are longhorn breed!). All the time he is so low down in the mix that you can barely hear him, but of course he is because what's really important here is Steve Hanley's hypnotic bass, which carries most of the songs in conjunction along with Scanlon and Riley. Listen to what they are doing on "Winter". Try not to start dancing as they dive into "The Classical". Try not to be mesmerized by "Hip Priest".

Hard to believe that this is music from the synth-drenched, overproduced 1980s. There are few albums from 1982 that are as raw, as energetic and as guitar/bass/drum-charged as The Fall's all-out barrage against, well, everything. There are few albums as completely timeless and as consistently appealing as Hex, which is certainly a milestone of British rock music.

"When what used to excite you does not, like you've used up all your allowance of experiences."

Hex Enduction Hour continues to excite me 20 years after I first listened to it.

This is it. This is the one Fall album you should get your hands on.

Well, that's not necessarily true. Is it the band's best album? Yes. Their defining statement? Not so much. Part of the beauty of The Fall is that in its own way each album is the band's defining statement since for all intents and purposes the band never stays the same two albums in a row. Mark E. Smith goes through bandmates like us mere mortals go through t-shirts: keep 'em around 'til they're not useful anymore and throw em away. So Hex Enduction Hour is this incarnation of The Fall's defining statement.

And what a statement it is...it's easy to see why this didn't set the world on fire back in '82, what with being released on a tiny label, featuring that trademark Smith rant style and vicious attacks on everyone and everything and sounding so far removed from the whole (post) punk scene, but it's a killer of an album. 60 or so minutes (the title doesn't lie) of The Fall im their prime. Well, at least this version of the Fall in their prime. The pre-Brix, pre-semi pop success Fall in their prime.

Recent re-listens of the album have just solidified how unlike anything else released around 1982 this album must have been. At its heart it's punk, that much is certain the minute Smith opens his mouth, but with the exception of "Fortress", "Just Step S'Ways" and "Mere Pseud. Mag. Ed." the guitars are almost incidental. The album's carried by the most formidable rhythm section The Fall ever housed, specifically bassist Steve Hanley who's the instrumental focal point for nearly every song. Even the aforementioned guitar-led ones have a huge bass presence, and as "Fortress" fades into the band's secret best song "Deer Park" you can hear the guitar just get quiet all of a sudden while Steve's bass stays pumped up. That's not to say that the album's all Steve's show (instrumentally). The drummers (yes, that's right, two of em plus an auxiliary percussionist/kazoo player) keep everything moving along nicely, and when he get a chance to shine guitarist Craig Scanlon is fantastic. Even when he's relegated to providing color to the bass-dominated songs he's doing some interesting things in the background, and those odd chords he comes through with at the end of "The Classical" and the huge, twangy guitar bit towards the end of "Deer Park" are some of the album's best moments (instrumentally).

But regardless of all this, you can't take the spotlight away from Mark E. Smith for too long. He's in fine form here, both vocally and lyrically, though especially the latter. From the moment in "The Classical" where he bursts forth with the most famous line on any Fall album ever (you can read it in someone else's review, undoubtedly) to the final staccato "There's no fucking respect (respite?) for us" on "And This Day" it's Mark E.'s show and the band are just window dressing. Very nice window dressing, but still. But it's not just Smith's oblique outbursts and rhetorical questions ("Who Makes the Nazis?" has so many answers to the titular question that it becomes a moot point) that make it his show, it's little things like his voice breaking partway through "Deer Park", the wobbly falsetto on "Hip Priest", the almost subliminal "Remember when I used to walk you home from school? Before they picked me up for paedophilia?" on "Who Makes the Nazis?". It's these little nuances that make this album particularly solid for ol' Mr. Smith.

I've already said that this is the on Fall album you absolutely need, but it bears repeating. As inconsistent as they are, The Fall pulled it all out for this album, creating what I'd call without hesitation the absolute pinnacle of post-punk and best album of the 80s. Is it an acquired taste? Certainly, but chances are if you're still reading this review you're already halfway to loving it.

To my ears, despite the 30-odd years since their formation, and possibly because of their constant personnel flux (or fan bias), no two Fall albums sound alike. While that is really just an awkward restatement of a certain clichd DJ quotation, I've only ever found it to be true. As such, there's not much of a consensus as to their great work - ask a group of Fall fans their favourite release and every one will likely be mentioned, in varying proportions. There are consistencies between groups of albums, sure, but each part of those 'eras' is itself a unique vignette of The Fall at that point in time. It's been said that the truly classic albums have a story behind them (eg. Trout Mask Replica, Paranoid, etc); with The Fall, the albums are the stories, chapters in a fractious, unreliable running autobiography. For example: Live at the Witch Trials is the death of the original, more punk, more democratic Fall; Dragnet documents their rebirth as Mark E. Smith's musical vehicle, an altogether stranger entity; and so on.

Hex Enduction Hour both has and is a story (whether or not the two match is another matter); for that, it's in contention for the title of The Fall's 'classic' album. So the tale goes: Fruitless touring, at best nonchalant audiences, and a general lack of recognition had taken their toll on The Fall. At odds with the industry (then championing torpid new wave) and the times (those of the better-forgotten New Romantics), Smith and his ragtag band of teenage acolytes got to work on what was meant to be the final album. The result was Hex Enduction Hour; an apt 60 minutes and 8 seconds of furious invective, caustic wit, and surreal horror, told by a Salfordian with a funny voice over the world's greatest pub band.

All the gushing praise in the world cannot fetter a simple truth: You'll know whether or not you're a Fall fan about a minute or two into "The Classical". Somewhere, in the abrasive murk brought on by the guitar's entrance; between the giant bassline, the confusing stereo drumkits, and that infamous caricature of contrived racial diversity; therein shall you find your judgment of Hex. Then the initial vitriolic spit breaks down, Smith mocks the 'new profile razor unit', and hails "The Classical" into its ironically anthemic refrain. Often hailed (justly) as their masterpiece, it's a trial-by-fire opener that is its own reward. One of those songs you yell to the sky while walking home, alone at night, blotto on cheap eastern European beer.

Perhaps due to Smith's insistence on playing the new tracks live (the issue with fired-soon-after guitarist Marc Riley was that "he wanted to play the hits"), Hex sounds considerably tighter, less ramshackle, than the previous albums. The Hanley/Hanley/Burns rhythm section are the vanguard of the sound; Hanley's bass carrying the riffs among the two drummers' co-operative motorik. The chief function of the guitars is textural thickening, like aural chicken fat in Hex's sonic milkshake*. I remember reading that The Fall and Michael Clark attempted an arrangement of "The Classical" for orchestra, thwarted by the song only having one chord throughout. Less ramshackle, maybe, but just as primitive. That's The Fall, though - repetitive sound canvas for free-verse frontman.

Of course, the lyrics and their inimitable MES delivery are the main draw of this album, and of the band in general. I don't think anybody has honed the art of insulting people in song form as perfectly as Smith. "Mere Pseud Mag. Ed." is the ultimate diss track; boasting a slew of scathing observational put-downs ("Heard Ramones in '81 / has a Spanish guitar"), evocative nonsense ("decadent sandwich quaff"), and a great yelp moment ("Twice each at LEAST!") - rappers, take note. "Iceland" seems introspective, a novel approach from the hitherto unromantic Fall (demonstrated best on Grotesque's, uh, grotesque "In the Park"). The narrative approach of the two albums prior is here downsized, Smith instead opting for cryptic free-association as social commentary. "Jawbone and the Air-Rifle", with its Twilight Zone tale of a poacher driven mad by the cursed titular object, could've been a track on Grotesque. "Winter", on the other hand, is a brilliantly surreal melding of the two styles, and I have no idea what it's about. That's half the fun. Maybe it's not meant to mean anything, doesn't need to; it was good enough for Captain Beefheart. Those seeking concrete meaning in Hex are referred to a choice phrase on the album cover (which, incidentally, may be the best Fall album art - by no means a grand achievement).

I've wittered on long enough; you want to know if it's worth the google.

  • 'Is it a good album?' It's a Fall album.

  • 'Is it their best album?' It's not my favourite release of theirs, but I think it's their most important release. It's a fin-de-sicle Fall LP**; it represents the culmination of an era that started with Dragnet, three years and as many albums prior; it has "The Classical", the superlative Fall jam, on it; and it's the path of second-least resistance, after This Nation's Saving Grace. Every Fall fan loves it, even some non-fans like it.

  • 'Is it deserving of its praise?' Mostly.

* Tenuous AND gross. Apologies!
** Room to Live is an anomaly, without era in the Hex-Brix hinterland.
