The rest of the Foo Fighters discography can go foo fuck itself, but I have room in my heart for this one. It's prime 90s alternative rock, full of melody, buzzing like a bee, and deep in debt to the Pixies. It's from before the Foo Fighters' singles started to sound like the soft-rock stuff that gets piped into Walgreen's and before Dave Grohl started to look like Bob Seger. This is from way back when "they"actually this album is all Dave Grohl as a one-man-band, with one guitar guest spot from Greg Dullirocked for a whole album and Grohl looked like a horse-faced lesbian. The closest thing to a ballad is "Big Me" and it's a lot closer to power pop. The rest ranges from hyperactive punk to heavy grunge shoegazers and it all works. The band went on to be hugely popular and long-lived, but this sounds like Grohl thought it would be his one and only shot and he makes it a good one. Sleeper favorite in this house: the part-Foreigner, part-Husker Du "Alone + Easy Target".

With the demise of one of my all-time favourite bands Nirvana in 1994, I was watching the remaining members of the band like a hawk, waiting to see what they'd do, if anything. Thankfully they didn't try to keep Nirvana going, the way some other bands have kept theirs going even with the deaths or departures of lynchpins in their groups. But imagine my teenaged delight (read: excited squealing and jumping around) when I found a little article in a teeny magazine that informed me Dave Grohl had a new band...Foo Fighters! We were told that there would be no video clip for any of the singles, but soon thereafter I watched back through a tape of the Australian music show Rage and stumbled across a video for "I'll Stick Around" - cue much more girly squealing and dancing.

I saw the Foo Fighters in concert for the first time in 1996 with the Summersault Music Festival that hit Perth. It was the best day of my life at that point in time, and I bought the Foo Fighters t-shirt with the debut album cover on it (I have no idea what happened to that t-shirt, which makes me sad).

Even today, 14 years after the album's release, I still listen to this album and love almost every second of it. It wasn't quite as grunge as Nirvana had been, but I was impressed that Dave Grohl had recorded every part on the album, running from one room with drums to another with bass, and another with guitar, etc. I was also impressed by the music itself...which I would call a mixture of rock, pop and grunge-esque moments. Highlights for me would be "Alone + Easy Target", "Oh, George", "X-Static", "Exhausted", "Floaty" and "Wattershed", but overall I can listen to the album and be reminded of my teenaged days...and also of the years I spent playing Donkey Kong Country, while listening to this album.

ozzystylez Oct 17 2005

The trouble with making friends is, at some point down the road of your friendship, they will no doubt peruse your record collection and spot something that maybe they have been wanting to hear for a little while but were too lazy to go out and buy. So comes the inescapable 'friend' question, "Oh, do you mind if I borrow this? I've heard it's really rather good," to which you reply, "Why yes, you heard right. I thoroughly enjoy it and don't much like being too far away from it for too long. Sure you can borrow it, but make a copy and bring it back asap as I will miss rocking out to this beast."
"Oh of course," comes their lying, backstabbing reply. "I'll bring it back tomorrow."
Which of course, they never do.

The problem with lending out your records is that your 'friends' rarely understand the somewhat obsessive compulsive way in which you behave toward your record collection. You sometimes struggle to understand it yourself, all you know is that cracked CD cases are a constant black spot on the mind and alphabetized collections seem like a natural way of storing records, even if it does take half an hour to fit a new purchase into your CD tower. Worse still, is having a gap in the tower where once there was an album. It stays there for years, always expecting to be filled once more with it's old friend, but after five years, you realise that your 'friends' continued failure to return with the album suggests that that gap in your life may never be filled again, at least not without you digging deep into your 'record money', with which you had hoped to buy other, uncharted records as opposed to a replacement for a record you technically already own.

So I was rather overjoyed to find Foo Fighter's debut at a very reasonable price in a second hand shop earlier this year. I snatched it up, finally accepting that my original copy, which I had loved dearly as an adolescent drummer with aspirations to learn the guitar, was never going to be seen again. I remember thinking that the hole between Dave Grohl's hairy bum cheeks was the source of light and sustenance for the planet. It rocked like a mofo, it sounded a bit like Nirvana, but a lot more fun, and the man, the myth, had recorded the whole damned thing all on his lonesome.
After making the purchase, barely able to contain my hormones stored up since being a simple teenager, I slipped the shiny disc into my trusty walkman and hit play, followed quickly by bass boost.

The trouble with anticipation is that the anticipated euphoric rush isn't a guarantee, and when it doesn't come you can end up feeling let down, or sticky and dirty depending on your disposition. After the first three tracks (a great start to a record by any standards) had played through and the fourth began I started to feel that I was entering the sticky dirty phase. The old memories were there, but the music wasn't as fantastic as I remember it being. In many ways, I felt a little childish, and wondered what other music I could have bought with my hard earned cash.

The problem with Foo Fighters records is that they are predictably a mixed bag. Amongst some quality tracks (and if we're naming names "This Is A Call", "I'll Stick Around", "Big Me" and "Watershed") there are always some songs that are so unenthralling you can almost see why Kurt packed it in.

Whilst this is at the good end of the Foo Fighters' spectrum for the most part, it still feels a little adolescent now I'm bored and old (Eh, eh), but on some days I will pull this out, stand in front of a full length mirror and rock out on whatever instrument the air instrument factory has sent me that day.

Foo Fighters - Foo Fighters

Foo Fighters, the debut album by Foo Fighters, marks a significant moment in rock history, being the first major project of Dave Grohl post-Nirvana. The album shows Grohl's talent as a multi-instrumentalist and songwriter, with its blend of grunge-infused alternative rock and catchy, melodic hooks. It's an album that is both a product of its time and a harbinger of the Foo Fighters' future sound.

The album kicks off with This Is a Call, an energetic and upbeat track that immediately establishes the tone of the album. It's a song that combines Grohl's grunge roots with a more polished, radio-friendly alternative rock sound, setting the stage for the Foo Fighters' subsequent commercial success. Big Me, another standout track, is a departure from the heavier sound of the rest of the album, offering a lighter, pop-influenced tune that would become one of the band's most recognisable hits.

However, Foo Fighters is not without its flaws. The album occasionally suffers from a lack of coherence, with some tracks feeling disjointed or underdeveloped. Songs like Weenie Beenie and Alone + Easy Target show Grohl's raw energy and musical talent but lack the refined songwriting that would characterise the band's later work. The album's production sometimes fails to capture the full dynamism of the songs, leaving them feeling somewhat flat.

Lyrically, the album varies in quality. While some tracks feature thoughtful and introspective lyrics, others are less memorable, lacking the depth and complexity of Grohl's later writing. The vocal delivery, while generally strong, occasionally veers into a style that is slightly grating.

Despite these criticisms, Foo Fighters is an important album in the band's discography and the broader rock landscape of the 1990s. It represents Grohl's transition from the tragedy of Nirvana's end to the genesis of a new musical chapter. The album's rawness and simplicity serve as a foundation for the band's future evolution, hinting at the more polished and ambitious projects to come.
