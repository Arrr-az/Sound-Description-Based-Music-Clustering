ozzystylez May 22 2010
+66 Vote downBookmarkVote up
James Murphy is the unlikeliest of front men; he is not handsome in an obvious way, neither is he handsome in a subtle way. He is slightly overweight and not the sharpest of dressers and he usually looks as though he has slept in his clothes for a few days, has a hangover and does not possess a razor. Yet there are moments on This Is Happening where he evokes memories of, or perhaps even channels, some of the greats; David Bowie ("All I Want"), The Idiot era Iggy Pop ("Somebody's Calling Me") and Talking Heads era David Byrne ("Pow Pow") all receive a nod and a tip of the hat during the album's length. It is fair to say that these men have all been influences on Murphy, but then if we listen to LCD Soundsystem's breakthrough single " Losing My Edge" which reads like a list of cool bands that have made up the history of rock and roll so far it is clear that he has been influenced by almost everyone worth being influenced by. He knows his stuff, and I think that he gives other music geeks like me and you some kind of hope. He is an everyman character who has modestly achieved some fantastic things musically and we are all lucky that he didn't throw in the towel at forty as he suggested he might and gave us a third installment of incredibly likable electro-pop instead.

This Is Happening still has that very unique DIY feel to it, it still seems plausible that these songs were written and possibly even recorded in Murphy's bedroom, possibly with a hangover, possibly whilst drunk. Opening gambit "Dance Yrself Clean" is a slow builder which starts off quietly so as to invite the listener to turn up the volume and then kicks in with a massive keyboard sound that gets the party underway at glass worryingly high volumes before it calms down again and allows Murphy a chance to draw our attention to a colossally bad drum fill which is almost comic in its execution.

And that is another key to the likability of LCD Soundsystem; they have a sense of humour about things and don't seem to take themselves too seriously. They are interested in having a good time, despite the existential ponderings that litter Murphy's lyrics and one need only listen to the acutely observed and satirical lyrics to "Drunk Girls" to realise this. And for those who need an added reminder the accompanying video should do the job.

Elsewhere Murphy is self deprecating during the unusual love song "I Can Change" where he informs the person he is perhaps trying to woo that "Love is a verse/Shoved in a Hearse/Love is an open book to a verse of your bad poetry/And this is coming from me" and ironic and playful when the song entitled "You Wanted a Hit" includes a lengthy three minute introduction before any vocals or main hooks begin which, added to the fact that it clocks in at nine minutes, instantly excludes it from any radio station's playlist despite the fact that, once it gets going, it is quite an infectious little number with a propulsive rhythm the like of which we have come to expect from the group.

But all joking aside, and in spite of the DIY feel to many of the album's songs, there are moments where something special happens. "All I Want" transcends mere party music and, with a guitar line which nostalgically conjures up the mood and sound of David Bowie's " Heroes" creates one of this years most touching and beautiful musical moments and it feels like a song which has always been part of the musical map, a song which has universal appeal and true magic to it. But why stop at just one such moment? The album's closer, "Home", has a soaring vocal line which suggests that Murphy is pushing his voice into territories he has never strayed into before and has become more than just a witty and original lyricist and discovered he is a singer with an ear for a life affirming melody. All of the beautiful moments seem to be born out of a twinge of sadness that seems to prevail throughout many of the lyrics. Perhaps sadness is the wrong word, but there is certainly a disillusionment to Murphy's ramblings that perhaps give us an insight into why he will be disbanding after this.

"Pow Pow" is another highlight which combines all of the pieces which make LCD Soundsystem great; a compelling beat driven by a bass line right out of the Tina Weymouth songbook, a gradual build to a moment of dreamy elation, and hilarious lyrics delivered in a truly unique manner (including my personal favourite "We have a black President and you don't so shut up!").

At over an hour in length and under ten tracks in make up, This Is Happening does run the risk of being too much for some people to take, but I can't think of what those people would be like and I'm not sure that they are the sort of people I'd want to spend much time with. There isn't a moment in which to be bored, there is always something to get your toe tapping, to coax a smile from your lips or to fill you with the same kind of elation that "All My Friends" delivered in 2007. Lyrically it is difficult to tell whether or not Murphy is lamenting or laughing which keeps you listening intently. If anything this all goes to prove that he might be an unusual frontman but he knows his music, perhaps even better than we do. The difference is that rather than using his musical geekery to prove himself as worthy to other music nerds in internet chatrooms he has used it as a springboard from which to jump into new territory and make some wonderful music of his own which remains his own despite recalling the greats.

Ambrose Bierce memorably defined the novel as "A short story, padded." Substitute "Eno" in for "a short story" and you have LCD Soundsystem.

That it brings people joy, well, I am glad for it, but what exactly does one do with this music? It's theoretically danceable, but frankly if I was inclined to dance I would rather do it to something J-poppy, or something with more autotune, or I don't know, SOMETHING. If music has a future- something that I'm not sure it needs or knows what to do with- this probably isn't it (easy for me to say on the night LCD Soundsystem breaks up).

Honestly I think my beef with James Whatshisface is that he is a clever boy. That sort of thing may have been all well and good back in 1974 when being a clever boy meant writing elaborate geopolitical analogies and having Robert Fripp solo over random typewriter sounds, but I think this world has had enough of clever boys. The purpose of music, the purpose of life, is not to prove to the world how smart you are. Music made by Internet smartasses, for Internet smartasses, well, it all leaves me feeling very, very tired.

We've seen this before. It has existed in every age: People who give the impression of knowing everything, and furthermore of actually being bored with everything, while simultaneously somehow managing not to know anything at all. James: Give us a tweet when you get bored of being bored. Until then, if you need me, I'll be off reading a book. Yes, a BOOK. Not a "Kindle".

This Is Happening starts with the 9-minute "Dance Yrself Clean", which despite the gargantuan duration is pretty simple electro-pop, as if an extended remix of The Human League's The Sound Of The Crowd. Next, "Drunk Girls" is a combination of power-pop and punk-funk that would be more at home in a Kaiser Chiefs album. The 8-minute "One Touch" is also hardly an evolution from the days of the Depeche Mode (circa Master And Servant), while the 7-minute "All I Want" is a rip-off of David Bowie's Heroes.

The 6-minute "I Can Change" is no less a tribute to the golden era of synth-pop (veering towards Soft Cell this time around), but at least it is the most catchy moment so far. The 9-minute "You Wanted A Hit" is again a very long and very tame track, which tries to be sarcastic, but fails, exactly because it's so tame and so very long (this could be a Bronski Beat b-side). Then, the 8-minute "Pow Pow" is a rip-off of Remain In Light-era Talking Heads, and later on adds a generic electro beat. The 7-minute "Somebody's Calling Me" and 8-minute "Home" sound just as mundane.

Maybe James Murphy wants to self-destruct, who knows. What's impressive is that the "indie" press isn't calling his bluff and labels this album a masterpiece. With journalism and "professional" critics failing to realize the world has moved on thanks to the internet (something which the music-business is all too aware of but still fails to react to it in any meaningful way), it won't be long before they are rendered completely obsolete.

Admittedly, I'm a passerby-fan of LCD Soundsystem. In theory, they are a group that shouldn't impress me much, thanks to James Murphy's coolest-guy-in-the-club vocal and lyrical posturing and their fondness for 80s new wave motifs. I've enjoyed them (especially their 2007 hit "All My Friends") more than their "dance-punk" contemporaries--like Interpol, The Killers, and Franz Ferdinand, to name just a few--because of their more minimal approach to dance music, their overt minimizing of the (punk) rock influence on their sound, as well as their lack of an overbearing public pretentiousness. Whereas the aforementioned groups are clearly influenced by 80s new wave and post-punk acts like The Human League, Duran Duran, Soft Cell, and New Order, LCD Soundsystem reaches back to David Bowie's Eno trilogy of albums (Low, "Heroes", and The Lodger), Blondie's "Heart of Glass," and, most importantly, Neu!'s "motorik" beats for their blueprint. With their third album, This is Happening, LCD Soundsystem have not only come closer to perfecting their approach, but have made the best album of Euro-American dance music since the Saturday Night Fever soundtrack.

While This is Happening's critically-acclaimed predecessor, Sound of Silver, had some fine moments on it, it featured too many self-conscious and goofy moments (like "North American Scum" and "New York, I Love You But You're Bringing Me Down") to fully work as both a fun dance record and as a serious piece of music. On This is Happening, there are no attempts at "serious music." It features what is essentially a great soundtrack for a night of clubbing, free from the coked-out gated reverb beats of 80s new wave or the Ecstasy-fueled layers of hallucinogenic sound that made up 90s electronica. The key to this approach is the group's knack for solid production, most of which is centered around analog and digital drums, and their ability to slowly build up this collection of long-ish tracks (only two of the album's nine songs album are shorter than six minutes long). "Dance Yrself Clean" begins rather inauspiciously, the music and vocals practically inaudible. Then the drums and a keyboard, which produces sounds not too dissimilar from Atari 2600 video games, radically alter the trajectory of the track, instantly making it a memorable one. The much-maligned "Drunk Girls" is LCD Soundsystem's one stab at a mainstream hit. It is pretty catchy. Sure, the lyrics are silly, but has anybody ever really (willingly) paid attention to their lyrics before? After a few slight missteps (including "I Can Change," the album's one attempt at a "love song"), things return to form with "You Wanted a Hit" (which features the best guitar work on the album) and the absolutely infectious "Pow Pow." "Home," with its wide array of 80s keyboard sound effects, effectively closes out the CD in as fun a way as it began. The least effective cuts on the album--and they are by no means bad--expose the group's biggest weakness: their absolute debt to David Bowie. "All I Want" tries really hard to be "Heroes," but fails largely because it's the one track on the album that really tries to layer globs of sound atop its backbeat, contradicting its relatively unadorned approach to dance music. Similarly, the dark "Somebody's Calling Me" resembles Iggy Pop's "Mass Production" (from The Idiot), which Bowie co-wrote and produced.

Aside from these minor detours, This is Happening largely works because the album's beats are its core, and LCD Soundsystem's strong pop instincts never strays too far from them. This is Happening might not change the world, but there are few albums so worthy of busting out all those awkward white-boy/white-girl dance moves to than this one.
