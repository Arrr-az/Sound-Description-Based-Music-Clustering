Bummer-phenia! Words hurt, yknow, Pete.

Sacrilege! How could anyone speak of the beloved Quadrophenia with anything less than unqualified praise? This is, after all, the magnum opus of rocks greatest group and greatest songwriter, next to the Beatles and McCartney-Lennon*.

Well, first of all, its not Petes magnum opus: that would be Lifehouse. So what if he didnt finish it: the songs, whether they came out on Whos Next or languished in a vault somewhere until the 21st century, are the best crop he wrote. Heres why: they combine the magically uplifting, transcendent vibe of Tommy with the sophistication of Quadrophenia.

Notice that thing that I said was good about Tommy? The vibe... the place it takes you to emotionally? Both Tommy and Lifehouse are transcendent. They make me happy to be alive. Quadrophenia makes me feel weak, sad, insecure, dirty, and small -- if I pay too much attention to the lyrics. I will acknowledge upfront that Im prone to moodswings, and I have to watch what I expose myself to. Thats why people like me frequently turn to religion. Like Pete did. And on this album he was veering toward full spiritual collapse, as The Who By Numbers (reviewed here) horrifically confirmed.

So, lyrically, this is one screwed-up album. Sure, sure, Love Reign Oer Me... I know thats the standard rebuttal to the bummer-accusation, and I dont buy it. Love Aint For Keeping or Sensation, to name two (supposedly) lesser numbers from Petes other two rock otters, are each more believable and more uplifting than Reign. The intention is real, but the actualization isnt there: thats why its a desperate prayer rather than a joyful song of celebration, dig? Quadrophenia doesnt even end happily; it just wants to.

OK, that was a bit unfair: Love, Reign Oer Me is a great song, no question. Not all great songs have to be all shiny and happy, of course not. Im one of many RYM-ers who loves In The Aeroplane Over The Sea, which is incredibly dark, but also incredibly healing at the same time. And I very much sympathize with Petes struggles; I just cant expose my brain to them very often. The problem with this album (as with, e.g., The Lamb Lies Down On Broadway, or much of Radiohead, or the Arcade Fire) is that there isnt enough love and redemption to balance out the darkness. Your mileage may vary here, of course. The music is amazing -- some of the most entrancing, rocking, glorious stuff Pete ever wrote -- but its not enough to redeem the vibe. At times, its even incongruent with the lyrics, again anticipating The Who By Numbers.

Besides Love Reign Oer Me is there any other song here thats undeniably positive, lyrically? Yes, there is one: Im One. And as the title telegraphs, its the only such One here. The allusion to Eastern monism is not coincidental. If only Pete had kept up his devotional practices to Meher Baba, this could have turned out differently! (I said, dont WORRY, be HAPPY, goddammit!) Anyway, Pete saved the Happy One for himself to sing, just as hed do with Blue, Red and Grey. But thats OK... we can count on Roger to soldier through even Petes most existential stuff and make it sound straightforward and anthemic.

So, Im trying to think of anything besides Im One where happiness even wins on points. I know this album nearly by heart, but Im checking the tracklist to see. Lets go over all of the non-instrumentals just to be sure.


Listening To You, I Get Depressed

- The Real Me - About rage, confusion, hopelessness, and fragmentation of self-identity. This reminds me of cuddling with my fianc by a crackling fire on a cold winter evening. With hot chocolate.

- Cut My Hair - About feeling socially outcast and alienated from ones parents, and doing so much speed that breakfast makes you sick. This reminds me of the best sex I ever had.

- The Punk And The Godfather - OK, I never knew what the hell this was about. Something about tension between older and younger generations, with each dismissing the other? I think that might be it. Reminds me of my happiest childhood birthday. All my friends and family were there and it was magical.

- Im One - stipulated. But might not Pete mean that by being one, hes all alone? No, he doesnt really mean that; I was just messing with you. Im sorry. I didnt mean to bring you down.

- The Dirty Jobs - I havent mentioned the music much yet, but doesnt this have a beautiful, uplifting melody? Its about a guy who looks after the pigs, and feels trampled upon by society. He feels beaten every day. This reminds me of when I took ecstacy the first time.

- Helpless Dancer - Another amazing melody. Rogers singing kinda fast; it sounds like hes making lists of things: what are they? Oh, OK: all the obstacles and defeats that make you feel so helpless that you stop doing the thing you were born to do: you stop da-ancing! This always reminded me of the miracle of birth.

- Is It In My Head wonderful melody again and the singer feels disturbed, and discovers why: nobody loves him. This reminds me of the first time I kissed a girl.

- Ive Had Enough - a rollicking recitation of all the things the singer is sick and tired of, culminating with life itself. This reminds me of my first orgasm.

- 5:15 - About a guy feeling out of his mind on drugs. Reminds me of the first time I took acid. Wait a minute, that was a difficult experience! Which was because I did it in the wrong environment. Later, Id take acid alone, in my room (concealing my intoxication from my parents) and listen to Quadrophenia and get all weirded out. I still hadnt learned my lesson.

- Sea And Sand - Some of Petes best music ever, matched with lyrics about what its like to search your soul and discover that you are utterly alone and unloved. This most reminds me of Thanksgiving. Grandma was the most amazing cook. Me and my cousins would play pretend games all evening and nobody wanted to go to bed, we were having so much fun.

- Drowned - the singer wants to die by drowning!! Reminds me of Christmas.

- Bell Boy - another one that rocks, and shows Petes continuing and amazing innovation with synthesizers... and its about being reduced from a budding musician to working a menial job, always running at someones heel. Reminds me of the vibe I get from Were Not Gonna Take It, especially the Listening To You part.

- Doctor Jimmy - in its penultimate lyric, the opera heads toward resolution. The singer goes on some sort of negative ego trip! He feels hostile and wants to fight all the guys! As for the girls? In perhaps one of the most uplifting lyrics ever penned by one of Rock Musics Gods, our protagonist asks: Who is she? ILL RAPE IT! (Being a groovy sort of chap, he also likes to get high.) This reminds me of when I adopted a kitten and cared for it and watched it grow. And raped it.**

- Love Reign Oer Me - something about redemption through love or some shit like that.


---------------------

Well, at least the music is good. Mostly one-chord industrial thrash stuff with death metal screams. No, wait, just kidding! I was messing with you again! Actually, yes, it is among the best music ever written by one of rocks greatest composers. And the Who are at the top of their game: rocks best drummer and best bassist achieve astonishing heights, with John also arranging all the horn parts. Stunning in its conception and execution, this will certainly live on for centuries... centuries after we have lived our hopeless, alienated, meaningless little lives and died our lonely, painful deaths.

LONG LIVE ROCK!!!

---------------------

* In keeping with the contentious spirit of the review, I had to put Paul first. He was the dominant Beatle during their later and best years. I dont see how anyone could seriously argue the point.

** That didnt happen. I was messing with you one more time! Im sorry. I didnt mean to bring you down.

...and now, the punchline:

THATS FREAKING PETE TOWNSHENDS JOB!!!

"I need to get back home."

After his failure to realise his full ambition ambition with Lifehouse, Pete Townshend must have been doubly determined to get his next concept album made and for it to be an all round improvement on The Whos conceptual high-water mark, Tommy. It needed to have a complex narrative, have all the trademarks of a landmark Who album and be utterly timeless. Hell yeah, Quadrophenia was going to be Townshends masterpiece.

First of all, lets tackle the narrative. Simply put, its too complex. If you havent read the accompanying sleeve notes or seen the late 70s film, then you have no chance of understanding what the bloody hell is going on. The story is over complicated, too obsessed with nostalgia at the expense of entertainment, and taken as a whole the album just isnt as enjoyable as Tommy was, where at least some of the iconography was open to interpretation. Quadrophenia has a rigidly set narrative, and while it has parts that can be taken as a metaphor of some kind or other, its still too restrictive.

What Quadrophenia also lacks is great tunes. Even Tommy could boast a dozen or so brilliant tunes weaving in and out of the main story arc, however Quadrophenia tries too hard to push the narrative forward. As a result there are only three tracks here which I could rate among The Who at their very best, The Real Me, 5:15 and Love Reign Oer Me. The band are playing as well as ever, in particular Entwistle and Moon, but the tunes just arent there, possibly because Townshend spends far too much time faffing around with his synths. The brass section could have worked really well, but sadly it lacks punch and its impact is lost because it seems to be buried way down in the mix. There are flashes of genius continuously throughout Quadrophenia, but they seem to be pulling in separate directions at the expense of good music. The ideas were there, they just fell short the it came to the execution, and even the title track seems to be just another lengthy instrumental track for the sake of a lengthy instrumental track, in the same way that Underture was on Tommy.

Perhaps the rest of the band let Townshend have his way with Quadrophenia because they still felt bad about vetoing Lifehouse, but to my ears at least, Quadrophenia falls short of being a success in the same way that Tommy was and perhaps they should have pushed Townshend to just record the songs as a straight rock album in the same manner as Who's Next. Like many double albums, Quadrophenia would no doubt have made a splendid single album, but only if they had abandoned the concept and allowed Entwistle to include some of his trademark lighthearted numbers. As it is Quadrophenia seems a little to leaden and self-important to be classed as one of The Whos best albums, and it has failed to date as well as the very best of their work.

Versteckes Meisterwerk
Nach dem sensationellen Whos Next, das allgemein als das beste Album der britischen Rock Band The Who gilt, wollte das Genie hinter The Who, Pete Townshend nach Tommy eine weitere Rockoper auf die Beine stellen. Er liess sich also nicht entmutigen, denn Whos Next war ja eigentlich als gigantisches Lifehouse Konzept konzipierte gewesen. Da aber niemand ausser dem Gitarrenzerstrer selber verstand um was es eigentlich ging, nahm man einfach die besten Song und stellte sie zu einem Album zusammen. Ein Konzeptalbum war dies aber nicht.

Pete Townshend hatte aber schon ein nextes Konzept in seinem Kopf entworfen und diesmal hat er es mit Quardophenia auch realisiert. Dieses Album erzhlt nmlich die Geschichte des englischen Mods (auch The Who waren zu Anfang ihrer Karriere Mods), der langsam erwachsen wird und reale Ereignisse wie eine Strassenschlacht zwischen Mods und Rockern miterlebt. Ausserdem hat Jimmy eine viergespaltene Persnlichkeit. Und jeder dieser vier Persnlichkeiten ist einem der vier Bandmitglieder zu zu ordnen. Da alle Texte von Pete selber verfasst wurden, kommen einige Bandmitglieder (eigentlich alle) nicht gut weg, vor allem sein Intimfeind Roger Daltrey stellt er als verrckt dar und auch vom Schlagzeuger Keith Moon und dem Basssisten John Entwistle zeichnet er kein ideales Bild. Aber Pete ist wenigstens fair und geht auch mit sich selbst selber hart ins Gericht.
Das Konzept ist nicht einfach aber wenn man die Texte und die beiliegenden Erklrungen von Pete Townshend liest macht es Klick und man beginnt die Zusammenhnge zu verstehen und findet auch einen besseren Zugang zur Musik. Denn das Album hat wirklich einiges zu bieten und zhlt meiner Meinung nach zu den drei besten Werken der Band.
Meine Lieblingssongs, die ich jetzt mal aus dem konzeptionellen Zusammenhang reisse sind, das rasante The Real Me, bei dem John Entwistle am Bass sein ganzes Knnen zeigen kann, der finale Epos Love Reign Oer Me, das rockenden Doctor Jimmy, bei dem Keith Moon sein Knnen zeigt, das furiose Instrumental The Rock sowie 5:15.

Alles in allem ist Quadrophenia ein wirklich tolles Album, das meiner Meinung nach zu sehr im Schatten von Tommy und Whos Next steht und mehr Annerkennung verdient htte.

The Who's second great rock opera at points blows Tommy out of the water, but its double album format means that it's a less lean and compact prospect than its predecessor. Incorporating the muscular hard rock approach and synthesiser experiments of Who's Next into a narrative structure based around a confused teenage Mod trying to integrate the different aspects of his personality (each of whom is based on a member of the Who), I found the album both more musically rewarding than Tommy, where occasionally the musical quality took a back seat to the demands of the narrative, as well as simply telling a better story - though one which relies a little too much on mythologising and hyping up the mod scene in question.

Quite simply, on a lyrical and narrative level Quadrophenia makes Tommy look like juvenile, shallow hippy nonsense. Taking a universal human dilemma - trying to work out which of the differing personas we present to the world best reflects our real selves - and weaving it into a semi-autobiographical story looking back on the Who's Mod roots, Townshend creates a story with more depth and genuine literary merit than Tommy or Tommy's many imitators, whilst the other band members give their all to this performance. The use of synthesisers are carefully and tastefully judged, and together with Who's Next this album contains some of the band's most powerful rock anthems.

In the wake of this album, it is difficult to see Tommy as anything other than an experimental prototype, a cobbled-together blueprint that was more successful in pointing the way to Quadrophenia than as a musical piece in its own right. It's on this album that the Who reached their peak in terms of producers of rock opera. At the same time, there's strands of filler here and there - particularly on the second disc, which is significantly less tight than the first disc - and the emphasis on telling a more coherent story does sometimes overpower the music.
