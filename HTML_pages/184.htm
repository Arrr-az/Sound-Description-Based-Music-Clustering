Of all their studio albums this is probably the best. It's deliciously dark, gorgeous and lugubrious and unmistakably 'The Cure'! I suppose in that respect this is probably the quintessential Cure album because it retains some of the melancholy of albums like 'Seventeen Seconds' and 'Pornography' but manages to be light enough to stand shoulder to shoulder with poppier efforts like 'Kiss Me. Kiss Me, Kiss Me'. Sitting somewhere between the nightmare and the dream, between the majestic and the maudlin. It's instrumental beauty evokes a deluge of emotionally effusive images in my mind's eye that is never anything short of spine tingling but despite it containing some really strong singles it's actually tracks like 'Last Dance' and 'Same Deep Water As You' that really keep me coming back to this gem.

Disintegration is the album The Cure will be remembered for, it's career defining and one of the decade defining albums. It's the perfect album to listen to on a cold dark winter night, where the only thing that's audible is this album and the rain tapping against your window. The atmosphere it creates is honestly like no other, it's The Cure returning to their gothic and moody roots, but with their Pop sensibilities still in tact. The thing that sets this apart from the other great Cure albums is it manages to create an atmosphere while having a collection of catchy, relatable and likeable songs. But the brilliance definitely lies in its atmosphere, it has a dark, brooding atmosphere that's pretty unmatched. A lot of albums are dark and atmospheric, but this definitely has that something different about it that sets it apart from most other albums. The album's quite long, but no other length would suffice, for The Cure to really be able to get this right - it had to be the right length, and they certainly had the talent to execute it perfectly.

Musically, it's an album to just get lost in. The lush cascading synths, with deep clear pristine chiming guitars, melodic basslines and drums with just the right amount of reverb. It's thick, layered, textured and there's layers upon layers of intricate melodies. One of the most ethereal albums ever made. The sprawling soundscapes are a one off, truly a unique and remarkable thing in music. Disintegration is strong, powerful, emotional, moody, brooding, gloomy and ethereal. It's one of the most beautiful albums I've ever listened to.

It's definitely one of the most essential albums in music, never mind its genre, just music in general. One of the great British albums. A stone cold classic.

Original review on the 27th of September, 2012.

Absolutely brilliant. The albums begins beautifully with the slowly unraveling and absolutely heart-melting 'Plainsong' and fades into 'Pictures of You'. 'Closedown' is a punchier song, but just as brilliant. Probably the most famous song from the album 'Love Song' follows, and then my least favourite track from the album 'Last Dance'. Don't get me wrong, it's still a great song. 'Lullaby' is dark and gloomy, but great. The same goes for the next song 'Fascination Street'. 'Prayers for Rain' is a lead-up to the two longest and largest songs on the album. 'The Same Deep Water As You' is a dark and depressing song but is brilliantly made, 'Disintegration' is less sad but still very powerful. The album concludes with 'Homesick' and 'Untitled', two songs which are in the same vein (instrumental with few lyrics) and while 'Homesick' is a sad tune 'Untitled' ends the album on a slightly cheerier note.

This album is The Cure's strongest album and all band members were absolutely top on the recording of this. This must be one of the most beautiful albums I've ever heard despite being gothic and depressing. Robert Smith is the ultimate alternative hero.

Final note: Screw Doolittle, Disintegration is the best album of 1989, if not the 80's together.

UPDATE 7 YEARS LATER

Holy shit. It's been seven years since I wrote this review and Disintegration remains my all-time favourite album. Seven years. I was fifteen at the time I wrote that review, I am 22 now. Nothing has changed, yet everything has changed. And through all this time Disintegration has been there for me, it doesn't even seem like a piece of music anymore, it's so much grander than that...

Disintegration, for me, is the emotional and instrumental peak of music. Not a single second is wasted, everything is important and everything adds up to make something extraordinary. And that's just the music, the physical LP. There is something behind this album that makes it so much larger than life...

I have so many memories attached to this album, both bad and good. I played it to a girl I was dating, as we were driving to a nearby camping site, and it really felt like absolutely everything was perfect. A couple of months later I was listening to it alone after us deciding not to pursue our relationship any further. For a moment I was scared that I had ruined the album, that the memories wouldn't allow me to enjoy it as much. The memories are still there, but they only add to this album, this absolute masterpiece.

I'm rambling, completely lost, but there just IS something about this album that makes me want to write, that inspires me, that makes me feel that despite everything, life is all good and there is joy. Not to say that this is a joyful album - no sir. There are some absolutely harrowing, soul-crushing moments here that affect me like no other piece of art in the world. The rain-filled desolation of "The Same Deep Water as You". The absolute devastation of "Disintegration". The distant loneliness of "Homesick". The list goes on and on.

But in the darkness you find moments of absolute light and beauty. "Pictures of You" may have the most wonderful, the most beautiful guitar line of all time, despite having some pretty sad lyrics about losing someone you love. Been there, done that. "Untitled" closes with an absolutely incredible instrumental piece that does not sound of this earth. It literally sounds like heaven.

So basically I have a massive emotional connection to this album. It just feels like this album gets me, you know? It's hard to explain. But the masterful playing on this album is also something to write about. The Cure obviously can write amazing pop songs, but on this album they let the songs breathe, they let the songs stretch themselves out and really get under your skin. That's another amazing thing about this beauty.

I could keep going on and on about these 'moments' in the songs. But I don't need to, words can't do this album any justice. Only listening to it, in any context really, you may be sad or happy, it doesn't matter.

So in the 7 years I have held this album as the absolute peak of music, I myself have grown up. From an edgy teen to a regular guy, but still goddamn Disintegration will always be the greatest thing to ever bless my ears. I sincerely hope that there will never be anything as good as this for me, because I couldn't handle not having Disintegration as my number 1 album of all time.

Final note: Doolittle isn't as bad as I once thought it was, but Disintegration still completely destroys it. Emotionally and physically. Thank you, The Cure.

PS: When the bass just absolutely hammers in on "Homesick", holy shit that is what music is.

PS2: Also that absolutely filthy guitar tone on the solo on "Lovesong".

FINAL PS: Whatever man every single part of this album is absolutely amazing. Every. Single. Goddamn. Second.

With all the powerfully evocative and uniquely visual aspects of The Cure's music that have often been justifiably pointed out, it's not often if ever been mentioned that their oeuvre over the first decade of their recording career is collectively something like a mammoth song cycle reflecting the changing seasons of the year, only obviously over a far larger time scale (ten times larger to be precise). Their 1979 debut Three Imaginary Boys depicts the urban atmosphere of a rain-drenched and misty London near the end of the year; the dark trilogy of 1980-82 clearly represents the bleakness and chill of winter, with Faith the coldest part, and Pornography the squalls and howling gales of early March. Then the much brighter poppy Japanese Whispers period from 1982-83 resembles the first unexpected warm spell of spring, with 1984's The Top conveying a later cooler and rainier part as is expected in such a fickle season. The Head on the Door from 1985 sees The Cure into the glorious and heady heat of summer and 1987's Kiss Me, Kiss Me, Kiss Me sees a return to autumn, with more unsettled weather, colder mornings but not without moments of pleasant warmth. Finally, with Disintegration, The Cure have come full circle and are back into the gloom of November, with grey skies and nights closing in.

Of course all this was purely circumstantial as no artist, however great, can plan, less so predict, how their career is going to map out over such a long period, and obviously Disintegration couldn't be more different sounding than Three Imaginary Boys - nevertheless, metaphorically speaking, the resemblances are uncanny. And the profoundly autumnal feel of Disintegration closely reflects the feelings of Smith towards the end of the 1980s, when at a pivotal point in his life amid increasing fame and fortune, was approaching 30 and felt that a more single-minded, less pop-oriented statement was in order. The main reason was a combination of disillusionment with the band's newfound popularity and a fear, that all of us have at least once when we approach a certain age, of getting older and depression at the thought that at 29 he was yet to release his true masterpiece, at an age that most of if not all of the major rock artists before him had long since done so. Of course, being the characteristically modest and self-effacing guy he was, he'd conveniently forgotten that the band already had at least two mighty fine albums under their belt with Faith and The Head on the Door and much to their credit were still creatively going strong after a whole decade, which is longer than most other bands have lasted, including many of the best. But that to Smith was unfinished business, and clearly The Cure had a lot left to say and as Disintegration supremely demonstrates, of monumental and far-reaching artistic importance. Similar to what Neil Young famously said during a similar critical point in his career and stage of the 1970s, it's better to burn out while trying than to miserably fade away on a tidal wave of apathy and self-pity.

Like all Cure albums during their heyday, Disintegration is notably difficult to categorize. Though not inaccurate, it would be overly simplistic to state that it's a culmination of everything the band, or more precisely, Smith (who was by now the sole remaining founder member after the contribution of Lol Tolhurst, despite being charitably credited as playing "other instrument [sic]" had tragically become non-existent) had been working on up to this point; nor can it be summed up as a back to basics album. Though accessible with hooks aplenty, apart from Lovesong there's little of the brightly coloured pop that distinguished Japanese Whispers, The Head on the Door and in part Kiss Me; and despite a return to the atmospheric gloom and minimalistic songwriting of old, with the exception of some of the more magisterial numbers of the gothic period like The Funeral Party, One Hundred Years and Cold, the songs are conceived on a far grander and more imposing scale, in terms of their lusher production, greater song lengths and wider emotional range. And though more uniform in sound than Kiss Me and darker than the Head on the Door, they're not monochromatic to the extent of any of Seventeen Seconds, Faith or Pornography; as the front and back covers beautifully suggest, it's more varying shades of blue, sea green and purple streaked with silver. The best way to attempt to describe this whole album pictorially is as a vast and magnificent subterranean palace or ancient cistern partly submerged in water, if such a place exists (if only in one of Smith's many strange LSD influenced dreams) with shafts of light sometimes penetrating through the gloom. Above the titillatingly intricate myriad of cavernous drums, constantly interacting bass and guitars, synthesizers (notably the Solina string ensemble) dominate, whether adding harmonic or tonal colour (or both), or carrying the main countermelody or one of many hooks that simultaneously add emotional weight and arrest the ear. It's The Cure's most unified full length release that is wholly conceived as an album, its twelve largely epic songs seamlessly bound together by recurring rhythmic motifs, simple harmonic progressions, hypnotic dance rhythms and its predominantly melancholy and despairing mood and in coherence, the quality of many of its individual songs and expressive heights is unsurpassed in their output. Smith has truly outdone himself as a singer - never have we heard him perform with such range, power, dynamic nuance, deeply stated sincerity and such heart-breaking and poignant emotion that makes his vocals form such a vital part of the sonic fabric that make the best songs so intensely moving. And on a purely technical level, Disintegration is The Cure's production zenith - the sheer attention to detail, whether through devilishly complex instrumental layers, carefully conceived overdubs, vocal reverb and countless other forms of inventive studio trickery is staggering.

Yet, with all those superlatives dished out, this album isn't a totally unqualified success. The first seven songs are simply flawless, with in several of them The Cure surpassing themselves either in quality or emotional impact, or both; if ever a list of the ten objectively greatest Cure songs could possibly be compiled, any of them could provide a strong case for being on it. However, thereafter as the songs become progressively longer on average and more monothematic and repetitive without quite the harmonic and tonal variety of before, there's an occasional tendency towards monotony which despite the largely very high quality of the individual tracks weakens the overall impact of the second half a little, with Prayers for Rain being the most problematic (if still good). And finally, Untitled, although quite interesting and pleasant as an emotionally ambiguous and enigmatic closer, seems a little tacked on, neither wholly convincing in providing a feel-good ending or fitting in emotionally with the rest of the album. It could be argued that The Head on the Door is at least Disintegration's equal as though it aims lower, it's a shade more consistent and definitely more varied, but in the end Disintegration earns the crown, because it simply hits if not exceeds the heights of its predecessor on a far more frequent basis, has more depth and more lasting impact. And it's this impact that has left such an indelible impression on and touched the lives, and collective heart and soul of multiple generations of Cure fans during the 24 years it hit the record shelves and stormed the charts, to the extent of it being almost universally regarded as the best Cure album, if not one of the best albums of all time - it's their most all-encompassing in appeal, as much if not more for its sheer musical beauty as its ability to strike a resounding chord in the hearts and minds of the depressed and lovesick.

The magical opening of Plainsong, depicting slowly fading in early morning peeling church bells followed by a thunderous crash of quasi-orchestral keyboard and percussion, emphatically sets the epic tone of the album in a song of awe-inspiring grandeur and nobility, as a kind of extended intro to Pictures of You. The string synthesiser establishes the main instrumental melody, sounding archaic yet timeless followed shortly by a simple but characteristically resonant phrase on Smith's six-string bass. His soft and distantly mournful vocals almost buried in the mix, arriving over two and a half minutes in, deliver the brief but powerful lyrics about "being old", "feeling pain" and feeling like "its the end of world" with deeply affecting resonance, accentuated by heavy use of reverb and delay. Another particularly impressive aspect of this song is Williams' drumming; totally avoiding any connection with the traditional rock 4/4 beat, to the extent that it takes over a minute in for the listener to detect any discernible pulse before the more strongly rhythmic bass comes in, even though the drum pattern remains constant throughout, it creates the song's sense of unease and feel of "living at the edge of the world".

As already The Cure's longest song, Pictures of You is something of a mini-milestone, as it's also their finest. It's a wonderful example of their multi-layered appeal and ambivalent mood, being simultaneously catchy yet deep, poppy yet epic, hopeful but ultimately forlorn, and life-affirming yet also quite sad, marrying a contemporary dance groove with traditional guitar textures with a beautifully organic warmth. Despite its buoyant rhythms and major key melodies, the tone is quite bleak and austere, similar to earlier Cure albums using few actual chords, with the harmonic support provided by a low bass and long keyboard notes oscillating between the tonal centre A and D, adding two more melodic basses, both flanged, with occasional striking washes of higher synthesizer. The simple but highly affecting main melody is beautifully sung, melancholy and tender, complementing the poetic lyrical imagery perfectly. The even simpler and hypnotic harmonic progression remains mostly the same throughout until the breath-taking change in the sixth minute, with the string synthesizer adding a sonic and expressive layer in the final verse to give it a heart-breaking intensity as the lyrics get still sadder. A desert island Cure song, for sure.

To many listeners, Closedown may be the slowest to make its impression, because on the surface it sounds quite similar to Pictures of You, sharing the same key, tempo and dance rhythms. However, on subsequent hearings this similarity is shown to be purely superficial, because this is the first song to truly signal The Cure's slide towards the emotional abyss. Despite its identical major key tonality, any sense of optimism felt from Pictures of You, however false, has been dissipated; the mood is desolate, cheerless and hopeless, such an impression established by its cavernous production, booming tribal drums and haunting string melody similar to that in Plainsong and Smith's despairing vocal delivery of the lyrics that are stripped down to the bare essentials. Lovesong, as The Cure's first internationally successful, er, love song that was written as a present for Smith's long term girlfriend and fiance Mary, provides a rare moment of respite, but though the shortest and most accessible song on Disintegration, it's hardly the upbeat, overtly radio friendly pop number that many like to make out. As the first of many successive songs on Disintegration in a minor key, in its solemn low organ countermelody, brooding bass, pensive guitar solo and wistful vocals, almost choking back the tears in the word "with" in the third verse, it seems to be as much about a fear of losing his beloved as it is an open declaration of love, with the brightly ringing keyboard in the chorus providing its ray of light and most striking hook.

But if Closedown set the alarm bells ringing, with Last Dance we've reached the point of almost no return. The opening forty seconds alone exemplify The Cure's mastery of breaking one's heart with the most economical of means, consisting of little more than a guitar fragment, more motto-like than melodic, accompanied by gently dissonant sustained string chords so simple, but oh so moving. The main song is quite different in tone, but no less affecting, with its unsettling swirl of flanged guitar anchored by another pulsating bass line that provides the characteristic mixture of edge of the seat tension and danceability topped by Smith's most desperate and anguished vocal so far on this album. It's criminal that a song of such quality, along with Homesick was left off the original vinyl edition why the hell wasn't that just made into a double LP? The ironically titled Lullaby, still the Cure's most successful UK single, peaking at #5 (and as one of the more unlikely songs of the late 1980s to do so, especially with its scary video that was deemed unsuitable for being shown on the BBC's Top of the Pops), is something of a lyrical anomaly, injecting a bit of much-needed humour, if strictly of the dark and macabre kind. Rather than being centred on the usual theme about heartbreak or fear of getting old, this concerns another of Smith's nightmares, and with lines like "The spiderman is having me for dinner tonight" and "I feel like I'm being eaten by a thousand million shivering furry holes", much like those unintentionally hilarious horror B-movies it recalls, could have come across as ridiculous and cheesy in the hands of many a lesser band, but instead in The Cure's hands is gripping, menacing and catchy but never in a banal way. Much of that is down to the constant sense of threat provided by Smiths breathless and hushed vocal, amidst as always an ingeniously constructed arrangement consisting of stabbing guitar and keyboard anchored by a funky low bass groove and an emphatic off-the-beat snare, joined by long string synthesizer lines and a melodic and expressive flanged bass in the wordless refrain. The edgy synthesizer melody in the minute-long outro is the most infectious and memorable hook of the song, if not the whole album; small wonder it became such a hit.

But now we get to the album's frenzied climax that is Fascination Street, the greatest song on Disintegration and therefore the greatest of The Cure's career, an artistic and production tour de force. It's scarcely possible to do justice to it within this single paragraph, such is the brilliance of its musical and technical construction, with possibly the Cure's most meticulously put together yet also uncluttered arrangement, with its wealth of arresting themes interwoven with each other with an incredible intricacy and nightmarish beauty. The first (if not the last) angry Cure song on this album, with a dance of death menace that harks back to A Forest, but far more elaborately produced if that's possible, it can best be summed up as having four main hooks; the opening moody flanged guitar line, a more abrasive and extrovert part, coming shortly after the pervasive jazzy descending four note figure, also for the guitar, is introduced, and a vaguely oriental bouncy keyboard riff. But that only scratches the surface in terms of how all these themes are combined and overlap, dropping out and re-introducing themselves with a thrilling complexity, with the disco-like funky bass and wailing sirens the only constants, and that's without a whole plethora of additional instrumental material that hasn't been mentioned. And Smith's hard-hitting vocals add enormously to the song's ominous and uncompromising energy, growing from merely sad to positively manic, with his voice and the rhythm section turning up the heat to unforgettable effect in the line "Let's move to the beat like we know that it's over". This song is The Cure's Stairway to Heaven, their Gimme Shelter, their Won't Get Fooled Again, their Living for the City yes, it's that good.

Prayers for Rain provides the inevitable relaxation in intensity, but only in a relative sense, announcing the still darker and more brooding latter half of the album, evoking a mysterious and psychedelic atmosphere through its backward guitars, obsessive riff created by its combination of creepy string synthesizer melody and tinkling guitar like water droplets that do little to relieve its suffocating feel, and Smith's bitter, mostly low vocal. This is a relative weak link, as it isn't as emotionally engaging as what precedes and follows it, repeating the same chord progression to less interesting effect, and feels more like an interlude than a really strong standalone song in its own right, but it's quite effective in its evocativeness. But with the crashing waves announcing The Same Deep Water as You, if not the best song on the album then certainly its emotional kernel, we have been plunged into the depths of despair, in the longest song of The Cure's career, a nine minute profoundly sad and achingly desolate epic of the most utmost poignancy, the apex of The Cure's metaphorically water-themed dirges, starting with The Drowning Man, and continuing with Lament and Sinking. Its sparse instrumental arrangement, consisting of little more than sweeping synthesizer strings and a doleful flanged bass melody only adds to its intensity, especially when lead by a vocal of such devastating sadness as Smith's, anchored by another repeated chord progression that this time is fully justified and never monotonous after all, in music as despondent as this, where else is there to go? After the final crash of waves, there's a long silence before the next song as The Cure wisely allow its shattering impact to sink in.

But The Cure have one final burst of highly charged and neurotic energy in store for us in the harrowing title track, relentless, fast-paced and the album's most emotionally dramatic but never overwrought. Propelled remorselessly by another simple but potent repeated chord progression, its hard-hitting drums and an obsessive recurring keyboard riff, this powerfully illustrates Smith's angry and often bitterly ironic rant against a destructive relationship in the album's most lyrically complex song, with its frequent and disturbingly weird combination of normal speed lead and sped-up backing vocals. The much gentler, intimate but still deeply felt Homesick with its profoundly affecting chord changes, has more of a chamber pop, almost traditional acoustic feel even though it still uses synthesizers, combining the twisting flanged guitar effects of Last Dance with a typically Cure mournful crawling bass line, jazzy piano part, with a beautiful synthesized violin near the start and finish. After a bewitching over three minute introduction, Smith comes in sounding almost drained of all energy and bereft of the will to live, his mid-song scat vocal showing him to be at times lost for words. Untitled, though lyrically not much more hopeful, musically ends on something of a consoling note, if not pretending to be a resolution if it seems a little out of place sonically and a little thin in incident compared to the best songs, it has an interesting juxtaposition of clattering drums and a softly singing 6 string bass melody framed by a suave accordion-like intro and outro. Its definitely a song that asks more questions than it answers, and for that reason alone makes it intriguing and worthwhile.

All in all, despite any minor reservations about this album later on, Disintegration is unquestionably The Cure's magnum opus. Just as ambitious as Kiss Me, but far more fully realized, and more bold and uncompromising, Disintegration is towering testimony to a band, and in particular Smith, wholly prepared to take risks in the face of commercial failure, yet still achieving success on their own terms through the courage of their convictions, and most importantly, the strength of their music, achieving artistic depth, popular appeal and critical acclaim in equal measure. With the exception of Prince and to a lesser extent Kate Bush, there aren't many 1980s artists that the same could be said about like Cure biographer Steve Sutherland wrote shortly after its release, they remain an inspiration to us all. A high 4.5
