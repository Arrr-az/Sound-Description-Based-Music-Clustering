The debut album from the Gorillaz.
An excellent mix of different genres: pop, alternative rock, hip-hop, punk, Latin, dub, electronic and acid jazz. That makes Gorillaz so special, where most bands follow one genre Gorillaz make many trips with several guest appearances (for example on this album: Del tha Funkee Homosapien, Ibrahim Ferrer). The funny thing is that Gorillaz, for some reason, can get away with strange things, in fact they are part of their sound. For example: in the majority of the songs the lyrics of 2D are unintelligible and actually seem to make no sense at all. Also they dare to mix different genres together: pop songs with hip hop beats but without rap and vice versa.

Gorillaz is just a very original band which makes very original music, it's hard to compare them with other bands. The virtual band, the original music, the video clips, the website and of course the artwork form a whole and bring the band to a higher level that goes beyond just music.

Best song: Clint Eastwood
Worst song: Clint Eastwood (Ed Case / Sweetie Erie Re-Fix) (Edit)

No one doubts that, beyond some point, musical taste is defined as much by memory as it is by preference. We prefer albums and songs that formed the soundtrack of the most memorable moments in our lives. You'll know you're just as susceptible to this phenomenon as the next because you're spouting your biography the moment someone asks you to rate the album.

Witness Gorillaz self-titled first album. The record earns mixed reviews on RYM and I can grasp some of the reason why. My take on Gorillaz is that it was Damon Albarn's attempt to break out of the creative cul-de-sac that he felt he'd reached with his day job band. And fair play to him for trying something bold in response. (Although it's clear in retrospect just how divergent this direction was from his past and future partner in crime.). Gorillaz reads to me like a sprawling, spliff-infused mess. Like the The Clash's Sandinista!, the only coherent theme here is a cannabis-inspired freewheeling spirit.

But that's all beside the point for this reviewer. Before I-pods permitted random track selection from thousands, we mortals frequently tromped around listening to the same set of CDs and tapes until they were worn out. There was some valor in that, too. For me, it was heady process of self realization navigating the streets of Berlin in 2001 during my days there at the Freie University. "Tomorrow Comes Today" is the Gedchtniskirche, "19-2000" is my nondescript student housing in the southern exurbs, "5/4" still brings memories of the pock-marked walls near Pankow. I reach out to trace my fingers along the cold concrete when it plays. They're treasured snapshots from my own memory that, of course, I'm helpless to truly communicate to anyone else.

And what of all this? Something from me that lends itself to an important piece of advice. If you ever decide to pen your own memoirs, make sure your CD, erm... .mp3, collection is handy and available. The melodies will take your mind back to places it would never find otherwise. I owe Damon and the rest at least four stars for an album that, for all its hits and misses, remains indelible to me.

When I first heard the Gorillaz, they blew me away. I was never much of a fan of hip-hop until recently, but something about Gorillaz was different. Now that I'm older and wiser, I can see that that's because they really don't have much to do with hip-hop beyond the production. No, apart from Clint Eastwood and Rock the House, the two songs featuring Del tha Funkee Homosapien's rapping, this is just pop music. But I'll be damned if they don't do it well. And why shouldn't they? With this largely being Damon Albarn's side-project from Blur, it comes as no surprise that this is catchy. Gorillaz is a big departure from the pop-rock of Blur, but his keen ability to write hooks remains, leaving Dan the Automator to work his production magic. This was rather innovative at the time of its release, and I still haven't heard much of anything else that's like this. It takes more than just originality to be a winning album, though, and Gorillaz delivers in the song department, too. This is some of the catchiest stuff ever recorded, and while it's not terribly deep, not everything needs to be. This album can do it all from downbeat songs like Tomorrow Comes Today to the hip-hop classic, Clint Eastwood, to the well, punk of, Punk, to even a little bit of Latin (ok, a lot of Latin) influence on Latin Simone. 19-2000 is the most like Blur, and it's also great. I can see why this album fails to do much for a lot of people, and there are definitely some songs that fall under the failed experiment category, but it's all interesting, and tons of fun. Certainly not for everyone, but I'd recommend you give it a chance.

And never mind the fact that they're a virtual band with awesome music videos and artwork by Jamie Hewlett.

Decided to begin relistening to every main Gorillaz record, as it's been a minute since I've properly spent time with their music. Like most music listeners my age, I credit Gorillaz (and, by extension, Damon Albarn) as the band which properly got me into music - I found a lot of my favorite musicians (LCD Soundsystem, MF DOOM, Danny Brown) through a combination of their collabs, artists discussed on very underrated fancast Hallelujah Monkeyz and talking about music with other Gorillaz fans. Even if that new record kinda sucked I'll always have a soft spot for these guys.

Anyways, my opinion on this hasn't radically changed since I first heard it back in 2018 -- a slightly awkward and jarring debut with some great highlights and hints of what's to come mixed in with questionable and occasionally bad experiments. It's at its best when it's going for the pure trip hop / dub / East Coast (despite Del being the only rap feature) nocturnal vibe; "Clint Eastwood" is overplayed but still pretty undeniable, I've always enjoyed the breaks and weird little vocalizations on "Tomorrow Comes Today" and deep cuts like "Man Research", "Starshine" and "Rehash" have their fairly solid potential expanded upon in multitudes with the highly underrated dub remix project. The lowlights aren't outright stinkers, but cuts like "Sound Check" and "19/2000" (sorry!) try my patience in a way other nadirs on subsequent Gorillaz projects don't.

I have to give special mention to "Slow Country", though. Easily my favorite song here -- the gorgeous piano riff, soothing white noise sample taken from early band influence The Specials, a genuinely vunerable Damon performance and lyrics that feel like they were actually written by a conscious human being. It, too, would be improved on Laika Come Home (which you need to stop sleeping on).

A fine start to Gorillaz, but outside of a couple tracks I most likely won't be returning that often.

One Of The Best Debuts Ever
Re-Hash: 7/10 - Catchy instrumental and light vocals from Damon and Miho Hatori's backing vocals during the chorus only add to the lighthearted bouncy feel of this opening. The repeating of "It's the money or stop" can be repetitive and can drag on.

5/4: 7.5/10 - Written in 5/4 signature gives the song a bizarre feel to those that know music, but Hatori's backing vocals help accompany Damon's again. Repetitiveness strikes again, but to a much lesser degree than Re-Hash.

Tomorrow Comes Today: 9/10 - As one of the few singles off of the debut album from Gorillaz, it comes with an earworm melody and a repetitive chorus and hook that you never tire of. An extra verse would help add some variety to the later parts of the song, but that's all the complaints I have.

New Genious (Brother): 8/10 - Added hip hop elements with vocal mixing near the mid point of the song helps with the eventual transition into more hip hop style tracks, especially Rock the House. Damon exercising his falsetto can be a little grating but his smooth vocals over the top of it make it not too distracting to the quality of the mixing and production.

Clint Eastwood: 9/10 - The track that brought the band to fame on MTV, a repeated loop of lyrics from Damon only interrupted by the legendary Del the Funky Homosapien slinging bars that go along almost too well with the instrumentation and sample. It's clear how this catchy song caught on with general audiences, but Damon's vocals can drag on if you've heard the song too much.

Man Research (Clapper): 6/10 - In the Hall of the Mountain Queen is sampled perfectly, but Damon's falsetto voice in the chorus drag this song down as the worst on the record.

Punk: 9/10 - What feels like a parody of the Punk genre comes together for an experimental and horribly catchy song that is very fun to sing along to. The chaotic instruments and boisterous drums only add to the song's feel. The fact that Damon played almost every instrument also adds to the song.

Sound Check (Gravity): 9/10 - The repetitive nature of these beginning songs continues on this track but is perfected here. The instrumental and tone of Damon's voice complete the song to make a complete vibe track. The bridge helps put the song together as a sort of break up track.

Double Bass: 8/10 - An ambient instrumental track which breaks the trend of repetitive lyrics so far, further cementing the experimental status of this debut record. While the song's ambience is nice to listen to casually, it can make a full album listen drag on slightly.

Rock the House: 10/10 - The backing track, Del, the horns making the song sounds boisterous creates the perfect track for this album. Del's personality is so clear on this track, only exemplified by the instrumentation from Damon and crew.

19-2000: 10/10 - A fun track with plain and clear variety with each verse, Damon and Miho bounce off of each other during the track perfectly as 2-D and Noodle. A fun track that begins the end of the record that closes their three singles from this era.

Latin Simone (Que Pasa Contigo): 8/10 - Ibrahim Ferrer brings another experimental track on the album with Spanish lyrics to a slow and funky track. Ibrahim's voice goes along with the instrumentation, making the end of the album more and more unique.

Starshine: 7/10 - The ambient sounds and smooth instrumentation along with Damon presenting a sick sounding pathetic voice present this song as a strange addition but fit with the ending uniqueness this record has. The instrumental track is the highlight of the song.

Slow Country: 9/10 - One of the best songs on the record takes advantage of Damon's voice as 2-D, and a synth lead track behind his catchy vocals. The song sadly ends with more repetitiveness, but it's much more tolerable than some of the other songs.

M1 A1: 10/10 - Using a sample from Romero's Day of the Dead give the beginning of this song a slow dread filled build up, along with the desperate yelling provided. As the intro progresses and the instruments overtake the yelling man, the breakdown into the real song starts with my favorite track on the album. Lots of extra sounds and erratic instrumentation helps the song become more complete as it finishes up.

Overall: 8/10 - An absolute classic alternative rock/hip hop project that brought my favorite band to popularity in the 2000's. Only held down by some repetitive lyrics and a rough falsetto from Damon Albarn, this album sets up the listener for the band's unique sound and every song being different from one another. The experimental feel of this album is brave, especially for a debut album but it comes together as what I think is one of the most relistenable and charming debut projects.
