Rum, Sodomy, and the Lash might be the better album - just - but If I Should Fall From Grace With God has the songs. There are tracks on here that define not just The Pogues, but the entire genre - alongside the greatest song in the history of the entire world ("Fairytale of New York", obviously), there are songs as brilliant as "If I Should Fall From Grace With God", "Turkish Song of the Damned", "Bottle of Smoke", "Thousands Are Sailing", "Fiesta", and "Streets of Sorrow/Birmingham Six" - all among the high points of the decade.

There are actually so many great songs on here that it takes a while for some of them to sink in. "Thousands are Sailing" didn't register as a highlight with me until I was ten or so listens in, when suddenly the chorus hit me - 'thousands are sailing / across the Western ocean / To a land of opportunity / That most of them will never see', and later, 'thousands are sailing / again across the ocean / where the hand of opportunity / draws tickets in a lottery'. The hopes, dreams, and futility of the Irish diaspora are summed up so clearly and hauntingly on this one that I'm surprised it's not a traditional song. Shane Macgowan didn't even write it, either - it was the guitarist, Phil Chevron. That's crazy - people used to talk about George Harrison, and how he would have been the lead songwriter in any band in the world other than The Beatles; this song suggests a similar story might be true for The Pogues. And yet, this was the first of his songs to make it onto a Pogues album.

Other highlights are obvious, though. "Fairytale of New York" needs no description, other than to say every listen reminds me why I love music so much, while "Fiesta" and "If I Should Fall From Grace With God" are great, big, shambolic drunken messes and fantastic party records as a result, while "Streets of Sorrow/Birmingham Six" is mournful and furious in equal measure. Even after the album has become ingrained in your subconscious, these all still pack some punch.

Shane Macgowan is such an obviously offputting presence to most people that it overshadows his music, but I honestly can't think of many people that wouldn't like this album on some level. The Pogues will always be linked to drinking, and that's fine, but there's more than enough depth and subtlety about what they do to ensure that their music sounds great in just about any circumstance. I could imagine some of these songs being played at funerals. To make an album that's so easy to enjoy, and so timeless, in a decade as brash and extreme as the '80s and in a genre as marginalized as traditional folk, is an extraordinary achievement. Then again, The Pogues were an extraordinary band.

The thing about the Irish, is that unlike any other nation that has vast numbers of ex-pats, it does not matter how many generations down the 'not actually born in Ireland line' they are...they still claim with a fervent passion that they are Irish (often despite never having been there - just go to Boston on St.Paddy's day for a feast of absolute plastic 'Irishness').

And who can blame them really as they have much to be proud of...not least of which is their distinctive music which you can recognise as being Irish from the very first note. You either love it or hate it (I love it) but either way you can not deny it's uniqueness or gravity.

However as the 'actually born in Ireland brigade' become less and less through the ravages of years and are replaced by the 'not actually born in Ireland line', Gaelic music has gradually become infused with the culture of the countries that the Irish Ex-pats have adopted.

As such this album is all things that is good about Irish music mixed with all things that is good about the Punk sensibilities of late 70's England and produces an album that is practically a thrill a minute, a manically driven festival of Punk drunken Gaelic revellry and is as close to the feel of the numerous gigs I have been to as is possible. All that could possibly make the live feel resonate more is Shane MacGowan collapsing on stage in a drunken stupor or forgetting the words (I have even been to a gig where he sang the wrong words to the wrong track !)...but even in the studio he is slurred enough to make it strangely great.

The entire album does not really have a throwaway track upon it. Perhaps some might dismiss Fiesta and South Australia as at the very least being 'not really required' but once again the devotion of the fan base at the gigs to these seemingly 'silly' tracks just adds to the festival like atmosphere of this album.

Perhaps those who are that way inclined might point to the fact that much of the songwriting is about the despair of the Irish refugees (a term used in the album - not mine) in their newly adopted homes and the undoubted hardhips they faced forging an identity and worthwhile existence there and therefore question the usage of the word 'festival'...so question away as much as you like - but to most who adore this album they could not give a rats arse about the lyrical brilliance of McGowan...this album just makes you want to get drunk, party and jig about it.

Brilliant stuff.

With a different production, The Pogues took a slightly different sound. For the most part, the Irish roots are still there, but you can hear shades of other music seeping in there. Whether it be the Spanish influenced Fiesta or Turkish Song of the Damned (Turkey, obviously), you can hear the difference. That doesn't hurt this album in the slightest. As a matter of fact, it makes it very satisfying listen. That's why this and Rum, Sodomy, & The Lash make such an awesome one-two punch. They're just different enough that they're both incredible and individual.

The title track appropriately picks off where they left off. A ripping, dashing piece featuring Shane's shrill scream and sounds much like it would've had a home on Rum. Bottle of Smoke is one of their best, and probably Shane's peak in terms of the word "fuck." Another fast, loud, fun track. Fairytale of New York is my favorite Christmas song. It features that storytelling McGowan is so good at. "It was Christmas Eve babe... in the drunk tank." That really sets the tone for a song about falling in and then out of love with a humorous bite. Thousands Are Sailing has an epic feel and is one of their best.

Side B kicks off with the fun-loving good-time Fiesta with Shane throwing some Spanish lyrics in for good measure. Streets of Sorrow/Birmingham Six is an awesome song that starts with a quiet, powerful acoustic piece before exploding into a fast, angry piece about the treatment of Irish prisoners by authorities. They performed this on a TV show and they cut to commercial because the song was so controversial in the UK at the time. Then things calm down with a little ballad Lullaby of London. The Broad Majestic Shannon is a sweet little piece and one of my favorites.

Overall, it's an awesome album and one that I wish more people knew about. It's fun, it's thought-out, and it's The Pogues during their peak. Can't go wrong with that.

If I Should Fall From Grace From God is my first Pogues album, and it's a riot. I've never been to Ireland despite it being where my Grandfather grew up, but I hope it's something like this album when I do make it over there. The first side is flawless, veering from punkish snarls to imaginative retellings of Irish history to the only xmas song to ever mean anything at other times of the year. "Thousands Are Sailing" is probably the highlight of the album, it's certainly the grand centrepiece. The second side is no let down either! How can anything that starts with "Fiesta" disappoint? That song never fails to slap a smile on my face. It's not all party music though, "Streets Of Sorrow" is one of the most heart-felt ballads I've ever heard while the creepy "Worms" is the perfect way to close an album with as much personality as this. Recommended to anyone with soul in their step.

This is a spectacular remaster of The Pogues' finest album. One of the best albums of the 80s, If I Should Fall Grace With God brought about a folk and celtic renaissance while infusing Irish music with punk energy and a world music cultural vouyerism. Politically conscious, funny, danceable and full of passion, this album contains many of The Pogues best songs, including the fiery "Bottle Of Smoke," the heartbreaking "Thousands Are Sailing" and, of course, the Xmas classic "A Fairytale Of New York."

My only quibble with the re-issue is minor. The band chose to issue the tracks in the original vinyl running order, subjugating "The Battle March Medley" and "South Australia" as bonus tracks with 4 non-album tracks. This interrupts the flow of the original US CD version that many are used of. That said, the remaster is a substantial improvement. While the original 1986 CD still sounds very good for a CD of its era, the intricacies of their arrangements are even more pronounced.

Because the Pogues never shied away from perpetuating stereotypes of Irish drinking and carousing, some at the time accused the them of destroying Irish music. But, in the end, they revitalized it and inspired many people (myself included) to explore Irish traditional folk music. If anyone eventually killed Irish music, it was Riverdance, Celtic Women and their ilk.

Caídos en desgracia, dulce desgracia
PUNK GAÉLICO. Tercer disco y tercer clásico del tirón. El disco de "Fairytale of New York" tiene aún más motivos para engancharse a él. Además de ese villancico pagano hecho para deleitar a las almas atormentadas en esos días tan entrañables como insoportables, encontramos al grupo entregado sin coartadas al folk más burbujeante y radical de su historia.

También ofrece el disco interesantes puntos de fuga que le dan un plus de peligrosidad y lo diferencian en el canon de los londinenses. Temas donde mezclan el rock y otros sonidos más exóticos alejados de la tradición gaélica que venían manejando. "Turkish Song of the Damned", "Metropolis", "Fiesta" o "Thousands Are Sailing" bullen entre violines y vientos de todo pelaje, pero en ellas también colea un veneno potente y nuevo que intriga, atrapa y, digámoslo, también irrita por momentos.

Todos estos retazos son los que conforman un disco original y poderoso. Una obra con no pocos ganchos para el fan y el oyente casual. Una obra muy imperfecta y no del todo cohesionada, pero uno de los motivos más suculentos para dejarse atrapar por la maquinaria celestial y mugrienta de un grupo que ya está en la historia entre los más grandes. Tres de tres.
