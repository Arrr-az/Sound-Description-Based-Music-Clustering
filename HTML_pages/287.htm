A sneering and vastly more punk-leaning take on the sound of Television and early Talking Heads, Richard Hell unleashes his Voidoids on the world with Blank Generation. The leering vocals, obscenity and anger evident provide the punk half of the equation, but the guitar work tends much more towards the artier approaches of the proto-New Wave; the end result is this strange situation where the album, as others have pointed out, somehow manages to form a bridge between proto-punk and post-punk. Veering wildly between the gloriously crude (Love Comes In Spurts) and the sloppily artsy (Another World), the album doesn't quite make it to the first tier of '77 punk releases but it's within spitting distance of it easily.

Okay, whoever the hell (no pun intended) keeps describing this as "Television minus the intricate guitar work" is totally right.

I mean, hell! (again, no pun intended) that sums it up pretty well, but I feel comes up short (and in spurts - that pun intended). Like, yeah, it's totally like Television minus the intricate guitar work... and minus the awesome bass work of someone like Fred Smith... and minus the artsy... and minus the poise... and minus the, um, well, Television.

This sounds NOTHING like Television.

Goddamn it, Richard Hell sounds like a snotty punk - not as annoying as say, Johnny Rotten, but a bit exaggerated in his own right. And the music? If ANYTHING, this is closer to a Pere Ubu/New York Dolls hybrid without the playful artiness of the first, and minus the full on attitude of the second. To say the least, I am disappointed (specially since I've been looking forward to this experience for a very long time)... BUT, I must be fair and take this album for what it is and not what it didn't live up to be.

Who knows, maybe this is a grower? We'll see...

UPDATE #1 (06/11/10): Wow, yeah, no. I docked this down half a star after listening to We Have Come for Your Children. Let's see how I feel about it after The Damned's debut.

UPDATE #2 (12/08/10): After a number of repeated listens over the last couple of months, I still don't see it. I mean, there are definitely artsy moments, but mostly it is just a guy with a grating voice (not as grating as David Thomas' voice - but definitely more annoying) singing anthemic little punk songs with quirk thrown in to make it seem more avant than it actually is. And yet, if asked to trade it, I can't bring myself to...

Blank Generation is one of the most respected punk/rock n' roll albums of all time. For what reason I don't truly understand. Sure it is the biggest achievement of Hells career but that isn't saying much as his musical career was more defined by those he played with rather than what he actually put out. The material on Blank Generation ranges from average to pretty good. Nothing here is exceptionally fantastic other than the fantastic title track.

'Love Comes In Spurts' is a sexually charged innuendo that really sets the pace for the rest of the album. The track is more about the guitar playing than anything else. Hells poetics accompany the music but it really is the guitar playing from Robert Quine that really makes the album worth a damn. This trend really follows suit through the entire album.

There is nothing really bad here just boring at times. Was Blank Generation an important album when it was released, of course. Though as time has it remained vital, I hardly think so.

Fondateur de tout, membre de rien (Television, Heartbreakers), cela résume Richard Hell, grand initiateur du punk (certes avec quelques autres...) après qu'il a décidé de se couper lui-même les cheveux sans se regarder dans la glace. Infiniment plus intello qu'un Rotten (c'est pas difficile), franchement plus "arty" qu'un Strummer, Hell est quasiment l'auteur d'un seul disque, ce fameux "Blank Generation". Le titre, à lui seul, constitue le manifeste du mouvement punk, et les Pistols lui répondront quelques mois plus tard avec "Pretty Vacant" (en gros le même sujet).
Mais au fond, n'avons-nous pas ici à faire à un bon disque américain de rock classique? Car nous sommes bien loin des éructations des Londoniens pré-cités, et Hell, ami du divin Tom Verlaine, s'est entouré de musiciens sachant jouer et non de délinquants juvéniles ramassés dans le caniveau à la Sid Vicious (je sais, j'abuse de la comparaison avec les Pistols...), dont le guitariste Richard Quine, déjà chauve à l'époque (et vous appelez ça des punks?), qu'on retrouvera plus tard avec Lou Reed. Autrement dit, ça tricote sec au niveau des six-cordes (deux guitaristes), les morceaux sont composés et non crachés à la face de l'auditeur. "Love Comes in Spurts", mais surtout "Blank Generation", évidemment, et "The Plan" sont des grands titres. Tout n'est hélas pas du même tonneau: "Another World" s'égare dans des méandres d'une durée fort peu punk (huit minutes!) et "Down at the Rock and Roll Club" ne décolle jamais vraiment.
Il existe une version CD de cet album avec quelques extras pas passionnants. Hell, retiré du monde depuis des lustres, doit bien posséder des archives qui justifieraient une réédition digne de ce nom avec des tas de bonus et un gros son. Qu'on se mette à sa recherche!
