[February 7 2014]

For me, it wasn't so much the formative years as it was a mainstream rap album I REALLY liked (or at least listened to a LOT), and back then that's not really so easy to come by. For me at that time it was basically one a year (at the time I wouldn't call what Eminem was doing "mainstream" for example) - Blueprint, Lord Willin, Get Rich or Die Tryin', College Dropout - until 2005 when the tides started to shift and the big hit albums were more my style for a little bit. But College Dropout bridged that gap between what still sounded underground and exclusive in a mostly pre-internet, totally pre-Youtube era and what still made for really catchy, enjoyable, digestible pop music.

Kanye also cleared the way for rappers who were more blatantly being sold as comedians as much as wordsmiths. No one put their fists to their mouths and jumped up and down screaming "oooooooooh!" to "got a dark-skinned friend look like Michael Jackson / Got a light skin friend look like Michael Jackson" the way they did to the "god damn she fine but she party all the time!" antics of Jay-Z's "Girls, Girls, Girls", but we probably shouldn't have been so blind to Kanye's ability to deliver songs balancing that line between comical lark and social satire over and over again.

Thinking back on this album in the weekend leading up to its 10th anniversary (really Def Jam, no attempt to cash in with a huge retrospective box including mastered Good Ass Job mixtapes most Kanye fans probably haven't heard and what not? Can someone get me a line to their office?) it's most striking to me just how compelling the idea of it was. It's hard to remember now in a post-irony, post-Graduation world, but there was a time when pop rap was mostly really bad, gangsta rap was struggling to express itself from within the confines of the major studio system and underground rap was fractioning off into both boom bap purism and abstract dismantling of the form's very foundation. And them, you know, The Ummah or whatever.

College Dropout - and I think even at the time it felt capable of doing this - helped put an end to all that. You could put a song like "Jesus Walks" ten minutes ahead of "The New Workout Plan" and it could make sense. You could do drug dealer raps like "We Don't Care" and gather-round-the-dinner table testimonial like "Family Business" as album bookends and be confident the listener would feel like they were walking out the same door they came in. There had been good-to-great balancing acts in hip-hop before, but to my ears no one made that more palpable with a single release than Kanye with this here. To an awkward high school freshman who loved Black on Both Sides and Get Rich or Die Tryin' in near-equal measure, The College Dropout quickly became my everything.

Re-listening to it now (iTunes is registering 0 plays for all tracks, meaning I probably haven't listened to this album since May 22 2009 when I first scored it for the handbook) I can't help but realize how nostalgic this album was for me. Kanye's verse on "Get 'Em High" was one of my very first "I MUST learn this whole thing!" moments, and yes I already knew what 36 Chambers and The Blueprint sounded like. I wanted to know that verse. "Family Business" was the first beat that caught me flabbergasted by how pretty a hip-hop song can be, the way "I Got 5 on It" or "Ambitionz az a Ridah" taught me music can let its nuts hang as low as the gangsta-est rappers. I got to hear Ludacris and Mos Def on the same album, which at the time was all I ever wanted. Talib Kweli created an Okayplayer lurker and eventual addict out of me with a single line.

I could probably go on for another two or three paragraphs, about my memories for each individual song or how weird it seems to be listening to this album while looking at my old scores for it. Instead, I just want to say thanks to Kanye. And read this: Billboard: Kanye West's College Dropout - An Oral History.

[May 22 2009]

It's never been a secret that Kanye West is not a gifted rapper. If anything, he's a guy that has an ear for good music, and perhaps more importantly good hip-hop, and has used that ear to produce for and glean knowledge off of some of the best MCs in the world. After spending the better part of a decade behind the boards with occasional chorus or adlib duty, Kanye was ready to take his shine just as his mentor Jay-Z was ready to relinquish his own. The Roc was poised to deliver us...

Eventually, the Roc was poised to deliver us yet another larger than life, poster board celebrity figure to rap enjoyably and accessibly about the finer things over some of the most banging and innovative beats in the mainstream hip-hop industry. But in the beginning it wasn't about innovation or superstardom, it was about Kanye nearly dying in a car accident, loving his family get togethers, losing faith in long-standing institutions like education and employment, and remembering that no matter how phat the basslines get there's still nobody that's competing with Marvin, Anita or Luther on the vox.

In this context, Kanye's shortcomings lyrically are often entirely ignorable, fading away into the deeper framework of who Kanye West is. Rather than gimmicky Cam'ronisms or heady, half-baked DOOMpostering, Kanye's whacky brand of pop culture humor mostly ends up pointing fun at himself. Not that he was all fun and games, singles like "All Falls Down" and "Jesus Walks" carried for one brief moment the torch of meaningful hip-hop and videos almost single-handedly.

It's almost a shame that Kanye would continue to harness his worst traits to continue making good music. Before the stardom he was an entertaining guy but he was also thoughtful, and he wasn't afraid to let his guard down. Perhaps through all the egotism, there was a period where Kanye was afraid his success might never come, where he might have been lost in his room wondering if his talent was worth what he thought it was worth. Because this sort of album must be the result of an artist who urgently needs to have his voice heard. Considering the pressure he was under to become a success for his label, I'd say he did an admirable job becoming a success for himself.

Upon his signing to Jay-Z and Dame Dash's label, producer turned rapper Kanye West proclaimed himself "the Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer of the Roc". Kanye hit it right on the head. Let's go back to late 2003. Following the release of The Black Album, Jay-Z announced his retirement from the rap game (we knew he'd be back, but that's another story). Jigga and Dame assured us that they would have someone fill the void that the former left. Could the disgruntled Cuban cigar carrier Memphis Bleek finally get his shine? Or perhaps Roc-A-Fella strongarm Beanie Sigel? Or maybe new kids on the block Young Gunz? Nope, it ain't none of them. It's the guy that produced "Izzo (H.O.V.A.)". First of all, since when did THAT guy rap?? Second of all, why is a rapper-producer replacing Jay-Z?!? We've all heard less than impressive raps from the likes of Swizz Beatz, Timbaland, Puffy, Pete Rock, and J Dilla!!! Third of all, what the fuck is with this guy?!?!!? He ain't from Marcy or South Philly! He's a suburban upper-middle class kid from Chicago!! And where's his throwback jersey and saggy jeans??! Why's he dressing so fruity with the pink polo shirt, backpack, and Louis Vuitton bag!! That's right, Jay-Z fans. Hov's passing the torch to motherfucking Carlton Banks from Fresh Prince of Bel-Air!! Who makes these decisions?!? Marketing geniuses, that's who. Jigga and Dame knew how much cash they could reap from Kanye's sudden presence and mystique. The public's skepticism turned into optimistic curiosity when they heard "Through the Wire". Mr. "H to the Izzo" can rap after all and instead of getting shot, he got a metal plate in his jaw after a near-fatal car crash. Let's also remember with the arrival of Kanye West, the media took more interest because they acted like they've never heard a rapper not talk about guns and drugs before (apparently A Tribe Called Quest, De La Soul, Common, Talib Kweli, Mos Def, OutKast, etc. never existed). Which all of this hype built up, this new dude Kanye West better show and prove. Don't worry, he certainly does, in sales and in quality. Sales don't matter in a review, so I'll talk about the quality of Kanye West's debut The College Dropout.

I'll start with the production. For all two of you that haven't heard this album yet, the beats suck. I'm kidding! As everybody knows, The College Dropout updated the soul-sampling style the RZA did years ago and made into something brand new. Just in case you forgot, this album created what is arguably the most popular production style of the 2000s; the sped-up chipmunk sampling thing. As annoying as that style has become, the OG chipmunk style here still sounds as fresh as ever. Actually, I''m very surprised how well these beats have aged over the years, considering that other bootleg-Kanye beats are already beginning to sound dated (even beats from the more acclaimed producers from a few years ago, like 9th Wonder). The "chipmunk/soul" thing is the only label you can put on Kanye's production here. Other than that, the beats are highly unique, original, accessible to different audiences, and just well-produced. There's a good reason that so many producers bit Kanye's style after this. Kanye may have given good beats to Jay-Z, the Roc-A-Fella weed carriers, and Talib Kweli, but he saved the best beats for himself. That's all I need to say about that.

Now his raps. Jay-Z and Dame Dash had been priding Kanye as a producer that raps better than most rappers. How good is Kanye exactly? Most people emphasize that Mr. West ain't the best rapper. I think that's underrating him a little bit. He's no Lord Finesse, but I personally think Kanye is tight. He has a great flow, commanding voice, and witty lines. I would say that The College Dropout's case, Kanye falls somewhere in between the RZA and Diamond D when it comes to rapping producers. Lyrically, Kanye talks about different things. Even though the term "backpacker" was probably coined because of Kanye, he's not the most cliche non-gangsta rapper in the world (even though he dressed nerdier than said rapper). Kanye does brag about his skills (every MC does), but he doesn't have any songs that blast wack MCs and sellouts or anything. And even though he doesn't dis women, he's not afraid to use the word "ho" and instead of looking for that nubian princess with the afro that writes poetry, reads a lot, and doesn't eat meat or dairy, he's a horny girl chaser (most rapper-producers are). And Kanye won't act like he's not materialistic either. This cat does like him some nice name-brand clothes, ca$h, and fancy cars. If you didn't know it by his nickname "Louis Vuitton Don", his line in "Breathe In, Breathe Out" confirms it ("now I'm rappin' about money, hoes, and rims again"). But Kanye doesn't stay occupied on cliche, megalomaniacal, No Limit/Cash Money subject matter. This brotha can be deep too. Check the songs "All Falls Down", "Spaceship", "Jesus Walks", "Never Let Me Down", and "Family Business". The songs "Through the Wire" and "Last Call" offer some interesting introspection as well. And unlike a lot of "conscious" rappers, Kanye doesn't fall into the boxhole in which he can't have shameless fun without pissing fans off. 'Ye has his gags on "We Don't Care", "Get 'Em High", "The New Workout Plan", "Breathe In, Breath Out" and "School Spirit". All of this show how very down-to-earth and charming Kanye West is. That's right; I said Kanye West is down-to-earth and charming! The guests here are pretty cool too. Jay-Z holds it down, Ludacris does a hook, the singers that did hooks give this album more soul, Talib Kweli, Common, GLC, Twista and Consequence hold it down as well, and how cool is it that Kanye put Mos Def and Freeway on the same song and made that shit classic?! I can already picture another ill posse cut featuring Common and Beanie Sigel lol.

Going back to all the hype surrounding this album and my review up to this point, The College Dropout sounds like a classic right? Yup, it is. The College Dropout has aged incredibly well. As the years went by, I found myself liking it more and more. Yeah, "The New Workout Plan" is novelty and out of place, but it's cute enough to not be a bad song. Yeah the skits ain't all that either, but they're not enough to topple the overall quality of the songs. Shit, at least they're not oral sex skits (I'm talking to you, Biggie).

I recommend to any fan of music that has no kind of anti-Kanye bias whatsoever. A lot of people tend to judge Kanye on his award show misadventures instead of his music, so a lot of people hate this guy. His drunken "I shoulda won this bullshit award instead of [insert bullshit pop artist here]!!! Give a black man a chance!!" rants annoy me too, but I think it's silly to judge a person's music based on their non-music activities. I don't care how of an ass, prick, or moron a musician is as long as his/her music is good. That's my opinion anyway. Oh well.

Congratulations to Roc-A-Fella Records. Jay-Z is left free to take a hiatus with a new artist to keep the money and fame rolling in. *Memphis Bleek grits his teeth while dusting Kanye's platinum plaque*

This is definitively a difficult one. On one hand, it's well known (if not sadly very true) that Kanye West isn't the greatest rapper alive. Hell he's not even close. On the other hand, we have to admit for a first outing, Kanye is one hell of a producer and one hell of a musician. Yes I know that the majority of his rhyme's aren't too complex but there really is something captivating about his music.

As I said, it's difficult to analyze this album just because there are so many flaws and yet so many things that redeem it. Without much further ado though, I'll just begin with a simple track breakdown:

Intro/We Don't Care: It's an interesting way to start off an album, the scathing comments on higher education and the catchy beats with a great fusion of ironic hooks, it's a pretty damn good way to start off. Admittedly I can't get away from Kanye's occasionally annoying voice but for what it's worth, it's a great song and infuses the essence of Kanye pretty well in one fell swoop. 5/5

Graduation Day: 3/5 Unnecessary skit, although it's amusing. Flows though.

All Falls Down: 5/5 One hell of a song, interesting to me just because it's a great form of commentary and reflective while being critical. The soul hook is pretty chilling and the bravado makes this song classic. Not to mention the feature actually works.

I'll Fly Away: 3/5 Another skit that wasn't really necessary.

Spaceship: 4/5 I'll have to admit, this song took a while to grow on me. Originally I skipped over this song, but as I kept listening to it it's a pretty deep and meaningful song. Or perhaps I'm delusional. At any rate it's an enjoyable song that works and has a great beat over some clever lyrics.

Jesus Walks: 5/5 Pretty much the pinnacle of this album. The great insecure Kanye rapping about the drug trade. It's got the arrogance, the apologetic nature, one hell of a hook and beat. It's just classic. Not to mention that the fact that it was actually played on radio never ceases to make me chuckle especially if one listens to the lyrics.

Never Let Me Down: 5/5 *Edit It's actually a great song, I just didn't listen to it very well. So consider me chuffed. I don't know what turned me off in the beginning but it definitely grew on me. And upon a few listens it's probably one of the stronger ones. Go figure, I'm deaf apparently.

Get Em High: 3.5/5: The lyricism isn't too bad, but it's not memorable by any long shot. It's also a bit jarring although it's not a bad song in it's own right. It's just not memorable to me.

Workout Plan: 4/5 Maybe I'm a sick bastard at heart, but this is one of the most hilarious skits I've ever had the pleasure to listen to.

The New Workout Plan: 4/5 It's a jarring song. It really is, it's hilarious, stupid and mysoginistic. I have no idea why it's included but if anything it does a good job of throwing off a listener. I personally found that the song was catchy and not very engaging. There's definitively some interesting beat-work in this song but I can't rate it reliably on the simple virtue that I don't know if it should be included. I like the song even though it's a bit weird if not very effective. But hey maybe it's just my strange sense of humor that made me appreciate this weird train wreck of a song.

Slow Jamz: 5/5 The interruption in the middle, the beat and the simple-mindedness of this song make it so damn endearing. Plus it's just aweosme.

Breathe In Breathe Out: 2/5 I hated this song. Even though Ludacris gave us one of the most badass hooks and chorus lines (when I pull my piece out n*ggas say peace out..) it's just jarring and not good within the context of this album.

School Spirit (inc. Skits): 5/5 I don't know why, but I absolutely loved this part of the album. Maybe it was the fact that Kanye's charismatic hook coupled with his scathing view on higher education that drew me in. But I can't help but laugh every time I hear this part of the album. Not to mention the song itself is just great all over.

Lil Jimmy Skit: 3/5 Oddly this is where the skits overstay their welcome. It's just not very good.

Two Words: 5/5 This is one hell of a song. Political, hard hitting. Not to mention Mos Def killed this song. It's just a great song.

Through the Wire: 5/5 Personal and very engaging. Although it was difficult for me to like it at first, it grew on me. This is probably one of the best Kanye songs ever made. The hook is a bit weird but the lyrics more than make up for it.

Family Business: 4/5 I enjoyed this bit, it was a good way to conclude the album and it wasn't bad at all.

Last Call: 2/5 Although interesting on a first listen through it makes the album drag on more than it's supposed to.

As I said, this album (and Kanye in general) is difficult business. On one hand there's huge heapings of talent and cohesion but on the other, it seems that Kanye decided to take a quick break or something. It's a fairly strange album in the respect that there's so much bad contrasts and perhaps too much of a concerted effort to elongate the album. Nevertheless it's definitively a more memorable album from the 2000s era. Not to mention that it made some great songs (some still underrated). My gripes happen to be with some of the jarring songs (Breathe in Breathe Out, Workout Plan (and song), Get Em High) and the fact that there seemed to be a schizophrenic nature of it.

Nevertheless I still gave this a 4.5 just because it's replay value is so damn high. It's catchy, not dumbed down and yet gloriously opulent at times. In other words, it reflects the Kanye we know and love/hate perfectly. Full of insecurities and imperfections. Hell the length of the album could be a sort of over-compensation in itself. It's weird though, even though I think it's too long, it somehow works. Grab this album, it's a great work with a few flaws but few enough that I can entirely forgive it and wholeheartedly recommend to most people. Anyone. The key though is to get through the stupid public image of Kanye.

Get it, it's a solid album. There's not too much wrong with it and it exudes a personality. Even though the lack of direction is a bit annoying at times and some songs are just a bit crap, Kanye pulls through. I'd recommend it to most people as it's pretty easy on the ears. Admittedly the middle part of the album isn't great but the rest saves it quite well.

What is best is that Kanye West isn't only great on the mic, but he absolutely slaughters his shit on the boards as well. It shouldn't be a surprise that he produces the whole album by himself (well 'Last Call' was co-produced by Evidence) with some of the best beats he has ever done. With this release he blasted the soulful/chipmunk style into popularity. In this past years some other producers went overboard with it, but Mr. West did it too perfection here. You just can't get him on any weak beats. From front to back, it's a very strong effort. He was able to match any of his songs with the right beat. Each beat is a perfect match to the lyrics and purpose of the song. That must be a huge pro of collaborating with yourself. You always know what kind of sound you are looking for. At any rate with this album Kanye West made what might be the defining sound of the 00's.

Kanye's sound was very influential and his lyrics stood out as a breath of fresh air in mainstream music. Which is one of the reasons this album was so very succesful, going triple platinum. While many tried to copy his sound, you can't say the same for his lyrical game. The game was and still is dominated with the gangsta/materialistic rappers. There is still no balance, back then I thought Kanye West would lead a whole group of like minded artists, but he still seems to be alone. Regardless, Kanye West made a monster of an album and it has aged wonderfully.
