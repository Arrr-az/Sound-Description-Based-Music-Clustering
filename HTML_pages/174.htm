yerblues Feb 21 2010
+7 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Creedence Clearwater Revival's Willy and the Poorboys is the antithesis of The Beatles' Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band. Despite the two sharing a pseudonymous band premise and being "concept albums" that barely hang together after the first song, The Beatles' celebrated long-player was the result of months of studio manipulation; the lyrics a meditation upon British middle-class life. Willy and the Poorboys features just guitars, bass, and drums (and, occasionally, a harmonica and a washboard). Lyrically, CCR's subject material is preoccupied with the American poor and working class. Lacking any of the psychedelic pretension of their peers, Creedence had the audacity to make a thematic album more interested in the roots of American music than in recesses of the human mind. The result is one of the finer LPs of the late 1960s.

On it, CCR prove they can knock out great hits ("Down on the Corner," "Fortunate Son"), create sturdy grooves ("Feelin' Blue," "The Midnight Special"), appreciate American folk music ("The Midnight Special," "Cotton Fields"), and tap into powerful emotional reserves ("Fortunate Son," "Don't Look Now," and "Effigy"). The versatile album hangs together because of its consistent sound and purity of focus. Surprisingly, amidst the group's re-visitation of traditional American music motifs, they churn out the greatest anti-Vietnam War song of the era in "Fortunate Son," a sizzling track that points out the hypocrisy of our politicians in just over two minutes! The surreal closer "Effigy" describes the "silent majority" celebrating the "fire burnin' on the palace lawn" with "mixed emotion." John Fogerty's guitar solo at the end of the track is one of his most powerful. To a certain extent, this understated album draws on the zeitgeist of the times just as much as The Rolling Stones' decadence-tinged revolutionaryisms on Beggar's Banquet and Let it Bleed. Willy and the Poorboys should be in everybody's record collection.

Willy and the Poor Boys came just 3 months after Green River. I find it hard to believe that John stashed away a bunch of songs prior to that release so he must've sure been busy in the intervening period. Now this might have been put together in record time but there is some serious quality to this album with 'Down on the Corner', 'Fortunate Son' and the fantastic 'Effigy' which is just perfect as a closing track. Admittedly, 'Poorboy Shuffle' feels like filler as the harmonica work isn't a patch on some of the harp work on Bayou Country but that's easily forgiveable. Additionally, 'Cotton Fields' and 'The Midnight Special' are not original so less time was needed on those, but somehow they managed to instil their own sound on those songs too.

So, what was the driving force behind CCR releasing three albums within one calendar year? I think it was unlikely to be the band's idea and more likely to be the greed of Fantasy Records which really was taking advantage of the goodwill of both the band and the punters. Still, the punters did buy this in droves, mainly because it was good, not because they were hanging out for a new CCR release.

Released after Bayou County and Green River, and before Cosmo's Factory (all of which I rated 5/5), Willy and the Poor Boys is in a position where it can't really afford to be sub-par.
The two hits of the album, Down on the Corner and Fortunate Son, are very strong and leave high hopes. Down on the Corner and another great track, It Came Out of the Sky, lead us off and make one think this album will be another home run for Fogerty and co.
All seven other songs on this album are annoying, boring, or both. Two instrumentals, one after another, is never a good sign when you have a singer/lyricist as strong as Fogerty. Neither is particularly interesting, either. The other five? Repetitive and/or lazy. I really don't know how else to say it.

By releasing three albums in 1969, CCR simply overextended themselves. It's a shame, but it happens.

Pheebs421 Jun 23 2010
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Incredible that (not counting "Mardi Gras" which is best forgotten) the period during which CCR ruled the rock universe lasted just over two years. But during that period, they released six excellent albums - four of which (from Bayou Country to Cosmo's Factory) stand among the greatest rock albums ever. That's a feat that's difficult to comprehend in an age when most artists take 2-3 years between releases and are lucky to have three or four really good songs on each.

One of the tricks, of course, is that each of these great albums had 10-12 songs that clocked in at a total time of about 30-35 minutes (someone was paying attention to the Beatles). But, most important, was that these guys played ferocious gutbucket R&R in a period when bands, like fellow Bay Area alumni The Grateful Dead and Jefferson Airplane, were taking the youth of America on a totally different trip. And, in that fertile period, "Willy" has to stand out as my favorite.

This was an ALBUM, when that meant something. There is a cohesive feel to this album that works seamlessly. Even "Poorboy Shuffle", a consciously sloppy blues shuffle, works perfectly as a bridge between "Cotton Fields" and "Feelin' Blue". "Down On The Corner" and "Fortunate Son" (which Rolling Stone once deemed the greatest rock song ever) were the (desrvedly) smash hits from this album. Everything else, excepting Effigy, is every bit as good. "It Came Out Of The Sky" may be a bit silly lyrically, but I can't think of a song that gives a better adrenaline rush blasting out of the car stereo. But one of the greatest accomplishments here is that they took a couple of Leadbelly tunes and made them sound as if they were always meant for a group of white boys. This was accomplished while demonstrating the greatest reverence for the music.

CCR kept the flame of roots rock burning brightly, long before the tentacles of corporate rock could prevent music this fresh and exciting from reaching the mainstream.

it's really easy to take ccr for granted, but it's equally difficult to overstate just how great this record is. stu cook and doug clifford were just about the tightest rhythm section rock music ever saw, and are all the better for not being particularly flashy. john fogerty was the genre's most astute political songwriter--fortunate son and don't look now say everything you need to know about class, patriotism, and war without any of the era's hippie platitudes. that they're about the catchiest things you'll ever hear just makes them all the more powerful. a record that transcends its era and genre while being a perfect representative of both.

What a great band CCR were, some of the best three minute singles you would ever hear in your life came from this group. The THIRD album the group released in 1969 was Willy and the Poor Boys, how many groups release three albums in one year? All of their albums are littered with hits, but some are real gems as well. The obvious classics here are "Down on the Corner" and "Fortunate Son". "Down on the Corner" was the first CCR song I heard, and despite hearing it numerous times, I never tire of it. There are a few gems here as well, with "It Came Out of the Sky", their great version of "The Midnight Special", and the great guitar track "Effigy". The rest are pretty solid as well, but those listed are the best here. It is often forgotten how big this group was at one point, which is a shame. This is one of the best group of their time, releasing quality albums at an unbelievable clip.
