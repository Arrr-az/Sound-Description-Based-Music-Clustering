It's clearly a good album, even if only for the fact that you can break it down into its constituent elements and enjoy them all individually - but so many people are calling out good kid, m.A.A.d city as the rap record of the year that you can't help feeling the need to hold it up to higher standards than that, and it can't hit them.

Really, there's something that seems a little sad about that accolade to me, even if I don't account for the likes of Killer Mike's R.A.P. Music and El-P's Cancer for Cure being better albums to these ears. I'm not suggesting that the best rap record of every year should match up to Paid in Full, Ready to Die, and Illmatic, but it should at least be in the same ballpark; at the very least it should try, or something about it should be remarkable. Nothing about good kid, m.A.A.d city is - it's garden variety good, the kind of record that deserves to pitch into an end of year list somewhere between 30 and 40 before the big guns come out. The only thing really out of the ordinary here is a negative, if anything - Lamar bounces around between tracks with really pretty, soulful beats and introspective lyrics and hard club bangers where he tried to say 'bitch' as many times as possible within three minutes that it sometimes feels like you're listening to two different rappers. Diversity's cool, obviously, but here it's disruptive to the album's flow and feeling, even moreso than Vicious Lies and Dangerous Rumours, which has courted much more criticism for the same thing - I can accept the he's trying to tell a story throughout the album and that the shifts in mood are a result of that, but his attempts to pull it off actively make him a worse rapper, becoming a preoccupation that takes away from his natural talent. The deeper he gets into the concept, the more the album loses focus - "Backseat Freestyle" is probably the best thing here and it sounds like something from a mixtape rather than a studio album. The producers don't help, either - only Sounwave and Terrace Martin contribute more than one song, with ten other producers only getting a track each (eleven if you count THC getting a credit on "m.A.A.d city" alongside both Sounwave and Martin). On a record that would be disjointed anyway, it's odd that so many cooks have been pulled in to spoil the broth - especially given Lemar's relationship with Dr. Dre. He gets an executive producer credit for the album, but very little of it sounds like he was involved. How much better might this have been if he'd taken on more of the production work himself?

It's harsh, because nearly all of these criticisms are brought on by the way people have reacted to this album, rather than the album itself. Ignoring the skittish nature of the way it plays out, it's a largely enjoyable album with no specific mis-steps - Lamar obviously puts a lot of work into his lyrics and his flow, and none of the individual tracks are anything less than good. I really do expect something better from something so praised, though; if as solid a 7/10 album as you'll ever hear is getting hailed as the best thing around right now, how far have our expectations fallen?

Theres this short period of time (roughly half an hour) that I get all alone before my niece wakes up from her nap and we resume activities, the babysitting portion of the day, i.e., playing dolls, watching cartoons, and even dress-up. (Yeah, I have no shame in admitting that I play the damsel in distress part perfectly). The house is completely silent. You hear each individual buckle in the wood as the sun topples blood orange over a snow-capped horizon. You can actually hear her breathing in the room adjacent. You know you are alive because of the busybody neighbors doing chores and checking the mail and coming home for the evening, the kids unleashed from the surrounding school zone, the fat cat rolling around effortlessly for attention.

I consistently read during these few minutes of solace, the incubation period before my duties reengage. I keep the TV off. I wait until the absolute last moment to close the blinds and turn on the lights; being impatient and turning on the lights when there still is actual sunlight is akin to putting on a thick sweater with wet hair. I hate it, and thus continue to recline in my continent of make believe before I become adult again, as sole proprietor of the five-year-old a few feet away.

We play, eventually. She shows me her latest dolls or dresses. I speak easy around her in childish tones I wouldnt be caught dead revealing to my contemporaries with jobs and mortgages and blind dates. I play a character candidly named, Bunny. Hes a mischievous one, Bunny, always causing trouble, springing with extraordinary dexterity to and fro, kidnapping princesses and Barbies just for the hell of it; he whacks Fairy Princess Barbie with his feet just so he can watch her die.

Scenario 5c involves Bunny skydiving from a gigantic oak tree that reaches the moon like a ladder (the unused ceiling fan) and ends with the stuffed rodent suffocating in space (my best interpretation of this included). I cant help but laugh. But unknowingly, each and every time, I break the most sacred rule of make believe. I destroy the fantasy of it. My niece stares at me bluntly, not moving, frowning, actually, which causes me to stop as well, caught in her murderous glare. She asks, Why are you laughing? I respond, Because its funny. What follows is more silence, deer in the headlights type silence, a rising perspective of things, and as if I shouldve known all along, her dispelling of the current situation with just a few words.

Dont laugh. Its not real. Its only make believe.

Were forty-five minutes into Kendrick Lamars magnum opus/autobiography, good kid, m.A.A.d city, and a sordid piano fades off into oblivion, a skit emerges through the twirling smoke, the narrators, Kendrick Lamars, brother has been shot dead after an evolutionary series of criminal activities that shape many inner city kids in this day and age, and he begins to throw the words out, panting, being chased by his own imminent death, What are we doing? Who are we fooling? Tired of running. Tired of hunting. My own kind. But retiring nothing.

The environment youre born into shapes who you become, whether you like it or not, and even worse: if youre not even capable of being aware of your transformation. A few are lucky. Theyre able to break reigns and create their own destiny. But can you really be so sure, for instance, that if you grew up in modern day Uganda you wouldnt be supporting the execution of men and women who openly display their feelings for someone of the same sex? These analogies are endless.

But this is a story first and foremost, and makes more sense if we start from the beginning.

Sherane lays the foundation. Kendrick constantly refers and foreshadows his interactions with the girl hes currently chasing; later, in explicit form, he says out right, Lay with a snitch, which puts the first song into perspective and the album in full circle. The southern beats on Bitch, Dont Kill My Vibe elevate usually stationary rhythms because it works in context with the whole album, and most importantly because it is in its own unique way unlike anything else on here. Playable. Radio friendly. But with sonic flourishes that you rarely hear on the radio, a flippant rap style that evokes 2Pac and Eminem among many others. Interludes connect the revolving pieces. Theyre funny most of the time, as his mother leaves Kendrick about a hundred voice mails telling him to come home to return her van. And Backseat Freestyle is prophetically jocular of modern rap, and while its impossible for a dick to get as big as the Eiffel Tower, the consummate fantasy isnt.

The kids spinning out of control.

Theyre playing house, essentially. The body and mind is presumed indestructible because it hasnt been tested yet. Fellow friends, allies on the battlefield, when they die they only do so in the periphery, like an already swollen fish the family has been watching contently in the aquarium for days, and finally a courageous someone grabs a ladle from the kitchen, scoops it up, then disposes of it in the toilet. Death is release. Release from a turntable of excess, of continuous failures, blunts deemed worthless just like the drunk who now needs three times as much alcohol as you and I to scrape the perimeter of a buzz. Swimming Pools addresses this profusion tragically.

Hearing Kendricks voice mutate doesnt rattle me because we are aware of a (d)evolving emotional state. Home invasion was persuasive. Hes a chameleon in the truest sense, the artist, and the character. The jazzy overtones that start off The Art of Peer Pressure aren't out of place here, but welcomed. He goes from amateur gangbanger one second to a determined sweetheart on the Janet Jackson sampled Poetic Justice. He begins to think he can alter his environmentlike all hysterically delusional people do. m.A.A.d city is self-explanatory at first, but at the current fork in the road in Kendricks story, it rivals the excess seen in early to mid 90s West Coast Rap, but this time as escapist fantasy, the completely on purpose *beep* that harkens back to the pre-parental advisory sticker, to the sirens that echo a crime drama starring Snoop "Doggy" Dogg and Kendrick Lamar.

Then with our bearings in hand we hear a song like Real, which just might be the most important song on the entire album, and the gist of getting to the Lord forgive me. Things I dont understand part. By now this is a wanted laidback detour. The character realizes that this isnt a fantasy, after all. There are no 1ups. No retrievable save data. We all shed this thing called blood. Pain is real. And sometimes we forget were real. You love him, you love them, you love her/You love so much, you love when love hurts/You love red-bottom and gold they say queen/You love handbag on the waist of your jean/You love French tip and trip that pay for/You love bank slip that tell you we pay more/You love a good hand whenever the card dealt/But what love got to do with it when you dont love yourself.

Who knows if any of this is true or if it is a fantasy created by a rap wizard. If I told you I killed a nigga at sixteen, would you believe me? If a fantasy, Kendrick Lamar is some sort of maestro for creating one as believable as this. And will it even matter?

These musicians weve taken to liking were real people once, too. Kids grow up. Dreams are sometimes wistfully poured down the drain. Others are obliterated entirely. A supernova in deep space destroys two of our worlds for every second we are alive. We progress, even though nature, our environment, our own mad cities try and destroy us.

Promise that youll sing about me forever.

All anyone really wants is a legacy. A fantasy on some level or another.

I did something the last time I watched my niece I now regret. I took out my phone so I could take a picture of her playing, so it would always be remembered, stored for safe keeping for the future, before it's too late, before she grows up, before her innocence is lost. It was a simple mistake, but she stopped me before I could snap the picture, reassuring me once again, Why are you taking a picture? Theyre not real. Theyre not real.

Its only make believe.

Due to its spoken word, rhythm-based nature, hip-hop has been the primary home for musical storytellers ever since Kool G Rap, Chuck D and Rakim posited that MCs were authors and DJs provided the paper. "I start to think / and then I sink / into the paper, like I was ink," Rakim said, and thus the game was changed. Nas arguably perfected this idea, cutting a 9-song album in 1994 with five different producers that came across as intensely singular, as though the MC's will and vision could enact marshall law on disparate sonics and attitudes, coalescing a fractured state of mind into one that represented a particular time and place. At the same time, Prince Paul was offering the opposite hyopthesis: give a genius three imaginative MCs (or, in the further along case of A Prince Among Thieves, nineteen guys playing minutely specific roles) and he'll spin you a yarn that builds from a beginning to an end. 1991's De la Soul Is Dead presented a story within a story, as a trio of crackheads listened to an album about life on Long Island and criticized it at every turn, metaphorically representing the group's opinion of the media's influence on the hardcore direction hip-hop was taking.

Other MCs, most notably Masta Ace's early-2000s work on M3 Records, have expanded on Prince Paul's formulas by creating albums that play as audio books, but besides Ace and Doseone's Subtle project few MCs have seen hip-hop's narrative tendencies as an opportunity to weave explicitly connected music, where albums act as acknowledged sequels and the duty of the fan is to piece together the artist's 1,000 piece puzzle. Lupe Fiasco, perhaps this generation's most revered and pored-over by message boards rapper, infamously shied away from straightforwardness by splashing the story of Michael Young across multiple albums and mixtapes, daring audiences to find where the storyline begins and ends. Kendrick Lamar appeals to both formulas, as good kid, m.A.A.d. city alludes to previous works (primarily Section.80's "Keisha's Song" and "Poe Man's Dreams") without needing them and follows a very specific structure with a beginning, middle and end as implied by the cover art's subtitle "A Short Film by Kendrick Lamar".

And what an incredible weave it is. Due to its lack of abstracted notes ala Kanye West's My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy I'll save most of the notes for those smart enough to seek this album out, but in general terms the album begins with a betrayal, and then doubles back to the beginning of the relationship to detail Kendrick Lamar's life as an "Average Joe" (Overly Dedicated mixtape) in the buildup to his escaping the hood via record deal. With a little digging, good kid reveals itself to be a remarkably personal release for Lamar, with stories like his accidental smoking of a cocaine-laced blunt and the death of his Uncle Tony lending not only narrative heft but a very real gravitas to Lamar's presence. Generally, this would be enough for a hip-hop album: we love to believe Ghostface Killah is a dangerous man with a soft spot for women because his details overcome the usual tropes of hardcore corner store MCs, or that Drake is a be-cardigan'd playboy who can't stop thinking about his high school girlfriends because his word choice delineates the progress from one to the other so cleanly. But again, Lamar concentrates the ideas of hip-hop narrative and nonfiction into such a form that's shocking for how simultaneously accessible yet full of depth it is.

Nowhere is this more interesting than the album's lead single, "Swimming Pools (Drank)", produced in the typical manner of "I'm on One" / "She Will" producer T-Minus. Where both of those tracks were explicitly about affluent escapees of the hood mentality throwing money at strippers and drugs, so too does "Swimming Pools" feel on its surface like an anthem for late night stumbling. "Fill a swimming pool full of liquor then you dive in it," Lamar instructs mainstream hip-hop's favorite subject, uninhibited and unnamed women. But even in its disembodied form the song felt acutely aware of its ability to play with expectations and criticize the very song radio scanners would take it to be; as the track which acts as an axis for the character of Kendrick Lamar to have certain epiphanies about the actions he's taken throughout good kid it becomes even more subversive, a moment of nihilism more accessible than those that came before it (robbery, gang activity, promiscuous sex) that serves as illumination: I am Compton...how real is that? The next three tracks find Lamar enraptured in the realization that he's been all too willing to succumb to his environment, with "Sing About Me" deconstructing the various characters he's come to call friends merely by economic identification and social stratification. Then onward to "Real", where he doubts the lessons he's learned in Compton are true, and "Compton", where he pays dues to the world that made him ready for the world despite all the tragedy he's witnessed.

Providing the sort of semi-autobiographical character arc good kid is would be enough for some, but it's the lush environs surrounding this "short film" that makes good kid not only academic but accessible. "Sherane" and "Bitch, Don't Kill My Vibe" open the album amidst the 2012 west coast's vision of OutKast's Aquemini, the former presenting Lamar's vision of an Andre 3000 word barrage storyline and the latter Big Boi's laid back masculinity. "Backseat Freestyle" is a chauvinistic "Racks" creed whipped together by Kanye henchman Hit-Boy couched in the thematic conceit of freestyling in the backseat of his boys' car, imagining a world that doesn't exist. "m.A.A.d. city" revives Compton's Most Wanted leader MC Eiht over a second half beat change from Terrace Martin that feels like Dr. Dre's The Chronic had a love child with Chronic 2001; much as good kid builds on what's come before it as a story, so does this one track show how the west coast's singularity has created a certain kind of linear knowledge that's largely disappeared from the other regions (one might be eager to compare the sonic effect of this album to Nas' nostalgically modern Life Is Good).

It's so easy to praise Kendrick Lamar's proper retail debut (Section.80 was only available through digital outlets) that I'd feel remiss to acknowledge there are reasons it's not perfect. Repetition and excess reigns over moments of this album, as it fills an entire CD at a mere twelve tracks. "Real" is the album's most obvious sufferer, with generally efficient Anna Wise being required to incessantly repeat "I'm real, I'm real, I'm really really real" throughout a seven-and-a-half minute track that very easily could have been closer to five. And much like narrative albums that have come before it, good kid hustles through a setlist that's not nearly as stand-out and introduction-ready as Section.80 (itself a conceptual album, in a less linear sense) was, creating moments during your fourth or fifth listen in a row that feel less engaging than they did before.

But if those minor nitpicks are what it takes to bring an album like this, one that so effortlessly bows to the needs of both mainstream and underground hip-hop heads while simultaneously succeeding at creating the one sort of album hip-hop is both most ready-made for and most intimidated to produce, bring them on.

Two albums in and Kendrick delivers an instant hip hop classic, done, thanks for coming everyone, its over. It's hard to tell why hipsters love some mainstream albums and despise others, similar in scope and style. So this sort of inexplicable inconsistency left us with albums like this or 'My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy' (or how that hideous Kanye West album's name really was?) at the tops not only of 2000s hip-hop charts but taking the reigning position of the RYM's yearly charts. So this phenomena needs a proper analysis which I can't offer as I rarely listen to hip-hop and surely can't be an expert in it. Yet nevertheless it certainly puzzles me why all the GY!BE fans go drooling with this album.

But I know that in order to be effective for me hip-hop must be intense, disturbing and in-your-face with a good pinch of social consciousness thrown into it. So I can really feel attached to something like 'It Takes A Nation Of Millions...' even if I rarely see people with African blood in their veins in my everyday life and never visited US (though in my home city of Khimky, Moscow region, I recently met one Black guy who worked as a taxi driver and spoke fluent Russian - a nice indication of the globalization's positive side). But commercial albums like this do nothing for me, only killing my not too strong hip-hop vibe (bon mot, FTW). It's like Lana Del Rey of hip-hop - inoffensive and clueless crap which simultaneously is mainstream through and through but flirts with those indie suburban kids with banged hair-style who wear skinny jeans and think that they are so damn creative and unlike those proles.

'good kid, m.A.A.d. city' comes straight out of Compton, CA but lacks any sense of urgency and, most importantly, balls. It looks like Kendrick lived in the friendly cocoon in such a rough neighbourhood and got all his gang references from watching early 90s hood movies sittin' on his sofa with popcorn, and much more often he watched the most lackluster examples of these movies like 'Poetic Justice' or something similar to it. So this album is laid back, relaxed, chilled out and languid. It's hard to 'yell' "Bitch, don't kill my vibe" with such a laughable nasal voice like Kendrick does. How can one describe the album? Well, it has primitive beats, unbearable level of sugar and syrup, some worthless pseudo-classical background sounds, laughable pretensions at being intelligent and even 'avantgarde' with using words like 'metaphor', 'empathy' or even 'punctuation', plus 'wise' use of skits, arranged as everyday telephone conversations, and (talking about musical background itself) some shoegazing and electronic glitches involved, complete lack of that morbid sense of uneasiness, blatant auto-biographical concept which gets praised for reasons unknown, trite 'romantic' female backs and finally poor rhyming (like 'It go Halle Berry or hallelujah Pick your poison tell me what you do') combined with meaningless lyrics -oh, that's ugly. Albums, posing as being influenced by wretched ghetto survival lifestyle, must sound pissed off, rough, rugged and somewhat provocative (and, in best cases, thought-provoking). And here we get none of these with pervasive pop sensibilities, glamour aesthetics and overdose of syrupy bullshit being most prominent on 'good kid m.A.A.d. city'.

'Backseat Freestyle' at least has some energy and modest sense of experimentation in it but song gets ruined by primitive ignorant macho-type lyrics yet 'I pray my dick get big as the Eiffel Tower// So I can fuck the world for 72 hours' shows Kendrick at his most ironic. Following song, which is 'The Art Of Peer Pressure', has half-decent atmospheric eerie overtones and there are some other less-awful-than-usually moments like particularly anxious parts of 'm.A.A.d. city' which is one of the most diverse and inventive compositions here, so this upgrades my initial rating by half a star. But overall it's a plain bad album and one of the prime examples of mainstream poppy hip-hop getting ridiculously over-hyped.
