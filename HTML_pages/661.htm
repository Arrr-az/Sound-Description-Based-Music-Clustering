Year 2000 this is Kid A. Kid A, Year 2000.
Year 2000: Aaaaaaaaaaaaaaah

Mr. Yorke, I can’t understand you. I hear gibberish, voice distortions, and clowns frightening children and immature adults. Could you lessen the voice distortions, man in the red nose with a half-closed eye? The piano displaces my confusion. I’m no longer losing my sanity. Everything is truly in its right place. Kid A begins with a good-sized helping of vertigo. OK Computer could not have prepared me for this intro.

[I click pause as the song ends]. Okay, I think I’m ready for the title track. This is nice. It’s calming my blood vessels. Only a few popped during the first song but no serious damage. The voice distortions continue but the images are so well done that I am repeatedly reminded of the components that set Radiohead apart from other bands and artists: Thom Yorke’s intriguing lyrics, Phil Selway’s vacant drums, to list a couple.

“The National Anthem” kicks off with a badass bassline. And… is that… oh yes, that’s Thom Yorke’s actual voice. A saxophone enters and a chaotic tone reemerges. My head spins a thousand times. I again hear the smooth bassline, jogging through the song. Is that a wild elephant stomping on lions and stealing audience members’ cashews? No, no, that’s a trumpet. All right, I see what you’re doing, Radiohead. No I don’t. I have no idea. What sort of circus is this? The song ends with subtle strings and is somehow a perfect transition into “How to Disappear Completely.” Ooh, this is depressing. I feel as if, “I’m not here,” as if, “this isn’t happening.” Some days I feel invisible, people walk right through me. Thom Yorke gets me and millions of others who struggle through day-to-day routines. I search far and wide for songs that put me into a deep depression while remaining serene and delicate more than an acoustic Radiohead song. I show up at a lame party and turn up Radiohead’s acoustic gems to max volume. What will result? Likely “strobe lights and broken speakers.” The circus is back in town. Lions are mauling trainers, tight rope walkers are on their hands. My curiosity has never been more intense.

I tug on the indoor trees’ branches that swing before me. This new setting resembles an asylum. I rip off the branches, and then the leaves. I tear them up, and eat them. They go down smooth. The variations in the song are enough to keep any music appreciator listening. I become optimistic at the possibility of what could follow such a well done, instrumental track. “Optimistic” exudes a jungle feel. The circus tent has been lifted. I’m with the audience outside. The snakes wrap around my ankles, the hyenas wail, “the big fish eat the little ones.” I become self-conscious. Fill me with chamomile because relaxation seems a distant destination. I try the best I can to, “fight off all the animals,” but I am bloody. I am beaten. This album is wiping me out. Still conscious, though. Yearning to hear how this circus ends. “Optimistic” ends funkily and I’m back on my feet. Tent overhead.

Thom Yorke understands the impact Kid A has on me. He’s on my side. The only explanation Radiohead could give me for creating this album is if they lost their minds, or if they were, “living in a fantasy.” The screams in the song haunt, as the instrumentation bewilders. Rain pounds on the circus tent. Leaves brush by my feet. I pick them all up and consume them – so tasty. Oh no. What is this? “Idioteque”?

I will surely play this song at a party as to liven the party attendees. The party attendees will shriek and dive at me with knives in hands.

“His vocals are in every speaker,” they will incredulously say to me, with their hands running down their faces. They feel the, “ice age coming.” They grow cold; they believe I laced the song with speed. I grow weary of their criticisms. These party attendees are young, naïve, “the first of the children.” Some try to stab me but I escape. I run outside but remain close enough to the party so that I can hear the remainder of the song. Thom Yorke reiterates, reiterates, reiterates, reiter–

I am back at the circus, under the tent. “Idioteque” has rolled over into “Morning Bell.” Yorke must have wished to leave the asylum but was not permitted. “Release me,” Yorke would beg, “release me, please.” The song’s lyrics take on a morbid tone, “cut the kids in half.”

The images of “Red wine and sleeping pills” and “cheap sex and sad films” make me drowsy. While “Motion Picture Soundtrack” plays, Thom Yorke believes I’m going to fall asleep, that I’m crazy. I feel the same away about him, Captain Clown. He has harps! Is the dead air supposed to kill my memory of this experience? Am i supposed to lose my brain only for it to return at the album’s completion or am i supposed to believe this is a dream that could only happen upon listening to the entirety of this brain screw? I’m disillusioned, I’m awestruck, I’m drooling – every single time I listen to Kid A.

~ msloan

This album is closest a band can get to perfection. Abstract yet poignant, Kid A is an album filled with contradiction fusing elements of acid, rock, folk, trance and house together to create a truly experimental and epic album. The album at times goes from being simple to theatrical. Kid A is amongst the greatest of the greatest and will influence music for years to come. It is the absolute grandeur of the Kid A that makes it very intimidating to listen to, which is something that hard to find in music. In addition, the fact that Radiohead took the risk of committing commercial suicide just not to compromise their musical integrity makes this album a thing of legends. If there is one album that deserves to be heard repeatedly, Kid A is that album.

What do you release after you've released one of the best albums ever made? Where do you go? What do you do? How do you do it? A lot of bands have something that defines them as a band, and if Ok Computer is what defined Radiohead then what could you possibly do to better it? I think a lot of bands make an album that's deemed as their best work and follow it up with something that has a similar sound, they try to repeat the same formula - which is all well and good, but sometimes it just pales in comparison.

Radiohead didn't do that. They managed to do something completely different, and they managed to make even bigger, bolder and more adventurous sounds - though not necessarily better. Some people believe Thom York wanted to make an album that would result in commercial suicide, and that may have some truth but I think he also wanted to make an honest record and make something a little out of the ordinary. It's hard to that when Radiohead started out, they were a carobon copy of everything that was popular in guitar music around that time. I imagine people that heard Pablo Honey in 1993, and then listened to Kid A 7 years later it'd be near impossible to believe they're the same band. But that's just how far they came as a band.

Radiohead, well, Thom made an album that reflects a part of himself, his mindset and where he was at when Radiohead wrote Kid A, and alienate a certain area of the band's fan base. Swirling soaring electronic soundscapes, Avant-Jazz, a subject matter that resonates with you. The album is fantastic on every single level. I never thought this album would be a favourite when I listened to it, in fact I considered Ok Computer to be in a different league. And although Ok Computer may be better, something kept bringing me back to Kid A. I have no idea what, but I found myself listening to it again and again. I didn't even like listening to it for a long time, until it clicked. When you see the genius of something you previously couldn't see, it's truly a special moment. This album is everything a follow up album should be. It's a massive compliment to Kid A when I had to have a good think over which album I preferred, this or Ok Computer.

There's a point often made when talking about the merits of highly-praised albums, especially the old "classics": You had to be there. Which of course is the worst argument ever to back up the timelessness of said albums, but anyway. The fact is that some of us were much too young, or weren't even born at the release of most of RYM's Top 100 albums. So yes, we were not there. Fortunately, some "contemporary" albums have made their way to the top, the most obvious one being OK Computer. In my case though, the highest album to fit the bill is Kid A. I missed Radiohead's 1997 masterpiece by just a few months, but with Kid A I can finally say it too: I WAS THERE.

Actually I couldn't have been more there than I was at the time, as 2000 marked the peak of my Radiohead fanboyism. Because of their music of course, but also because of my burgeoning relation with my then-future-wife-and-Radiohead-fan girlfriend. We were on all message boards, collecting crappy bootleg downloads from iconic unreleased songs like "Motion Picture Soundtrack", "True Love Waits" or "Big Ideas" with a 56k modem. We went to see the band during the summer of 2000, when they toured to try their new songs live, and of course we also downloaded bootlegs from this tour. It was really the first time I was following the conception of an album that closely and building that much anticipation for it. I WAS THERE.

I was there, and I now know for certain that it's the main reason why, in all honesty, I don't really like Kid A that much. Hearing most of the songs live beforehand and getting a blast out of them was probably the worst thing that could happen to me, as it led to what I now call the "Hearing Most of the Songs Live Beforehand and Getting a Blast Out of Them" curse. When I finally heard the sloppy version of "Optimistic", the chaotic mess that was "In Limbo", or the excruciatingly cheesy "Motion Picture Soundtrack" featured on Kid A, that was pretty much the end of it (and I experienced the same feeling later with "I Might Be Wrong" and "Nude"). I could do all my best to let them sink in, or focus on the songs I hadn't previously heard, it was too late. To this day I still view Kid A – and, to a certain extent, Amnesiac – as The Album It Could Have Been (or That I Would've Liked to Be).

So let me tell you that seeing how its reputation has been getting higher and higher over the years, and how pretty much everyone and their mother are holding it in such high regard, is the most baffling thing to me. Because of what I just said, but also because, after all these years, I still don't really see what the fuss is all about. It's a nice album, I guess, but it's far from being the towering achievement of the band's career, much less one of the most original and influencial albums of the last decade as I've read here and there. Sorry Kid A.

I bought this because I had been hearing good things about Radiohead. At the time, I was surprised to find out they were still around. I thought of them as a one-hit wonder, and not even a good hit at that: the trite, whiney grunge song "Creep".

So, like I said, I heard good things. Words like experimental and progressive, comparisons to Pink Floyd. O.K. Computer was considered the better, but this one popped up in my BMG catalogue, and I needed to fulfill that membership requirement. So, Kid A it was.

So, what did I hear? Let's see. Experimental? Well, yes, there is some great use of strings and brass in a few places, and most of this does have its own unique sound. Progressive? Not so much. The music is more ambient that progressive. Interesting? Not in the least bit.

They may have done the smart thing and hopped off the grunge bandwagon, but it's still as whiney as ever. Somewhere around 50 to 60 minutes of navel gazing and emo lyrics with recycled beats, an overload of vocoder and a few blips here and there. The interesting parts? "The National Anthem" has a great groove that is missing from the rest of this album, and "How to Disappear Completely" fulfills what I had heard about them.

I guess this music is experimental and meaningful if you're someone who hasn't heard much of what else is out there. Otherwise, you're in for a hollow and uninteresting experience.

Edit 8/11/12:

Usually I don't give an album, especially one I really didn't like, another chance. It was tempted to just rip the two songs I had previously liked off of here and be done with it. However, this is quite highly rated, and by a number of people who have good musical taste. So, I thought, let's give it another spin.

I still can't find myself words to use like "genius" or "life-changing" or any of that, but I think I get a bit more why this album is liked so much. There is quite a variety of sounds on here. My complaints about the whineyness still stand, though, as my feeling that a lot of the music is repetitive and forgettable. Still, there are plenty of places on the album where this isn't so, and I have no idea how I overlooked a great song like "Optimistic".
