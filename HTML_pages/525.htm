Very good. Starts really strong with lots of rhythmic drive and then mellows somewhat as it goes on, not unlike Eno's Before and After Science, though unlike that record it never wallows in depression, it stays in touch with the rhythm that makes the first half go. And though Byrne's lyrics remain oblique for the most part, politics bubble up to the surface repeatedly (plus songs of social and ecological concern, which of course are in themselves political). But of course I like it best when it's political and rhythmic, as on the lead cut "Blind" - my favorite, unsurprisingly - but I think every song is well made here, guests are well-utilized, and the production that seems to rub some people the wrong way doesn't bother me, though it does lack the depth of their Eno era. But so what? They needed to go their own way, and from Speaking in Tongues on they had a new sound that reaches something of an apex here. But the end was near and even back when it came out I seem to recall that the word was that this was a difficult labor of an album for the group. I understand that the rest of the band was pretty fed up with Byrne's perceived domination of things and they had every right to split from him after his numerous side projects rather than waiting on hold until he chose to return to the fold and dedicate his energies to the group. But it's really a shame, because neither Byrne solo nor The Heads (solo or as a unit) have managed to come even close to the heights of what the group accomplished. This may not have been the swan song it could've been had they really gone into it thinking it was a final album, but it's a solid farewell to a great band.

Note - original U.S. vinyl that I got did not have the (then) CD only bonus track "Bill," easily the slightest song here. That song alone would receive a 3-star rating, but in the context of the CD it sounds OK surrounded by a bunch of better songs. But it leads me to question the accuracy of the track listings provided here.

Naked (1988)
Funnily enough, this is probably the wierdest and goofiest album I think I have listened to. This feels like a piece of work that is genuinely hard to rate because of these reasons. Little Creatures is endearing in the experimentation that it does and goes from a slightly different lyrical angle that David Byrne had been doing before. The following record, True Stories was an abomination and did not work in my view but with the direction the band was heading in I think Chris Frantz, Tina Weymouth and Jerry Harrison are fairly blameless. The fault of the post-Speaking in Tongues leg of Talking Heads' career lies in the fact that David Byrne became such an overbearing presence.

'Naked' is such a bizarre album for a few reasons. I personally don't have a problem with the instrumental palette, I think they keep you on your toes. The production just underlying the whole project makes the songs seem less versatile and more in your face a-la 'True Stories' (we don't like this), and not a-la Remain in Light (we like this). The experimentation on this album is ruined by something that is just not experimental. It demeans the sound of the band and ruins the potential that some of these songs had.

I give credit and take away credit from this album due to how uncomfortable it made me. 'Blind' is a bombastic and groovy opening track, backed by loud brass and is probably the easiest song to get into. 'Mr Jones' is a bit of a non-starter as these lyrics are just so annoying and then some more horns that sound like fucking Gloria Estefan. The country influences from 'Little Creatures' present themselves again on the track, 'Totally Nude' and there is something a bit more endearing about it but it still is not as interesting as many of the other songs on 'Little Creatures'.

Lots of people have compared this to 'Remain In Light' (please stop) because it has polyrhythms and some brass. 'Remain In Light' is experimental, that sound was mostly new to them (bar 'I Zimbra'). Trying to replicate a previous album of yours is not experimental. 'Naked' is a little bit of a whimper of a note to go out on for Talking Heads but at least they didn't do something that was incredibly conventional and not trying to challenge you in some way.

FAVOURITE TRACKS: BLIND, RUBY DEAR

WORST TRACK: MOMMY DADDY YOU AND I

I'm actually shocked at how much of a dip in quality this album is compared to everything else before it, including True Stories. I mean, it isn't awful or anything, but this truly feels like it finally misses everything that made previous albums Talking Heads. You can barely hear any of the other members styles in this, and David Byrne feels like he is doing an impression of himself most of the times. The wildly changing styles of the last album are still here, but they are for more forgettable. I think side A is actually pretty decent, and has something going on that I quite enjoy, but a good chunk of side B is forgettable at best, and I'd even argue annoying. It does end on a really cool track that feels like a cool style change, and feels like an angry end to the band. I mean, it's still Talking Heads, so of course it's still decent, I just think this is most definitely the worst in the discography. I'd hope that any of their solo efforts (or Tom Tom Club) would capture the energy lacking here, but I've heard mixed things there, save for the debut of Tom Tom Club, so I'm hesitant to jump in.

good albums episode 17: Naked (Talking Heads)
Talking Heads were one of the most critically acclaimed bands of their time. Essentially the poster child of the new wave movement, their quirky combination of rock with influences from all over the world has remained endearing to this day. A lot of the band's personality can be attributed to frontman David Byrne, who is certainly one of the more... interesting specimens of the music world. His jerky performances treading the line between being awkward and relaxed brought the band's first five albums from good to great, some even amazing.

While I think that they were at their best from 1977-1980, they didn't hit their commercial peak until 1983's Speaking in Tongues, which produced their only top-ten hit in the US in Burning Down the House. Now, I'm just making assumptions here, but I believe that the success of Speaking in Tongues inspired David to try and balance their new wave/art rock style with a more conventional pop rock approach. If this is true or not, it can't be denied that the back half of the Talking Heads' discography is far more pop than the first.

1985's Little Creatures is a bit of a jarring change of sound, but I'd argue that it's still a pretty good album. The cracks began to appear in the very next record, 1986's True Stories, which was a soundtrack for David Byrne's film of the same name. Mediocre reception besides, band tensions were rising due to David's increased control. In August of 1987, the group would return to the recording studio to record what would become the final nail in their coffin: 1988's Naked. While contemporary reviews were mostly positive, time hasn't been kind to this album with it now being considered one of the worst Talking Heads records. The group would go on a hiatus until David announced their breakup in 1991.

Was Naked THAT bad though? Well, it certainly isn't a good album, but bad is also pushing it quite a bit.

Well, to get one thing out of the way, the production isn't TOO bad. I don't know why, but so many great artists just somehow forget how to produce music after a few albums. Thankfully, Naked's production is competent enough to not be too annoying on the ears. It's still shockingly corporate for a Talking Heads record, and nothing sticks out whatsoever, but this is still one of the better sounding albums I've covered on this series.

Wow, there really isn't much to say for these songs. Most of it isn't even bad, just bland, forgettable, and mediocre. It feels very much like Talking Heads at home. Still, that doesn't stop the back half from being the baffling mess that it is.

The Facts of Life is absolutely terrible. Not only is the beat laughably bad, but the lyrics are just... wow. David just sounds like he's shouting words. They make absolutely no sense whatsoever, David is just rambling about shit like public hair and chromosomes and chimpanzees without stringing anything together. Just saying words without actually saying anything.

The lyrical lowlight of the record is Big Daddy. If that title isn't extremely telling of the song's quality, then I don't know what to tell you. Just look at these lyrics:

"Big Daddy
Oh, daddy's scraping bottom now
Oh, momma
Daddy, daddy, daddy does the talking now
Mesmerized like horny toads"

Yikes, David! These honestly might be some of the worst lyrics I've ever read in my life (and I've listened to Revival. Multiple times).

Yeah, uhhhh. Not much to say about this particular album. Cool Water was a surprisingly good song, but the rest of Naked is boring, forgettable, and completely unnecessary. David Byrne's ego got too big for the band to handle, resulting in the Talking Heads disbanding in 1991. The rest of the band actually continued to record music together under the names Shrunken Heads and The Heads. David even sued them for naming the band that. He lost. This isn't really that bad of an album, nowhere near bad enough to taint the name of the band. Meh.
