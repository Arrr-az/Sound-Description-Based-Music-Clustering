While _Tommy_ lacks the all out punch of _Who's Next_, or _Live At Leeds_, I still am going to give it 5. I'm doing it with great consideration of what what happening at the time _Tommy_ was coming to life in the mind of Mr. Townsend, one of only a handful of artists who truly deserves the over used "genius" moniker. I have a copy of the complete Rolling Stone magazine on DVD, and the first mention of "Tommy" comes during a very early 1968 interview with Pete. So The Who are on the heels of _Sell Out_, The Beatles had done _Pepper_ and _Magical Mystery Tour_, and the Stones had foisted _Satanic Majesty_ on the world. Townsend wanted to take the prevailing psychedelia of the time, and take it beyond the flowers, fairies, and gnomes. The working title for many months was "Deaf Dumb and Blind Boy", and the hype leading up to it was immense. Thankfully, the payoff was just as big. Credited by most as the first Rock Opera, the story line is engaging, and easy to follow. The music while often times powerful, still has a certain softness to it. Daltry has stated that he really didn't find his "real voice", that one capable of delivering the great "Won't Get Fooled Again" scream, until the band was touring behind _Tommy_. Like an opera, the album is better enjoyed as a sum of it's parts, rather than breaking down individual songs, some that clock in at less than a minute. It took someone with the brains of Townsend to maintain the spirit and even protocol of a true classical opera while making a rock opera. That helps keep it from falling over the cliff of excess and pretension, something that would be tossed totally out the window in making the movie version. It also made it something that could be performed live by The Who, often with spectacular results. So while I don't put this in the player as much as other Who albums, I still think it deserves praise for its historical significance, as well as it's musical quality and quality of performance.

Nicht Liebe auf den ersten Blick
„Tommy“ und Ich war alles Andere als Liebe auf den ersten Blick. Das Album musste Ich einige Male durchhören bis Ich es tatsächlich verstand und zu lieben lernte.
Die komplexen Gedankengänge vom Mastermind Pete Townshed sollen ja auch für Englischsprechende nicht ganz leicht nach zu vollziehen sein und deshalb bin Ich sicher nicht der Einzige der so über diese Rockoper denkt.
Das Album wurde 1969 eingespielt und erschien im Mai, des selben Jahres. Wie so oft spielte Herr Townshed das komplette Album schon mal vorab in seinem Heimstudio ein.
Diese Demoversionen sollten der Band beim Umsetzten der Ideen von Townshed helfen.
Heute verstehe Ich dank besseren Englischkenntnissen einen Grossteil des Albums. Ausstehende Fragen wurden mit der Veröffentlichung des Buches „Maximum Rock- Die Geschichte der verrücktesten Rockband der Welt“ von Christoph Geisselhart geklärt, der hier ausdrücklich als Quelle diente. Übrigens das Buch, die Bücher (3 Bände) sind sehr empfehlenswert auch wenn Ich nicht der grösste Fan der Band bin, so machte die Lektüre doch mächtig Laune aufgrund der spannenden Erzählungen und zahlreichen Anekdoten.  
Aber zurück zum Album: Das Drama nimmt seinen Anfang, denn der Vater von Tommy kehrt nicht aus dem Krieg zurück („Overture“). Tommy selber ist zu diesem Zeitpunkt noch gar nicht auf der Welt. Er wird am ersten Friedenstag geboren („It’s a Boy“).
Dann findet ein Zeitsprung statt und die Geschichte wird im Jahre 1921 fortgeführt. An Silvester platzt plötzlich der tot geglaubte Vater von Tommy herein. Die Mutter hat zu diesem Zeitpunkt wieder einen neuen Liebhaber, da ihr Mann schon seit 6 Jahren verschollen ist. Unter den Augen von Tommy wird der Liebhaber vom Mann umgebracht und die Eltern beschwören Tommy, dass er nicht gesehen hätte.
Tommy wird nach diesem Vorfall taub, blind und stumm und begibt sich in sich selbst auf eine spirituelle Reise („Amazing Journey“).
Zahlreiche Wunderheiler versuchen in der folge Tommy zu folgen, doch alle samt haben sie kein Erfolg („Eyesight to the Blind“).
Tommy’s Vater wird vom schlechten Gewissen verfolgt, da er erkennt hat, dass der beobachtete Mord Schuld an der Taubheit, Stummheit und Blindheit ist. Er versucht seinen Sohn am Weihnachtsmorgen wach zu rütteln und zu ihm durch zu dringen, doch dieser reagiert nur noch auf einen kleinen Flipperkasten. Aufgrund der Vibrationen kann er dieses Spiel trotz fehlender Sinne spielen („Christmas“).
Als wäre Tommy nicht schon genug gequält so wird er nun auch non von seinem Cousin Kevin gedemütigt, missbraucht und beschimpft. Dementsprechend entfernt er sich weiters von der Realität.
Eine Zigeunerin schafft es durch Rituale und bezahltem Sex zu Tommy durchzudringen, doch dies heilt ihn nicht, sondern verstört ihn nur noch mehr („The Acid Queen“).
Die Qualen von Tommy nehmen kein Ende: Obwohl die Mutter anfangs dagegen ist überlässt die Familie Tommy dem perversen Onkel Ernie, der ihn missbraucht. („Fiddle About“). All das erlebte wandelt Tommy in Energie um. Diese Energie konzentriert sich auf das einzige, was er wirklich kann. Er spielt Pinnball, also am Flipperautomaten („Pinnball Wizzard“). Er wird so gut, dass er schliesslich zum Meister des Spiels gekrönt wird. Sein Können macht Tommy und vor allem seine Eltern reich. Tommy wird fortan von einer Schar von Bewunderer begleitet und seine Eltern konsultieren einen Psychiater, der den verstörten Jungen ein für alle Mal retten soll („There’s a Doctor“).
Der Psychiater ist davon überzeugt, dass Tommys Beschwerden psychischer Natur sind und schickt diesen vor den Spiegel („Go to the Mirror“).
Tatsächlich erkennt sich Tommy im Spiegel selbst wieder und vernimmt die Stimme seines Vaters („Tommy, Can You Hear Me“).
Tommy ist nun vollständig geheilt und lässt die Eindrücke der Welt auf sich Wirken („Sensation“). Die Wunderheilung macht schnell die Runde. Der Pinballmeister wird zum gefeierten Medienstar, den Jedermann sehen will („Miracle Cure“).
Tommy wird von seinen Fans als eine Art Messias angesehen und sieht nach und nach auch selber als Einer an. Tommy fordert sein Gefolge dazu auf über das Flipperspielen zur Erleuchtung zu gelangen. („I’m Free“).
Tommy’s Popularität ist mittlerweile so gross, dass auch Onkel Ernie durch ein Ferienlager von Tommys Ruhm profitieren muss. („Tommy’s Holiday Camp“)
Die Anhänger legen die anfängliche Blindheit ab und glauben, dass sie Opfer von Selbsttäuschung und Manipulationen wurden. Doch Tommy ist uneinsichtig und hält an seinen Lehren fest. Seine Anhänger wenden sich daraufhin von ihm ab und sogar gegen ihn. Tommy bleibt alleine zurück. Er wendet sich in einem Gebet an die Götter oder den Gott. So endet die Reise von Tommy. Zum Schluss ist er zwar alleine aber nicht einsam.
(„We’re Not Gonna Take It“)
Wie gesagt muss man dem Album etwas Zeit lassen, doch dafür wird es von Mal zu Mal besser. Und erst nach dem man den Sinn des Albums einigermassen verstanden hat, erkennt man wie genial das ganze Konzept umgesetzt wurde.
Das Album funktioniert im Stile einer Rockoper mehr als Gesamtwerk als durch Einzelsongs. Obwohl es mit „Pinnball Wizzard“, dem fulminanten Instrumental „Sparks“, „Christmas“, sowie mit dem finalen „We’re Not Gonna Take It“ einige Hits draufhat.
Die Herren Daltrey, Townshed, Moon und Entwistle befanden sich 1969 musikalisch ohnehin auf dem Höhepunkt und deshalb muss man über die Qualität der Song nicht streiten.

If one has the ear and the time, you can hear chords from Tommy being worked out as far back as “The Magic Bus” by The Who. And while it may seem easy to dismiss such anomalies as just matter of fact, or that these are the chords Pete Townshend hears in his head, I can't help but feel that Pete had been working toward this release subconsciously for many many years.

Like most people in 1969, I dug the album, but then I didn’t really know the words, as I had gotten the release on cassette, where I was filling in a lot of spaces with la la la’s and such. And despite the beauty of the music, and the fact that this piece had been released as a rock opera of sorts (though I grow gravely tired of the notion of a rock opera) from the very first listen, there was something that did not sit just right with me. Yeah, I listened to it, I played it for friends, I even recommended it as a great body of work, yet in the same breath, there was a bad taste left in my mouth with each listen, something that festered within me, and after a month of listening, I put the album away, never to be played again.

Rather then the music, I am speaking to the concept of the album here, where for me, the notion of the story of Tommy is akin to the notion of a rape fantasy, where the girl has been awakened to her sexuality, even though by rape, and has now blossomed into a raving beauty, strong and secure, perhaps even glad that she had been deflowered in such a violent manner ... no longer young, deaf, dumb or blind. But this work is far more insidious and dark, and perhaps true on a subconscious level for Mr. Townshend.

Townshend later wrote, “The greatest terror for an adult who remembers sexual abuse is the thought that other children might suffer as they did. This terror echoes for me. In my writing in the past, especially within the context of Tommy, I have created unusually unmerciful worlds for any infant characters. I am often disturbed by what I see on the page when I write these things, never more so than when I draw on my own childhood.” That said and paradoxically strange, Townshend claims to have come up with the concept of Tommy after being introduced to the work of Meher Baba, and attempted to translate Baba's teachings into music. Nevertheless, the story of Tommy is filled with dark and often subtle references to child abuse, and these references can be found on many levels on other albums by The Who. One of the most disturbing is when Tommy is left in the care of a bizarre relatives, Uncle Ernie and his cousin Kevin, despicable characters who prove that families do not offer safety, but are the first to take advantage of their own. He is subjected to the consumption of LSD without his knowledge or understanding, he suffers a mental breakdown without the necessary capacity to comprehend, and yet rises like a Phoenix from the ashes, as all great and exceptional artists are expected to do, drawing their inspirations from pain, suffering and tragedy, instead of a happy childhood.

This might seem acceptable, though tragic, but even after all of this abuse, once Tommy becomes the Pinball Wizard, his family again tempts and exploits the boy. This would certainly lend credence to the notion that Pete was abused as a child and that his parents ignored him until he became rich and famous, only then wanting their share of his fame.

For me, the album Tommy is as disturbing as Pink Floyd’s The Wall, another album that I can not listen to as a conceptual piece. In The Wall we finally see that the context of all Pink Floyd albums have revolved around the loss of Syd, the loss of Roger’s father during World War II, and the mental abuse inflicted on the school children by the instructors. This is all horror of the first magnitude, it is reality in this strange world, where I don’t believe that most fans connect with these releases on that level. If that is by choice or by omission, I could not venture to say. What I do know and understand, is that anyone who can listen to Tommy without feeling anger and rage, lacks a certain core value in their humanity.

As beautifully done and artistically excellent as this album is, this is still nothing more than pornography, yet another exploration into the “Story Of O,” where the truly helpless come out on the other side pleased that the lessons they have learned at the hands of others have valued their lives and made them the successes they are. Unfortunate that’s just not true, and the life of Pete Townshend proves that ultimately with a great deal of sadness.

It would be remiss of me not to say that looking at such issues found on Tommy does not have merit, as certainly anyone who enjoys this release should not be considered to support the notion of child abuse, very much like the book “The Basketball Diaries” by Jim Carroll, who kept detailed notes, poems and thoughts during his downward spiral into drug addiction, where simply watching or reading his story is not tantamount to supporting or glorifying drug addiction ... it is all a visual and musical journey, dark as it may be, in the life of one of the greatest musicians of our time.

With this review (and others like it that I have written), I rate the edition, not the album itself. My hope is to provide food for thought when deciding whether to "upgrade" to this or that edition.


First, one rarely mentioned reason for getting this particular edition is the fact that the song "Eyesight To The Blind" is a different mix, with entirely different vocals (a la "Bargain"). The story I heard (from a record store manager who claims to have read the blurb that came with the release) is that the guys at Mobile Fidelity found this track on one of the tapes and contacted Daltry about it. They played it for him and (he tells me) Daltry didn't even remember doing it! He did, however, recommend replacing the regular version with this track.

The singing style of the Mobile Fidelity track has Daltry playing with the bold, out-front style that quickly afterwards became his signature. The regular version, as you know, continues the earlier sound, using the harmonized falsetto that kept myself and many others from fully appreciating The Who until the Woodstock performance and, later, _Who's Next_ and _Quadrophenia._ This change is seen clearly by comparing the bootleg of the Amsterdam hash bar performance against what we have of the Woodstock performance and even the _Live At Leeds_ ("Deluxe Edition") show. The Mobile Fidelity version of this song (rightly) puts Daltry out in front with the powerful sound that brought The Who -- and, I might say, Rock -- out into the limelight for all to hear.

My bet is that most Who fans will like the Mobile Fidelity version a lot better than the original. Yes, it comes off like the unpolished "Alternate Take" that it is. But one can only wish that they'd worked up this version rather than sticking with the one we ended up with! (Indeed, I much prefer the Mobile Fidelity version of The Velvet Underground's "All Tomorrow's Parties," with a single vocal track rather than the familiar two!)


As gold reissues go, Mobile Fidelity's _Tommy_ came out pretty good.

I can't say that for all of the gold reissues, however: the _Blood, Sweat ,and Tears_ (David Clayton Thomas) sounds quite muddy, _Aqualung_ is so "clean" that you can actually hear the tape dropouts (unlike the Anniversary Edition, which is clean as a whistle), and a most crucial element in "If" by Pink Floyd is missing: the very high-pitched tone that _should_ be occurring when he sings, "If I go insane / Please don't put your wires in my brain" (though I haven't examined the regular version to compare).

But many of the gold reissues are excellent, and that's why I'll take my chances with them even on an album that hasn't always been one of my favorites. Heck, although I'm closing in on my fifties, I've still got a lot of learning to catch up on, musically.

What I like the most about the Mobile Fidelity reissues is that for the most part, they tried to sound as much like the original album as possible. This seems no longer to be the case with the tendency to "remaster" albums, attempting to improve on the original (and often succeeding). But where does the unintentioned emotion of an original's guitar chord (say) end, and the "better" fidelity of its midi-ized replacement begin? Again, I haven't known Mobile Fidelity to "remaster" anything. They tend only toward finding a sound that's as close to what they sought with the first pressing as is humanly possible.

That said, I can remember my first copy of "Tommy": the unique tactile "feel" of the album cover, the errors in the lyric sheet ("Hey you smoking mother nature / You missed the bus" and others), the very odd, not-quite-psychedelic graphics, and, most of all, the sound -- the crisp, clean sound of the acoustic guitar, the piano, and, for practically the first time, ever, the amazingly clean reproduction of the overamplified electric guitars and the horrifyingly abused drum kits.

I can remember in about 1968 or so, my mother was reading an excerpt from a magazine article about how they had to completely redesign the cymbal for Rock music in the late 1960s because regular cymbals couldn't take the abuse that Rock drummers dished out! It was only a year or so before, you remember, that the engineers who recorded Hendrix at the Olympia simply didn't know how to commit loud music to tape. Those experiments and learning experiences were left for the likes of Wally Heider and others. Ah, but Tommy, as hokey as it got in places, was a masterful work of sound engineering -- for its time!

Although I cannot speak for the other reworks of this album, this Mobile Fidelity version gives us a clear vision of just what they could do with the limited equipment available to them at the time. I suppose that any skilled engineer could take an old tape and process it so that it matches the sound of today. That's not what we have in these old masterpieces, however. Acts such as The Who, Jimi Hendrix, Pink Floyd, The Beatles, and many others used raw ingenuity to develop new sounds that had never been made before.

The difference dawned on me one day while shopping at a musical instrument store during the 1970s. While buying pads for my flute. One of the salesmen was demonstrating this new guitar pedal-effect box that made his guitar sound, among other things, like this one Van Halen song that had recently become a hit. Never mind that Van Halen may have worked with his engineers for weeks to come up with the desired sound: now a kid who can strike the right chords can have the same sound -- except that it's not the same.

Methinks the same goes for many of these "remastered" CDs: what I want is to refurbish the original sound rather than re-creating it.

Cliff
Retired Audio Engineer
