Heaven and Hell a 'volta por cima' do Black Sabbath. 'Sabotage' um dos meus discos preferidos, e acho que se Ozzy tivesse sado da banda depois do lanamento desse lbum, ele estaria fechando uma fase com chave de ouro, e logo depois entraria outro fantstico cantor, Ronnie James Dio. Porm, foi lanado o 'Never Say Die!', que no foi msica para os meus ouvidos e no foi bem sucedido como os discos anteriores. At Ozzy chegou a dizer anos mais tarde que na poca o pessoal da banda andava afastado, principalmente ele e Tony, e 'ningum estava realmente adorando fazer aquilo'.

Ento, veio Dio, trazendo um som mais srio banda. Comparando as fases Ozzy e Dio no Black Sabbath, parece que o som da banda com Ozzy mais cru, j que sua voz favorece esse tipo de msica; com Dio, o som ficou mais tcnico, mais 'arrumado', e, ao mesmo tempo, meldico. Em 'Never Say Die!' havia algo como 'precisamos lanar esse disco, precisamos resolver os problemas financeiros', e sim, eles resolveram graas ao bom nmero de vendas do disco na Gr-Bretanha. Acredito que a chegada de Dio tenha dado um novo esprito banda: sua voz flexvel deu mais liberdade aos riffs criados por Iommi.

"Wishing Well" e "Walk Away" so, dentre todas as msicas, as que escuto com menos frequncia, porm, continuam sendo muito boas.

Milestone with Dio
While not being Sabbath's overall best album (Paranoid and Master of Reality are the most definitive masterpieces, for those wondering), this is the essence of the Dio-era.

It's monstrously heavy but still it's an atmosphere-filled album, mostly due to the tastefully used keyboards of Geoff Nicholls. Iommi's soloing also hits the peak here, maybe, and the melody craftsmanship really deserves a special mention (Die Young comes to mind).

Although there are parts which are modernized and this is instantly recognizably sabbath-ish, it marks Sabbath's entrance into the '80s with style.

There is a high amount of greatness, so let's see the highlights: Neon Knights is fast pounding attacking metal anthem, not very different from the flourishing NWoBHM movement, Children of the Sea is a ballad with strong atmosphere provided by the keyboards, the title-track is a monstrously heavy mid-paced song with one hell of a riff and is an eternal milestone in heavy music. Die Young is the highest of the high, and the fastest one here, with some smooth soloing and Dio's godlike vocals, while the closer Lonely Is the Word crushes everything in its path with mammoth doom riffs (and remember it's 1980!)

This is essential. Not only for Sabbath, Dio, heavy metal or '80s metal fans. For metal fans widely

Greatest songs: Die Young, Heaven and Hell, Neon Knights
Least great songs: Walk Away, Lady Evil

#2247 Best Album of All Time (acclaimedmusic.net)
Heaven and Hell is only my second Black Sabbath album, the first being obviously Paranoid. I cannot offer an opinion with regards to Ozzy Osbourne leaving the band without hearing the state of their albums shortly before this one. Perhaps Dio did save the band, who am I to argue? All I can say is that I knew immediately that I'm not a huge fan of Ronnie James Dio's annoying melodramatic delivery. Technically he has a better voice than Ozzy but there is no charm or character to his performance. Also it's not just the lead singer that I have issues with: to my ears Heaven and Hell sounds awfully average and very generic. Paranoid was an album which piqued my interest in a genre which I generally abhor. Black Sabbath sounded different, but Heaven and Hell sounds like everything I hate in Hard Rock. There is no intriguing experimentation, it sounds safe and the lyrics are embarrassingly stupid. If you like all those other generically dumb eighties hard rock bands, I can see why this might appeal. While I believe Heaven and Hell is generic sounding, it's not to say that it is bad, just thoroughly disappointing. 'Neon Knights' has energy backing it up and the guitar riff is true to the Black Sabbath sound that I loved, but Dio is seriously ruining any enthusiasm that I may have. 'Children of the Sea' comes in with Dio's annoying operatics, which to be completely honest I find to be embarrassing. 'Lady Evil' is equally as embarrassing, I would be ashamed to play this with an audience. Things take a bigger nosedive into mediocrity after those few tracks. If things couldn't get even more embarrassing, Wishing Well comes in with the most cringe-inducing lyrics ever. Some people moan that this album is too short, I say that it is 40 minutes too long; that's time that I wish I had back.

Album Rating: 59/100

In early 1979 Ozzy Osbourne's behaviour became too much for the rest of Black Sabbath, and Tony Iommi in particular, to tolerate and it was clear something would have to give. Over a year had been wasted in drug fuelled alcoholic stupors and little or no new material had been produced. With record company pressure to record a new album increasing Iommi took the decision to fire Osbourne from the band.

At the time, for many a Black Sabbath without Ozzy Osbourne was an unthinkable proposition. When it had been attempted briefly in 1977, with Dave Walker on vocals, reaction to their appearance on the BBC television programme "Look !Hear !" had not been particularly favourable and Osbourne had returned soon after. However, 1979 was a different time altogether and with rock music just about to embark on a new lease of life thanks to NWOBHM Black Sabbath, as one of the pioneers of hard rock music, could not afford to be left toiling in the wake of the younger hungrier bands. On the suggestion of Sharon Arden, daughter of Sabbath manager Don Arden and ironically future wife of Osbourne, Ronnie James Dio who had recently fallen foul of Ritchie Blackmore's constant need for change and been fired himself from Rainbow was approached with a view to replacing Osbourne.

Dio officially joined the band in June 1979 after a few secret writing sessions with Iommi which took place before Osbourne had been officially fired. At the time Geezer Butler was unsure of whether he wished to remain in the band and briefly departed to be replaced by Craig Gruber, Dio's old mate from the Elf and Rainbow days. Butler later returned and is pictured on the back of the cover but Gruber has claimed that it is actually him playing on the album although this was denied by Iommi. Geoff Nicholls of Quartz was also brought in to play keyboards and may at one point have handled some bass playing duties in the initial sessions. The use of Martin Birch who had worked on many of the Deep Purple, Rainbow and other Purple family associated albums as producer led to the band briefly being sarcastically referred to as Black Rainbow ! In fact he went straight from this to the "Ready An Willing" album by Whitesnake. Not a bad one-two there Mr Birch !

"Heaven and Hell" was recorded at Criteria in Miami and Studio Forber in Paris with the initial sessions starting on October 1st 1979 and was a complete change of style for a band known for hard riffing and less than melodic vocals. Dio was about as far removed from Osbourne as you could imagine and there was a great deal of anti Dio feeling from hardcore Sabbath fans at the time. Osbourne had a limited range but Dio had one of operatic stature and as he was happy to sing across the riff and bring in his own vocal melody rather than sing directly to the tune of the song it gave them more scope to experiment with song structure and gave them a more tuneful and melodious sound but without having to sacrifice any of the heaviness. Opening track 'Neon Knights' is a perfect example of this as it comes thundering out of the speakers at a million miles an hour with a typical Iommi riff and Dio's vocal melody bears no resemblence to it whatsoever. It is as heavy as anything the Osbourne line up came up with but has far more melody and is far more accessible to a mainstream audience. A fact proven by its lofty chart position of 22 in the UK singles chart. All of the tracks on the album are credited as Dio/Iommi/Butler/Ward compositions but in all likelihood all tracks were penned by Dio and Iommi alone. Butler's entire contribution to the album is open to speculation but 'Neon Knights' was the last of the new tracks to be written and by that time Butler was back on board so it is fairly safe to assume that it is Butler playing on the track although whether he really had any input into the composition is doubftul. Drummer Bill Ward, by his own admission, has no recollection of any of the recording sessions due to his substance abuse at the time and I would seriously doubt that he had any input into song composition. The fact that the riffs always came from the prolific Iommi and that the Dio lyrics were obviously ones he had in mind for the next Rainbow album as they bear no resemblence to the usual "Sabbath" type lyrics lead me to believe the decision to declare group composition was a political one rather than an artistic one. From the final track written and recorded we go straight to the first one 'Children of the Sea' which began life at the very first Dio and Iommi writing session in early 1979 before Osbourne had been fired. Starting with an acoustic intro and a slightly falsetto lyric from Dio it soon builds into a classic Sabbath riff and song. The low key choral moaning chant towards the end is classic Sabbath. It quickly became a live favourite and is clearly not just one of the albums highlights but is also one of the best Sabbath ever recorded. 'Lady Evil' starts with a bouncy almost funky riff and with its subject matter vaguely reminds of 'Startstruck' from Dio's Rainbow days. In fact it could almost be 'Starstruck' revisited and along with the album closer is the most Rainbow like offering here. Probably explains why it is one of my favourites too. Iommi throws in a great dual paced solo and I'm surprised it didn't see a worldwide 7" release. There was an edited version which has since been issued on the remastered version of the album but as far as I'm aware this didn't see mainstream release in the larger territories. The first half of the album ends with the title track, a track which has since become a staple of rock radio stations the world over and is as recognisable as any rock song ever written. Iommi came up with the initial riff and tune during the aborted final sessions with Osbourne and there were suggestions that a version, obviously with different lyrics and a very different vocal melody was recorded. However if this happened they were destroyed and apparently no copy exists. It starts with a typical Iommi riff and it is not difficult to imagine Osbourne singing with over it. However, when Dio starts to sing the song gets a new lease of life and it is taken to a completely different place than the original line up would have managed. Iommi's slower bluesy solo at the midway point is amongst his best before he also throws in a couple of shorter faster ones during the track including a particularly nice fast one towards the end before he ends the song with a gentle acoustic outro. In fact the song is the perfect vehicle for extended solo's and improvisation and it was not unusual for it to be extended over the twelve minute mark in a live setting. It has been suggested that the distinctive bass line was written by keyboard player Geoff Nicholls as it bears some similarity to the Quartz track 'Mainline Riders'.

The second half of the album is not quite on the same level as the first and starts with the pleasant but pretty ordinary 'Wishing Well'. As with 'Lady Evil' this track has a strong Rainbow feel. 'Die Young' starts with a slow atmospheric guitar solo which was extended considerably during live performances before launching into a fast paced stomp. Then the middle section of the song sees it drop down to a soft acoustic ballad pace and Dio delivers a soft falsetto vocal before the song thunders back to full power again. Iommi's solo towards the end of the song is particularly fast and the light and shade of the soft and heavy parts give the track its majesty. Craig Gruber has claimed that he brought the initial idea of the song and the tune to the table but this has never been confirmed by any of the other members and no writing credit was ever given to him. ' 'Walk Away' is another pleasant enough song without ever really getting out of the ordinary and along with 'Wishing Well' would count as the low point of the album. Having said that though they are both still pretty decent tunes. Throughout the whole album you are reminded more of Dio's Rainbow albums than the previous Black Sabbath ones and final track 'Lonely Is The Word' is maybe the biggest culprit. An extended bluesey tune with a typical Dio vocal similar to many on his Rainbow albums accentuates this point, as does the use of the strings during Iommi's closing solo. It is still a great song though and a great end to a classic album.

When the first Black Sabbath album not to feature Ozzy Osbourne was unleashed on the world in April 1980 initially it met with a mixed response. Fans of Dio era Rainbow loved it and took it to their hearts more than the current Rainbow album of the time "Down To Earth" which featured Graham Bonnett on vocals. Bonnett was often heckled at Rainbow gigs by fans calling for Dio. For many of them this is the Rainbow album that never was and is a natural progression from "Long Live Rock n Roll". That of course is a little ludicrous as the music on the album was composed by Iommi who is a far different guitarist and composer to Ritchie Blackmore. Lyrically and vocal melody wise though you have to accept that they had a point. For some die hard Ozzy Osbourne fans a sense of loyalty to him made it difficult for them to accept Dio at first but the diminutive singer's charisma and respect for the back catalogue soon helped him to win them over. A new era for Black Sabbath was born with a devils horm salute, the first of three purple tinted vocalists was now at the mike and the future looked very bright indeed.

"Heaven and Hell" is a classic rock album from a classic golden time of British rock music. Whether you think of it as a Black Sabbath album or as an album by a different band going under that name is up to you. But not to listen to it and accept it for the classic album it is regardless of line up would just be plain stupid.

...and here is where Sabbath pulls off the greatest trick in their grim metal existence. This is a totally new Sabbath, nimble and forceful to a degree that belies their years, this looming maturity and new-found zest for life leaving virtually everything else released in the year 1980 in the dust, smoking the NWOBHM, steamrolling the new simplified Priest, really only AC/DC's landmark "Back in Black" and Ozzy's "Blizzard of Ozz" (honourable mention: the first Maiden) matching it's longevity and quality.

Losing singer Ozzy Osbourne, the face of the band, could have been their deathknell. Many bands who soldier on without their star vocalist do so by attempting to establish continuity, by begging fans to pretend nothing has changed. Sabbath were coming off their worst run of records ever, and what fans remained were largely fed up with their slidewise druggy bloat. Surprisingly, they turned this to their advantage, because it freed them up to go in an entirely new direction. No more experimentation, way more riffs, solos and melodrama, all concerned with making the shiny, rock-solid album that would bring them back to prominence. Bringing in the regal roar of ex-Rainbow vocalist Ronnie James Dio was a statement. Ronnie wasn't a hippie, Ronnie wasn't a drunk or an alcoholic, and Ronnie could sing. Not like Ozzy's charming "Aww, he's making people noises! Isn't that adorable?" singing, but actual honest to Beelzebub singing. You could assault a castle to his voice; no surprise then that he would pull the band into the moat metal sweetspot he'd been enjoying during his time with Ritchie Blackmore and company.

Tony Iommi is writing lightyears away from his previous doomy comfort zone, pre-power metal derived more from Purple and Rainbow than anything else. These compositions have a real epic feeling that Sabbath had never had, even on lengthy dirges like "The Writ" and "War Pigs", a feel of cinematic scope that was the realm of basically Priest, Rainbow, and a burgeoning Maiden alone. This is the way metal SHOULD feel. Ward is tight as a drum (ironically) despite his horribly wasted state, more of a standard time-keeping player than the progressive drumming monster last seen on Sabotage. Geezer Butler is as dynamic as ever, like Ward more inside the box (probably something to do with Ronnie's writing) but just amazing within the confines of a "normal" metal band.

This album would be worth it for the title track alone, which was once my favourite song ever, regardless of genre or period. There's almost no use talking about the thespian grandeur of it all, the incredible slow-build and the level of BAM!-connection you feel the moment Ronnie starts singing. It is instantaneous and unshakeable. Other highlights include "Lonely is the Word," the most insistently metallic track on the album. It has otherworldly soloing, powerful vocals, and most pertinently a raw scraping guitar tone that cuts the ear and wrings the neck. "Die Young" is emotional and unconventional speed metal, the soulful (!) vocals of RJD in the slow break and the passion of Iommi's playing lifting this soaring and piercing speedster into the pantheon, well above the whole wallowing power metal genre struggling to reach this high, to sound this damn good. "Neon Knights" is like a cleaned up "Symptom of the Universe" or "Kill the King" (Rainbow) part 2, hyper and clean with some amazing soloing from Tony and catchier-than-thou melodies up the wazoo.

Okay enough, I don't want to repeat the obvious. If you want more (and why wouldn't you...), e-mail me or something and I'll talk your ear off about this or any other album I've reviewed and a good many I've yet to. Suffice to say, Heaven and Hell is the last Sabbath classic (there are a few more good ones to go though) and maybe just shy of being the best damn thing they or anyone else ever recorded. Just buy the damned thing, it's essential Dio, Dio at his best, and also Dio in the middle of quite possibly the greatest hot-streak in metal history. R.I.P. to a true legend.

(Written 2004, Revised 2010)

For some strange reason, this is considered a classic, the best Ozzy-less Sabbath record, whatever whatever whatever. I think the main reason for all the hype is Ronnie James Dio, who admittedly is in strong voice here, even though his lyrics are hilariously ridiculous D&D ramblings. My favorite lyric is the infamous one from Children of the Sea, And then we did the DEMON DANCE!!!, which is one of the funniest things Ive heard in my life, not counting We made the mountain shake with laughter every day, which is also a lyric from Children of the Sea, and the I AM IRON MAN! intro from Iron Man. Ah, Black Sabbath. Prime comic material.

Anyway, Children of the Sea is actually pretty cool despite its ridiculousness (and I havent even gotten into the Gothic chanting or the Hotel California-like acoustic parts). Its got a nice guitar solo, if nothing else. And Neon Knights is a standout speed-metal number. Plus the camp factor, along with Dios coooool voice, keeps songs like Lady Evil from dipping into crappy banality, even if these songs are about as melodic as a broken vase. However, it still sounds to me like the groups just sitting back and relishing in their previous triumphs, only without the same quality as those triumphs. A good example is the seven-minute title track, with perhaps the most generic riff in Sabbaths whole history. Its one of Sabbaths most famous songs, but I dont think its really all that good. I think they dragged it out way too long. And hey, whats up with that disco bassline? And the fast parts are hardly better than the slow ones.

Lets see, what else? Well, ums, Im not too much of a fan of Wishing Well, other than some cool speedy soloing. Its fast, but its unimpressive. Die Young has a lame, stupid synth n guitar introduction and an even more stupid synth n guitar chorus, Walk Away is notable for being mindless, pointless, and dumb. I dont get anything out of Walk Away, in fact. At least Wishing Well and Die Young have some memorable stuff going on them, even if the memorable part of Walk Away is shit. And Lonely is the Word is notable only for ripping off the Stairway to Heaven chord sequence in the guitar solo, replacing the overdubbed guitars with crappy synths.

So it's no Paranoid (which is so much better than Sab's other releases it's hard to believe that they're by the same band), but at least it's no Born Again. However, it is the most overrated album in Sab's discog. I'd recommend it based on its reputation (chances are you'll enjoy it more than I do) and because of Ronnie's cool voice (hey, maybe I should listen to some Dio and Rainbow stuff!(nevermind, I don't like Rainbow)), but I don't get it.

By the time Ozzy Osbourne fled the Black Sabbath camp, no one held out much hope for the group. However, Tony Iommi wouldn't let the beast die. Scoring gold at the end of the Rainbow, the mighty Black Sabbath machine marched on with Ronnie James Dio fronting the group. Tony Iommi's dark, grinding, industrial strength riffs drive the epic eight cut monster. There isn't a lame song in the lot. The ambitious Heaven And Hell is a stellar heavy metal recording from the masters of gloom 'n' doom. The disc rivals the classics from Judas Priest during the late '70s/early '80s.

"Neon Knights" rolls the disc off to a roaring start. The title track pounds with authority... a riveting number that builds to a furious finish. Other killer cuts include "Die Young", "Lady Evil", "Wishing Well" and "Children of the Sea".

Martin Birch deserves big time props for his incredible production work, proving once again that the man knows how to command from the studio. Pure genius from the guru of metal production.

Unfortunately, it didn't take long for Ronnie Dio to wear out his welcome in the Sabbath camp. However, his tenure during the early '80s with Black Sabbath produced timeless heavy metal, as Heaven And Hell, and Mob Rules take no prisoners and give no quarter.

Heaven And Hell is the real deal. Play it loud 'n' play it often...
