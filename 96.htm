When I first graded this nearly two years ago, long before I wanted (or knew I'd have to) put words under my scores, I either looked at song titles and guessed what I'd enter or just felt like being picky. The first side is a bunch of great bluesy pop songs injected with soul, jazz and Bowie theatrics. "Starman" has one of Bowie's best hooks and "Soul Love" is just affecting, while "Moonage Daydream" flaunts his rock chops (more on this on the flip). "It Ain't Easy" mostly prepares you for exactly how heartbreaking the first song after those few seconds of silence between sides will be, but it's still a really solid song.

"Lady Stardust" gave us REO Speedwagon and "More Than Words" eventually, but that doesn't take away from the song's bro potential, or its status among Bowie worshipping college girls. While Ziggy Stardust is a narrative from start to finish it's really on this side that the plot becomes as pivotal as the music. The songs are all snappy and instantly memorable. It sounds like Bowie's attempt to do something like Abbey Road the way he blends extreme pop with more experimental melodies and arrangements around an abstract narrative. "Hang Onto Yourself" comes off like punk Beach Boys and "Ziggy Stardust" is immortal to the post-Guitar Hero generation (and people exist that prefer the cover version in the game?).

It was really easy to like this album as much as I did just now, and I feel like this is how much I liked this album when I had first discovered it, free of cynicism. So I'm going to assume it's good to be back, even if just for right now.

Ziggy Stardust is the Thriller of the 70s, except with more substance and no child molesters involved. It is a pop masterpiece which doubles as an interesting concept album. Ironically, if I am to think of my favorite concept albums, almost none of them come from progressive rock artists, who seem to, as a cliche', own the theme album concept. Not so though. Lou Reed's Berlin is still my favorite concept album-- hardly composed by a progressive artist-- and my other favorite would be Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars which, despite its excellent guitar playing and unique theme, is as pop as it gets.

Opener "Five Years" is one of Bowie's best written songs of all time, but the next two on the record rival it. "It Ain't Easy" is a personal favorite of mine, and I find myself often singing along. The title track is as memorable as each, as the melodies are particularly strong, track by track. This is the album that got my girlfriend into David Bowie, although like me, she has later resigned that Hunky Dory is in fact his best.


Rock 'n' Roll Suicide is the necessary closure for the concept, but it also makes for a strong song on its own. And such is the case the whole way through the album. While a lot of concept albums seem to require that the album be heard in context, I would like to contend that part of what makes Ziggy Stardust such a superior concept album to most is that each track is definitive with or without the concept. Truly a record done right. Clearly a favorite of mine.

You get over it in time (well, most of us do) but there is a large chunk of life when hype can kill a good album. Ziggy Stardust is perhaps the only album that has such massive acclaim and hyperbole about "this is when rock n' roll really started", and it can all work wonderfully for the snob and the uninitiated. Even odder, it is even better for the snob. Whatever you think this album was, once you hear "Five Years" you find it to be very different than you probably imagined it to be. It completely floors you and surprises you.

In the same way that a song off The White Album has just the right amount of familiar sounds, wow factor, and the simple-but-stunning feeling of knowing the song before ever hearing it, Ziggy Stardust has enough immediate pleasing to carry a lifetime or two until you start looking for depth. It’s a rare feat and I’m not sure if it makes an album/song necessarily better, but nearly all the songs on Ziggy Stardust feel so simple and familiar that you can swear you heard them before. The most recent song being Fiery Furnaces “Birdy Brain”, you can swear these songs were played to you as a child but your father never even owned a vinyl player. I think I found a reason.

These a lullabies and rockabies do their dance with a tad bit more than your mother’s voice, yet it has the very same effect. David Bowie has become supremely confident in his voice and strange qualities, while he has grown even stronger in his songwriting and overall direction. You can hum every Goddamn line like a cellphone and any true fan can hit the red buzzer and call that shit out. The best thing though? Ziggy Stardust becomes another flat book once it’s put back on the shelf and you are left with only the memories. This is a good explanation for the spotty, mega-ultra-hyperbole that this album has been victim to for so long. No one really knows what they are talking about, as it’s never there when they are writing about it. Even as a seasoned listener of Ziggy Stardust, I forget how these melodies evolve and explode time and time again. You pull it out from its tight place on the shelf, blow off the dust, and open it up. Oh, it’s a pop-up book!

+2 Vote downBookmarkVote up
I must be the only David Bowie fan on the planet who doesn't have a favorite Bowie "period," so to speak. I mean, I absolutely adore the man, but my likings of him are all over the place. Ziggy Stardust is just barely edged out by Station To Station when it comes to my favorite album of his, and Scary Monsters is right on its heels as well. Then again, I couldn't really say that I have a special affinity for either the glam or the Berlin period, as somewhat inexplicably, both Hunky Dory and Low leave me rather cold. But nevertheless, although it may not be the first Bowie album I usually reach for nowadays, particularly since I normally crave his disco-funk and quirk-pop phases over glam, which is too rich for me to listen to constantly, Ziggy Stardust holds a special place in my heart since this was the album that nearly four years ago first introduced me to the wonders of the Thin White Duke. I got into Bowie a bit later than some other favorites of mine since at 15, I was more sensitive to my parents' tastes and the only song of his my dad professed to like was "Young Americans"—that should tell you quite a lot about his tastes—and Ziggy Stardust impressed me so much upon hearing it for the first time that for a long time this was the only Bowie album I owned...although that could also have to do with how bloody expensive his CDs are. I'd heard "Suffragette City" on the radio for the first time not long before getting this album, and I was not disappointed with the rest, a delicious, lush mix of well-crafted tunes that I still greatly enjoy to this day.

Some people want to call Ziggy Stardust a concept album, but there is no real thread that ties all the songs together, just recurrent lyrical themes of space (an astounding four songs on the album contain the word "star" in some way), the impending apocalypse, rock 'n' roll and androgyny. The Spiders from Mars, particularly Mick Ronson on guitar, were in fine playing form throughout the album, delivering meaty musical backgrounds for Bowie's fey near-falsetto to prance around on, embellished by theatrical flourishes of piano, strings and organ. Picking favorites is nearly impossible on this album, as Bowie's gift for memorable hooks that first showed itself on the singles from Hunky Dory came into full flower on Ziggy Stardust. The opener, "Five Years," is a piano-driven warning to the public to prepare for alien destruction; as people continue to blithely go about their business, it churns along like a waltzing drinking song with the boisterous singalong at the end. "Moonage Daydream" is the first introduction of Ziggy himself, the gender-bending alien sex god: "I'm an alligator, I'm a mama-papa coming for you/I'm a space invader, I'll be a rockin'-rollin' bitch for you!" Strings swell as the song comes to a climax, and ends with a blistering guitar solo. "Lady Stardust" is probably the closest thing to a ballad on the album, a loving tribute to Bowie's friend and rumored lover, fellow glam rocker Marc Bolan of T. Rex. "Hang On To Yourself" is a perfect song to dance frenetically around the room to, speeding along at a breakneck pace with its handclaps and its relentless, immortal bassline. The title track flashes between physical admiration of Ziggy himself (full of many descriptions that could very well fit Bowie himself, "screwed-up eyes and screwed-down hairdo," and a "snow-white tan") in the verses and the quarreling that caused the band to break up in the chorus. "Suffragette City" is one of the most memorable songs of the glam era, and for good reason: anchored by a zooming keyboard, playful guitar and rhythmic piano, along with Bowie's catchy lyrics about being down on one's luck but not seeming to mind, it is very hard to not get it stuck in your head in the best way possible. Bringing things to a perfect closer, "Rock 'n' Roll Suicide" starts with only an acoustic guitar but quickly builds to near-orchestral grandeur as strings back Bowie as he makes an impassioned plea to a friend not to surrender to death: "YOU'RE NOT ALONE! 'Cause you're wonderful...give me your hands!"

Though he would soon move away from glam, and prove that no two of his albums ever completely sound alike, in creating Ziggy Stardust, David Bowie pulled off the rare feat of making an album that's just as good when taken as a whole and when listening to the individual songs. He would go on to create much more wonderful music, but Ziggy Stardust is his first true masterpiece.
