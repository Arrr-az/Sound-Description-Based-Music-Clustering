Each half closes on its worst song, but in each case they're also welcome as changes of pace from Bowie's futuristic funk/disco concoction. "Word on a Wing" is a basic Bowie ballad, while "Wild Is the Wind" goes for (and achieves) campy excess. But the lead-up to each is some of the most brilliant record-making Bowie ever engaged in. Title cut moves through a few phases - each of them compelling - over its 10 minutes and "Golden Years" is a stone classic of his catalog. Second half kicks off with one of my top 3 Bowie tunes - the impenetrable "TVC15" and the earnest rocker "Stay" before it hits its Nina Simone camp/tribute. But it flows like an album should, moving through different moods and paced brilliantly to afford some dramatic ups and downs. More and more as I get older I'm thinking that it's more important how a record sounds while it's playing than how much you're poring over it later, and this sounds like one of his best to me.

Although Bowie had been a trendsetter since the release of Ziggy Stardust, none of his albums from the early-70's could be properly qualified as "experimental". That had obviously changed by the time of his Berlin Trilogy, which famously brought together aspects of mainstream rock and soundscape-driven electronic music, but seeds of Bowie's genre-busting experimentalism actually began one year earlier. Enter 1976's Station to Station, which stands as one of the most significant transitional albums of the decade. It's an album that somehow bridges the gap between the blue-eyed soul of Young Americans and the full-on art rock of Low, all while maintaining a cohesive identity of its own. It may fall short of Bowie's best releases from the era (see Hunky Dory, Ziggy Stardust, and Low), but it's nearly as important to his overall career arc. An overwhelmingly great release that's essential listening for every sort of Bowie fan.

Station to Station bears considerable similarity to the vocal-led tracks that constitute the A-side of Low, but its stylistic scope is broader than that half of Low. On one hand, you have the slow-burning title track, which marries the "cold" production of Low with a multipart vocal melody that sounds unlike anything else in Bowie's discography. Definitely not progressive rock, but just as ambitious from a compositional standout. And then you have tracks like "TVC 15" and "Stay", which extend the funkiest aspects of Young Americans in a direction that could be described as proto-art-punk. Undoubtedly a primary influence on Television's Marquee Moon, which was recorded later in 1976. Balancing things out are "Golden Years", a comparatively straightforward art rock tune that was a clear choice for lead single, and a pair of soul-influenced yet atypically-accompanied ballads ("Word on a Wing", "Wild Is the Wind"). It all makes for what is arguably Bowie's most diverse offering of the decade. Equal parts experimental and accessible, held together by a distinctive mix of funk, soul, and spacey pop instrumentation.

I've always placed Station to Station near the top of Bowie's "second-tier". In particular, I'd rank it somewhere alongside Diamond Dogs and Scary Monsters as a minor classic of the era, trailing only Hunky Dory, Ziggy Stardust, and (parts of) Low. The biggest asset of Station to Station is undoubtedly its overall vibe: Bowie's Thin White Duke persona doesn't get as much notoriety as Ziggy Stardust, but the Duke's uber-suave vocal stylings and sardonic lyricism are at least on par with anything from the Ziggy era. Special kudos also go out to the album's production (largely the work of Bowie itself), which is clinically precise without sounding lifeless. Epitomizing all of these strengths are the album's title track, which might be the most compositionally engaging thing in Bowie's entire discography. "Golden Years" and "TVC 15" are also fantastic, proving that Station to Station is at its best when Bowie pairs his adventurous musical ideas with catchy vocal melodies.

Of course, the problem is that the melodies off Station to Station aren't quite as memorable as those from Bowie's best releases. This manifests primarily with "Words on a Wing" and "Wild Is the Wild", which underwhelm me at least as much as the ballads off of Young Americans. Both tracks have their moments, as with the falsetto-laden climaxes off "Words on a Wing", but neither track comes close to justifying its extended, six-minute runtime. For whatever reason, I also revisit Station to Station less than most Bowie albums of the era. And when I do find time for the record, I'm oftentimes tempted to stop listening after the title track. I'm forced to conclude that Station to Station is a record that works better in concept than in practice: not quite a case of "style of substance", but a record where Bowie's most ambitious tendencies occasionally detract from the overall flow of the music.

Despite a pair of ballads that have never won me over, the rest of Station to Station is fantastic enough to earn the album a 4.0-star rating. Not an all-time classic, and definitely not a good starting point within Bowie's discography, but strongly recommended to anyone who enjoys the Berlin Trilogy or the Ziggy Stardust era.

7.9/10

Equipped with insane amounts of cocaine, Bowie and friends deconstructed the Philly soul of his previous record. There is a haunting accuracy in the intro of the record, which is the sound of incoming trains indicating the arrival of the latest Bowie persona, the harrowing Thin White Duke. The Duke is basically his disco persona, who went through a soul removal procedure by Nazi scientists. The Duke hates Love and everything good, he wants to conquer the world. Charming fella :D

Musically it draws a lot from Krautrock with synthesizers and noisey guitars. The title track of course is one of Bowies finest tracks, clocking in at 10 minutes. It progresses through four movements, the beginns avantgarde sounds of the trains. Then some kind of villain song begins, a slow, menacing groove. The third movement is a faster art rock, almost musical-like section introducing Roy Bittan's percussive piano (what the heck is that guy doing here?). The final movement evolves around a disco beat interpolated with a noisey guitar.

Golden Years then is the hit, a funk song, completely arrogant and soulless. Word on a Wing is the complete opposite, an out of place ballad to god in which Bowie slips past the Thin White Duke persona as if emitting a cry for help. I love this song.

TVC15 is a funny vignette about a girl being swallowed during a drug binge. The music is very unusual, with crescendos and weird guitar lines. Stay is the second cocaine funk song. Wild is the Wind is the second ballad, originally a movie song.

Bowie claimed he could not remember making this album. I can imagine why.

8.5/10

Everybody's got one of those albums that mean so much to them, they feel no amount of words can do it justice. That no matter how many superlatives you attatch to its name, you'll always feel like you're underrating it. To me, Station to Station is just that. Its epic scope is unmatched, with so much complexity you can't grasp in the first handful of listens. Its sheer diversity touching on every corner Bowie has explored up to this point in his career, and perfecting them, while exploring yet more avenues. Bowie being at his technical best with the most sympathetic vocal performances you'll hear on a pop record. As a previous reviewer described, Station to Station is his cry for help(although it is unclear what, seeing as though the lyrics are so severely fucked and not of this world). And that's not to mention the band, with masterful guitar work and basslines that are so deliciously funky, its makes one sad that they never would feature on another Bowie album again.

This time round, Bowie trades quality for quantity. Sure, Bowie has always had pretty steller albums on terms of consistency, but none accumalite this amount of mindblowing songs collected to a single album. There's many records I consider all killer no filler, but the difference here is that all six tracks here are downright classics. For the lack of material, there's so much layers and complexity to the individual tracks, they feel like the amount of energy and emotion put into these songs could have put this at a full lentghed album.

No better song to prove this than the opener. "Station to Station" starts off it's first half as a very simplistic progressive funk track. But for the second half, it bursts into a montage of about five different melodies. All as catchy as the next, and to think Bowie could have made yet more spellbinding singles to add to his discography, he uses them to create his very own "Stairway to Heaven" (sorry for the cliche). "Golden Years", arguably the only well known single here, is the pop highlight here. Although its far from accessible when compared to other singles. It's a very disco-esque song and sounds 80-ish - more proof why Bowie was ahead of his time. The verses have Bowie crooning until it bursts into a terrific and ever so catchy chorus. "Word on a Wing" is without a doubt the highlight for me. Another song where the verse has an entire different meoldy to the hook. The verses have a positive beat to it and Bowie almost sounding hopeful, until it hits the chorus where it takes a complete 180 turn. Bowie almost sounds like he's breaking down here, whilst still retaining those incredible vocals. It loses its optimistic melody rather for a much more darker, heartbreaking piano loop. The entire song hits you so hard by the chorus, and truly has a lasting effect on me. "TVC15" doesn't seem to get much love here, but it's without a doubt the second best track here for me. Only recently have I found what a weirdly captivating and what such fun it is to sing along with it. Yet again, for an album lacking them, Bowie really kicks out a killer hook, and once it's embedded in your brain, you will seriously go out of your way just to listen to this one again. If you was to put a gun to my head and ask me what the low point was, I'd hesitate, but I would eventually say "Stay". But of course, thats not to take away from the song. To me, it always feels like this should have been the lead single. Its probably the most accessible here, and being that Station to Station's primary aspect is funk, "Stay" is certainly the funkiest here. "Wild is the Wind" is the second and closing ballad here, and probably the most hard hitting performances you're likely to here. It's a cover, but this song has Bowie written all over it and he's truly made it his own that I have no intention in listening to the original.

It took me a good while to warm up to this one compared to previous efforts. It wasn't that I couldn't get into it, it's just the melodies seemed too simple and lacking catchiness. I remember thinking why do even people label this as pop? But how wrong I was. I eventually grasped the hooks and insturmentals and came to worship this album. You know Station to Station is of grand scale when even the pop songs are complex and ambitious masterpieces. The sheer scope Bowie has achieved here is awe inspiring, and he wasn't even aware of it!

Key track: Golden Years
Personal faves: Word on a Wing, TVC15, Wild is the Wind

Station to Station is where David Bowie peaked as an artist, but hit rock bottom as a human being.

A man starved to the bone, wearing a white shirt and a waistcoat, approaches the stage. He snorts 5 lines of cocaine before performing the 6 most important songs of his career, but no one is cheering, cause they're still baffled by the Nazi-salute..[...]

The Mad Aristocrat
The man, the myth, the genius. We all have different perceptions of Bowie, some hailing everything he's ever done, while some prefer certain parts of his career, and then there's the bunch who despise him like the plague. My question to those who belong in the first group: doesn't it take an extreme amount of dedication to do such a thing? The man after all released Never Let Me Down, something that, despite the title, let everyone down. Well, we all have different opinions about the guy, which seems fair if you look at the range of styles he covered. He took several aliases, one being 'The Thin White Duke'. The surface of this character might seem like an upgrade from some of Bowie's earlier personas, but underneath the surface laid a fierce and, as some might say, repulsive figure. You can read interviews, and hear stories about a fallen man, that Bowie was out of sync with reality, and even himself. A man who's just snorted a mountain of cocaine can not be taken seriously, but his music can still be heard. This album therefore tears down a morally satisfying myth, the one that says 'drugs never lead to anything'.. Cause this album was made by drugs, so no one, not even Bowie himself, can say whether it was him or Cocaine who created this masterpiece.

The Amoral Zombie
Six songs make up the most satisfying experience I've ever had with Bowie, and that surprises even me, but there's no denying it. Hunky Dory is for the calm persona, Ziggy Stardust is for the high one, Low is for the experimental one, and Station to Station is for the fucked up one. Six songs almost make it seem like Bowie were minimizing himself for a theme, but it turns out he covered more ground in 10 minutes than he had done in the previous 10 years. It might turn out that "Station to Station" was the most perfected improv session ever recorded, cause it almost sounds like 10 minutes of controlled cocaine chaos. It's no surprise that "Golden Years" became the hit from this album, I imagine that this is how a Bee Gees song would have sounded if they had a fling of artistic integrity. Every song on this album seems to be a part of some weird cohesive project Bowie was conducting, but at the same time they seem random and just another case of how many lines of coke till we reach the finish line. "Station to Station" and "Golden Years" end on "Word on a Wing", and "TVC15" and "Stay" ends on "Wild is the Wind", probably makes no sense, but that's how I usually arrange it in my head. Why? Cause "Word on a Wing" and "Wild is the Wind" are the two most beautiful things Bowie has ever done. With the latter he proves that he can handle and even improve a song that he hasn't written himself, and on the former he proves that what is sung can never be as important as how it is sung.

The Emotionless Aryan Superman
Much have been written about Bowie's state of mind during his 'Thin White Duke' period, and some of it is even disturbing. Like his flirt with fascism, which he later blamed solely on drugs. I don't believe for a second that Bowie was a fascist, but that he at a point in his career took on a persona that almost destroyed him, well, that I can believe. That he in this destruction process even touched a notion of far-right fascination? clearly plausible, but let's not forget that this could have something to do with some kind of blank generation void that sailed the dark oceans of late 70's cling to reality. I dunno what this story is all about, and have barely read anything on the matter, so I'll leave it all to the fanatics. In my mind, the music speaks for itself, and never have music spoken so clearly as it does on Station to Station.

The pretty conventional ho-hum club of Young Americans has an interesting secret to it. If you stay long enough in the club until it's late beyond late, a witching hour beyond witching hour, then shit gets really really weird. It's pretty normal at first, but you slowly start to lose your sense of place and people. Things start to blur and contort just a bit. First the arrangements lose their Funk and soul backup singers, the Soul remains but it starts to get a bit stilted and paranoid. The walls start to turn from their normal form to some sort of subterranean industrial art piece. The singer on the stage begins to look less like a dapper white guy, and more like a calmly psychotic gentlemanly Nazi aristocrat. He begins to sing less with Soul than with sophisticated understated derangement. And there's no way you can mistake the mountains of white powder that are now piled high on the back of the stage. It's like the simple soul club slipped and fell down the back of the night into somewhere right in the middle of a pleasant dream and a nightmare, at an awkward angle, and just kept the show going. It's goddamned awesome. What exactly twisted Bowie into this within about a year since his pedestrian work on Young Americans I'm not entirely sure, though the honest too easy answer would seem to be the obscene amount of cocaine. I mean he really was a zombie of sorts during this period, I've heard he doesn't even remember the sessions for this album at all. That's pretty insane, all the more because of how very very good they are. Bowie's made lots of great stuff thus far in his career and would after, but these are high tightest efforts he'd ever make! And he doesn't even recall the work! His performance, the songwriting, the arrangements, all perfect. Granted there's only six songs (with one of those being ten minutes at least), but like later with the Wiper's Youth of America, I absolutely do NOT give a fuck. Unlike last time Bowie has definitely delivered a full and utterly satisfying package here. To the point that I wish more acts would take the relative risk and release shortish albums of just really focused fully developed killer songs. All killer no filler might just mean sacrificing length of time, and this IS all killer no filler folks. All six songs are memorable and fantastic, if not outright masterpieces. I'd tell Bowie to get badly hooked on coke again if I wasn't a more moral man. And it's more than very high tight construction here, much more. The very sound of the album I hinted at above is really neat, it's such a strange transitional mutant, the very opposite of what he was doing last time and easily his most original sound he ever played with. It's kind of hard to describe beyond what I already did, some sort of askew slightly mutated version of Soul and Rock that has no easy comparison. It's clearly mutated sure, but just as much it's not so mutated that you can't help but kind of not really notice TOO much. It's like how in dreams even if everything's crazy around you, you usually take it for granted and don't see it as strange. That's the sensation this gives, and few albums have that sort of thing going for them, particularly with such natural eerie ease. To me perhaps, the twist this has to it highly suggests that Bowie is already leaning and falling forward into his Berlin period. That's right, Station to Station is the true first installment of the Berlin Trilogy of lore. Why people count Lodger I'll never know, it has so little claim to the title that it blows my mind and than some. This on the other hands definitely shows Bowie taking his guitars places that have a sort of odd German edge to them (the title track especially), and hell even the lyrics! The European canon is here? It certainly is! Bowie was still in America but was wicked homesick even back in 1973 with Aladdin Sane, his insane downward spiral into the creepy Nazi esque Thin White Duke character seems as much inspired by this sickness as the blow. TVC15 is at least as strange with it's theme and more Euro inspired music. Maybe that's part of what makes this so odd and lovable? The off kilter hybrid of black/american musics and white/european musics? Maybe. In many ways this feels like a huge moment in Rock music, the very start of a major tilt toward an avalanche of things that would change the genre forever, a tilt toward pouring in German influences into the British canon. With this tilt, in would pour electronic musics (with Low), Krautrock, and just something altogether extremely fresh. Station to station indeed, the Punks may have been the soldiers of revolution, but Bowie (along with Eno) would be the intellectuals at the head of the storming of the Rock bastille.

Rating: 5
Highlights: Station to Station, Golden Years, TVC15, Stay
