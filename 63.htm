Una de las ventajas de la música moderna, santa ventaja, es la reproducción y el consumo a nivel de bolsillo que permite repetir cuantas veces haga falta. El disco en cuestión me paso bastante desapercibido en su momento. La repetición del éxito "Loser" desenfocó el repertorio del disco. Cuando le vi en directo, un frio domingo de noviembre 1994, todos esperábamos ese "so why don't you kill me" para poder irnos a casa a calentar la cama. El asunto de la comercialización permite que ahora pueda recuperar el disco con el estómago tranquilo, reescucharlo y degustar lo que no hice en su momento. Como leí por ahí, coincido. Beck es uno de los artistas más decisivos de la década de los 90's, uno de los que se jugó el tipo por sumar estilos que parecían antitéticos, que, de hecho, lo eran, pero que el forzó para hacerlos amigos. El disco en cuestión, si en su momento, 1994, sonó incomprensible, ahora, con la información del futuro en la cabeza, suena clarividente. Como una auténtica epifanía. Así, estoy dispuesto a fumar crack cada vez que toque ver MTV o follarme una montaña con el cráneo por muy difícil que parezca. Dios salve a Beck y le de un montón de salud. Por muy perdedor que se sienta.

Zephos Jan 18 2012
+6 Vote downBookmarkVote up
If you want to understand why my generation (and Gen X too) doesn't get along with the boomers look no further. Beck's first proper album, the one that sees him emerging out of his start in the anti-folk scene. The anti-folk scene. Take your olden days Joan Baez style East Village folky earnest protest hippie music (the people who scorned Dylan when he went electric or wrote funny lyrics), it kept around and probably is still around, but in the 90's? Well as they say, the times they were a changin'. And new generational mentalities were sneaking on up. And those new folks, well they were mighty tired of the bunch of blowhards singer songwriter types sitting around bohemia out earnesting eachother. And so they did things. Sacreligious things. Mr Beck? What does he do with the sacred folk music? Well let's just say if those early 60's types who raged at Dylan for having electric guitars heard this album they would be pulverized by the pure anger that would well up in their heads. Because what Beck's done with folk is ...fuck, where to begin. He's poured in a half pound of snark, sarcasm, deadpan, self obsessed mope, humor, redneck jokes...basically mocking the overly serious snobs with their own music and that's just the subject matter! The actual music is where the real blasphemy sneaks in! Because Beck's mixing folk with some VERY un-folk things. There's the wacko noise experiments side of things that hums around, and then there's the hip-hop. Oh yes. The hip-hop. From some angles this may even be considered a hip-hop album (and you can trust me to know the difference). There's a healthy dose of samples, drum machines with some pretty jeep ready thumping, and well...rapping even. Considering hip-hop in 94' this was pretty unorthodox for rap as much as it was folk, pretty much only Divine Styler was making hip-hop esque material this left afield at the time (though that would change quickly). The result of all this mutation and blaspheming is a stunningly strange and really darn original mix. Sure it is borrowing tricks and trades from both the Prince Pauls and Dust Brothers of the world, and the original sarcastic sacreligious rock man, Frank Zappa... but the result is still definitely Beck's own bizarre baby at the end of the day. What's more, Beck had a hit on his hands with it! That's so 90's isn't it! Say thanks to Nirvana for the money Beck. I mean what other decade would reward with fame an album upending sacred classic rock music trends with cover art of some kind of horned robot cow skull monster marching in front of an apocalyptic sky? Beck was definitely a bit ahead of his time (not nowhere near as much as Zappa of course), because this type of genre smashing sarcastic shit was not to become common place until the next century! Give this man some props. His songs are as fun as they are strange and confusing, with some genuine pop songs forming out of the swirling hodge podge (everyone's heard Loser right?). Think back to how everything sounded ten years before this. That's change folks.

Rating: 4
Highlights: Loser, Fuckin' With My Head, Soul Suckin' Jerk, Beercan

My freshman year of college Beck was THE man. I worshipped the ground he walked on and thought he was a musical genius. Here I stand four years later and while I am a bit jaded as to Beck's genius status, I still do appreciate his work and I enjoy a lot of it. All in all this album isn't that great, it's entertaining and it shows promise, but regardless of what many say, I don't think it's Beck's best work, or even close for that matter. I understand it's a crazy mix of bluegrass, hip-hop, punk and whatever else was going through Beck's head. But because it's ambitious doesn't mean it's good. I think it took some time for Beck to grow into things as an artist. There are standout tracks, to me "Fuckin With My Head" being the best, but then there are also some downer moments where things are just a bit weirdly bad. This album is worth listening to for any fans, and definitely for those children of the 90's, but I think on the whole it's a pretty passable album in comparison to Guero, Odelay or even dare I say The Information.

The year is 1994. It's the summer. I'm driving in my car, listening to my favorite alternative station. A Smashing Pumpkins hit just finished up, and now I'm hearing what sounds like a hip-hop beat. I wonder why there's hip-hop on the alternative station, but hey, it's pretty cool anyway. The thing that really strikes me about it is the beat. It sounds almost like someone was using an old blues-rock lick here, an Allman Brothers riff or something like that, except that there's also a sitar. All this to a hip-hop beat.

The lyrics start, and they take me aback once again. I have no idea what this guy with the lazy-sounding voice is talking about, and while he's not exactly a great rapper, his free-association rhymes are still a blast. Within the first verse, he's claimed to be a monkey, found a way to work butane in there, brought up a shotgun wedding, spray-painted vegetables and... well, I'm not exactly following, but like I said before, it's a good time, because hey, I've always liked completely random lyrics. The chorus, which I'm singing along with even though I've never heard the song before, has this half-Spanish thing going, and it makes "I'm a loser baby, so why don't you kill me?" sound like the catchiest phrase ever uttered. Is this a dig at grunge, I wonder? But then the second verse comes around, and manages to make even less sense than the first, and it suddenly hits me to just forget the lyrics and go with it. Besides, it's not like anyone who throws in the toss-off line "get crazy with the cheez whiz" wants to be taken seriously, right?

Okay, so it's a bit stupid, and it's a bit of a novelty, but it's a great song anyway, so I don't care. That's what I tell myself as it wraps up. The DJ comes on, and after announcing that he just learned Pearl Jam has an album on schedule for release in December '94, he gives me the goods on the song that I just heard. "Loser," it's called. By Beck. I've never heard of this Beck guy before. He must be an up-and-coming talent. I still have no idea why this is being played on alternative radio, but once again, I love it, so it doesn't really matter. I immediately decide that I need the album this is on. I mean, how cool would it be to pop the cassette in and play "Loser" as loud as possible? I decide that picking my brother up from soccer practice can wait a while. I MUST have this song.

So I head on over to Tower Records. The place costs too damn much, but it's right there, so I go anyway and I blow all my hard-earned money on music. My parents tell me I should be saving for college, but you try saving when you hear this great song on the radio every third day. So I walk up to the guy at the counter, and ask him if he knows what Beck album "Loser" is on. He points me towards Mellow Gold.

"I warn you, though," he says. "The rest of the album sounds nothing like it."

So anyway, I get it, and I head back to my car. I'm already five minutes late for my brother, so I speed just a little so I don't come home to a screaming lecture. I pop the tape in, and it starts off with "Loser," so I'm all excited, because it's "Loser" and all. Now, despite what the guy at Tower Records says, I'm expecting more like "Loser," but I don't get anything like it. The next one sounds like a Bob Dylan song, followed by a weird slice of freaky folk, some perfectly normal folk, and a bizarre sing-along with some seriously creepy voices. I like 'em, though! I mean, yeah, they're weird, but in a cool, different sort of way. I especially like the singalong one, since I'm working for a soul-sucking jerk at an ice cream place right now, and I hate it. My brother, who I picked up midway through the second one and who wasn't happy with me showing up late, disagrees. But he's, like, six, so it doesn't matter.

The next two I'm not so big on. The sixth sounds like the five before it if the five before it were really shitty songs, but they're not! They're great! And this one blows! The next tune blows even worse. I find myself wishing that there was an easier way to skip over songs I don't like, but sadly, that's not how cassettes work. My brother agrees - if I wasn't so much bigger than him, he probably would've thrown the cassette out the window long ago. I just find myself wishing that he doesn't pick up on the phrase "fuckin' with my head" that's all over track three. I don't think my parents would be happy with that.

I get home, and of course mom's all pissed at me because I was so late getting the young 'un and she had all these things she wanted me to do at home and a lot of other shit I really don't care about. Me, I just want to finish Mellow Gold. Although I'm really having my doubts now. Two complete shitters in a row. Not an exciting prospect for the rest. Well, albums often totally die after side one ends, let's hear how they... HOLY SHIT WHERE HAS "BEERCAN" BEEN HIDING THIS ENTIRE ALBUM? AND WILL IT STAY LIKE THIS? Oh. No it won't. There's a decent Indian-type dirge with funny kazoos, an endearingly freaky folk thing, a bizarre metal concoction that doesn't quite fit, and an awful Indian-type dirge with no funny kazoos. I stop the tape right after that one ends, too. I've had way too many bad experiences with albums that drop nasty surprises on you when you don't do that.

So I'm left completely unsure of Mellow Gold. "Loser" ruled pretty hard, and I quite liked the Dylan-y one and "Beercan," too, but the rest was... I don't know. I really don't know about this Beck guy. Maybe in a few years he'll put it together and release something great, but in the meantime, we'll just have to wait. Oh well. "In the time of the chimpanzees I was a monkey, butane in my veins and I'm out to cut the junkie..."

(author's note: none of this actually happened to me)

This is a near masterpiece - a soundtrack for Generation X, and the period of the early 90s that found so many of us (myself included) leaving college completely disaffected, disillusioned and disappointed with the prospects for a future as good as our parents have it. I still believe the basic tenets of that position: the baby boomers have totally screwed America, and their kids' generation. From government and corporate corruption to being the first generation to making single-parent families the norm, they have already left their indelible stain on each of our lives. I could go on and on, about the rise of special-interest groups who hide behind a "cause" that was long ago recognized and corrected, only to continue forward with increasingly ridiculous agendas for no other purpose than to sustain the high lifestyle of their management who contribute nothing to society and have no concern for whether their touted policies and agendas are good for America as a whole (AARP, Unions, PETA, MADD, many environmental groups). But that's not what we're here for. Beck is something of a scrapyard inventor. He cobbles together a little bit of a lot of things that no one wants anymore, and combines them to create pretty incredible - and fairly accessible - music. On Mellow Gold, he runs the gamut from folk to blues to rock to rap, often within the same song, and yet it remains tight and coherent. On top of that, he features out-of-date and obscure musical equipment, sounds, and samples. Beck has created a sound unto himself. "Loser", of course, is the slacker anthem, using a rap/boogie beat, catchy blues slide guitar, and cut-and-paste pop-culture lyrics. It still holds up today, but this is a great album. "Soul Sucking Jerk" is just a badass stomp about a crappy job working for an asshole (had a few of those). "Truckdrivin ..." is freaking hilarious! Featuring 1/2 speed vocals, and the making fun of rednecks (had a few of those neighbors). On to side two, "Beercan" is every bit the song that "Loser" is ... superfunky. "Nitemare Hippy Girl" is a dead-on description of "that girl" that anyone who went to college in the early 90s knew, and another top-shelf track. Look - unless you're totally allergic to anything outside the basic rock vox/drum/bass/elec guitar vibe, you really should own this.
